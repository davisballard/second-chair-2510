## Two Audiences, Two Registers

Second Chair speaks to two audiences in two completely different registers. The B2B audience (attorneys) and the B2C audience (claimants, via Fair Case) require different messages, different tones, and different creative — but the B2C ad IS the B2B proof. They are the same creative act.

---

## B2B Audience: The Decision-Maker

### Jennifer Walsh (Archetype)

Managing attorney of a 3–6 person PI firm. She runs the practice, controls the marketing spend, and makes vendor decisions herself. She may have a paralegal who filters calls — but if someone gets through, it's her on the other line.

She entered PI law because she believed in fighting for people. She has spent years buying leads from vendors whose advertising she would never show to a client. She knows the category is broken. She has said so — privately, to a colleague over drinks. She has never found the alternative.

**What she is NOT looking for:**
- Inspiration
- A brand that shares her values
- Another vendor pitch

**What she IS looking for:**
- Someone who can deliver what they promise without requiring constant verification
- A vendor she does not feel she needs to be defended against
- Proof before belief

**What she currently thinks:**
*"Every vendor says they're different. None of them are. I don't know what this company is actually doing to generate their leads, and I've learned not to ask because the answer is either evasive or something I don't want to know."*

**What we want her to think:**
*"This is the first vendor who showed me what they do before asking me to sign anything. I can see how they reach people. I can see that the advertising is something I could stand behind. I want to try it."*

**One behavior change:** She agrees to see the advertising before she commits to anything.

### The Four Buying Moments

Jennifer becomes a buyer at four specific moments:

1. **The 2 AM Ceiling Math** — Alone with the numbers, cases in, revenue out. Maximum buying readiness, zero defenses.
2. **The Peer Hallway Recommendation** — "I've been using Second Chair and it's actually working." Most efficient conversion available.
3. **The Switching Trigger** — Discovered "exclusive" leads sold to three firms. Compliance warning. Active buyer for 2–4 weeks.
4. **The Sales Call That Understands the Practice** — Sasha's call demonstrates SC knows contingency fees, Q1 seasonality, soft tissue case mix. Peer conversation, not vendor pitch.

## B2C Audience: The Claimant

### Maria (Archetype)

Early 30s. Had an accident two weeks ago. Received a call from the insurance company. Hasn't responded. Not sure what she has or what she should do. She's not "a personal injury claimant" in her own head — she's someone whose life got disrupted and who doesn't want to make a mistake.

**What she is NOT looking for:**
- A lawyer
- To "sue someone"
- An aggressive advocate

**What she IS looking for:**
- Information
- What she actually has before she decides anything
- To not be taken advantage of

**What she currently thinks:**
*"I don't know if I have a case. I don't know if I'm the kind of person who sues. I just want to know what I'm entitled to before I say yes or no to anything."*

**What we want her to think:**
*"That ad was talking directly to me. I made the call because it felt like the right thing to do — not because I was scared into it."*

**One behavior change:** She makes one call before she makes any decision.

## B2C Timing Tiers

The claimant's emotional state and decision context change dramatically based on timing. The advertising must speak to who they are in that specific moment.

### Tier 1 — Days 0–30 (Decision Fully Open)

| Attribute | Value |
|---|---|
| **Emotional state** | Confused, uncertain, possibly in pain, worried about money |
| **Decision state** | Haven't accepted or declined anything. May not know they have a case. |
| **Self-identity** | Someone who got hurt. NOT "a claimant." |
| **What they need** | One specific fact. One minimum-barrier action. |
| **Creative approach** | Information that respects their intelligence. "Before you respond — hear this." |

### Tier 2 — 1–6 Months (Accumulation Phase)

| Attribute | Value |
|---|---|
| **Emotional state** | Financial anxiety. Bills accumulating. Situation unresolved. |
| **Decision state** | May have accepted first offer and regret it. Or still negotiating. |
| **Self-identity** | Someone living with consequences. |
| **What they need** | To know they may still have options. |
| **Creative approach** | Acknowledge the accumulation. State what's possible. |

### Tier 3 — 6+ Months to SOL (Deadline Approaching)

| Attribute | Value |
|---|---|
| **Emotional state** | May not know there's a deadline. Not emotional — informational need. |
| **Decision state** | Haven't acted. May still have a viable case. |
| **Self-identity** | Someone who put it off. |
| **What they need** | A fact: the deadline, stated precisely. |
| **Creative approach** | Informational, not emotional. State the SOL deadline specifically by state. Do NOT manufacture fear. |

## The 99%/1% Mandate

Under Meta Special Ad Categories, demographic and interest targeting is eliminated. This is the defining constraint of B2C advertising for Second Chair.

**What this means:** Creative is the only targeting mechanism. The advertising must be specific enough that the 1% of people who had a recent accident self-select in, while the 99% who didn't self-select out.

**How creative self-selection works:**
- The ad contains a specific, recognizable detail about the claimant's situation
- Someone who hasn't had an accident scrolls past — it doesn't apply to them
- Someone who HAS had an accident stops — it speaks directly to their reality
- This is creative self-selection, not platform targeting

**What this prohibits:**
- Generic "Were you in an accident?" hooks (too broad, triggers everyone's curiosity)
- Demographic targeting by age, gender, zip code (platform restrictions)
- Interest-based targeting (not available under Special Ad Categories)
- Custom audiences built on health/injury signals
- Lookalikes based on injury lead data

**What IS available:**
- Broad geographic targeting (DMA-level)
- Age range (18+)
- Language
- Creative that does the qualifying

## Channel Strategy

### B2C: Meta is Primary

**Meta (Facebook + Instagram) = 95–100% of B2C ad spend.**

This is not a diversification play. Meta is where the claimant audience lives, and Special Ad Categories restrictions apply uniformly regardless of platform. The creative self-selection strategy is optimized for Meta's feed environment.

**Why not other platforms:**
- **Google Search:** High intent but $100–$500+ per click. Different buying motion — the claimant is already searching for a lawyer. Second Chair's differentiation is in reaching people BEFORE they search.
- **YouTube:** Potential for Tier 1 (pre-roll informational), but secondary to Meta feed.
- **Other platforms:** Not primary channels for B2C PI lead gen under current strategy.

### B2B: LinkedIn + Direct Outreach

- **LinkedIn:** Primary B2B content and advertising platform. Professional register.
- **Cold email:** Sasha's direct outreach to managing attorneys.
- **Podcast/content:** For Moment 1 (2 AM Ceiling Math) — educational, not sales.
- **Peer referral system:** For Moment 2 — systematically activated.

### Traffic Profile
- **Mobile: 75%+** — All B2C creative designed mobile-first
- **Desktop: 25%** — B2B website experience must work on desktop (attorneys research at their desk)

## What B2C Creative Must NOT Do

These are drawn from the brand's non-negotiables and the Creative Brief:

- Manufacture urgency that isn't real
- Overclaim what a lawyer can do
- Talk down to the claimant
- Make them feel like they're being sold something
- Promise dollar amounts that can't be substantiated
- Use fear-based hooks or manipulative patterns
- Tell the claimant they "definitely have a case"
- Use crash footage or injury imagery
- Target demographics (prohibited by Special Ad Categories)

---

## What B2C Creative Must DO

- Show the claimant something specifically true about their situation
- Give them a reason to make one small, free, reversible action
- Respect their intelligence and their right to make their own decision
- Be tier-specific — know which timing tier it's speaking to
- Pass the dual test: Maria calls because it gave her something real; Jennifer wants to show it
