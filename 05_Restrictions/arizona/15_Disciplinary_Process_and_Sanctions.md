# AZ Disciplinary Process and Sanctions Ladder

> **Saul:** "Most complaints don't make it past intake. Most that do don't make it past the Probable Cause Committee. The ones that hit the Presiding Disciplinary Judge are the ones with serious factual problems. Knowing the process tells you where to engage and where to stay quiet — and what 'reciprocal discipline' really means for WCTL's CA-licensed attorneys."

---

## 1. THE FRAMEWORK

The discipline process is governed by **Arizona Supreme Court Rules 41-80** (with Rules 46-74 covering most discipline procedure and Rules 70 / 80 governing access/confidentiality). Substantive ethics rules are **ER 1.1-8.5** under Rule 42.

---

## 2. PROCESS STEPS

### Step 1 — Intake / Complaint
Anyone may file a charge by phone, mail, or online submission to the State Bar Intake Department.
- Intake screens for jurisdiction and facial sufficiency
- May dismiss, resolve informally, or refer to Litigation
- Dismissal at this stage may be appealed to **Chief Bar Counsel**

### Step 2 — Screening Investigation (Lawyer Regulation Office)
LRO Litigation requests a **written response from respondent within ~14-21 days** (the bar's letter typically demands response within 14 days, with extension available up to ~20 days).
- Assigned bar counsel prepares a Report of Investigation
- Recommendation: dismiss, divert, informal sanction, or refer for formal proceedings

### Step 3 — Attorney Discipline Probable Cause Committee (ADPCC)
An **independent committee of the Arizona Supreme Court** (not the State Bar) that meets monthly.
- Receives LRO recommendation when bar counsel proposes formal sanction OR respondent rejects proposed informal disposition
- May affirm, modify, dismiss, order further investigation, or **issue a probable-cause order** authorizing the formal complaint

### Step 4 — Formal Complaint and PDJ Hearing
On probable cause, the State Bar files a formal complaint with the **Disciplinary Clerk of the Supreme Court**.
- Heard by a **Hearing Panel** chaired by the **Presiding Disciplinary Judge (PDJ)**
- Joined by one volunteer attorney and one volunteer public member
- Discovery, motions, and trial proceed under rules paralleling civil litigation
- Panel issues a written decision with findings of fact, conclusions of law, and recommended sanction

### Step 5 — Disciplinary Commission Review
A **nine-member Commission** appointed by the Supreme Court reviews PDJ decisions on appeal (by either party).
- May affirm, modify, or remand
- Some matters bypass the Commission and go directly to the Supreme Court under recent rule revisions
- Commission retains key review functions for objections and certain dispositive rulings

### Step 6 — Arizona Supreme Court Final Review
The Supreme Court reviews disciplinary matters de novo as the ultimate authority over admission and discipline of Arizona lawyers.
- Issues the final order (Reprimand, Suspension, Disbarment, or affirmance/reversal)

## 3. SANCTIONS LADDER (Least to Most Severe)

| Sanction | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Dismissal** | No discipline; matter closed |
| **Diversion** | Not technically discipline; CLE, Law Office Management Assistance Program (LOMAP), trust-account school, mentoring. Confidential. |
| **Admonition** | Informal, lowest level of discipline; private in some contexts but increasingly part of the public file |
| **Reprimand** | Formal, public; issued by PDJ or Supreme Court |
| **Censure** | Used in some opinions interchangeably with reprimand; check the specific Order language |
| **Probation** | May stand alone or accompany suspension, with conditions (CLE, monitoring, audits, restitution) |
| **Suspension — short-term** | Less than 6 months; reinstatement on affidavit under Rule 64 |
| **Suspension — long-term** | 6 months and one day or longer; formal reinstatement proceeding under Rule 65 |
| **Interim Suspension (Rule 61)** | Pending final disposition where the lawyer poses a risk |
| **Disbarment** | Minimum 5 years before any reapplication; AZ generally treats disbarment as effectively permanent absent extraordinary showing |

## 4. RECIPROCAL DISCIPLINE — KEY FOR WCTL CA-LICENSED ATTORNEYS

Arizona imposes **reciprocal discipline** on attorneys disciplined in another jurisdiction who are admitted in Arizona (typically pro hac vice or as in-house registered, since AZ generally does not admit CA attorneys on motion — California is **not** on Arizona's reciprocal-admission list).

**Conversely:** Discipline imposed by the Arizona Supreme Court will trigger reciprocal proceedings in California under:
- Cal. Rules of Court 9.20
- Cal. B&P Code § 6049.1

For WCTL: an Arizona disciplinary order against a California-licensed attorney appearing pro hac vice in Arizona, or marketing services in Arizona, will be reported to California and likely produce identical or proportionate California discipline.

**This is a real-stakes risk vector for any cross-border lead-gen operation.** A single AZ complaint can ripple through both bars.

---

## 5. STATUTE OF LIMITATIONS

Arizona's discipline rules **do not impose a fixed limitations period**. The Arizona Supreme Court has held that delay is a factor in laches/due-process analysis but does not auto-bar discipline.

- Misconduct from years (or decades) earlier remains chargeable
- Particularly true for trust-account, fraud, and fitness-to-practice matters
- Practical filter: most actions are brought within 1-3 years of the underlying conduct or its discovery
- No bright-line bar

---

## 6. PUBLIC vs. CONFIDENTIAL

Under **Rule 70**, lawyer regulation records are generally **confidential through the screening/probable-cause stage** (intake and investigation, before formal complaint).

**Once formal complaint filed → matter becomes public:**
- Pleadings public
- Hearings before PDJ public
- Commission review public
- Supreme Court orders public
- Final orders posted to azcourts.gov (Disciplinary Cases Matrix) and azbar.org (Formal Disciplinary Decisions)
- Reported to the National Lawyer Regulatory Data Bank

ER 1.6 and Rule 70 protect respondent communications and bar-counsel work product even after the matter becomes public, but **the case itself is searchable**.

---

## 7. COSTS

Disciplined attorneys are **ordered to pay the costs of the disciplinary proceedings**, including:
- Investigative costs
- Hearing costs
- Transcription
- Expert fees
- Administrative fee

Costs in formal cases routinely run **$1,200 to $5,000+** for typical proceedings and significantly more for complex multi-day hearings.

**Failure to pay costs is itself a basis for further discipline** (suspension for non-payment) and bars reinstatement.

## 8. WHAT TO DO IF A COMPLAINT LANDS

### Hour 0 — Triage
- Notify WCTL compliance lawyer immediately
- Identify the specific allegations and the targeted attorney(s)
- Confirm the response deadline (typically 14 days)

### Hours 1-24 — Mobilize
- Engage defense counsel (reciprocal discipline risk = bring CA defense counsel for CA-licensed attorneys)
- Pull the substantiation file (see [`13_Substantiation_File_Spec.md`](13_Substantiation_File_Spec.md))
- Pull the Practice 2.0 review log (see [`14_Voluntary_Review_Practice_2.0.md`](14_Voluntary_Review_Practice_2.0.md))
- Document all communications about the complaint

### Days 1-12 — Prepare Response
- Draft response addressing each allegation
- Attach substantiation
- Reference any Practice 2.0 prior reviews of the asset at issue
- Get final review from defense counsel + WCTL compliance lawyer

### Days 12-14 — Submit
- Submit response to LRO before deadline
- Confirm receipt
- Document submission in the SC compliance log

### Throughout
- Do NOT communicate directly with the complainant outside formal channels
- Do NOT alter or destroy records
- Do NOT make public statements about the matter

## 9. STAGES WHERE MOST CASES END

| Stage | Typical disposition |
|---|---|
| Intake | Many complaints dismissed for facial insufficiency or no-jurisdiction |
| LRO screening | Many resolve informally or via diversion |
| ADPCC | Probable cause issued only when LRO recommends formal proceedings |
| PDJ hearing | Substantive discipline decisions issued |
| Commission/Supreme Court | Few cases reach final-review level |

The earlier the case can be resolved (intake, LRO screening), the better — both for cost and for keeping the matter confidential.

---

## 10. THE PLAY (Disciplinary Process Specific)

**Do:**
- Maintain the substantiation file ready for 14-day response (see [`13_Substantiation_File_Spec.md`](13_Substantiation_File_Spec.md))
- Maintain the Practice 2.0 review log for mitigation evidence
- Identify defense counsel relationships in advance — don't be shopping during the response window
- Train SC and WCTL staff on "complaint received → notify compliance lawyer immediately" protocol
- Take the reciprocal discipline risk seriously — CA-licensed WCTL attorneys are exposed in two jurisdictions

**Don't:**
- Don't communicate with complainants directly outside formal channels
- Don't alter or destroy records once a complaint is in motion
- Don't underestimate the 14-day deadline — extensions are limited
- Don't try to handle a complaint without defense counsel
- Don't treat dismissal at intake as evidence the underlying conduct is fine — it's evidence the complaint was facially insufficient, which is a different thing
