# Tribal Jurisdiction (Arizona)

> **Saul:** "Arizona has 22 federally recognized tribes covering ~28% of the state. I-40 cuts through Navajo for ~180 miles. US-89, US-160, US-191, SR-264 — all reservation corridors. The Fair Case form has to ask 'where did the crash happen' and triage on tribal-land answers. Most non-Indian-on-non-Indian crashes on state highways through tribal land go to state court (per *Strate*) — but anything with an Indian party or tribal entity needs separate analysis. Sovereign immunity is real. Notice deadlines are short. Get this wrong and the case is gone before WCTL even opens it."

---

## 1. THE TRIBAL LANDSCAPE IN ARIZONA

**22 federally recognized tribes**, with reservations and trust lands covering approximately **27.7 million acres — roughly 27-28% of the state's land base.** Arizona has the third-largest Native American population in the U.S. and the **largest tribal land base of any state**.

Major reservations and tribes:
- **Navajo Nation** (largest reservation in the U.S.; AZ + NM + UT)
- **Tohono O'odham Nation**
- **San Carlos Apache**
- **White Mountain Apache (Fort Apache)**
- **Hualapai**
- **Havasupai**
- **Salt River Pima-Maricopa Indian Community**
- **Gila River Indian Community**
- **Hopi Tribe**
- Plus 13 others (Yavapai-Apache, Cocopah, Yaqui, Kaibab-Paiute, Pascua Yaqui, Quechan, etc.)

## 2. PUBLIC LAW 280 — AZ IS NOT A FULL P.L. 280 STATE

**Mandatory P.L. 280 states** (where the state assumed broad jurisdiction over Indian country): California, Minnesota, Nebraska, Oregon, Wisconsin, Alaska. **Arizona is NOT one of them.**

Arizona elected to assume **partial** jurisdiction under the 1968 amendments to P.L. 280 (Pub. L. 90-284, 82 Stat. 73 — Title IV of the Civil Rights Act of 1968). Arizona's 1967 election was **narrow**: it covers only **air and water pollution** matters under A.R.S. § 36-1801 et seq.

**Practical takeaway: For PI / tort purposes, Arizona is effectively a non-P.L. 280 state.** State courts generally lack jurisdiction over civil disputes arising on tribal land that involve Indian parties.

*Williams v. Lee*, 358 U.S. 217 (1959) (involving the Navajo Reservation in Arizona) is the foundational holding that state courts may not adjudicate civil cases brought against tribal members for on-reservation conduct because doing so would "infringe on the right of the Indians to govern themselves."

## 3. THE MONTANA / STRATE FRAMEWORK

### Montana v. United States, 450 U.S. 544 (1981)
Established the general rule that **tribes lack civil regulatory and adjudicatory authority over non-members** on non-Indian fee land within a reservation, subject to two exceptions:

1. **Consensual relationship exception** — tribes may regulate non-members "who enter consensual relationships with the tribe or its members, through commercial dealing, contracts, leases, or other arrangements"
2. **Tribal self-government exception** — tribes retain inherent power over non-Indian conduct on fee land "when that conduct threatens or has some direct effect on the political integrity, the economic security, or the health or welfare of the tribe"

### Strate v. A-1 Contractors, 520 U.S. 438 (1997) — KEY CASE FOR PI
Unanimous decision (Ginsburg, J.) extending *Montana* to **state-maintained public highways crossing reservation land**, holding that such rights-of-way are **treated like non-Indian fee land for jurisdictional purposes**.

The facts mirror a typical PI case: a non-Indian motorist (Fredericks) sued a non-Indian gravel-truck driver/employer (Stockert / A-1 Contractors) in tribal court for a crash on a state highway through the Fort Berthold Reservation. The Court held the **tribal court lacked jurisdiction**.

### Plains Commerce Bank v. Long Family Land & Cattle Co., 554 U.S. 316 (2008)
Further narrowed tribal jurisdiction over non-Indians, reading the *Montana* exceptions strictly.

## 4. SOVEREIGN IMMUNITY

The Indian Civil Rights Act of 1968 (25 U.S.C. §§ 1301-1304) imposes Bill-of-Rights-like restraints on tribal governments, but ***Santa Clara Pueblo v. Martinez***, 436 U.S. 49 (1978) held ICRA does **NOT waive tribal sovereign immunity** in federal court except for habeas.

**Tribes (and tribal entities, casinos, transit authorities, housing authorities) enjoy sovereign immunity** unless Congress abrogates it or the tribe expressly waives it.

Many AZ tribes have limited tort-claims acts with damages caps and short notice-of-claim periods (e.g., Navajo Nation Sovereign Immunity Act, 1 N.N.C. § 551 et seq.). **Notice deadlines on some tribes are 30-90 days**, versus AZ's 180-day public-entity notice. **A PI lawyer must check the specific tribe's ordinance before filing.**

## 5. PRACTICAL APPLICATION TO COMMON AZ SCENARIOS

### Scenario A: Non-Indian motorist v. non-Indian motorist on I-40 (Navajo Nation)
Under *Strate*, tribal court has **no jurisdiction**. Case proceeds in:
- **AZ state court** (Apache, Navajo, or Coconino County) — typical
- **Federal diversity court** if amount-in-controversy and diversity met

State court has jurisdiction because the highway right-of-way is treated like non-Indian land.

### Scenario B: Indian motorist injured by non-Indian commercial trucker on tribal land
- Plaintiff can typically sue in **tribal court**, particularly if the trucker has a consensual relationship with the tribe (commercial license, hauling contract, fuel-tax permit, ICC permit recognized by tribe)
- *Williams v. Lee* protects tribal-court jurisdiction when the tribal member is the defendant
- For tribal-member plaintiff against non-Indian defendant, tribal jurisdiction often exists under the first *Montana* exception, but the non-Indian defendant may challenge under *Plains Commerce Bank*
- State court jurisdiction depends on facts; federal court is also available where amount-in-controversy and diversity met

### Scenario C: State highway right-of-way through tribal land
Per *Strate*, treated as non-Indian fee land for civil jurisdiction. **State court has jurisdiction** for crashes between non-members; tribal court generally does not.

### Scenario D: Tribal entity / employee defendant
- Tribal police vehicle, casino shuttle, tribal transit, tribal public works
- **Sovereign immunity analysis required**
- Check the specific tribe's tort claims act
- Check if FTCA applies (tribal employee under § 638 self-determination contract — common for IHS providers and many tribal police)

| **I-17** northern stretches | Yavapai-Apache | Limited mileage |

| **US-60** east of Globe | San Carlos Apache | Eastern AZ |

**These are all *Strate* zones — most non-Indian-on-non-Indian crashes go to state court.**

## 7. McGIRT — DOES IT MATTER FOR AZ?

**McGirt v. Oklahoma, 591 U.S. 894 (2020)** held the Muscogee (Creek) Reservation was never disestablished, recognizing it as Indian country for Major Crimes Act purposes.

**Direct AZ application is minimal:**
- AZ reservation boundaries were established by executive order or statute (not by treaty for most tribes, with limited exceptions like the Navajo 1868 treaty)
- Most boundaries are not seriously disputed
- *McGirt* is primarily a criminal jurisdiction case and does not change the *Montana/Strate* civil framework for PI cases

It does signal a Court more receptive to tribal sovereignty arguments generally — worth tracking.

## 8. LEAD-GEN OPERATIONAL IMPLICATIONS

### Quiz funnel question architecture
**The Fair Case quiz should ask "Where did the crash happen?"** with logic that flags:
- Reservation land (drop-down with all 22 tribes)
- I-40 mile-marker range through Navajo (MM 0 to ~180)
- US-160, US-89, US-191, SR-264, SR-87, SR-86, SR-386, US-60 east of Globe
- Off-reservation but adjacent (border-zone follow-up question)

### Triage rules
- **Non-Indian v. non-Indian on state highway through tribal land** → proceed normally (state court jurisdiction)
- **Any Indian party (plaintiff or defendant)** → route to a tribal-jurisdiction-competent attorney or decline if WCTL cannot litigate in Navajo District Court, Hopi Tribal Court, etc. WCTL's CA license is not the same as tribal-court admission; Navajo Nation Bar requires its own bar exam
- **Tribal entity/employee defendant** (tribal police, casino shuttle, tribal transit) → flag immediately for sovereign-immunity / tort-claims-act analysis. **Notice deadlines on some tribes are 30-90 days versus AZ's 180-day public-entity notice**
- **Decline** cases the firm cannot competently litigate in tribal forum unless co-counsel arrangement exists

## 9. AD COPY IMPLICATIONS

### Don't
- Don't run ad copy that promises representation on tribal-land cases unless WCTL has confirmed tribal-court capacity
- Don't ignore the special rules — claimants on tribal land have different urgency timelines and forum considerations
- Don't conflate state public-entity notice (180 days) with tribal tort-claims notice (often 30-90 days)

### Do
- Educational content about tribal-land jurisdiction can serve specific corridors (e.g., I-40 Navajo) if WCTL has the right tribal-court capacity
- "Crash on tribal land has different rules — call us to find out where your case can go" is a defensible educational framing if WCTL has the capacity to handle the analysis

---

## 10. THE PLAY (Tribal Jurisdiction Specific)

**Do:**
- Build "where did the crash happen" question into the Fair Case quiz with tribal-land branching
- Maintain a triage matrix: state court, federal court, tribal court, federal-FTCA-claim, decline
- Know the specific tribal tort-claims acts and notice deadlines for the tribes whose territory WCTL serves
- Verify WCTL's tribal-court admission capacity before accepting tribal-court cases

**Don't:**
- Don't assume state court can handle every AZ-located crash — *Williams v. Lee* and tribal sovereign immunity carve out significant chunks
- Don't miss the 30-90 day notice windows on tribal entity defendants
- Don't run ad copy that promises tribal-land representation without the underlying capacity
