# ER 5.4 — Eliminated + Alternative Business Structure (ABS) Program

> **Saul:** "Arizona did something nobody else has done — they killed the fee-sharing prohibition. ER 5.4 used to be the rule that made every lead-gen lawyer in America nervous. In Arizona, it's gone. Non-lawyers can own pieces of law firms. Non-lawyers can share legal fees. The lead-gen model is on stronger footing in Arizona than anywhere else in the country. *Anywhere.*"

---

## 1. THE ORDER THAT CHANGED EVERYTHING

**Citation:** Arizona Supreme Court Administrative Order **R-20-0034** (entered August 27, 2020; amended November 4, 2020; **effective January 1, 2021**).

The order's title summarizes its scope:
> *In the Matter of Petition to Restyle and Amend Supreme Court Rule 31; Adopt New Rule 33.1; Amend Rules 41, 42 (ERs 1.0, 1.5, 1.7, 1.10, 1.17, 5.1, 5.3, 5.4, 5.7, 7.1, 7.2, 7.3, 7.5, and 8.4) and Adopt New ACJA § 7-209.*

What it did:
- **Repealed ER 5.4 in its entirety** — the historical prohibition on (a) sharing legal fees with non-lawyers, (b) forming partnerships with non-lawyers if any of the activities consist of the practice of law, and (c) permitting non-lawyer ownership of a law firm.
- **Repealed former ER 7.2(b)** — which had prohibited paying anyone to recommend the lawyer's services.
- **Adopted new Sup. Ct. Rule 31.1(c)** — authorizing Alternative Business Structures (ABS).
- **Adopted new Sup. Ct. Rule 33.1** — establishing the ABS Committee and licensure framework.
- **Adopted new ACJA § 7-209** — the operational regulation for ABS licensing.
- **Amended ER 1.5** — added subsection (e) (fee divisions with lawyers outside the firm) and (f) (fee divisions with non-lawyers).

**Arizona is the first U.S. state to fully repeal ER 5.4.** This is a structural, not cosmetic, change.

---

## 2. WHAT AN "ABS" IS

Per ACJA § 7-209, an Alternative Business Structure is a business entity that:
- "Includes non-lawyers who have an economic interest or decision-making authority in the firm"
- AND provides legal services

An ABS is NOT a vendor relationship between a law firm and a marketing company — that's just a vendor relationship. An ABS is when the **non-lawyer is an owner or decision-maker inside the firm itself**.

| Configuration | ABS required? |
|---|---|
| Law firm pays a marketing company a per-lead fee | No (just a vendor/advertising relationship) |
| Law firm pays a lead-gen company a percentage of recovery (referral fee) | No, post-2021 — but ER 1.5(f) client written consent required |
| A non-lawyer owns 25% of a law firm | **Yes — ABS license required** |
| A non-lawyer holds a board seat with voting power | **Yes — ABS license required** |
| Law firm and accounting firm operate jointly delivering legal+tax services | **Yes — ABS license required** |
| KPMG Law US delivering legal services with non-lawyer ownership | **Yes — ABS license required (KPMG was approved Feb 2025)** |

## 3. ABS LICENSING — KEY REQUIREMENTS (ACJA § 7-209)

### Authorized Persons
Any person with **≥10% economic interest or decision-making authority** in the ABS is an "Authorized Person." Each Authorized Person must:
- Complete approval forms
- Undergo background investigation
- Be re-vetted on annual renewal

### Compliance Lawyer
Each ABS must designate a **Compliance Lawyer** who must:
- Be an active AZ Bar member in good standing
- Be a manager or employee of the ABS
- Be free of State Bar discipline within the prior 10 years
- Establish and oversee policies for ethical compliance with the AZ Rules of Professional Conduct
- Function as the ER 5.3-style supervisor over non-lawyers

### Designated Principal
Administrative point of contact with the AZ Supreme Court for the ABS.

### Approval Body
The **Committee on Alternative Business Structures** reviews applications and recommends licensure to the AZ Supreme Court (Rule 33.1).

### Fee Schedule (current 2026 per ACJA § 7-209)

| Category | Initial | Annual Renewal |
|---|---|---|
| Nonprofit ABS | $3,000 | $2,000 |
| Equity ABS | $4,500 | $3,000 |
| Regular ABS | $9,000 | $9,000 |
| Modifications | $250 each | — |

### Application Components
1. AZ-licensed compliance lawyer hired
2. Designated principal named
3. Approval forms for entity, compliance lawyer, principal, every Authorized Person
4. Ownership and decision-making disclosures (full cap-table style)
5. Financial disclosures
6. Background investigations on all Authorized Persons
7. Compliance plan demonstrating ER conformity
8. Submit to ABS Committee → AZ Supreme Court issues license
9. Annual renewal + ongoing background updates

### Approved Entities (as of early 2026)
- ~**151 licensed ABS entities** in Arizona (per AZ State Law Journal, Jan 2026)
- KPMG Law US approved February 2025 — first Big Four professional-services firm with a U.S. ABS license
- Range: legal-tech platforms, PE-backed mass-tort firms, multidisciplinary practices, legacy nonlawyer-equity firms

## 4. WHAT THIS MEANS FOR SECOND CHAIR (and Lead-Gen Generally)

### The "Do We Need an ABS?" Question — **No.**

Second Chair is a **vendor** to WCTL — Second Chair runs ads, captures leads, and routes them to WCTL. Second Chair has no ownership or decision-making authority **inside** WCTL. Therefore:

- **Second Chair does NOT need an ABS license**
- WCTL can lawfully pay Second Chair a per-lead fee (advertising / lead-gen vendor relationship — always permitted)
- WCTL can lawfully pay Second Chair a percentage of recovery / contingent referral fee — under ER 1.5(f), with client written consent

ABS would only be required if Second Chair wanted to **co-own** WCTL or jointly deliver legal services as an integrated entity.

### Fee-Sharing Architecture (Post-ER 5.4 Repeal)

| Counterparty | Pre-2021 | Post-2021 |
|---|---|---|
| Lawyer outside firm (referral) | Allowed only with proportional work or joint responsibility + client consent | Allowed with **client written consent** alone — ER 1.5(e) |
| Non-lawyer outside firm (lead-gen, marketing, financier) | **Prohibited** by old ER 5.4(a) and old ER 7.2(b) | **Allowed** under ER 1.5(f) with informed-consent disclosure |
| Non-lawyer co-owner of the firm | **Prohibited** | Allowed only inside a **licensed ABS** under Rule 31.1(c) + ACJA § 7-209 |

(See [`ER_1.5_Fees_and_Contingency.md`](ER_1.5_Fees_and_Contingency.md) for the full ER 1.5(e) and (f) treatment.)

## 5. CRITICAL CROSS-BORDER WARNING — California AB 931

**Effective January 1, 2026** (sunset January 1, 2030), California Assembly Bill 931 **prohibits California lawyers from sharing fees with an out-of-state ABS**.

This is potentially relevant to the WCTL/Second Chair structure because WCTL is a California-headquartered firm. Davis has confirmed that **WCTL has out-of-state office operations handled** — meaning the AZ work is done through AZ-licensed attorneys at WCTL's AZ office, and AZ fee flows do not depend on California-attorney compensation that would trigger CA AB 931.

**Documenting the rule for posterity:**
- CA AB 931 targets fee-splits between CA lawyers and out-of-state ABS entities
- It does NOT bar CA lawyers from being paid by AZ ABS for actual work performed
- It does NOT bar CA lawyers from receiving home-state fees from a CA case that happens to involve an AZ ABS partner
- Texas, Maryland have adopted similar restrictive postures; expect more states to follow

**For the WCTL/SC structure:** the cleanest architecture is the one already in place — AZ-admitted attorneys at WCTL's AZ office handle AZ matters end-to-end, AZ fees flow to AZ attorneys, Second Chair is paid as an advertising vendor (not an ABS, not a fee-share counterparty in any way that involves CA-licensed attorneys). This keeps both AZ ER 1.5 and CA AB 931 satisfied.

(See [`ER_5.5_Multijurisdictional_Practice.md`](ER_5.5_Multijurisdictional_Practice.md) and [`../07_Multijurisdictional_WCTL_Specific.md`](../07_Multijurisdictional_WCTL_Specific.md) for the multijurisdictional deep dive.)

## 6. WHY THIS IS A STRUCTURAL ADVANTAGE FOR AZ

In every other major US legal market (CA, TX, FL, NY), ER 5.4-equivalent prohibits non-lawyer fee-sharing. Lead-gen operations in those states have to engineer around it: per-lead flat fees framed as advertising costs, cap-and-collar contracts, etc. — careful structures that can collapse if a regulator decides the operation is *de facto* a fee-share.

In Arizona, that whole engineering problem **doesn't exist**. ER 5.4 is gone. Fee-sharing is permitted with client written consent. That's the rule.

> **Saul:** "If you've ever sat across from a CA bar regulator trying to explain why your $200-per-lead arrangement isn't fee-sharing, you know how exhausting that is. In Arizona, you don't have that conversation. Fee-sharing is allowed. End of conversation. Just paper the client consent and move on."

## 7. THE PLAY (ER 5.4 / ABS Specific)

**Do:**
- Operate Second Chair as a non-ABS advertising vendor to WCTL
- Document fee structure (per-lead, percentage of recovery, or hybrid) clearly in the SC↔WCTL contract
- Build client-consent into the engagement letter / TCPA consent flow (per ER 1.5(f))
- Maintain compliance documentation in case AZ Bar inquires about the SC/WCTL relationship

**Don't:**
- Don't treat ER 5.4 elimination as a license to ignore ER 7.1, ER 7.5, or any other rule — only the *fee-sharing* restriction was lifted
- Don't co-own WCTL (or any AZ-licensed firm) without an ABS license
- Don't structure fee flows that would trigger CA AB 931 on the WCTL/CA-attorney side
- Don't assume other states' lead-gen rules are similarly relaxed — AZ is currently the outlier
