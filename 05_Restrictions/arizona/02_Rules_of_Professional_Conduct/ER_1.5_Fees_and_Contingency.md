# ER 1.5 — Fees and Contingency Agreements

> **Saul:** "ER 1.5 has more sharp edges than people think. The contingency-fee writing requirement isn't a paperwork formality — Arizona Court of Appeals has flat-out held that a lawyer with no signed writing has *no claim to the fee*. Engagement letters before the case starts. Always. No exceptions."

---

## 1. THE RULE

**Citation:** Ariz. R. Sup. Ct. 42, ER 1.5 (as amended by Order R-20-0034 effective January 1, 2021).

### ER 1.5(a) — Reasonableness (Eight Factors)
A lawyer shall not make an agreement for, charge, or collect an unreasonable fee. Reasonableness factors:
1. Time and labor required
2. Novelty/difficulty of questions
3. Skill requisite to perform the service properly
4. Likelihood that acceptance precludes other employment
5. Fee customarily charged in the locality
6. Amount involved + results obtained
7. Time limitations imposed by client/circumstances
8. Nature/length of professional relationship + experience/reputation/ability of the lawyer + fixed/contingent

### ER 1.5(b) — Communication of Fees
"The scope of the representation and the basis or rate of the fee and expenses for which the client will be responsible shall be **communicated to the client, preferably in writing, before or within a reasonable time after commencing the representation**."

### ER 1.5(c) — Contingency Fees (the operative subsection for PI)

**MUST be in writing and signed by the client.**

Required content:
1. The **method by which the fee is to be determined**, including the percentage(s) accruing to the lawyer in the event of **settlement, trial, or appeal**
2. **Litigation and other expenses** to be deducted from the recovery, and **whether such expenses are deducted before or after** the contingent fee is calculated
3. **Clear notification of any expenses for which the client will be liable** whether or not the client is the prevailing party

**Closing statement requirement:** Upon conclusion of any contingent-fee matter, the lawyer must furnish the client a **written statement** of the outcome and, if there is a recovery, the **remittance to the client and the method of its determination**.

### ER 1.5(c) Categorical Prohibitions
Contingent fees are **forbidden** in:
- **Domestic relations matters** (divorce, support, property settlement amount)
- **Criminal defense**

(These mirror ABA Model Rule 1.5(d). PI is fine — contingent fees are the dominant model.)

## 2. THE WRITING REQUIREMENT IS STRICT

AZ Court of Appeals has held: **"Attorneys Have No Claim for Contingent Fee Unless the Agreement is in Writing."** (See *Levine v. Haralson, Miller, Pitt, Feldman & McAnally* and progeny; *Geller v. Lesk* line.)

**Practical implication for WCTL:**
- Engagement letter must be papered **at engagement**, not after
- Post-engagement papering creates substantial collection-risk and ER 1.5 enforcement exposure
- Best practice: signed retainer **before** any substantive work begins
- For the SC → WCTL handoff: WCTL's intake team must execute the engagement letter at the start of the attorney-client relationship, before any case investigation or settlement discussions

---

## 3. ER 1.5(e) — Division of Fees with Lawyers Outside the Firm (Post-2021 Liberalized)

Lawyers may divide a fee with a lawyer **not in the same firm** if:
- The total fee is reasonable, AND
- **The client agrees, including to the share each lawyer will receive**, and the agreement is **confirmed in writing**

**Pre-2021:** Required the receiving lawyer to either (a) do proportional work OR (b) assume joint responsibility.

**Post-2021:** Dropped that requirement. Lawyers can now receive a referral fee **without working the case or being jointly responsible**, so long as the client consents in writing.

This is unusually permissive and is a structural advantage for AZ legal practice.

---

## 4. ER 1.5(f) — Division of Fees with NON-LAWYERS Outside the Firm (NEW post-ER 5.4)

Permits fee divisions with **non-lawyers outside the firm** (e.g., lead-gen vendors, marketing companies, financiers), **provided**:

1. The total fee is reasonable
2. **The client gives informed consent**, confirmed in writing, identifying the recipient and the share
3. The arrangement does not interfere with the lawyer's independent professional judgment or with the lawyer-client relationship

**This is what makes the SC/WCTL fee structure lawful in Arizona.** WCTL can pay Second Chair a percentage of recovery (or per-lead, or hybrid) — the structure works as long as:
- The client is told (in writing, at engagement) that Second Chair receives compensation from WCTL for the referral
- The total fee remains reasonable
- The arrangement doesn't compromise WCTL's independent judgment

The AZ Bar's guidance: *"the requirements of ER 1.5(f) impose a minimal burden on Arizona lawyers, who can obtain client consent as part of their written fee agreement."* Folding the disclosure into WCTL's standard engagement letter is the standard approach.

---

## 5. NON-REFUNDABLE / "EARNED-ON-RECEIPT" RETAINERS

Permitted **only if** the client is advised **in writing** that:
- They may **discharge the lawyer at any time**, AND
- They **may be entitled to a refund** of any unearned portion despite the "earned on receipt" label

This mirrors the ABA position. "True" non-refundable retainers are disfavored and generally treated as advance-fee deposits subject to ER 1.16(d) refund obligations.

---

## 6. PERMISSIBLE / IMPERMISSIBLE FEE STRUCTURES (Post-2021 in AZ)

### Permissible
- Standard hourly, flat, contingent (with ER 1.5(c) writing for contingent)
- **Hybrid fees** (reduced hourly + reduced contingency) — see AZ State Bar Ethics Op. 03-06
- **Fee-financing arrangements** where a lender retains a portion of the fee for collection management (informed consent required)
- **Referral fees to non-lawyers** under ER 1.5(f) with informed-consent disclosure
- **Pure referral fees among lawyers** without joint responsibility, under amended ER 1.5(e)

### Impermissible
- Contingent fees in family/criminal cases
- Fees that interfere with independent professional judgment
- Unreasonable fees (post-hoc reasonableness review at conclusion)

## 7. APPLICATION TO SC/WCTL ENGAGEMENT LETTER

The WCTL engagement letter for AZ matters must include:

| Element | Source |
|---|---|
| Contingent fee percentage at settlement | ER 1.5(c)(1) |
| Contingent fee percentage at trial | ER 1.5(c)(1) |
| Contingent fee percentage at appeal | ER 1.5(c)(1) |
| Treatment of expenses (before or after fee calculation) | ER 1.5(c)(2) |
| Client liability for expenses if not prevailing | ER 1.5(c)(3) |
| Disclosure of Second Chair compensation arrangement | ER 1.5(f) |
| Identification of recipient (Second Chair) and share | ER 1.5(f)(2) |
| Non-interference acknowledgment | ER 1.5(f)(3) |
| Closing-statement promise (written outcome statement at conclusion) | ER 1.5(c) closing |

## 8. FEE INFORMATION IN ADVERTISING

If fees are mentioned in any Fair Case ad or landing page (per ER 7.1 cross-reference):
- Specify whether **contingency or hourly**
- For contingency fees: disclose whether the fee is calculated **before or after expenses**
- Disclose the client's potential portion of any costs

**Compliant copy examples:**
- "No fee unless we win your case" (standard contingency framing — common, ER 1.5/ER 7.1 compliant)
- "We work on a contingency-fee basis: no recovery, no fee. Costs of investigation deducted from any recovery."

**Non-compliant:**
- "Free if we don't win" without explaining costs allocation (omits material fact under ER 1.5(c))
- Specific fee percentages without context ("Only 25%!") — risk of being treated as misleading without comparison basis

---

## 9. THE PLAY (ER 1.5 Specific)

**Do:**
- Paper the contingency-fee agreement **before** any substantive work
- Include all ER 1.5(c) required elements (settlement/trial/appeal percentages, expense treatment, client cost liability)
- Bake the ER 1.5(f) Second Chair disclosure into WCTL's standard engagement letter
- Provide the closing statement at conclusion of every contingent-fee matter

**Don't:**
- Don't take an AZ case without a signed retainer at engagement
- Don't treat non-refundable retainer language as actually non-refundable
- Don't take contingency in family or criminal (categorical prohibition)
- Don't omit the Second Chair compensation disclosure from the engagement letter
