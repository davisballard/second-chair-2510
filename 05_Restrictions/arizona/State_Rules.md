## 2. ARIZONA RULES OF PROFESSIONAL CONDUCT (ER) — ADVERTISING

### Important Structural Note: 2021 Consolidation

Effective January 1, 2021, Arizona consolidated its advertising rules. The substance of former ER 7.2 (Advertising) was merged into **ER 7.1**. Former ER 7.4 (Communication of Fields of Practice and Specialization) was also folded into ER 7.1. What remains as standalone rules are **ER 7.1** (the master advertising rule), **ER 7.3** (Solicitation), and **ER 7.5** (Firm Names and Letterheads).

---

### Rule ER 7.1: Communications Concerning a Lawyer's Services

**The Core Prohibition:**
A lawyer shall not make a false or misleading communication about the lawyer or the lawyer's services. A lawyer shall not knowingly permit another person to make such communications on the lawyer's behalf.

**What Makes a Communication "False or Misleading":**
- Contains a material misrepresentation of fact or law
- Omits a fact necessary to make the statement as a whole not materially misleading
- Creates unjustified expectations about results the lawyer can achieve
- States or implies the lawyer can achieve results by means that violate the Rules of Professional Conduct or other law
- Compares the lawyer's services with other lawyers' services without factual substantiation

**Required Disclosures (from former ER 7.2, now in ER 7.1):**
- [ ] Name and contact information of at least one lawyer or law firm responsible for the content
- [ ] Contact information must include at least one of: office address, website, telephone number, or email address
- [ ] All disclosures must be **clear and conspicuous** — same size, color, duration, cadence, contrast, location, and audibility as the primary content, such that an ordinary person would be able to easily read, hear, notice, and understand them

**Specialization Language (from former ER 7.4, now in ER 7.1):**
- As amended in 2021, lawyers **may** say they "specialize" in a practice area if they actually have expertise or limit their practice to that area
- Only lawyers certified by the Arizona Board of Legal Specialization (or ABA-accredited equivalent) may say they are a **"certified specialist"**
- Alternative language permitted: "focuses on," "experienced in," "practice limited to," "concentrates in"

**Past Results:**
- May be included, but must not be misleading
- Disclaimers are **recommended but not technically required** under AZ rules
- Best practice: include disclaimer language ("Past results do not guarantee future outcomes")
- **Client consent required** before referencing prior cases that may identify a former client

**Fee Information:**
- If fees are mentioned, must specify whether contingency or hourly
- Must disclose whether contingency fees are calculated before or after expenses
- Must disclose client's potential portion of any costs

> **Saul:** "Arizona is one of the few states that actually lets lawyers say 'specialize' now — as long as they actually do. That's a 2021 change. But 'certified specialist' is still reserved for bar-certified folks. Use 'focuses on' or 'experienced in' for WCTL copy and you're golden."

### Rule ER 7.2: Advertising (Legacy — Substantively Merged into ER 7.1)

As of January 1, 2021, the substance of ER 7.2 was consolidated into ER 7.1. The key provisions that were moved include:

**What Was Permitted (Still Permitted Under ER 7.1):**
- Advertising through written, recorded, or electronic communication
- Public media including television, radio, print, internet, social media (Meta, Instagram, TikTok, etc.)
- Payment for the reasonable cost of advertisements

**Lead Generator Payment Rules (Critical for Second Chair):**
- A lawyer **may** pay others for generating client leads (e.g., internet-based leads)
- The lead generator **must not** recommend the lawyer — it must present options or be neutral
- The lead generator **must not** state, imply, or create a reasonable impression that:
  - It is recommending the lawyer
  - The referral is being made without payment from the lawyer
  - It has analyzed the person's legal problems to determine which lawyer should receive the referral
- Any payment must be consistent with ER 1.5(e) (Fees) and the advertising rules
- The lead generator's communications must comply with ER 7.1

**Record-Keeping:**
- Arizona does **not** mandate retention of advertising copies (unlike FL, TX, NV)
- Best practice: retain copies for internal compliance documentation

---

### Rule ER 7.3: Solicitation of Clients

**Prohibited:**
- Live person-to-person solicitation for pecuniary gain (in-person, live telephone, real-time electronic contact)
- Soliciting persons known to be in need of legal services when the lawyer's primary motivation is pecuniary gain
- Continuing to solicit after the prospective client has made known a desire not to be solicited

**Permitted:**
- Written, recorded, or electronic communications (including direct mail and email)
- Digital advertising and lead generation (not considered live solicitation)
- Contact with other lawyers, close personal friends, family members, and persons who routinely use the type of legal services offered

**Written Solicitation Requirements (Direct Mail / Email):**
- Physical mail: Envelope and first page must include in **red ink**: "NOTICE: THIS IS AN ADVERTISEMENT!"
- Must be in **all capital letters** in type size at least **double** the largest type size in the body
- Electronic messages: Subject line must contain "Advertising Material"
- "Advertising Material" must appear at beginning and end of email in a font **twice the size** of the body text
- Must contain accurate transmission and routing information
- Must not contain false or misleading information

**Key Distinction for Second Chair:**
Digital ads on Meta, TikTok, Instagram, Google, etc. are classified as **advertising**, not **solicitation**. The ER 7.3 solicitation restrictions (red ink, "NOTICE: THIS IS AN ADVERTISEMENT!" etc.) apply to **targeted written communications** (direct mail, email to specific individuals), NOT to general digital ads.

> **Saul:** "Here's where people get confused. The red ink, double-size font stuff? That's for direct mail and email solicitation — when you're targeting a specific person you know has a legal need. Your Meta ads? Those are general advertising under 7.1. Different rules. Don't paste 'NOTICE: THIS IS AN ADVERTISEMENT!' in red across your Instagram creative. That's not what the rule says."

---

### Rule ER 7.4: Communication of Fields of Practice and Specialization (Legacy — Merged into ER 7.1)

Substantively consolidated into ER 7.1 as of January 1, 2021. See Specialization Language section under ER 7.1 above.

**Key Points:**
- A lawyer may communicate that they do or do not practice in particular fields of law
- "Specialist" language is now permitted if the lawyer has genuine expertise (2021 amendment)
- "Certified specialist" still requires certification by the AZ Board of Legal Specialization or ABA-accredited entity
- If claiming certification, must identify the certifying organization

---

### Rule ER 7.5: Firm Names and Letterheads

- A lawyer shall not use a firm name, letterhead, or other professional designation that violates ER 7.1
- Trade names are permitted if they do not imply a connection with a government agency or charitable legal services organization
- Firm name shall not include the name of a lawyer holding public office during the period of service
- Multi-jurisdictional disclosure: if lawyers from multiple jurisdictions are in the same firm, the jurisdictions of each must be identified
- Cannot use "and Associates" without actually having multiple attorney associates

## 5. ARIZONA-SPECIFIC NOTES

### Market Context

- **Primary Target:** Phoenix metro area (Maricopa County) — 37,472 crashes, 10,449 injuries, 265 fatalities in 2024
- **Secondary:** Tucson (Pima County) — 5,977 crashes, 2,571 injuries, 93 fatalities in 2024
- **Key Corridors:** I-10 (Phoenix-Tucson, highest fatality corridor), I-17 (Phoenix north-south spine), Loop 101, Loop 202
- **Deadliest Segments:** I-10 exits 143A-147A Phoenix (47 fatalities); I-17 exits 210-215B Phoenix (44 fatalities)
- **Special Hazards:** Dust storms cause multi-vehicle pileups annually on I-10 between Phoenix and Tucson; extreme heat causes tire blowouts and road surface issues
- **Market Saturation:** Phoenix PI is highly competitive — major local firms (Lamber Goodnow, Phillips Law, Torgenson) dominate billboards and digital

### Copy Localization

- "Phoenix accident" / "Arizona crash" / "Tucson injury"
- Reference Arizona statute of limitations: **2 years for PI** (A.R.S. 12-542)
- Discovery rule may extend deadline in some cases (medical malpractice, latent injuries)
- Government claims: **180-day notice** requirement (A.R.S. 12-821.01)
- "Maricopa County" / "Pima County" for geo-targeting
- Highway references: I-10, I-17, Loop 101, Loop 202, US-60

### Pure Comparative Fault Implications

Arizona is a **pure comparative fault** state (A.R.S. 12-2505). This is a significant advertising advantage:

- **What it means:** A plaintiff can recover damages even if they are **up to 99% at fault**. Recovery is reduced proportionally by the plaintiff's percentage of fault.
- **Advertising opportunity:** Ads can emphasize that injury victims may still have a case even if they were partially at fault — "Even if you think the accident was partly your fault, you may still be entitled to compensation."
- **What NOT to say:** Don't say "not at fault" as a prerequisite. Arizona doesn't require it. Avoid implying that only "innocent" victims have claims.
- **What TO say:** "Injured in an accident? Even if you were partially at fault, Arizona law may entitle you to compensation." / "Don't let the insurance company tell you it was your fault — Arizona law protects you."
- **Caveat:** All language must still be truthful and not misleading under ER 7.1. Don't overstate — make clear that recovery is reduced proportionally.

> **Saul:** "Pure comparative fault is a gift for ad copy. In a modified comparative fault state, you can't recover if you're 50%+ at fault. In Arizona? Even at 99% fault, you technically have a claim. That's a powerful message — 'You may still have a case' — and it's *true*. Just don't promise the moon."

### Lead Generator vs. Lawyer Referral Service

**Critical distinction under Arizona law:**

| Factor | Lead Generator (Second Chair) | Lawyer Referral Service |
|--------|-------------------------------|------------------------|
| **What it does** | Generates leads via advertising, passes to law firm | Holds itself out as matching clients to lawyers based on case analysis |
| **Recommends specific lawyer?** | No — passes lead to contracted firm | Yes — analyzes case and recommends |
| **Payment model** | Per-lead or per-case fee | Membership/participation fees |
| **Registration required?** | No AZ registration required | May require qualification as "qualified referral service" |
| **Fee sharing permitted?** | Yes (ER 5.4 eliminated) | Yes, with conditions |

**Second Chair's Position:**
Second Chair operates as a lead generator, NOT a lawyer referral service. This is permissible under Arizona rules provided:
1. Second Chair does not recommend WCTL specifically — it passes leads to a contracted firm
2. Second Chair does not state, imply, or create the impression it has analyzed the person's legal problem to determine which lawyer should receive the referral
3. All communications comply with ER 7.1 (truthful, not misleading)
4. WCTL is identified as the responsible attorney/firm in all advertising

**Arizona's Unique Advantage — ER 5.4 Elimination:**
Effective January 1, 2021, Arizona eliminated ER 5.4, becoming the **first state** to allow:
- Non-lawyers to acquire ownership interests in law firms
- Fee sharing between lawyers and non-lawyers (flat fee or percentage)
- Referral fee payments to anyone, lawyer or non-lawyer
- Alternative Business Structures (ABS) licensing

This means the Second Chair model is on **stronger footing in Arizona than in virtually any other state**. The traditional prohibition against fee-sharing with non-lawyers — the biggest legal risk for lead gen companies nationally — simply does not exist in Arizona.

**Restrictions Still in Effect:**
- Client must give **informed consent** before the lawyer can acknowledge the referral source (even if the referral source already knows)
- The fact that someone consults with a lawyer is confidential under ER 1.6 and ER 1.18
- Lead generator communications must still comply with ER 7.1

### Enforcement

- Arizona State Bar enforcement is **complaint-driven**, not proactive
- No pre-screening or pre-filing requirements for advertisements
- Voluntary review available through the Practice 2.0 Department (formerly Ethics Department)
- The State Bar no longer has a standing ethics committee that issues formal opinions — formal opinions are now handled by the Arizona Supreme Court's Ethics Advisory Committee
- Ethics Hotline available for lawyers: **602-252-4804** (M-F, 8:30am-4:30pm)
- Attorney/Consumer Assistance Program (for public complaints): **602-340-7280**
- Disciplinary process: complaint -> investigation by Lawyer Regulation Office (LRO) -> possible formal proceedings before Presiding Disciplinary Judge
- Sanctions range: dismissal, diversion, probation, reprimand, censure, suspension, disbarment
- Advertising-specific disciplinary actions are **rare** — most enforcement is for misrepresentation, trust account violations, and client abandonment
- Historical note: Arizona is the home of **Bates v. State Bar of Arizona (1977)** — the landmark SCOTUS case that established attorneys' First Amendment right to advertise

> **Saul:** "Arizona literally invented the right to lawyer advertising. Bates v. State Bar happened here. The Bar knows that history. They're not looking to pick fights over ads unless you do something genuinely misleading. Complaint-driven means someone has to get mad enough to report you. Keep the ads honest and you'll be fine."

### Bilingual / Spanish-Language Considerations

- Arizona has **no specific statute** governing Spanish-language legal advertising
- Arizona's official language is English (Proposition 203, 2000), but this applies to government operations, not private advertising
- The Consumer Fraud Act (A.R.S. 44-1521) applies equally to advertising in any language — deceptive practices are prohibited regardless of language used
- **Best practice:** If ad campaigns are conducted in Spanish, all required disclosures should appear in Spanish within the ad and in English on the landing page
- If a transaction or consultation is conducted in Spanish, some federal and state consumer protection frameworks may require translated disclosures
- Arizona's large Hispanic/Latino population (approximately 32% of state population) makes Spanish-language advertising a significant market opportunity, particularly in Phoenix, Tucson, Mesa, and border communities
- No requirement to file or register Spanish-language ads separately

### Arizona Consumer Fraud Act (A.R.S. 44-1521)

While not specific to legal advertising, the Arizona Consumer Fraud Act applies to all advertising in the state:
- Prohibits deception, false pretense, misrepresentation, or omission of material fact in connection with any advertisement
- Applies to "merchandise" defined broadly to include services (including legal services)
- Enforcement by Arizona Attorney General or private action
- **Civil penalties:** Up to $10,000 per willful violation
- **Private action statute of limitations:** 1 year from date claim arises
- **Implication for Second Chair:** All ad copy must be truthful. The Consumer Fraud Act provides an independent basis for liability beyond the Rules of Professional Conduct — and can be enforced by consumers directly, not just the Bar

- [ ] No false or misleading claims (ER 7.1)
