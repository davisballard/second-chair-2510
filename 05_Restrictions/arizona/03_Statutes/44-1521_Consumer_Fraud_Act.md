# A.R.S. § 44-1521 et seq. — Arizona Consumer Fraud Act

> **Saul:** "The Consumer Fraud Act is the second liability lane. Even if the AZ Bar shrugs at your ad, an unhappy claimant can sue under the CFA, the Attorney General can investigate, and you're staring at $10,000-per-violation civil penalties. Truthfulness isn't just a Bar rule — it's a state-law cause of action with its own private right of action."

---

## 1. THE STATUTE

The Arizona Consumer Fraud Act (A.R.S. §§ 44-1521 through 44-1534) prohibits, in connection with the sale or advertisement of any **merchandise** (broadly defined to include services):

> "The act, use or employment by any person of any deception, deceptive or unfair act or practice, fraud, false pretense, false promise, misrepresentation, or concealment, suppression or omission of any material fact with intent that others rely upon such concealment, suppression or omission, in connection with the sale or advertisement of any merchandise..."

Key features:
- Applies to **all advertising in the state**, not just lawyer advertising
- "Merchandise" includes **services**, including legal services
- Enforced by the Arizona Attorney General **and** by private action
- Applies regardless of whether the conduct violates any specific Bar rule
- Civil penalties up to **$10,000 per willful violation**

---

## 2. WHY IT MATTERS FOR SECOND CHAIR

The Consumer Fraud Act is a **separate and independent liability lane** from the Rules of Professional Conduct. A complaint that the AZ Bar dismisses (because it doesn't violate ER 7.1 specifically) can still be brought under the CFA — and the standard is sometimes stricter, because the CFA covers omissions of material fact.

Three implications for SC operations:

1. **Substantiation file isn't just for the Bar.** It also defends against CFA private actions. The AG can subpoena it. So can a plaintiff's discovery in a CFA case.
2. **Second Chair (not just WCTL) is exposed.** ER 7.1 disciplinary risk falls on WCTL (the lawyer). CFA risk falls on whoever made the deceptive advertising — that includes Second Chair as the actual advertiser.
3. **"Material fact" omissions matter.** ER 7.1 forbids omissions that make the message materially misleading. The CFA goes further — any "concealment, suppression, or omission of any material fact with intent that others rely" is actionable.

---

## 3. ELEMENTS OF A CFA CLAIM

A private plaintiff suing under the CFA must show:
1. A false promise, misrepresentation, or material omission;
2. Made in connection with the sale or advertisement of merchandise/services;
3. With intent that consumers rely;
4. Consumer **did rely** to their detriment;
5. Resulting in actual damages.

**Note:** Reliance is required for a private action. The AG action does not require individual reliance and can be brought even where no consumer was actually harmed.

---

## 4. STATUTE OF LIMITATIONS

| Action type | SOL |
|---|---|
| Private CFA action | **1 year** from date claim arises (Alaface v. National Investment Co., 181 Ariz. 586 (App. 1994)) |
| AG enforcement action | Generally subject to general 1-year statute on penalties |

---

## 5. COMMON CFA TRIPWIRES IN PI LEAD-GEN ADS

| Conduct | CFA exposure |
|---|---|
| Promised settlement amount that doesn't materialize | Could ground reliance + damages |
| Implying a specific case will be handled by senior partner when it's actually associates | Misrepresentation |
| "Free consultation" with hidden fee triggers | False promise |
| "100% refund if we don't win" but contingency-fee structure means there's never a refund | Misleading guarantee framing |
| Hidden lead-sale to multiple firms when claimant believes they're contacting one | Material omission of fact |
| Trade name implying a service or affiliation that doesn't exist (e.g., government endorsement) | Misrepresentation + ER 7.5 violation |

---

## 6. ATTORNEY GENERAL ENFORCEMENT POSTURE

The Arizona Attorney General's Consumer Protection Section actively investigates deceptive consumer practices. Recent (2020s) AG enforcement priorities have included:
- Online lead-generation in financial-services and legal-services verticals
- Deceptive "free trial" subscriptions
- Fake review aggregation
- Misleading discount/comparison pricing

The AG's office issues Civil Investigative Demands (CIDs) — administrative subpoenas with broad scope — before filing formal complaints. A CID is the first warning sign that an AG investigation is live.

---

## 7. THE PLAY (CFA Specific)

**Do:**
- Treat substantiation as a CFA matter, not just an ER 7.1 matter
- Document material disclosures (not just fee structure but also: how the lead is routed, who else may receive it, the firm-claimant relationship)
- Make sure consent flows are clear, not buried
- Keep ad creative claims aligned with what the user actually experiences after submitting

**Don't:**
- Don't promise specific outcomes or settlement amounts
- Don't omit the fact that a non-law-firm entity is the form operator
- Don't promise services that aren't actually delivered (e.g., "personalized case review by an attorney" if the form is actually evaluated by a paralegal or routed mechanically)
- Don't run "free consultation" copy if any fees can attach to that consultation
