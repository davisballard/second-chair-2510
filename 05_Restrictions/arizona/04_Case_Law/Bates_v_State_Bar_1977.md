# Bates v. State Bar of Arizona, 433 U.S. 350 (1977)

> **Saul:** "Arizona literally invented the right to lawyer advertising. Bates happened here. The Bar knows that history. They're not looking to pick fights over ads unless you do something genuinely misleading. Complaint-driven enforcement is the *posture*; Bates is the *reason*. Read the case. It tells you exactly where the lines are — and where they aren't."

---

## 1. THE FACTS

**John R. Bates** and **Van O'Steen** were Arizona-licensed lawyers (Phoenix) who in March 1974 opened a self-styled **"legal clinic"** designed to deliver routine legal services at low fixed prices to moderate-income clients. Their practice was deliberately limited to high-volume, standardizable matters: uncontested divorces, uncontested adoptions, simple personal bankruptcies, and name changes.

After two years they concluded that to survive economically they needed to advertise. On **February 22, 1976**, they ran a newspaper ad in the *Arizona Republic* stating they offered "legal services at very reasonable fees" and listing prices:

| Service | Price |
|---|---|
| Uncontested divorce | $175 + $20 filing fee |
| Name change | $95 + $20 filing fee |
| Simple personal bankruptcy (non-business) | $250 + $55 filing fee |
| Uncontested adoption | $225 + $10 publication |

The State Bar of Arizona's president filed a complaint charging Bates and O'Steen with violating **Disciplinary Rule 2-101(B)** of the AZ Supreme Court's then-existing disciplinary rules (adopted from the ABA Model Code), which forbade lawyers from publicizing themselves "through newspaper or magazine advertisements, radio or television announcements, display advertisements in the city or telephone directories or other means of commercial publicity."

The Arizona Supreme Court upheld the bar committee's recommendation of a **1-week suspension** for each.

---

## 2. THE HOLDING

The U.S. Supreme Court (Blackmun, J., **5–4** on the First Amendment issue, **4–4** on the Sherman Act issue) **reversed** as to the First Amendment claim.

> **Truthful advertising of the prices of routine legal services is commercial speech protected by the First Amendment**, and a state's blanket prohibition on attorney price advertising violates the First and Fourteenth Amendments.

---

## 3. THE REASONING (Commercial-Speech Doctrine)

Building on *Virginia State Board of Pharmacy v. Virginia Citizens Consumer Council*, 425 U.S. 748 (1976), the Court held that commercial speech serves "individual and societal interests in assuring informed and reliable decisionmaking."

The Court systematically rejected each justification the Arizona Bar advanced:

1. **Adverse effect on professionalism** — the "above trade" view of lawyers is "an anachronism"
2. **Inherently misleading nature of attorney advertising** — the routine services Bates advertised were susceptible to standardized pricing; the State Bar's own prepaid legal services program demonstrated this
3. **Adverse effect on the administration of justice** — speculative
4. **Undesirable economic effects** (higher overhead, barriers to entry) — unsupported
5. **Adverse effect on quality of service** — irrelevant to truthful price information
6. **Enforcement difficulties** — administrable through case-by-case discipline

---

## 4. THE CARVE-OUTS — RESTRICTIONS BATES EXPRESSLY LEFT INTACT

This is the strategic core for AZ lead-gen operations. Bates didn't make all lawyer advertising untouchable — it specified what regulators could still do:

### 1. False, deceptive, or misleading advertising
> "Advertising that is false, deceptive, or misleading of course is subject to restraint."

This is the foundation of AZ ER 7.1 today. Every advertising rule in Arizona's modern framework descends from this carve-out.

### 2. Quality claims
> "Advertising claims as to the quality of legal services...may be so likely to be misleading as to warrant restriction."

Hence the AZ Bar's continuing skepticism of "best lawyer," "top-rated," "winningest" claims absent clear factual foundation.

### 3. In-person solicitation
> "Problems associated with in-person solicitation of clients at the hospital room or accident site...might well pose dangers of overreaching."

Confirmed as flatly prohibitable in *Ohralik v. Ohio State Bar*, 436 U.S. 447 (1978). This is the foundation of AZ ER 7.3's prohibition on live person-to-person solicitation.

### 4. Time, place, manner restrictions
Reasonable t/p/m regulations remain available.

### 5. Electronic broadcast media
> "Special problems...will warrant special consideration."

Although later cases (Zauderer, Shapero) made clear blanket broadcast bans cannot stand, regulators retain wider latitude in broadcast contexts.

---

## 5. THE DISSENT (Powell, joined in relevant part by Burger, Stewart, Rehnquist)

Powell's dissent argued:
- Legal services are unlike standardized pharmacy products — even "uncontested" matters involve unpredictable variables (custody disputes that emerge mid-divorce, asset disputes, etc.)
- Lay consumers cannot accurately assess what services they need in advance, so price advertising is inherently distorting
- The bar's enforcement capacity is inadequate
- Predicted "competitive advertising on an escalating basis" benefiting aggressive firms over ethical ones

Powell's prediction has been widely viewed as borne out by modern PI advertising markets. Aggressive PI advertising — which Powell foresaw — is the dominant cultural perception of attorney advertising 50 years post-Bates.

## 6. THE BATES → MODERN-DOCTRINE CHAIN

Bates is the foundation. The doctrinal building blocks since:

| Case | Year | Holding |
|---|---|---|
| **Bates v. State Bar of Arizona** | 1977 | Truthful price advertising of routine services is protected |
| **Ohralik v. Ohio State Bar** | 1978 | In-person solicitation of accident victim in hospital flatly prohibitable |
| **In re R.M.J.** | 1982 | Listings of practice areas/jurisdictions protected; non-misleading commercial speech requires substantial state interest narrowly tailored |
| **Zauderer v. Office of Disciplinary Counsel** | 1985 | Targeted print advertising (Dalkon Shield ad seeking IUD plaintiffs) protected; required factual disclosures (contingency-fee mechanics) permitted |
| **Shapero v. Kentucky Bar Ass'n** | 1988 | Targeted direct-mail solicitation of prospective clients known to need particular services protected |
| **Peel v. Attorney Registration & Disciplinary Comm'n** | 1990 | Truthful certification claims (e.g., "Certified Civil Trial Specialist") protected |
| **Florida Bar v. Went For It, Inc.** | 1995 | 5-4 — **upheld** Florida's 30-day blackout on direct-mail solicitation of accident victims and their families |

Florida Bar v. Went For It is the **first post-Bates case sustaining a meaningful new restriction beyond the original carve-outs**. AZ doesn't have a 30-day blackout — but Florida-inspired arguments could surface in any AZ rulemaking.

## 7. STRATEGIC IMPLICATION FOR ARIZONA PI ADVERTISING

Arizona is the **birthplace of modern lawyer advertising rights**. The State Bar of Arizona has historically been **less aggressive than Florida, New York, or California** in policing advertising content — partly because:
- The AZ Supreme Court was directly reversed in *Bates*
- AZ's 2020 elimination of ER 5.4 (creating ABS / non-lawyer ownership) signals a deregulatory posture
- AZ's current rules (ER 7.1–7.5, restructured 2020) mostly track ABA Model Rules but with ABS-friendly modifications

### Where the live enforcement zones are today (AZ)

1. **Misleading specifics** — fake "Top 100 Trial Lawyer" badges with no factual basis
2. **Case-results testimonials** without disclaimers and full context
3. **ABS firms** running multi-state ads that confuse Arizona consumers about who they're hiring (see Legal Help Partners PDJ-2024-9035 in [`Recent_AZ_Discipline_Advertising.md`](Recent_AZ_Discipline_Advertising.md))
4. **Trade names** that imply government affiliation or specialty certifications the firm doesn't have

### Where the live enforcement zones aren't

- Crash footage in itself (no SB37 analogue)
- Settlement amounts with disclaimers
- Fighter / advocate positioning
- Specialization language ("focuses on," "experienced in," "specialize" — if true)
- Lead-gen vendor relationships (post-ER 5.4 elimination)

## 9. THE PLAY (Bates Specific)

**Do:**
- Operate with the confidence that aggressive, truthful PI advertising is constitutionally protected
- Use settlement amounts, fighter positioning, urgency framing — these are protected commercial speech
- Recognize that AZ specifically has the deepest constitutional anchor for advertising freedom

**Don't:**
- Don't slip into the carve-outs Bates explicitly preserved: false/misleading content, quality superlatives without basis, in-person solicitation
- Don't read Bates as immunizing all advertising — it immunizes *truthful* advertising
- Don't assume Florida Bar v. Went For It-style restrictions are coming to AZ; the AZ Bar's posture has been the opposite (deregulation)
