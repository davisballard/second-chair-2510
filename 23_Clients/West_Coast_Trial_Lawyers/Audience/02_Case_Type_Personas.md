# Arizona Non-Soft-Tissue PI Personas
## B2C Audience Profiles — $10K Meta Pilot Campaign

**Prepared:** April 13, 2026
**Lead Agent:** Jon Steel (Narrative Strategy — Deprivation Thinking)
**Campaign:** Second Chair x West Coast Trial Lawyers — $10K Arizona Pilot
**Status:** Creative strategy document — informs all ad hooks, visual direction, and copy tone
**Method:** Natural habitat research + deprivation thinking. Core principle: find the emotional truth by asking "what did this person LOSE?" That's where the insight lives.
**Key constraint:** Meta Special Ad Categories — creative IS the targeting. These personas must inform ad hooks that make the right person stop scrolling.

> **Methodology transparency (2026-04-21):** These six personas were built from (a) ADOT / NHTSA / Census demographic and crash data applied to Arizona-specific geography (Doc 1), (b) secondary research on post-trauma psychology and PI intake patterns, and (c) professional judgment from the agency team — Davis (18 years brand advertising) and Jon Steel-method deprivation framing. **They were not validated against interviews with actual WCTL claimants or primary ethnographic research.** The specificity (Marco's $2,100 mortgage, Miguel's remittance to mom, Diana's 6-hour work tolerance) is constructed detail grounded in demographic and behavioral reality, not quotes from real people. This is the common agency-planner practice Jon Steel-style "natural habitat" work actually represents — educated archetype-building, not ethnography. Treat these personas as sharp creative scaffolding, not as quantitative claims. Where WCTL has or can share intake data, these profiles should be re-validated after the pilot for Phase 2.

> **⚠️ Persona ≠ Casting (2026-04-22).** The personas in this document are **psychological scaffolding** — emotional profiles of real market segments — not demographic mandates for on-screen protagonists. The Phase 1 creative protagonists in `Creative/Week_1_Launch_Concepts.md` are cast for **AZ demographic reach under Meta Special Ad Categories** (51% White / 32% Hispanic / 4.6% Black / 4.2% AI-AN / 3.6% Asian), optimized against homophily-bias research showing audiences engage more readily with in-group faces. Concretely:
>
> | Persona (this doc — psychological truth) | Phase 1 Casting (Creative doc — on-screen face) | Rationale |
> |---|---|---|
> | Marco Reyes (Mexican-American HVAC tech, §1) | White Arizona-weathered rider (Creative doc) | Motorcycle base in AZ skews ~55-65% white; rider identity cue carries across ethnicity |
> | Diana Nguyen (Vietnamese-American office manager, §2) | White Arizona working mom (Creative doc) | Broadest-pool MVA catch-all asset; white casting reaches largest AZ demographic |
> | Miguel (Mexican-origin pedestrian, §3) | **Deferred to Phase 2** — Spanish creative parked with dedicated budget | Spanish ad set not starved as 1-of-6 in a broad English ad set |
> | Sarah (§4, trucking nurse) | Mixed white-Latina (Creative doc) | White-passing in some frames / Latina in others — pulls both audiences |
> | James (§5, DUI victim) | **Excluded per signed contract §1.3** — out of scope | DUI not in pilot scope |
> | Sofia (§6, rideshare) | **Phase 2 scaffold** — not produced for Phase 1 | Volume too low (~17/month) to justify dedicated creative |
>
> **The persona's emotional truth does not require demographic match on-screen for resonance.** Marco's loss-of-identity-as-rider lands whether the actor reads as white or Mexican-American; Diana's loss-of-household-control lands whether the actor reads as Vietnamese-American or white. Ethnicity of the protagonist is a reach decision under SAC constraints, not a persona-authenticity decision. When you see "Marco Reyes, Mexican-American" in this doc and "Marco, white rider" in the Creative doc, that is **by design, not a contradiction**.

## How to Use This Document

Each persona represents a real type of person who exists in volume across Arizona. They are not aspirational composites. They are drawn from demographic data, crash statistics, geographic reality, and behavioral patterns specific to this state.

For the creative team: read each persona end to end before concepting. The insight is not in the profile table -- it is in what they lost. Start there. Work backward to the hook.

For media planning: the "Where to Reach Them" section maps directly to platform, timing, and format decisions within the $10K pilot budget.

## 1. Marco — The Motorcyclist

### Quick Profile

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Name** | Marco Reyes |
| **Age** | 35 |
| **Occupation** | HVAC Technician (commercial) |
| **Location** | Mesa, AZ (East Valley) |
| **Household Income** | ~$78K |
| **Family Status** | Married, two kids (7 and 4) |
| **Bike** | 2019 Harley-Davidson Street Glide |
| **Riding** | Year-round commuter + weekend club rides |

### Background

Marco has been riding since he was 19. He started on a used Sportster he bought from a coworker and upgraded twice since then. The Street Glide is the first bike he bought new -- he remembers the exact day, because his wife gave him the look that said this is the last big purchase for a while, and he nodded, and they both knew it was worth it. He rides to job sites across the Valley when his van isn't needed. On Saturdays, he rides with a loose group of guys out of a Mesa shop -- not a formal club, but the same six or seven faces every week. Carefree Highway, Bush Highway, the Beeline. His Facebook feed is 40% motorcycle content. He follows three Arizona rider groups and checks them daily.

Marco is not reckless. He wears a half-helmet (not full-face -- too hot), boots, and a leather vest. He watches for cars. He has been watching for cars for sixteen years. He knows intersections are where riders die.

### The Accident

Tuesday, 4:45 PM. Eastbound on Southern Avenue approaching Dobson Road. A woman in a white Nissan Rogue made a left turn across his lane. She said she didn't see him. Marco laid the bike down at 40 mph. He slid 35 feet on asphalt.

Broken right femur (mid-shaft fracture). Road rash covering his right arm, right hip, and right shoulder -- deep enough to require debridement in the ER. The Street Glide was totaled. An ambulance took him to Banner Desert. He was in surgery by 8 PM. Titanium rod, femoral nail, six screws.

### What They Lost (Deprivation Thinking)

This is the core insight.

**Mobility.** Marco went from a man who moved through the world on his own terms -- truck, bike, two wheels, four wheels, his choice -- to a man who cannot stand without crutches. He cannot drive for 8 weeks minimum. He cannot ride for 6 months, if ever again. His wife drives him to follow-up appointments. He sits in the passenger seat and does not talk much on the way home.

**Income.** HVAC work is physical. Climbing ladders, carrying equipment, crawling through attic spaces in 115-degree Arizona summers. Marco cannot work for at least 3 months, probably 4-5 before he can do full duty. His company offers no short-term disability. His wife works part-time at a dental office. The mortgage is $2,100/month. They have about $4,500 in savings.

**Identity.** This is the one that cuts deepest and the one Marco will never say out loud. He is a rider. That is not what he does -- it is who he is. His friends are riders. His weekends are rides. His profile picture is him on the Street Glide. The bike is totaled. His leg may never be the same. He does not know yet if he will ride again, and the not-knowing is worse than a definite no would be. He cannot go to the shop on Saturdays. He is not part of the Saturday morning text chain anymore, because what would he say? His world got smaller by exactly the circumference of two wheels.

**Routine.** Marco's days had a rhythm: up at 5:30, ride or drive to the first job, three or four sites, home by 5, dinner with the kids, check Facebook for ride plans, bed. Now his days have no rhythm. He wakes up, takes pain meds, watches TV, tries to help with the kids, fails because he can't pick up his 4-year-old, and counts hours until the next dose. The absence of structure is disorienting for a man who has worked with his hands since he was 17.

**The bike itself.** The Street Glide was not just transportation. It was the most expensive thing Marco ever bought for himself. It was the physical proof that he had made it far enough in life to own something purely because he wanted it. It is gone. Insurance will pay out maybe $18K. He owed $12K. The math leaves him with nothing and no bike.

### Post-Accident Psychology

**Week 1:**
Marco is in a haze of pain medication, hospital fluorescent lights, and the slow realization that this is not a week-long problem. His wife is scared and trying not to show it. His mother-in-law is watching the kids. The at-fault driver's insurance company called on Day 3 and offered to "help with medical bills" -- Marco's wife took the call because Marco was asleep. She wrote down the number but didn't call back. She doesn't know what to do. Marco's riding buddies started a GoFundMe that raised $2,800 in three days. He was grateful and humiliated in equal measure.

**Month 1:**
The femur is healing but slowly. Physical therapy has started and it is brutal. Marco can hobble on crutches. He has not been outside the house except for PT and doctor appointments. The first medical bills arrived -- the ER alone was $47,000. He has insurance through his employer (a small commercial HVAC company), but his deductible is $6,000 and the out-of-pocket max is $12,000. He has applied for short-term disability through the state and been denied on a technicality he doesn't understand. His wife increased her hours to full-time at the dental office. They are behind on the mortgage by one payment. The at-fault driver's insurance company made a formal offer of $35,000 to settle everything. Marco knows this is not enough but has no framework for knowing what "enough" looks like.

**Month 3-6:**
Marco is walking without crutches but with a visible limp. He returned to work on light duty at 14 weeks -- answering phones, ordering parts, doing paperwork. He makes 60% of what he made in the field. He has not been on a motorcycle. He has not been to the shop. Two of his riding buddies have texted less and less frequently -- not out of malice, but because the friendship was built on a shared activity that no longer exists. Marco scrolls past motorcycle content on Facebook and feels something between longing and nausea. His marriage is strained, not from anger, but from the quiet weight of a household running on fumes. He still has not hired a lawyer. He does not know how lawyer fees work. He is afraid a lawyer will cost money he doesn't have. He is also afraid the insurance company's $35K offer is the best he will get, and if he rejects it, he'll end up with nothing.

### Goals (What He Wants from a Lawyer)

1. **Someone who will not cost him money upfront.** This is the threshold question. If Marco thinks a lawyer costs money out of pocket, the conversation is over before it starts.
2. **Someone who understands what happened to him.** Not generically "injured in a motorcycle accident" but specifically: a rider who was doing nothing wrong, who was seen as invisible by a cager, who lost his bike and his livelihood because someone didn't check their mirror.
3. **A clear answer on what the case is worth.** Marco does not want vague promises. He wants someone to explain: here is what your medical bills are, here is what your lost wages are, here is what the at-fault driver's policy limits are, here is the range of what this case could settle for.
4. **To not feel like a number.** Marco has dealt with insurance adjusters who called him "the claimant." He wants a human being who will call him Marco.
5. **To get his life back.** He doesn't articulate it this way, but what he wants is for someone to take the legal and financial weight off his shoulders so he can focus on healing and getting back to work -- and maybe, eventually, back on a bike.

### Frustrations (What's Blocking Him)

- **He thinks lawyers are expensive.** He grew up in a household where lawyers were for rich people or for trouble. Contingency fees are not in his vocabulary.
- **He has already been contacted by the insurance company.** They were friendly. They made an offer. He is unsure whether hiring a lawyer means starting over or losing the offer.
- **He doesn't know who to trust.** He has seen PI lawyer ads on TV and they all feel the same -- aggressive, loud, promising millions. He doesn't identify with that world.
- **Pride.** Marco fixed things for a living. He is not comfortable asking for help. Filing a claim feels like admitting he can't handle his own problems.
- **Time has passed.** It has been 3 months. He worries it's "too late" to hire a lawyer (it isn't -- Arizona's statute of limitations is 2 years for PI).

### Messaging That Resonates

- **"A driver didn't see you. An insurance company doesn't want to."** -- This mirrors his lived experience: invisible on the road, invisible to the adjuster.
- **"No fee unless you win."** -- The single most important piece of information for Marco. This must be prominent, not buried.
- **"You shouldn't have to fight an insurance company while learning to walk again."** -- Acknowledges the reality of recovery + legal battle simultaneously.
- **Motorcycle-specific imagery.** A bike on its side. A garage with an empty space. A helmet on a shelf. NOT crash footage. The absence of the bike, not the crash itself.
- **Community language.** "Fellow riders" / "If you ride in Arizona" / references to year-round riding. Marco self-identifies as a rider. Speak to that identity.
- **Financial reality framing.** "Bills don't stop because you can't work." This is not fearmongering -- it is Marco's exact situation.

### Messaging That Repels

- **Crash footage or graphic injury imagery.** Marco does not want to relive the crash. He scrolls past anything that looks like an accident scene.
- **"Get the millions you deserve!"** -- Marco knows his case is not a million-dollar case. Inflated promises make him distrust the messenger.
- **Aggressive, predatory tone.** "Don't let them get away with it!" / "Fight back!" -- Marco is exhausted, not angry. Combative language doesn't match his emotional state.
- **Generic stock photos of men in suits.** Marco has never hired a lawyer. A stock photo of a grinning attorney in a $3,000 suit makes him feel like the target, not the client.
- **Anything that implies the accident was his fault.** "Were you riding without a helmet?" / "Even if you were partially at fault..." -- Marco was doing nothing wrong. Creative that hedges on fault reads as doubt.
- **Settlement amount promises.** He's been burned by the insurance company's lowball. Another number from a stranger means nothing.

### Where to Reach Him

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Platform** | Facebook (primary). He checks it daily, mostly Groups and Feed. |
| **Time of Day** | Evening (7-10 PM) when he is on the couch post-dinner. Mornings (6-7 AM) during what used to be his pre-ride scroll. |
| **Content Context** | In or near motorcycle-related content. His feed is full of ride photos, bike builds, and Arizona rider group posts. |
| **Format** | Video (15-30 sec) or single image with text overlay. He watches videos in-feed with sound off. Captions are essential. |
| **Language** | English. Marco is bilingual but consumes social media primarily in English. Spanglish elements (a phrase, a visual cue) would signal cultural familiarity without feeling forced. |
| **Device** | Mobile. 95% of his Facebook use is on his phone. |

## 2. Diana — The Commuter (Moderate-Severe MVA)

### Quick Profile

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Name** | Diana Nguyen |
| **Age** | 42 |
| **Occupation** | Office Manager at a property management company |
| **Location** | Chandler, AZ |
| **Household Income** | ~$92K (combined with husband) |
| **Family Status** | Married, three kids (14, 11, 8) |
| **Vehicle** | 2021 Honda CR-V |
| **Commute** | Chandler to central Phoenix via I-10, 35 minutes each way |

### Background

Diana is the person who holds everything together and would never describe herself that way. She manages a seven-person office for a property management company near 7th Street and Camelback. She has been there nine years. She handles accounts payable, tenant communications, vendor scheduling, and the two property managers who can't keep their own calendars straight. She is very good at her job.

Her mornings are a machine: alarm at 5:40, lunches packed by 6:10, kids fed and moving by 6:30, husband handles the school drop-off, she is on I-10 northbound by 6:50, at her desk by 7:30. She drives the same route every day. She could do it in her sleep. She almost does, some mornings, on three hours of rest after staying up with her 8-year-old who had a nightmare.

Diana is not a person who thinks about car accidents. She is a person who thinks about whether the 11-year-old's soccer cleats still fit and whether the rent checks from the Glendale property came in.

### The Accident

Thursday, 5:55 PM. Westbound I-10, just past the I-17 interchange, stop-and-go traffic. Diana was at a full stop. A Ford F-250 rear-ended her at approximately 35 mph -- the driver was looking at his phone. The CR-V's rear crumple zone absorbed some of the impact but her head snapped backward and then forward. She felt a sharp, electric pain shoot from her neck down her right arm. She couldn't turn her head.

Paramedics arrived. She refused the ambulance because she was thinking about who would pick up her kids. Her husband came to the scene. She drove home (he followed in his truck). She woke up the next morning unable to get out of bed.

ER visit Friday morning: herniated disc at C5-C6, Grade 2 concussion. The doctor told her no screens for two weeks, no driving for at least four, and to see a neurosurgeon for the disc. She sat in the parking lot of Banner Gateway and cried for twenty minutes, not because of the pain, but because she did not know who would run the office on Monday.

### What They Lost (Deprivation Thinking)

**Control.** Diana's entire life is organized around being the person who manages things. She manages the office, the household, the kids' schedules, the grocery list, the bills, the dog's vet appointments. A herniated disc and a concussion did not just injure her body -- they removed the instrument she uses to hold her world together. She cannot drive. She cannot look at a screen for more than ten minutes without a headache that makes her nauseous. She cannot organize. She cannot manage. The thing she is -- the person who holds it all together -- she cannot be that right now.

**The invisible labor.** Here is what Diana's family discovered in Week 2: nobody knew where anything was. The login for the electric bill autopay. Which kid has soccer on Tuesday versus Thursday. Where the pediatrician's number is stored. What the dog eats (brand, not just "dog food"). These are not things Diana did for recognition. They are things she did because nobody else did them, and now that she can't, the household is in a kind of slow-motion collapse that confirms both her worst fear (that she's indispensable) and her deepest resentment (that she shouldn't have to be).

**Income.** Diana's salary is $48K. Her husband is a foreman for a residential construction company and makes $44K. Combined, they are comfortable but not cushioned. Diana burned through her two weeks of PTO in the first two weeks. Her company offers no short-term disability. She is now on unpaid FMLA leave. They have lost $1,850/paycheck every two weeks. The mortgage is $2,350/month. Car payment on the CR-V: $410. They were not living paycheck to paycheck, but they were not living with a 3-month emergency fund either.

**Cognitive function.** The concussion is the injury Diana didn't expect to be debilitating. She can't follow a conversation with more than two people. She forgets words. She reads a paragraph and can't recall what it said. She tried to help her 14-year-old with algebra and couldn't track the steps. This terrifies her more than the neck pain, because the neck will heal, but what if her brain doesn't come all the way back? She hasn't said this out loud to anyone.

**The commute.** This sounds minor but it isn't. The I-10 drive was Diana's only time alone in a day. Thirty-five minutes of podcast, of silence, of not being needed by anyone. The accident happened on that road. When she eventually gets behind the wheel again, she will flinch every time someone approaches from behind in stop-and-go traffic. The one sliver of solitude in her day has been converted into a source of anxiety.

### Post-Accident Psychology

**Week 1:**
Diana is on the couch, in the dark (light sensitivity from the concussion), trying to manage her household by giving verbal instructions to her husband and failing because he doesn't know the systems. She has texted her office manager colleague seven times with instructions. Her boss called to check in and she could hear the unasked question beneath the concern: when are you coming back? She is furious at the man who hit her but the fury is beneath three layers of guilt about not being at work, not being a functional parent, and not being able to explain to her 8-year-old why Mom can't read him a bedtime story.

**Month 1:**
The concussion symptoms have improved but not resolved. She can look at screens for 30-40 minutes before the headache starts. The herniated disc requires an epidural injection before the neurosurgeon will discuss next steps. She had the injection on Day 21 and it helped about 40%. She is driving short distances (school, grocery) but not the freeway. She is back at work part-time, 4 hours a day, and she is exhausted by noon. The at-fault driver's insurance (State Farm) assigned an adjuster who called twice, was polite, and asked for her medical records. She signed a release because she didn't know she shouldn't have. She has not hired a lawyer. She googled "do I need a lawyer for a car accident" at 11 PM one Tuesday and read three articles that contradicted each other.

**Month 3-6:**
The disc is not responding to conservative treatment. The neurosurgeon is recommending an anterior cervical discectomy and fusion (ACDF) -- a surgery that means 6-8 weeks of additional recovery, a neck brace, and no lifting more than 10 pounds. Diana has not scheduled the surgery because she cannot afford to miss more work and she is terrified of spinal surgery. She is back at work full-time but medicating through the pain with ibuprofen and muscle relaxants. She is not the same at work -- she makes mistakes she never used to make, and she notices them before anyone else does, which is somehow worse. The insurance company has gone quiet. She doesn't know if that's good or bad. She feels like she is slowly being forgotten.

### Goals (What She Wants from a Lawyer)

1. **Someone who will explain what's happening.** Diana is a manager -- she processes information and makes decisions. But she has no framework for understanding insurance claims, medical liens, liability disputes, or surgical case valuations. She wants someone to lay it out clearly, step by step, the way she would brief her own staff.
2. **Permission to get the surgery.** This sounds strange but it is the truth. Diana is delaying a surgery she needs because she is afraid of the financial consequences. She needs someone to tell her: get the surgery, we will make sure the bills are accounted for in the claim.
3. **For someone to deal with the insurance company.** She signed that medical release and she knows it was a mistake. She wants someone to step in and take over the communication she is not equipped to handle.
4. **A realistic number.** Diana does not want to hear "millions." She wants to hear: your medical bills are X, your lost wages are Y, your future treatment costs are Z, and we believe the case value is in this range. Give her a spreadsheet and she'll sleep better.
5. **Not to be patronized.** Diana is smart, organized, and competent. She wants a lawyer who talks to her like a professional, not like a victim.

### Frustrations (What's Blocking Her)

- **She already signed the medical release.** She is embarrassed about this and worried a lawyer will judge her for it.
- **She doesn't have time to "deal with" a lawyer.** She is barely managing work, kids, and recovery. Adding a legal process feels like one more thing on a list that is already too long.
- **She thinks her case "isn't bad enough."** She didn't break a bone. She can walk. She is back at work. She compares herself to people with worse injuries and talks herself out of taking action.
- **She doesn't know the surgery changes the case value.** A pre-surgery herniated disc case and a post-surgery ACDF case are very different claims. Diana doesn't know this.
- **Her husband thinks they should just "handle it themselves."** He is not against lawyers per se, but he grew up in a household where you didn't "make a big deal" out of things. Diana is the one who would hire a lawyer, but she needs to be able to justify it to herself and to him.

### Messaging That Resonates

- **"You're still in pain. The bills didn't stop. The insurance company went quiet."** -- This is Diana's exact experience at Month 3. It names the silence that feels like abandonment.
- **"Your doctor says you need surgery. Who's making sure it's covered?"** -- Speaks directly to the ACDF decision Diana is delaying.
- **"No fee unless your case wins. Free consultation."** -- Same as Marco: the financial barrier must be removed in the first three seconds.
- **"You held everything together after the crash. Let someone hold this."** -- Acknowledges Diana's identity as the person who manages everything, and gives her permission to hand off one piece.
- **I-10 imagery.** The freeway, stop-and-go traffic, the specific visual language of a Phoenix commute. Not crash footage -- commute footage. The road she knows.
- **Soft, professional tone.** Diana responds to competence, not aggression. The ad should feel like a trusted advisor, not a billboard.

### Messaging That Repels

- **"You deserve MAXIMUM compensation!"** -- Diana's internal calculator immediately flags this as empty. She wants specifics, not superlatives.
- **Victimhood framing.** "You're suffering and you deserve justice" -- Diana does not see herself as a victim. She sees herself as a person dealing with a problem. Victimhood language makes her scroll past.
- **Urgency manufacturing.** "Time is running out!" / "Don't wait another day!" -- Diana's life is already operating in crisis mode. Adding false urgency reads as manipulation.
- **Testimonials with dollar amounts.** "We got $1.2M for a client just like you" -- Diana's BS detector is finely tuned. She knows that number has nothing to do with her situation.
- **Anything that looks like it was made for someone else.** Generic creative with "Phoenix" swapped in. Diana notices details. She will notice if the creative doesn't feel specific.

### Where to Reach Her

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Platform** | Facebook (primary). She scrolls the feed during her 8-year-old's soccer practice and in bed before sleep. Instagram (secondary) for brief, visual content. |
| **Time of Day** | 9-10 PM (after kids are in bed -- this is her window). Saturday mornings. Lunch break (12-1 PM) if she's scrolling at her desk. |
| **Content Context** | Between parenting content, local community posts, and home/DIY content. She does not follow legal content. The ad must interrupt a non-legal scroll. |
| **Format** | Single image with clear text overlay, or 15-second video. She scrolls fast. The hook must land in 2 seconds. |
| **Language** | English. |
| **Device** | Mobile, almost exclusively. |

## 3. Miguel — The Pedestrian

### Quick Profile

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Name** | Miguel Angel Hernandez |
| **Age** | 28 |
| **Occupation** | Line cook at a Mexican restaurant (West Phoenix) |
| **Location** | Maryvale (75th Ave & Indian School area), Phoenix, AZ |
| **Household Income** | ~$31K |
| **Family Status** | Single, sends money to his mother in Hermosillo monthly |
| **Vehicle** | None. Walks and takes the bus. |
| **Primary Language** | Spanish (functional English, reads slowly, does not trust English-only documents) |

### Background

Miguel has been in Phoenix for six years. He works the dinner shift at a family-owned restaurant on 51st Avenue -- 3 PM to midnight, six days a week. He walks 22 minutes from his apartment to the restaurant. He takes the bus when it rains or when it's over 110 and the sidewalk burns through his shoes. He sends $400 to his mother every month and keeps enough to cover rent ($675 for a room in a shared house), food, a prepaid phone, and not much else.

Miguel's life is small and deliberate. He does not go out. He works, he eats, he calls his mother on Sundays, he watches futbol on his phone, he sleeps. He has a cousin in Glendale he sees once or twice a month. He is quiet, reliable, and invisible -- invisible to his employer in the way that good cooks are invisible, invisible to the city of Phoenix in the way that pedestrians in Maryvale are invisible.

He crosses Indian School Road at 75th Avenue every night on his way home. There is a crosswalk. There is no pedestrian signal. The streetlight on the southeast corner has been out for at least three months.

### The Accident

Wednesday, 12:15 AM. Miguel was crossing Indian School Road in the crosswalk, heading south. He was wearing dark clothes (his kitchen blacks). A driver in a Dodge Ram, turning right from 75th Avenue, did not see him and struck him at approximately 20 mph. Miguel was thrown onto the hood and then to the pavement.

Broken pelvis (left superior ramus fracture). Fractured left wrist. Road abrasion on his left side. A bystander called 911. The ambulance took him to Valleywise Medical Center -- the safety-net hospital that serves most of Maryvale. He does not remember the ambulance ride.

The driver stayed at the scene. Police arrived. The report was written in English. Miguel was in too much pain to explain what happened. A nurse at Valleywise who spoke Spanish translated during intake. Nobody asked Miguel if he wanted a copy of the police report.

### What They Lost (Deprivation Thinking)

**The ability to move through the world.** Miguel's entire life was built on walking. He walked to work. He walked to the grocery store. He walked to the bus stop. He walked to his cousin's apartment. A broken pelvis does not just hurt -- it erases the only transportation system Miguel had. He is now confined to a room in a house where four other men live, and he cannot get to the bathroom without help. He is 28 years old and he cannot walk to the toilet alone. The indignity of this is enormous and he has no one to express it to.

**Income.** Miguel has zero paid leave, zero disability insurance, zero savings beyond $800 in a checking account. His employer pays cash, partially off the books. He has no documentation of his wages beyond the deposit patterns in his bank app. He cannot work for at least 8 weeks. His employer told him his job would be there when he came back, but Miguel knows that means someone else is doing his station right now, and if that someone is good, there is no station to come back to.

**The $400 to Hermosillo.** This is the detail that breaks him. His mother depends on that money. She is 62, has arthritis, and cleans houses three days a week. Miguel's $400/month pays her rent. The accident didn't just injure him in the present -- it bent the arc. The knee surgery means 4-6 months of rehab. The second surgery (ACL reconstruction) means another 6-9 months. He is looking at a full year of diminished physical capacity during the period of his life when he was supposed to be building momentum. Job performance is affected because he can't travel to client sites. Commission is down. The gym is gone. The hiking is gone. The casual, effortless physicality of being a healthy 31-year-old man -- gone. And it was taken from him by a stranger who chose to drive drunk.

- **"You could be entitled to compensation."** -- Too vague. Too generic. Sounds like every late-night TV ad James has ever muted.
- **Celebrity lawyer branding.** A firm where the lawyer's face is the logo. James distrusts personal branding in professional services.
- **Anything that implies he should have done something differently.** "Were you at a bar?" / "Were you out late?" -- James was sober, driving home, obeying every law. Creative that even slightly implies shared responsibility will lose him instantly.
- **Settlement amount promises.** "We recovered $2M for a DUI victim!" -- James knows every case is different. Specific numbers from unrelated cases feel like marketing, not information.

### Where to Reach Him

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Platform** | Facebook (primary -- he scrolls the feed daily, mostly sports and local content). Instagram (secondary). LinkedIn (he checks it for work but would never engage with a PI ad there). |
| **Time of Day** | 9-11 PM (his nightly scroll). Weekend mornings (Saturday/Sunday 8-10 AM, when he used to hike and now sits with coffee). |
| **Content Context** | Between sports content, Scottsdale local pages, tech industry posts, and friend updates. He does not follow legal content. |
| **Format** | Clean single image with bold text hook, or 15-second video. James is a fast scroller -- the hook must land in the first 2 seconds or he's gone. |
| **Language** | English. |
| **Device** | Mobile (iPhone). |

## 6. Sofia — The Rideshare Passenger

> **⚠️ PHASE 2 SCAFFOLD — DO NOT PRODUCE PHASE 1 CREATIVE.** Rideshare = ~17 non-ST incidents/month (smallest volume of any case type in scope). At Phase 1 budget, dedicated rideshare creative would be starved as 1-of-7 in a broad ad set. Any rideshare leads in Phase 1 arrive incidentally via the S5 general-severe catch-all. Sofia persona retained here as groundwork for Phase 2 audience expansion only.


> **Scope note (2026-04-21):** Rideshare represents ~17 non-soft-tissue incidents/month in Arizona (Register 3.7, `extrapolated`) — the smallest volume of any case type in this playbook. Sofia's persona should be treated as **Phase-2 scaffolding, not Phase-1 priority.** The campaign's two-ad-set architecture (Doc 3 §12) intentionally does not give rideshare its own budget line; any rideshare lead that arrives in Phase 1 comes through the Severe Injury ad set as an incidental capture. Additionally, Sofia's injuries as described (cervical strain, disc bulge at C4-C5, annular tear at C5-C6, fractured wrist) sit at the borderline of non-soft-tissue — the wrist is clearly qualifying, but the cervical work would need to clear the injury-severity quiz to pass. Keep the persona for Phase 2 audience expansion; do not build dedicated Phase 1 creative for it.

### Quick Profile

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Name** | Sofia Patel |
| **Age** | 24 |
| **Occupation** | Digital marketing coordinator at a Tempe startup |
| **Location** | Tempe, AZ (near ASU campus, post-grad) |
| **Household Income** | ~$58K |
| **Family Status** | Single, lives with a roommate |
| **Vehicle** | Owns a 2018 Honda Civic but uses Uber 3-4x/week for going out |

### Background

Sofia graduated from ASU two years ago with a marketing degree and stayed because she liked the city, liked her job, and liked that her apartment was $1,100/month instead of what her friends in LA and New York were paying. She works at a B2B SaaS startup in Tempe that does something with inventory management. She doesn't love the product but she loves the team, the happy hours, and the fact that her boss trusts her to run the company's Instagram and TikTok without micromanaging.

Sofia is social. Thursday through Saturday, she is out -- Mill Avenue, Old Town Scottsdale, the Roosevelt Row bars in Downtown Phoenix. She takes Uber because it's responsible, because parking is a nightmare, and because her friend group has a rule: nobody drives if they're drinking. Sofia follows the rule. She takes Uber. She trusts Uber. She has a 4.91 rider rating and a favorite driver named David who somehow always pops up on her Friday night requests.

She has taken approximately 400 Uber rides in Phoenix. She has never once thought about what would happen if there were an accident during one of them.

### The Accident

Saturday, 12:20 AM. Sofia and her roommate were in an UberX (a gray Toyota Camry) heading south on Scottsdale Road toward Tempe. The Uber driver was making a left turn onto McKellips Road. A northbound driver ran a yellow-to-red light and hit the Camry on the passenger side at approximately 40 mph. Sofia was in the rear passenger seat -- the point of impact.

The Camry's rear passenger door folded inward. Sofia's right arm was pinned briefly. The impact threw her against the door and then against her roommate. She heard the glass and felt the crunch before she felt the pain.

Injuries: fractured right wrist (distal radius fracture requiring a cast, not surgery), cervical strain with radiating pain into both shoulders (more than standard whiplash -- her MRI showed a disc bulge at C4-C5 and a small annular tear at C5-C6), and a contusion on her right temple from hitting the window. She was taken to Tempe St. Luke's. Her roommate had a bruised rib and was released the same night.

### What They Lost (Deprivation Thinking)

**Trust in the thing that was supposed to keep her safe.** This is the core insight for the rideshare persona. Sofia took an Uber because it was the responsible choice. She didn't drive because she might have a drink. She got into a stranger's car because a $38-billion company told her it was safe, and now she has a broken wrist and a neck injury that her doctor says could take 6-12 months to fully resolve. The contract was: I give up control (I get in someone else's car, I go where the GPS says, I trust the driver), and in exchange, I get safety. The contract was broken. Not by Sofia. Not even by the Uber driver. By a third party -- but it happened while Sofia was in a car she didn't choose, on a route she didn't pick, and she had no ability to prevent it.

**Her right hand.** Sofia is right-handed. She types for a living. She edits video for TikTok. She manages an Instagram account. She takes photos for content. The cast on her right wrist makes her functionally one-handed for 6-8 weeks. She can type slowly with her left hand. She cannot edit video. She cannot take photos. She told her boss and he said "no worries, take it easy" -- but she can feel the work piling up, and the freelancer they brought in to cover her social media is posting content that doesn't match the brand voice she built. She is anxious about being replaceable in a way she wasn't before the accident.

**The social life.** Sofia's going-out routine -- the Thursday happy hours, the Friday dinners, the Saturday bar-hopping -- was the architecture of her social life. It was how she maintained friendships, met new people, and decompressed from a job that was 90% screens. The going-out requires Uber. Sofia has not taken an Uber since the accident (5 weeks). She drives her Civic everywhere now, which means she doesn't drink, which means she's the designated driver, which means she leaves early because being the sober person in a bar at 1 AM is not fun. Her friends have been understanding. But understanding has an expiration date when you're 24, and Sofia can already feel the invitations becoming less frequent.

**The sense that she's "making a big deal out of nothing."** This is the insidious one. Sofia's wrist will heal. The neck pain is real but invisible. She didn't have surgery. She wasn't hospitalized overnight. She compares herself to people in worse accidents and concludes that her situation doesn't merit legal action. Her mom told her to see a lawyer. Her roommate (who was in the same car) told her to see a lawyer. Sofia Googled it, read a Reddit thread where someone said "you don't need a lawyer for a minor accident, the insurance will pay out," and closed the browser. She is being gaslit by her own minimization instinct.

### Post-Accident Psychology

**Week 1:**
Sofia is at home with a cast on her right wrist and a neck brace she's supposed to wear for the first 72 hours (she wore it for 24 because it was uncomfortable and she felt ridiculous). Her roommate is shaken but physically okay. They have not discussed the accident much beyond "that was insane." Sofia's mom called from Dallas and cried on the phone. Sofia found herself comforting her mother instead of being comforted, which is a dynamic she is used to. She texted the Uber driver to ask if he was okay (he was). He sent her a message saying Uber's insurance would "take care of everything." She doesn't know what that means.

**Month 1:**
The cast is still on. The neck pain has not improved -- in fact, it's worse in the mornings. She started physical therapy for the cervical strain. Her wrist aches constantly. She is back at work full-time but producing at maybe 60% capacity. The Uber driver's message about insurance prompted her to open the Uber app, where she found a "Report a Safety Issue" flow that led her through a series of screens and eventually connected her to Uber's insurance claims process. She received a call from a claims adjuster at Progressive (Uber's commercial insurer). The adjuster was polite, asked her to describe her injuries, and said they would "review her claim." Sofia has received no follow-up in three weeks. She does not know whether the claim is against the Uber driver, the other driver, or Uber itself. She does not know that Uber carries a $1M commercial liability policy.

**Month 3-6:**
The cast came off at Week 7. Her wrist is stiff and weak. She is in occupational therapy for grip strength. The neck: the disc bulge at C4-C5 is stable but the annular tear at C5-C6 is causing persistent pain, especially when she looks down at her phone or computer for extended periods -- which is, unfortunately, her entire job. Her doctor has mentioned the possibility of epidural injections. She is 24 and hearing the words "epidural injection" in the context of her own spine. Uber's insurance made an offer: $8,500, covering her medical bills to date and a small amount for "pain and suffering." Sofia's out-of-pocket medical costs were $2,800 (she has health insurance through work with a $3,000 deductible). The $8,500 sounds like a lot of money to someone who makes $58K and has $3,400 in savings. She is tempted to take it. She does not know that the annular tear -- the injury she hasn't fully treated yet -- changes the case value substantially. She does not know the $1M policy exists.

### Goals (What She Wants from a Lawyer)

1. **Someone to explain the rideshare insurance structure.** Sofia does not understand who is liable, whose insurance she is claiming against, or that the Uber commercial policy exists. She needs someone to explain it simply.
2. **An honest assessment of whether her case "is worth it."** Sofia genuinely doesn't know if her injuries merit legal action. She wants someone to look at her medical records and tell her straight.
3. **No upfront cost.** Sofia's savings are thin. The idea of paying a lawyer out of pocket is a non-starter.
4. **Someone who communicates like a human being.** Sofia does not want to be talked to like a "case." She wants text updates, quick email replies, and a person she can reach -- not a call center.
5. **To stop feeling like she's overreacting.** This is the unspoken goal. Sofia needs validation that her injuries are real, that the ongoing neck pain matters, and that taking legal action is a reasonable and responsible thing to do -- not a dramatic one.

### Frustrations (What's Blocking Her)

- **She thinks her case is "too small."** A broken wrist and neck pain. No surgery. She wasn't even driving. She compares herself to the people in PI commercials -- the ones in wheelchairs -- and concludes she doesn't qualify.
- **The Uber insurance offer feels like a resolution.** $8,500 is real money to Sofia. She doesn't have the context to know it's drastically low relative to her actual claim value.
- **She doesn't know anyone who's been through this.** Her friends are 24. None of them have hired a PI lawyer. There is no social proof in her immediate network.
- **Lawyer ads feel gross.** Sofia is a marketing professional. She can identify a manipulative ad from across the room. The PI advertising she's seen (billboards, TV, Facebook) triggers her professional skepticism, not her trust.
- **The process feels intimidating and adult.** Sofia is two years out of college. "Hiring a lawyer" is something her parents' generation does. It feels like a threshold she's not ready to cross, even though she's living with the consequences of not crossing it.

### Messaging That Resonates

- **"You did the right thing. You took an Uber. Then someone else's mistake changed your life."** -- Validates the responsible choice and names the injustice of being hurt in a vehicle you trusted.
- **"Uber carries a $1M insurance policy. Did they tell you that?"** -- The information gap. This is the single fact that could change Sofia's calculus from "it's not worth it" to "wait, I need to look into this."
- **"Your wrist will heal. But what about the neck pain that hasn't gone away?"** -- Names the injury she's been minimizing and gives it weight.
- **"A free consultation isn't a commitment. It's a conversation."** -- Lowers the threshold. Sofia doesn't need to "hire a lawyer" -- she needs to have a conversation.
- **Clean, modern creative.** Sofia is a visual professional. The ad must look like something she would make -- well-designed, well-shot, not over-produced. Think: Instagram-native, not TV-commercial-repurposed.
- **"Stop wondering if you're overreacting. Find out what your claim is actually worth."** -- Directly addresses the self-doubt that is her primary barrier to action.

### Messaging That Repels

- **Anything that looks like a late-night TV ad.** 1-800 numbers, men in suits pointing at the camera, gold-and-black color schemes, "INJURED?" in red block letters. Sofia's entire aesthetic sensibility rejects this visual language.
- **"Get the money you deserve!"** -- Sofia doesn't think about money. She thinks about whether this is worth the hassle. Money-first messaging confirms her suspicion that PI lawyers are ambulance chasers.
- **Overpromising.** "We'll get you MAXIMUM compensation!" -- Sofia is a marketer. She knows what overclaim looks like. It repels her.
- **Aggressive urgency.** "Time is running out!" / "Act NOW!" -- Sofia is 24. She doesn't respond to urgency tactics in any context (she ignores them in her own marketing strategy). She responds to relevance.
- **Stock photography.** Any generic image of a car accident, a gavel, or a person on crutches. Sofia lives on Instagram. She can spot a stock photo instantly. It signals "this brand doesn't care about quality."
- **Boomer-coded tone.** Any creative that sounds like it was written for someone twice her age. "Are you a victim of a rideshare accident?" sounds like a pamphlet. "Your Uber crash isn't a minor thing" sounds like a person.

### Where to Reach Her

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Platform** | Instagram (primary -- she checks it 15-20x/day, it's literally her job). TikTok (secondary). Facebook (she has an account but uses it for Marketplace and events only). |
| **Time of Day** | Throughout the day (she's always on her phone), but highest engagement: 8-9 AM (morning scroll in bed), 12-1 PM (lunch), 10 PM-midnight (before sleep). |
| **Content Context** | Between friends' Stories, startup/marketing content, Scottsdale/Tempe local accounts, and meme pages. Her Instagram feed is curated. An ad must fit the visual standard of her feed or she will scroll past it reflexively. |
| **Format** | Instagram Story (full-screen, 15 sec max, native feel) or Reels (9:16, under 30 sec, text-on-screen). Static feed posts can work if the design is strong. Carousel format could work for an "explainer" (slide 1: hook, slide 2-3: Uber's $1M policy explained, slide 4: CTA). |
| **Language** | English. Casual, direct, no legalese. |
| **Device** | Mobile (iPhone). |

### The One Question to Ask Before Every Piece of Creative

Before concepting any ad for any case type, the creative team should ask:

**"If this person saw this ad at 11 PM, in pain, scrolling past their normal feed -- would they stop? Would they feel seen? Would they feel safe enough to tap?"**

If the answer to any of those is no, the creative is not ready.
