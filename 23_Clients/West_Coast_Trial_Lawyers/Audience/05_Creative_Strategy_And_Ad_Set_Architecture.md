## Section A: Self-Qualification Strategy

### How Creative Filters Out Soft Tissue — Without Targeting

This is the single most important creative challenge in the campaign. We cannot target by age, gender, interest, or behavior. Meta's Special Ad Categories rules (Social Issues designation) strip every precision tool from the media buyer's toolkit. The audience for every ad is the same: all adults in Arizona (~4.1M Facebook users). The question is not "who sees the ad?" -- everyone sees the ad. The question is: **who stops scrolling?**

The answer must be: people with non-soft-tissue injuries. Not people with whiplash. Not people with minor sprains. Not people whose neck is a little stiff after a fender bender. People whose bones are broken, whose bodies required surgery, whose lives have been materially disrupted by the severity of what happened to them.

If we get this wrong, we fill the funnel with soft-tissue inquiries that West Coast Trial Lawyers cannot sign. The $10K produces nothing. The pilot fails.

Here is how we get it right.

### Strategy 1: Injury-Severity Language as a Filter

The words in the hook do the first-pass filtering. A person with whiplash reads "broken bones" and does not identify. A person with a fractured femur reads "broken bones" and feels seen. The language itself creates a psychological gate.

**Use these words and phrases in hooks and body copy:**

| Severity Signal | Why It Works | Who It Filters Out |
|----------------|-------------|-------------------|
| "Broken bones" | Specific, physical, undeniable. You know if you have one. | Soft-tissue (sprains, strains, whiplash-only) |
| "Surgery" / "Required surgery" | Surgery is a bright line. People who had it know. People who didn't will not claim it. | Anyone who was treated and released without surgical intervention |
| "Hospitalized" / "Kept overnight" | Hospitalization signals severity. Soft-tissue victims are rarely admitted. | ER visit-and-release patients |
| "Can't work" / "Months of recovery" | Functional disruption. Soft-tissue victims are typically back at work within days or weeks. | People whose injuries resolved quickly |
| "Serious injuries" / "Life-altering injuries" | Broad but directional. Creates a severity threshold in the reader's mind. | People who self-assess as "not that serious" |
| "Titanium rods," "plates and screws," "arm in a sling," "wheelchair" | Hyper-specific physical imagery. Only people who have experienced these will identify. | Everyone else |

**Do NOT use:**

| Weak Language | Why It Fails |
|--------------|-------------|
| "Injured in an accident?" | Too broad. Everyone who bumped their head qualifies. Attracts maximum soft-tissue volume. |
| "Car accident?" | Situation-only, no severity filter. A parking lot tap and a rollover both answer yes. |
| "Fender bender?" | Actively targets soft tissue. This phrase is the opposite of what we want. |
| "Hurt?" / "In pain?" | Subjective, unquantifiable. A person with a stiff neck answers yes. A person with a shattered pelvis also answers yes. No filtering power. |
| "Whiplash?" / "Neck pain?" | Directly invites soft-tissue claims. |
| "Any injury" / "No matter how small" | Destroys the severity gate entirely. |

### Strategy 2: Situation Specificity

Certain accident situations almost never produce soft-tissue-only injuries. When the hook names a specific situation, it self-qualifies by the physics of what happened.

**High-severity situations to name in creative:**

- "Hit by a semi on I-10" -- A passenger vehicle struck by a commercial truck at highway speed produces catastrophic injuries. Soft-tissue-only outcomes are statistically rare.
- "Struck while crossing the street" -- Pedestrian impacts involve a human body versus a 4,000-pound vehicle. The injury floor is high.
- "Motorcycle crash in Arizona" -- Motorcycle crashes produce non-soft-tissue injuries in nearly 100% of cases. The body has no crumple zone.
- "T-boned at an intersection" -- Side impacts bypass the vehicle's front and rear crumple zones. Broken ribs, pelvic fractures, and head injuries are common.
- "Hit by a drunk driver" -- DUI crashes tend to occur at high speed with no braking. Impact severity is structurally higher than average MVAs.

**Situation specificity also helps with Meta's algorithm.** The more specific the language, the more distinct the engagement signal. People who click on "hit by a semi on I-10" are a cleaner data point for the algorithm than people who click on "injured in an accident?" The algorithm learns faster what kind of person converts when the top-of-funnel signal is precise.

### Strategy 3: Visual Self-Qualification

Images and video communicate severity before a single word is read. The scroll-stopping visual must signal "this is about serious injuries" within 0.5 seconds.

**Visuals that signal non-soft-tissue:**

| Visual | What It Communicates | Compliance Status |
|--------|---------------------|-------------------|
| Arm in a cast / sling | Broken bone. Undeniable severity. | Compliant -- no graphic content |
| Person on crutches | Cannot walk normally. Extended recovery. | Compliant |
| Hospital hallway / bed | Was admitted. This was serious. | Compliant -- no patient in distress |
| Wheelchair | Severe mobility loss. | Compliant |
| Stack of medical bills | Financial catastrophe from medical treatment. Implies extensive care = serious injury. | Compliant |
| Empty desk at work / tools on shelf | Cannot work. Life disrupted. | Compliant |
| External fixator (metal frame on limb) | Severe fracture. Unmistakable. | Compliant if shown cleanly -- no blood, no wound |

**Visuals that fail to self-qualify (DO NOT USE):**

| Visual | Why It Fails |
|--------|-------------|
| Crash footage (real or simulated) | Banned by Meta, banned by SB-37 principles, and does not communicate injury severity -- only crash severity. A dramatic crash can produce minor injuries. A low-speed impact can produce catastrophic ones. |
| Neck brace alone | Signals whiplash. Invites soft tissue. |
| Person rubbing their neck / back | Universal pain signal. Zero severity specificity. |
| Gavel / courtroom | Signals "legal" but says nothing about injury severity. Attracts everyone who has ever thought about suing anyone. |
| Dollar signs / money imagery | Attracts claimants motivated by payout, not by genuine injury. Quality degrades. |
| Generic stock photos of car damage | Car damage does not correlate with injury severity. A totaled car can produce whiplash. A barely dented car can produce a TBI from head-on-steering-wheel contact. |

### Strategy 4: Negative Self-Qualification

Phrases that implicitly tell soft-tissue people "this ad is not for you" -- without saying it directly.

**Effective negative self-qualification phrases:**

- "When the injuries are serious..." -- The word "serious" creates a threshold. People with minor injuries will not self-identify.
- "If your life has been turned upside down..." -- Implies a level of disruption beyond inconvenience. A person with a stiff neck for two weeks will not describe their life as "turned upside down."
- "When the injuries keep you from working for months..." -- The word "months" is the filter. Soft-tissue recovery is typically weeks.
- "If you're facing surgery you didn't expect..." -- Surgery is the bright line. This phrase explicitly gates on it.
- "For injuries that change everything..." -- Vague but directional. "Change everything" implies permanence or near-permanence.

**The psychology:** People self-select based on identity-congruence (Shotton, 2018). When the ad describes a situation more severe than theirs, most people will not claim it. They will scroll past because the ad doesn't match their internal narrative. The few who over-claim are caught by the quiz funnel's injury filter (see Section E).

### Strategy 5: Combined Application

No single strategy works alone. Every piece of creative must deploy at least three of these four mechanisms simultaneously:

| Creative Element | Filter 1: Severity Language | Filter 2: Situation Specificity | Filter 3: Visual Qualification | Filter 4: Negative Self-Qualification |
|-----------------|---------------------------|-------------------------------|-------------------------------|--------------------------------------|
| Hook (first 3 words) | Required | Recommended | N/A (text-based) | Optional |
| Primary image/video | N/A | Recommended (scene) | Required | N/A |
| Body copy | Required | Required | N/A | Required |
| CTA | N/A | N/A | N/A | Recommended ("See if you qualify" implies a threshold) |

**Example of a fully-qualified creative (General Severe Injury):**

> **Hook:** "Broken bones after a crash in Arizona?"
> **Image:** Person on crutches with an arm sling, standing in front of a stack of mail (bills).
> **Body:** "When the injuries are serious -- surgery, months of recovery, bills piling up -- the insurance company's first offer is designed to save them money, not cover your costs. Those seriously injured in Arizona car accidents may be entitled to more than what insurance offers."
> **CTA:** "See If You Qualify -- Free Case Review"

This creative deploys all four filters: severity language ("broken bones," "surgery," "months of recovery"), situation specificity ("crash in Arizona"), visual qualification (crutches, arm sling, bills), and negative self-qualification ("when the injuries are serious" and "see if you qualify").

## Section B: Ad Set 1 -- Motorcycle Creative Strategy

### Budget: 25% (~$1,938 media) | Daily: ~$55

### Why Motorcycle Gets Its Own Ad Set

Doc 3 established this definitively: motorcycle is the only case type that scores 5/5 on creative distinctiveness. The rationale is not about incident volume or budget math. It is about the nature of the creative itself.

Motorcycle advertising is **identity-based**, not situation-based. Every other case type in this campaign targets people based on something that happened *to* them -- they were hit by a truck, they were struck crossing the street, someone drove drunk into them. These are experiences. Motorcycle targets people based on who they *are*. Marco does not need to have been in a crash to identify with motorcycle creative. He identifies because he is a rider. The creative triggers a pre-existing identity, not a recent memory.

This distinction has three consequences:

1. **Self-qualification is near-perfect.** A non-rider will never engage with motorcycle creative. The imagery, language, and community signals are invisible to anyone outside the riding world. This means the click-through rate on motorcycle creative within a motorcycle audience is structurally higher than any other creative-audience pairing in the campaign.

2. **The algorithm needs fewer events to learn.** When clicks are clean (high relevance, low false-positive rate), the algorithm converges faster. Fifty weekly quiz completions in a motorcycle ad set with a 1.3%+ CTR teaches the algorithm more than 100 completions in a broad ad set with a 0.9% CTR, because the signal-to-noise ratio is better.

3. **Mixing motorcycle with other creative dilutes its identity power.** If motorcycle creative runs alongside trucking and pedestrian creative in the same ad set, the algorithm will test motorcycle creative against a general audience. The people who convert on motorcycle creative and the people who convert on trucking creative look very different to the algorithm. Combining them creates noise in the learning signal.

### The Target: Marco and His World

Marco Reyes (Doc 2) is 35, an HVAC tech in Mesa, rides a 2019 Street Glide, and broke his right femur when a Nissan Rogue turned left across his lane. But the target is not Marco-the-person. The target is Marco-the-identity: a rider.

**What defines the motorcycle audience in Arizona:**
- 92-94% male (Doc 1)
- 25-44 core age, with meaningful volume through 64 (Doc 1)
- Year-round riders -- Arizona has no off-season (Doc 1)
- Club culture is active: AZCMC, brand-specific groups, veteran-affiliated clubs, co-ed clubs (Doc 1)
- Facebook is the primary platform: rider groups, event coordination, ride planning (Docs 1 and 4)
- Helmet use is optional and low (63.9%) -- the no-mandate culture is part of the identity (Doc 1)
- The "professional who rides" profile dominates: 72% have some college, median HHI $62,500, Harley owners ~$90K (Doc 1)

### Hook Strategy: Three Angles

The 3-2-2 testing framework produces 12 total creative combinations from 3 hooks, 2 body styles, and 2 CTAs. This gives the algorithm enough variation to learn what resonates without overwhelming a $1,938 ad set.

**Hook 1: Identity**

The rider doesn't need to be told they ride. They need to be told someone understands what riding means.

- "Riders don't ask for sympathy. They ask for what's fair."
- "Arizona riders know the risk. After a crash, know your rights."
- "You've been riding long enough to know the road doesn't care. Neither does the insurance company."
- "The road is home. What happens when someone takes that from you?"

**Why it works (Shotton):** Identity-based hooks activate social identity theory. When Marco reads "riders don't ask for sympathy," he recognizes his own psychology being described back to him. He stops scrolling not because he's alarmed, but because he's acknowledged.

**Hook 2: Loss / Deprivation**

The deprivation insight from Jon Steel's persona work (Doc 2): Marco lost his identity as a rider. The bike is gone. The Saturday rides are gone. The person he was is gone.

- "The road took something from you. Don't let the insurance company take more."
- "An empty garage where a bike used to be. Bills where paychecks used to be."
- "The crash changed everything. The insurance company's offer won't change it back."
- "They totaled the bike. Now they want to lowball the person who rode it."

**Why it works (Ferrier):** Loss language leverages Kahneman's loss aversion -- the felt intensity of losing something is 2x greater than the satisfaction of gaining the equivalent. Marco already feels the loss. The hook names it, then redirects the emotional energy toward action.

**Hook 3: Injustice / Systemic Anger**

The insurance company is not on the rider's side. The driver "didn't see" them. The system treats riders as disposable.

- "She said she didn't see you. The insurance company says you're worth $35K. Both are wrong."
- "A driver made a left turn. Your femur paid for it. The insurance company wants to pay as little as possible."
- "Motorcycle riders injured in Arizona get lowballed by insurance companies. It doesn't have to be that way."
- "The driver who hit you has moved on. Their insurance company hopes you will too."

**Why it works (Mandalia):** Injustice hooks create an enemy. Under Special Ad Categories, we cannot target by interest, so the creative must generate its own emotional urgency. Injustice framing gives the viewer a reason to act that is external, not internal -- "the insurance company is screwing you" is more actionable than "you are hurting."

### Body Copy: Two Styles

**Style A: Story-Driven (Narrative)**

Short, concrete, specific. Reads like a real person's situation.

> "A motorcycle crash in Arizona can mean broken bones, months without work, and an insurance company that calls you 'the claimant.' Motorcycle riders injured on Arizona's roads deserve a legal team that understands what was lost -- not just the medical bills, but the ride, the routine, the life that was built around two wheels. No fee unless the case wins."

**Style B: Fact-Driven (Informational)**

Data, rights, and process. For riders like Marco who want to understand the system before they trust it.

> "Arizona motorcycle crashes produce non-soft-tissue injuries in nearly every case. Insurance companies know this -- and they still lowball. Motorcycle riders injured in Arizona crashes have the right to pursue full compensation for medical bills, lost wages, and long-term impact. Free case review. No fee unless the case wins."

### CTA: Two Options

| CTA | Psychology | Best Paired With |
|-----|-----------|-----------------|
| **"See If You Qualify"** | Creates a threshold. Implies selectivity. The viewer is not being sold to -- they are being evaluated. This reduces perceived risk because it frames the click as informational, not transactional. | Identity hooks, story-driven body |
| **"Get Your Free Case Review"** | Direct, explicit value. Zero ambiguity about what happens next. The word "free" removes the financial barrier that is Marco's #1 blocker. | Injustice hooks, fact-driven body |

### Visual Strategy

**The principle:** Show the identity, not the injury. Show the absence, not the crash.

**Primary visual set:**

| Visual | Description | Why It Works |
|--------|------------|-------------|
| **Parked bike, desert road** | A motorcycle (Harley-style cruiser, not a sport bike -- matches AZ rider demo) parked on the shoulder of a desert highway. No rider. | The empty bike is the symbol. It signals "rider" instantly. The absence of the rider carries the emotional weight. |
| **Helmet on a shelf / garage hook** | A half-helmet (not full-face -- matches AZ no-mandate culture) sitting unused. | Speaks to the rider who can't ride. The helmet is the most intimate piece of gear. It's personal. |
| **Empty garage / covered bike** | A garage with a bike under a cover, tools on the wall, a pair of boots on the floor. | The covered bike = the ride that isn't happening. Riders will recognize this scene because they have lived it in some form. |
| **Hospital bed with leather vest** | A leather vest or jacket draped over a hospital bed rail. | Collision of two worlds: the rider identity meets the injury reality. Immediately self-qualifying for motorcycle crashes specifically. |
| **Crutches next to a motorcycle boot** | Close-up of crutches leaning against a wall next to a pair of riding boots. | Physical limitation + rider identity in a single frame. |

**What NOT to show:**
- Crash footage (banned)
- The actual crash scene (triggering, not useful, banned)
- Sport bikes (does not match the AZ cruiser/touring dominant demo)
- A rider on the bike in motion (signals "riding" not "injured" -- wrong emotional register)
- Blood, road rash, visible wounds (banned and counterproductive)

### Format Recommendation

**Primary: Video (15-30 seconds)**

Follows Barry Hott's Ugly Ads Doctrine: native-feel, iPhone-quality, text overlays, not studio-polished. The ad should look like a fellow rider posted it, not like an agency made it.

Structure:
- **0-3 sec:** Hook text on screen + visual (helmet on shelf, empty garage). No narrator. Text-on-screen only with ambient sound.
- **3-10 sec:** Body copy on screen, one sentence at a time, over slow push-in on visual.
- **10-20 sec:** Shift to second visual (hospital bed with vest, or crutches next to boots). Additional body copy.
- **20-30 sec:** CTA slide. "See If You Qualify -- Free Case Review." Third-person compliance line: "For motorcycle riders injured in Arizona accidents."

**Secondary: Static Image with Text Overlay**

Single image (from visual set above) with bold, clean text overlay. Two lines maximum on the image. Full body copy in the Facebook ad text field (primary text).

**Sizing:**
- 1:1 (square) for Facebook Feed
- 9:16 (vertical) for Stories and Reels
- Same visual, reformatted for each placement. Advantage+ placements will automatically serve the right format.

### Third-Person Compliance

All motorcycle creative must use third-person language. Under Arizona State Bar advertising rules and SB-37-aligned compliance standards:

- **Compliant:** "Motorcycle riders injured in Arizona..." / "Those injured in motorcycle accidents..." / "Arizona riders who have been hurt in a crash..."
- **Non-compliant:** "Were you in a motorcycle accident?" / "If you were injured on your bike..." / "Your motorcycle crash..."

The third-person frame does not have to feel clinical. "Riders don't ask for sympathy. They ask for what's fair." is third-person in construction ("riders" not "you") but first-person in emotional resonance.

### 3-2-2 Testing Matrix

| # | Hook | Body | CTA | Priority |
|---|------|------|-----|----------|
| 1 | Identity ("Riders don't ask for sympathy") | Story-driven | See If You Qualify | **Launch Week 1** |
| 2 | Loss ("The road took something from you") | Story-driven | Get Your Free Case Review | **Launch Week 1** |
| 3 | Injustice ("She said she didn't see you") | Fact-driven | See If You Qualify | **Launch Week 1** |
| 4 | Identity | Fact-driven | Get Your Free Case Review | Week 2 rotation |
| 5 | Loss | Fact-driven | See If You Qualify | Week 2 rotation |
| 6 | Injustice | Story-driven | Get Your Free Case Review | Week 2 rotation |

Launch with the top three variants in Week 1. Add the remaining three in Week 2 if the ad set is spending and learning. If the ad set is struggling on budget, consolidate to the top two performers and cut the rest.

## Section C: Ad Set 2 -- Multi-Variant Severe Injury Creative Strategy

### Budget: 75% (~$5,813 media) | Daily: ~$166

### How This Ad Set Works

Ad Set 2 runs 5+ creative variants targeting different case types within the same broad Arizona audience. This is not a compromise -- it is the optimal architecture at this budget level (Doc 3, Section 12). Meta's Advantage+ creative optimization tests all variants across the full audience and identifies which creative resonates with which subsets of people. The algorithm does the case-type segmentation that we would otherwise be guessing at.

Within Ad Set 2, these are the distinct creative variants:

| Variant | Case Type | Language | Target Persona |
|---------|----------|----------|---------------|
| C1 | Trucking / Commercial Vehicle | English | Sarah (ICU nurse, Flagstaff) |
| C2 | Pedestrian | English | General pedestrian / Diana crossover |
| C3 | Pedestrian | Spanish | Miguel (line cook, Maryvale) |
| C4 | DUI Victim | English | James (sales engineer, Scottsdale) |
| C5 | General Severe MVA | English | Diana (office manager, Chandler) + catch-all |

Each variant must be strong enough to stand alone. The algorithm will starve underperformers. There are no participation trophies.

### C1: Trucking / Commercial Vehicle

**Monthly incident volume:** 229 crashes, 91 injuries (Doc 1)
**Average case value:** $90,000+ (commercial policies often $1M+) (Doc 3)
**Persona anchor:** Sarah Mitchell, 38, ICU nurse, Flagstaff. Compound humeral fracture, broken ribs, lacerated spleen. Hit by an 18-wheeler on I-17. Seven months pregnant at time of crash.

**The insight:** Trucking accidents are not car accidents. The insurance is different ($1M+ commercial policies vs. $25K-$100K personal auto). The liability rules are different (FMCSA regulations, driver logs, maintenance records, fleet company negligence). The lawyers on the other side are different (experienced defense teams retained by commercial carriers). The creative must signal this distinction because the people who have been hit by trucks can feel the asymmetry -- they just don't have the language for it yet.

**Hook Strategy: Anger + Injustice + David vs. Goliath**

Sarah's persona rejects emotional manipulation. She is an ICU nurse -- she processes information clinically. The hook must be factual, specific, and carry controlled anger. Not rage. Righteous indignation backed by data.

**Primary hooks:**

- "Hit by a truck that shouldn't have been on the road. The trucking company has lawyers. Where are yours?"
- "Commercial trucking companies carry $1M+ in insurance. Their lawyers are already working. Are yours?"
- "Truck accidents aren't car accidents. The insurance is different. The stakes are different. The lawyer should be different."
- "Hit by an 18-wheeler on Arizona's highways. The trucking company's insurer called within 48 hours. They're not calling to help."

**Body copy direction:**

Sarah-informed, fact-forward:

> "Commercial vehicle accidents in Arizona involve liability rules that don't apply to car accidents. FMCSA driver logs, vehicle maintenance records, fleet company negligence -- these are the details that determine what a trucking case is actually worth. Those injured by commercial trucks on Arizona highways may be entitled to significantly more than the insurance company's first offer. Free case review. No fee unless the case wins."

**Visual strategy:**

| Visual | Description | Why |
|--------|------------|-----|
| **Empty highway with semi** | An 18-wheeler seen from behind or the side, shot on a stretch of desert highway (I-10 or I-17 aesthetic). | Signals the case type instantly. Everyone who drives Arizona highways has felt small next to these trucks. The David vs. Goliath visual is immediate. |
| **Hospital hallway** | A long, empty hospital corridor. Clean, institutional, cold. | Signals severity. Sarah's world -- the hospital -- but from the patient's side. |
| **I-17 canyon corridor** | The winding mountain section between Flagstaff and Camp Verde. Recognizable to anyone who drives it. | Geographic self-qualification. I-17 is one of Arizona's most dangerous trucking corridors. Arizonans who use it know it. |

**Third-person compliance:**
- "Those injured by commercial trucks in Arizona..."
- "People hurt in truck accidents on Arizona highways..."
- "Arizonans seriously injured by 18-wheelers..."

### C2: Pedestrian (English)

**Monthly incident volume:** 288 crashes, ~268 injuries (Doc 1)
**Average case value:** $75,000-$300,000 (Doc 3)
**Demographic reality:** 76% of pedestrian fatalities occur in dark conditions. Arizona has the 2nd highest pedestrian fatality rate in the nation. Phoenix alone: 88 pedestrian fatalities in 2024.

**The insight:** Pedestrian victims carry a unique psychological burden -- innocence. They were not operating a vehicle. They were not making a driving decision. They were walking. The simplest, most human act of locomotion. And someone in a 4,000-pound machine hit them. The creative must amplify this innocence without creating victimhood. The distinction matters: innocence is factual ("they were crossing the street"), victimhood is emotional ("poor them"). Diana, our commuter persona, rejects victimhood framing. The pedestrian audience will too.

**Hook Strategy: Innocence + Rights**

- "They were just crossing the street."
- "A walk home shouldn't end in the hospital."
- "Pedestrians have the right of way. Insurance companies act like they don't."
- "Struck while crossing the street in Arizona. You weren't the one driving."
- "Hit while walking. Broken bones, surgery, months of recovery -- and the insurance company says it was your fault for crossing."

**Body copy direction:**

> "Every year, hundreds of pedestrians in Arizona are struck by vehicles while doing nothing more than crossing the street. Broken bones, surgery, hospitalization -- and then the insurance company tries to argue the walker was at fault. Pedestrians struck by vehicles in Arizona have the right to pursue full compensation for medical bills, lost wages, and long-term recovery. Free case review. No fee unless the case wins."

**Visual strategy:**

| Visual | Description | Why |
|--------|------------|-----|
| **Crosswalk at dusk** | An urban crosswalk -- painted lines, intersection, streetlights beginning to turn on. No person in frame. The emptiness is the point. | 76% of pedestrian fatalities happen in the dark. The dusk/evening crosswalk is the single most evocative visual for this case type. It's also a geographic self-qualifier: Arizona intersections have a specific look (wide arterials, sparse pedestrian infrastructure, desert landscaping) that Arizonans recognize. |
| **Crutches at an intersection** | Someone on crutches standing at a crosswalk, waiting for the walk signal. Shot from behind. | Marries the injury signal (crutches) with the case type signal (crosswalk). |
| **Person on crutches, viewed from the side** | Walking on a wide Phoenix-style sidewalk in the fading light. | Conveys vulnerability and the ongoing reality of recovery. Not pitiful -- factual. This is what life looks like now. |

**Third-person compliance:**
- "Pedestrians struck by vehicles in Arizona..."
- "Those injured while walking in Arizona..."
- "Arizonans hit by vehicles while crossing the street..."

### C3: Pedestrian (Spanish)

**This is the competitive advantage variant.** 

Most PI firms in Arizona run English-only creative. Arizona is 32.1% Hispanic (~2.43M people). In the pedestrian hotspot neighborhoods -- Maryvale (75% Hispanic), South Phoenix (65-70% Hispanic) -- Spanish is not a secondary language. It is the primary language of daily life. A Spanish-language pedestrian ad in these neighborhoods is not just inclusive. It is the only ad that will actually be seen by a significant portion of the target audience.

**The data (Doc 1, Doc 4):**
- 60% of Hispanic social media users consume in English
- 29% primarily in Spanish
- 25% bilingual
- Among Spanish-dominant users, Facebook usage is equivalent to English-dominant users
- Miguel's persona (Doc 2): Spanish-dominant, DACA, functional English but does not trust English-only documents. His Facebook feed is Spanish-language.

**The critical difference: This creative must EDUCATE, not just persuade.**

Miguel does not know what a personal injury claim is. He does not know he is entitled to compensation from the driver's insurance. Nobody in his network has ever hired a PI lawyer. The ad must tell him something exists before it can tell him to pursue it. This is an awareness problem masquerading as a persuasion problem.

Adam Ferrier's diagnostic: Miguel's barrier is **ability** (he lacks the knowledge and the language access), not **motivation** (once he understands the system, his motivation to recoup lost income and resume his remittances is intense). Creative that tries to motivate him ("don't let insurance take advantage of you!") will fail because he doesn't yet understand what insurance is supposed to do for him. Creative that informs him ("if you were hit by a car and it wasn't your fault, you may have a legal claim -- at no cost to you") opens the door.

**Hook Strategy: Family Impact + Education + Trust**

- "Su familia depende de usted. No deje que el seguro decida su futuro." (Your family depends on you. Don't let insurance decide your future.)
- "Le atropellaron y no fue su culpa? Puede tener un caso legal -- sin costo para usted." (Were you hit and it wasn't your fault? You may have a legal case -- at no cost to you.)
- "Lastimado caminando en Arizona? Huesos rotos, cirugia, sin poder trabajar -- hay ayuda legal gratuita." (Injured while walking in Arizona? Broken bones, surgery, can't work -- there's free legal help.)
- "No necesita papeles para tener derechos. Si le atropellaron, hay ayuda." (You don't need papers to have rights. If you were hit, there's help.)

**Cultural adaptation -- NOT translation:**

### C4: DUI Victim

**Monthly incident volume:** 460 DUI crashes, 298 injuries, ~60% non-soft-tissue (Doc 1)

**The insight:** DUI victim creative has a unique emotional register: injustice without victimhood. James does not want sympathy. He wants accountability. The crash was not an accident -- it was a choice. That word -- "choice" -- is the creative linchpin. Every other case type in this campaign involves accidents (mechanical failure, inattention, circumstance). DUI involves a decision. The drunk driver decided to drive. James is living with the consequences of someone else's decision. The anger this produces is not the hot anger of road rage. It is the cold anger of watching someone else's bad decision rewrite your life.

**Hook Strategy: Accountability + Injustice + Controlled Anger**

## Section D: Behavioral Science Layer

### How Behavioral Levers Apply to the Creative

Richard Shotton's work (The Choice Factory, 2018) and Adam Ferrier's framework (The Advertising Effect, 2014) provide the behavioral science architecture that sits beneath every creative decision in this campaign. These are not theoretical additions. They are the mechanisms that make someone who is in pain, skeptical, overwhelmed, and scrolling at 11 PM actually stop, read, and tap.

### Lever 1: Loss Aversion (Kahneman & Tversky)

**The principle:** People are approximately 2x more motivated by the prospect of losing something than by the prospect of gaining the equivalent. A $50,000 loss feels roughly twice as painful as a $50,000 gain feels pleasurable.

**Application to creative:**

Frame the message around what the viewer will LOSE if they don't act -- not what they'll GAIN if they do.

| Loss Frame (Use This) | Gain Frame (Don't Use This) |
|-----------------------|---------------------------|
| "Every day without representation, your case loses value." | "Hire a lawyer and get more money." |
| "The insurance company is betting you won't hire a lawyer." | "A lawyer can help you get compensated." |
| "Evidence disappears. Witnesses forget. Deadlines pass." | "A lawyer will gather evidence and talk to witnesses." |
| "Their offer is designed to cost them less than you deserve." | "You deserve fair compensation." |

The loss frame is structurally more urgent because it implies deterioration. The viewer's situation is getting worse *right now*, with every day they don't act. The gain frame is static -- it offers improvement but doesn't suggest active damage from inaction.

**Where to deploy:** CTA-adjacent copy and body copy. The hook gets attention. The body copy builds the case. The loss-frame line should appear in the transition between body and CTA, where it converts emotional engagement into behavioral action.

### Lever 2: Statute of Limitations as Genuine Scarcity

**The principle (Cialdini, 2001):** Scarcity increases perceived value and urgency. But manufactured scarcity ("only 3 spots left!") triggers skepticism in educated audiences. Genuine scarcity -- a real deadline that cannot be extended -- is both more ethical and more effective.

**Arizona's statute of limitations for personal injury is 2 years from the date of injury.** This is a real, legally binding deadline. It cannot be extended. If the statute expires, the case is dead.

**Application to creative:**

- "Arizona law gives you 2 years from the date of your accident. The clock started the day it happened."
- "The statute of limitations doesn't wait for you to feel ready."
- "You have a legal deadline you may not know about. A free case review can tell you exactly where you stand."

**What NOT to do:**

- "Time is running out!" -- Vague, manufactured-feeling urgency. Diana, Sarah, and James will scroll past this.
- "Don't wait another day!" -- Empty urgency with no specific basis.
- "ACT NOW!" -- The language of late-night infomercials. Repels every persona in our campaign.

The statute of limitations is most effective as a fact, not as an exclamation. State it plainly. Let the viewer's own math (date of accident + 2 years = deadline) create the urgency. The ad doesn't need to shout. The calendar does the work.

### Lever 3: Social Proof (Cialdini)

**The principle:** People follow the behavior of others, especially others who are similar to them. Social proof is most powerful when it is specific, numerous, and proximate (same location, similar situation).

**Application to creative:**

- "Thousands of Arizonans have gotten the help they needed after serious accidents."
- "Every day, people across Arizona take the first step toward holding insurance companies accountable."
- For motorcycle specifically: "Arizona riders who've been through this know -- the insurance company's first offer isn't their best offer."

**Social proof in the funnel (not the ad):**

The quiz funnel can deploy social proof at the highest-friction moments:
- On the quiz landing page: "Over [X] Arizonans have used this free tool to understand their case."
- After the injury-type question: "People with injuries like yours have successfully pursued legal claims."
- On the contact submission page: "A case specialist will call within [X] minutes -- just like they did for [X] other Arizonans this month."

**Constraints:** We do not have WCTL-specific Arizona social proof yet (this is the pilot). Use category-level proof ("thousands of Arizonans") rather than firm-specific proof ("we've recovered $X") until real data exists. Do not fabricate numbers.

### Lever 4: The IKEA Effect + Micro-Commitments (Norton, Mochon & Ariely, 2012; Cialdini & Guadagno, 2004)

**The principle:** People value things more when they have invested effort in creating them. In a quiz funnel, each step the viewer completes is a micro-investment. By Step 5, they have invested 60-90 seconds and answered 4-5 questions about their situation. Psychologically, they now "own" the process. Abandoning it means losing the investment they've already made.

**Application to the quiz funnel:**

| Step | Question | Psychological Function |
|------|----------|----------------------|
| 1 | "Where did the accident happen?" (Arizona location) | Geographic qualification + first micro-commitment. Lowest friction. |
| 2 | "When did the accident happen?" (Date range) | Statute of limitations check + temporal commitment. They are now invested in a timeline. |
| 3 | "Have you already hired a lawyer?" (Yes/No) | Qualification filter + commitment deepening. Saying "no" implicitly acknowledges the need. |
| 4 | "What type of accident?" (Motorcycle / Truck / Pedestrian / DUI / Car / Rideshare / Other) | Case type routing + self-identification. Selecting their case type is an act of ownership. |
| 5 | "Which best describes your injuries?" (Severity filter) | **The critical self-qualification moment.** See Section E. |

By Step 5, the viewer has invested enough that the completion rate from Step 5 to contact submission is high (~85% based on comparable funnel benchmarks). The IKEA effect is not manipulative here -- the person genuinely has a case and genuinely needs help. The micro-commitment structure simply prevents the inertia that would otherwise stop them.

### Lever 5: Status Quo Bias (Samuelson & Zeckhauser, 1988)

**The principle:** People prefer the current state of affairs even when changing would benefit them. The default action is to do nothing. Doing nothing requires zero effort, zero risk, and zero vulnerability.

**The implication for this campaign:** The biggest competitor is not another PI firm. It is doing nothing. Marco has been sitting with a $35K lowball offer for three months. Diana has been managing pain with ibuprofen instead of getting the surgery she needs. Miguel has gone back to work early and doesn't know he has a claim. They are all in the status quo.

**Application to creative:**

The creative must name the status quo as a choice -- and name its consequences.

- "Doing nothing is a decision. It's just not the one that helps you."
- "The insurance company is counting on you doing nothing. That's their business model."
- "Waiting doesn't make the bills smaller. It makes the case weaker."
- "If you've been putting this off, there's a reason -- and there's also a deadline."

**The reframe:** The status quo feels safe because it requires no action. The creative must reframe doing nothing as an active choice with active consequences. This is not manufactured urgency -- it is the factual reality of the insurance industry's strategy. Insurance companies benefit from delay. Their adjusters are trained to wait. The longer the claimant waits, the more leverage the insurer has.

### Lever 6: Ferrier's Motivation vs. Ability Diagnostic

**The principle (Ferrier, 2014):** Every behavior change requires both motivation (the person wants to act) and ability (the person can act). If motivation is high but ability is low, the intervention should reduce friction. If ability is high but motivation is low, the intervention should increase emotional engagement.

**Persona-level diagnostic:**

| Persona | Barrier Type | Motivation Level | Ability Level | Creative Prescription |
|---------|-------------|-----------------|--------------|----------------------|
| **Marco** (Motorcycle) | **Motivation** | Low (pride, distrust of lawyers, thinks it costs money) | High (he knows how to make a phone call, speaks English) | Increase emotional engagement. Identity hooks, community language, "no fee" in the first 3 seconds. Don't explain the process -- ignite the desire. |
| **Diana** (MVA) | **Both** (moderate) | Medium (she knows something is wrong, but status quo feels safer) | Medium (she's overwhelmed, has no bandwidth for a new process) | Give permission + reduce friction. "Let someone hold this." Short funnel, clear expectations, fast callback. |
| **Miguel** (Pedestrian) | **Ability** | High once informed (his motivation to provide for his mother is intense) | Very low (doesn't know PI claims exist, language barrier, fears the system) | Educate first, persuade second. Spanish-language creative that explains what a claim IS before asking him to pursue one. Spanish funnel. "Hablamos espanol." "No importa su estatus migratorio." |
| **Sarah** (Trucking) | **Ability** | High (she knows she needs a lawyer, she Googled it at 2 AM) | Low (cognitive overload -- newborn, broken arm, C-section recovery, expiring FMLA) | Reduce friction radically. "One call. We handle everything else." Don't require research. Don't require forms. Make the first step a single phone call or a 60-second quiz. |
| **James** (DUI) | **Motivation** | Medium (he has a lawyer already, inertia is strong) | High (he is sophisticated, employed, English-speaking, financially stable) | Increase motivation through differentiation. "DUI crashes aren't accidents. Your lawyer should know the difference." Signal that DUI-specific representation exists and is distinct from what he currently has. |
| **Sofia** (Rideshare) | **Motivation** | Low (thinks her case is "too small," $8,500 offer feels like resolution) | High (she is digitally native, speaks English, navigates funnels naturally) | Reframe the situation. "Uber carries a $1M policy. Did they tell you that?" One fact changes her calculus from "this isn't worth it" to "wait, I need to learn more." |

**Implementation:** Each creative variant should be pressure-tested against this diagnostic before launch. Ask: "Does this creative address the right barrier for the persona it serves?" If a trucking ad tries to motivate Sarah (she's already motivated), it's wasting the hook. It should instead reduce her friction: "One free call. A specialist handles the rest."

## Section E: Funnel Qualification Strategy

### How the Quiz Funnel Handles the Soft-Tissue Filter

The creative does the first-pass filtering (Section A). The quiz funnel does the definitive filter. Together, they form a two-stage gate that ensures only non-soft-tissue injuries reach West Coast Trial Lawyers' intake team.

### Funnel Architecture: Single Funnel with Branching Logic

One quiz funnel. All ad sets (motorcycle + all other) drive to the same entry point. The funnel branches internally based on answers.

**Why a single funnel:**
- Reduces production cost (one funnel, not two or five)
- Simplifies Meta pixel tracking (one conversion event, one landing page URL)
- The quiz itself does the routing -- the ad set doesn't need to

**Exception:** Spanish-language creative (C3) drives to a Spanish-language version of the same funnel. Same logic, same questions, same branching -- entirely in Spanish. This is a parallel funnel, not a branch within the English funnel. Miguel will abandon a funnel that switches from Spanish to English at any point.

### Step-by-Step Funnel Flow

**Step 1: Location**
> "Where did the accident happen?"
> - [Arizona -- city/county selector]
> - [Outside Arizona] --> Soft disqualification: "This free tool is currently available for Arizona accidents. If your accident happened in another state, [link to general resource]."

**Purpose:** Geographic qualification. Also a zero-friction first commitment (Lever 4: micro-commitment).

**Step 2: Timing**
> "When did the accident happen?"
> - [Within the last 6 months]
> - [6 months to 1 year ago]
> - [1 to 2 years ago]
> - [More than 2 years ago] --> Soft disqualification: "Arizona's statute of limitations for personal injury is 2 years. If your accident was more than 2 years ago, your legal options may be limited. We recommend consulting a local attorney to discuss your specific situation."

**Purpose:** Statute of limitations check. Also deepens temporal commitment.

**Step 3: Representation**
> "Have you already hired a lawyer for this accident?"
> - [No] --> Continue
> - [Yes, but I'm considering a change] --> Continue (flag for intake team)
> - [Yes, and I'm satisfied] --> Soft disqualification: "It sounds like you're in good hands. If your situation changes, this tool will be here."

**Purpose:** Filters out already-represented claimants. The "considering a change" option captures James's persona -- he has a lawyer he's unsure about. This is a legitimate lead but flagged for the intake team to handle carefully.

**Step 4: Accident Type**
> "What best describes your accident?"
> - [Motorcycle crash]
> - [Hit by a commercial truck or 18-wheeler]
> - [Struck as a pedestrian or cyclist]
> - [Hit by a drunk or impaired driver]
> - [Car accident (not involving the above)]
> - [Rideshare accident (Uber/Lyft)]
> - [Other]

**Purpose:** Case-type routing. This data feeds the intake team's preparation and WCTL's case evaluation. Also self-identification commitment (Lever 4).

**Step 5: Injury Severity -- THE CRITICAL FILTER**

> "Which best describes your injuries? (Select all that apply)"

**Non-soft-tissue options (PASS):**
- Broken bones (arm, leg, ribs, pelvis, etc.)
- Surgery was required
- Hospitalized overnight or longer
- Cannot work due to injuries
- Herniated disc or bulging disc
- Traumatic brain injury (TBI) or concussion
- Burns
- Amputation
- Internal organ damage
- Other serious injury (open text field)

**Soft-tissue indicators (FAIL -- soft disqualification):**
- Neck or back pain only (no other injuries selected)
- Minor bruising only
- Whiplash only (no other injuries selected)

**Soft disqualification screen for soft-tissue:**

> "Based on your answers, your injuries may not meet the criteria for the type of legal representation this tool connects you with. This does not mean you don't have a case -- it means a different type of attorney may be a better fit. We recommend contacting [Arizona State Bar Lawyer Referral Service] for a referral in your area."

**Why "soft" disqualification:** We do not tell people they have no case. We tell them this particular service may not be the best fit. This is honest (WCTL does not take soft-tissue cases), respectful (the person's injuries are real even if they don't meet our criteria), and legally safe (we are not providing legal advice by saying "you don't have a case").

**Edge case handling:**
- If someone selects "whiplash only" AND "cannot work due to injuries" --> PASS. An inability to work despite whiplash-only injuries may indicate an underlying condition worth evaluating.
- If someone selects "herniated disc" alone --> PASS. Herniated discs are frequently the basis of strong non-soft-tissue claims, especially if surgery is indicated (Diana's exact situation).
- If someone selects "concussion" alone --> PASS. Concussions can produce significant cognitive and vocational impacts.

**Step 6: Medical Treatment**
> "Have you received medical treatment for your injuries?"
> - [Yes -- emergency room or hospital]
> - [Yes -- doctor or urgent care]
> - [Yes -- ongoing treatment (physical therapy, specialists, surgery scheduled)]
> - [Not yet]

**Purpose:** Validates injury severity. "Not yet" does not disqualify -- many unrepresented claimants delay treatment due to financial barriers (Miguel's exact situation). But it flags the lead for intake.

**Step 7: Contact Information**
> "Almost done. A case specialist from West Coast Trial Lawyers will review your answers and call you to discuss your options. There's no cost and no obligation."
>
> - Name
> - Phone number
> - Email (optional)
> - Best time to call: [Morning / Afternoon / Evening]
> - [checkbox] "I consent to be contacted by West Coast Trial Lawyers regarding my potential case."

**TCPA compliance:** The consent checkbox must name West Coast Trial Lawyers specifically. Generic consent ("I agree to be contacted") does not satisfy the FCC's 2025 one-to-one consent rule. The checkbox text must name the specific entity that will contact them.

**Thank-you screen:**
> "Thank you, [Name]. A case specialist from West Coast Trial Lawyers will call you within [X minutes/hours] to discuss your case. This call is free and confidential."

Setting the time expectation is critical. If the thank-you screen says "someone will call" without a timeframe, the claimant's anxiety fills the gap. A specific window ("within 15 minutes during business hours" or "within 2 hours") reduces post-submission abandonment.

### Spanish-Language Funnel

Identical logic, identical branching, fully translated into Spanish. Key differences:

- All text in Spanish, including error messages, disqualification screens, and the thank-you page
- "Abogado de accidentes" not "abogado de lesiones personales"
- Consent checkbox: "Autorizo que me contacte West Coast Trial Lawyers sobre mi posible caso."
- Thank-you screen: "Gracias, [Nombre]. Un especialista de casos de West Coast Trial Lawyers le llamara dentro de [X minutos/horas] para hablar sobre su caso. Esta llamada es gratis y confidencial."
- The phone number should connect to a Spanish-speaking intake specialist. If the callback comes in English, the funnel's trust-building work is destroyed.

| **Advantage+ Placements** | Recommended for maximum reach and algorithmic flexibility. Manual placement selection is acceptable but limits the algorithm's ability to find cheap inventory. |

### TCPA Compliance

| Rule | Detail |
|------|--------|
| **One-to-one consent** | The consent checkbox on the quiz funnel must name West Coast Trial Lawyers by name. "I agree to be contacted by a legal professional" is NOT compliant under FCC 2025 rules. |
| **Consent before contact** | No outbound calls or texts until the consumer has submitted the quiz with the consent checkbox checked. |
| **Recorded consent** | The quiz submission event (with consent checkbox) must be logged with a timestamp and IP address. TrustedForm certificate recommended. |

### Spanish-Language Compliance

| Rule | Detail |
|------|--------|
| **"Abogado de accidentes"** | Use this term, not "abogado de lesiones personales" or "accident lawyer" in Spanish creative. It is the commonly understood term and the culturally appropriate one. |
| **Full Spanish funnel** | Spanish ads must drive to a fully Spanish-language funnel. Mixed-language funnels (Spanish ad to English landing page) violate the trust contract and crater conversion. |
| **No machine translation** | All Spanish copy must be written or reviewed by a native speaker. Google Translate-quality Spanish is immediately obvious to native speakers and signals disrespect. |
| **"Sin costo hasta ganar"** | The Spanish equivalent of "no fee unless you win." Must appear prominently in Spanish creative, same as the English version. |

### Arizona State Bar Advertising Rules

| Rule | Detail |
|------|--------|
| **No misleading statements** | All claims in advertising must be truthful and not misleading. Arizona follows Model Rule 7.1. |
| **No solicitation of specific individuals** | Advertising to the general public is permitted. Direct solicitation of a known accident victim is not (unless the person has a family, close personal, or prior professional relationship with the lawyer). |
| **Advertising disclaimer** | "Advertising material" disclaimer may be required depending on format. Check with WCTL's compliance counsel. |
| **No comparison claims without substantiation** | "We're the best PI firm in Arizona" requires substantiation. Avoid superlative claims. |

### Pre-Launch Compliance Sign-Off

Before any creative goes live:

1. Davis or Sasha reviews all copy against this checklist
2. Spanish copy reviewed by native speaker
3. WCTL reviews and approves (if standing approval requirement is in place)
4. Meta ad preview checked for Special Ad Category compliance flags
5. Quiz funnel tested end-to-end in both English and Spanish
6. TCPA consent flow tested and TrustedForm certificate confirmed
7. All compliance items documented in campaign log for audit trail

### Production Standards: Barry Hott's Ugly Ads Doctrine

Every video and static asset in this campaign must follow these production principles:

| Principle | What It Means | What It Does NOT Mean |
|-----------|--------------|----------------------|
| **Native feel** | The ad should look like organic content, not paid advertising. It should blend into the feed before it disrupts it. | It does not mean low-effort. Native feel is a deliberate aesthetic choice, not a shortcut. |
| **iPhone quality** | Shot on a phone (or graded to look like it). Natural lighting. No studio setups. No ring lights. No green screen. | It does not mean shaky, out-of-focus, or poorly composed. iPhone quality means "real person with a steady hand," not "accidental video." |
| **Text-on-screen** | Primary messaging delivered as text overlays on video. Sound-off comprehension is mandatory -- 85% of Facebook video is watched without sound. | It does not mean walls of text. 5-8 words per screen. Clean font. High contrast. Readable at mobile scale. |
| **Not polished studio work** | No voiceover artists. No motion graphics transitions. No corporate B-roll of handshakes and conference rooms. | It does not mean unprofessional. The content must be accurate, compliant, and emotionally resonant. The aesthetic is lo-fi. The thinking is not. |
| **Real locations** | Arizona landscapes, Arizona roads, Arizona intersections. Not generic stock footage of "a highway somewhere." | It does not mean we need to fly to Arizona. Stock footage of recognizable AZ locations (I-10, I-17 canyon, Phoenix arterials, Scottsdale intersections) is acceptable if it looks real and specific. |
