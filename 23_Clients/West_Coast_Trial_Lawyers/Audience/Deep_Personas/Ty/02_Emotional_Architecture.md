## Jon Steel's Deprivation Frame — The Five Losses

Before Ty can be reached, we have to name what he's lost. Steel's rule: don't ask "what does he want?" — ask "what has been taken?" The answer is never one thing. It's a stack.

### Loss 1 — The bike (the concrete, the easiest to name)

The 2020 R6 was paid off. It was the *first thing in his adult life that was entirely his* — not a rental, not a lease, not co-signed. Rachel didn't buy half of it. It wasn't a wedding gift. It wasn't his dad's. Eleven thousand eight hundred dollars from a private seller in Gilbert, worked two summers of overtime HVAC calls to afford it. **Insurance will pay out ~$9K on a $11K-replacement-value bike.** The totaled bike is the first loss because it's the loss he can point to. He's allowed to grieve this one in public.

### Loss 2 — The ride (the ritual, the thing that was his)

Saturdays at 5:30 AM at the Circle K at Rural & Warner. Four or five phones lighting up in the "dawn patrol" thread. Bush Highway before the tourists wake up. The specific sound of four different inline-fours idling at a gas pump in the desert predawn. **Mt. Lemmon** — the canyon religion, 27 miles of switchbacks he knows in his sleep. None of that exists right now. The guys rode last Saturday without him. They're not going to stop riding because he's down. He didn't expect them to. That's also the part that hurts.

### Loss 3 — The body that worked (the capability)

Ty's entire adult life has been built on the presumption that his body does what he asks. HVAC on a 140°F Phoenix rooftop, he can do. Carrying a compressor up a two-story ladder, he can do. Hitting a late apex on Bush Hwy at 75 mph leaned to the pegs, he can do. **Now his body is the thing that needs help.** He can't pick up Bandit. He can't get to the bathroom without thinking about it. Rachel has to help him shower. This is the loss he cannot talk about to his canyon crew — they respect the guy who wrenches his own bike at midnight. That guy doesn't exist right now.

### Loss 4 — The timeline (the future he'd been building toward)

The wedding was Spring 2027. $12K saved of an $18K target. Wedding pushed to Fall 2027 — maybe. The house he and Rachel had been half-looking at in east Chandler (30-year-old starter home, $385K, stretch but doable with both incomes) is now gone. **He's not making $70K this year.** He's making about $18K if disability kicks in, which it doesn't reliably for commercial HVAC techs. Every milestone in his adult plan got a 12-to-24-month asterisk in the 1.8 seconds between the Nissan Rogue's turn signal and impact.

### Loss 5 — The presumption of innocence (the social weight)

This is the one Steel calls "the loss under the loss." **Ty is a motorcyclist in a culture that assumes motorcyclists cause their own crashes.** His neighbor looked at him weird when he came home in a cast. Rachel's mother — who has never approved of the R6 — said "well, at least now you know." The nurse in the ER on Day 1 asked "were you speeding?" before she asked "how much pain are you in?" The insurance adjuster called on Day 3 and the *tone* of her voice carried it: *you chose this.* **No one close to him has said out loud "this wasn't your fault."** Including himself.

This is where the campaign lives. Everything else is exterior. This is the private injury, and it's the thing the creative has to speak to without being heavy-handed about it. The audience for M3 ("A driver didn't see the rider") isn't being told new information — they're being given *permission* to believe what they already know.

## What He Wants (Explicit)

Ranked by how consciously he'd name each one if you asked:

1. **The money to cover the medical bills, lost wages, and the bike** — in that priority order. He knows the ER bill is $47K. He'd like to not owe that in October.
2. **Someone competent to handle this so he can focus on healing.** He doesn't want to become a part-time claims adjuster. He has one job right now: recover.
3. **A lawyer who doesn't talk to him like a TV commercial.** Not "fight for your rights." Ty responds to tradesman-to-tradesman competence.
4. **To get back on a bike.** He won't say this to Rachel because she's scared enough. But it's there. Probably a 2027 Yamaha R7 — a step down from the R6 on paper, a step forward in practice.
5. **Not to feel like the statistic everyone thinks he is.**

## What He Won't Say Out Loud (The Shadow)

Steel's rule: the campaign's emotional payload has to reach the thing that isn't said.

- **He's scared.** First time in his adult life he's been in a hospital bed. He watched a compound fracture reduction on YouTube to understand what they did to his leg. He wished he hadn't.
- **He's quietly relieved the airbag worked.** He almost didn't buy the Tech-Air. Rachel pushed him to get it. He was going to tell her she was right and he hasn't yet.
- **He thinks about the woman in the Nissan a lot.** She's 52, a dental receptionist in Ahwatukee, wasn't drunk or high, just didn't see him. He's not angry at her. That confuses him. He thinks he should be.
- **He is scared he won't ride again.** Not because of the leg. Because of Rachel. Because of the conversation they're about to have that they haven't had yet.
- **He is scared he'll hire a lawyer and screw it up** — sign the wrong thing, pick a volume firm, end up with less than the adjuster is offering. The adjuster said $35K to close. Ty knows it's low. He doesn't know *how* low. That gap — between "I know it's not enough" and "I don't know how to prove that" — is where he is on day 22.

## The Emotional Arc (Timeline)

### Week 1 — Hospital / early recovery

Surgery the night of the crash. Titanium rod, intramedullary femoral nail, 6 screws. Two nights at Banner Desert. Rachel at the bedside, his mom drove down from Flagstaff, his canyon-crew Jake visited and left the Shoei (the cracked one) on a chair without saying anything. **Day 3 — adjuster's call.** "We'd like to help with your medical bills. We're authorized to offer $35,000 to close this out today." Rachel took the call because Ty was on hydrocodone and couldn't make a sentence. She wrote down the number. Didn't call back.

### Month 1 — Home / first wave of bills

Discharged on crutches. Physical therapy starts at week 2 and it's brutal. ER bill arrives: $47,000. Surgery bill: $38,000. Insurance pays the contracted rate, leaves him with a $14K deductible + out-of-pocket. **Ty can hobble to the kitchen.** He can't pick up Bandit. He's on FMLA, unpaid after his first 2 weeks of PTO. Rachel is working 4 days instead of 3. They're burning through the wedding fund. Two canyon-crew guys have texted less. Not malice — the friendship was built on a shared activity that doesn't exist right now.

### Month 3–6 — The decision window

Walking without crutches but with a limp. Back at work on light duty at ~14 weeks, making 60% of field pay. **He scrolls past motorcycle content on Facebook and feels something between longing and nausea.** He still hasn't hired a lawyer. He is afraid a lawyer will cost money he doesn't have (contingency fees are not in his vocabulary yet). He's afraid the $35K offer is the best he'll get. He is also angry in a way he couldn't have named in Week 1 — not at the woman, but at the system that says his recovery is worth $35K and everybody's going to nod and go home. **This is when WCTL's ad lands.** This is the audience.

## Adam Ferrier's Motivation Architecture — COM-B Map

For Ty to take the action we want (quiz completion → consultation → sign), three conditions have to be met simultaneously:

**Capability** — does he have the know-how to act?
- ✅ *Physical:* can fill out a quiz on his phone, one-handed if needed.
- ⚠️ *Psychological:* doesn't know how contingency fees work, doesn't know what his case is worth, doesn't know what "discovery" or "demand letter" mean. **The creative + funnel have to build capability without condescending.**

**Opportunity** — does his environment allow it?
- ✅ *Physical:* he's on his phone 4–6 hours/day right now. Time is not the constraint.
- ✅ *Social:* Rachel supports it; his canyon crew would respect it; his mom would prefer it.
- ⚠️ *Environmental:* the ad has to actually reach him in a mood + moment where he'd tap. **M2's 11PM couch-scroll moment is the target.**

**Motivation** — does he want to enough to act?
- ⚠️ *Reflective (planned):* he thinks he *probably* needs a lawyer but hasn't committed.
- ⚠️ *Automatic (emotional):* the adjuster's $35K sits in his head as a problem he has to solve.
- **The activation lever:** a creative that converts the simmering injustice into a specific, low-friction action (the quiz). Not a creative that creates new emotion — one that gives shape to emotion he already has.

## Richard Shotton's Cognitive Barriers

Four biases actively working against Ty hiring WCTL:

1. **Status-quo bias.** Doing nothing feels safer than picking a lawyer wrong. The $35K offer is a concrete known; a legal process is an unknown. Status quo wins unless the creative actively reframes inaction as the riskier choice. (This is what "time limits apply" + "no fee unless we win" lines are for — they convert inaction into the loss-framed choice.)
2. **Ambiguity aversion.** Contingency fees, medical liens, comparative negligence — all foreign vocabulary. Ambiguity makes people defer. The landing page + quiz has to demystify the process in the first 60 seconds, or he bounces.
3. **Loss aversion around the existing offer.** $35K in hand feels solidly better than "$X possibly, eventually" even when X is >$35K. The creative + funnel have to reframe the $35K as the *guaranteed loss* — the thing he leaves on the table when the case is actually worth more.
4. **Social-proof anchoring.** Ty has never hired a PI lawyer. His social circle — blue-collar trades, riders — skews toward "lawyers are for rich people or for trouble." Neither the aggressive billboard-lawyer aesthetic nor the "get millions" aesthetic anchors to his social proof. **Plain, specific, competent** — like the other tradesmen in his life — does.

## The Identity Frame

Ty's self-image, in three sentences he'd never say out loud but would recognize if spoken back to him:

> I'm a guy who takes the trade seriously, the bike seriously, and the wedding seriously. I'm not reckless, I'm not a kid, I'm not the guy at the bar in a $3,000 leather vest claiming to be a biker. I'm just a guy who rides, and a woman didn't see me, and everyone wants to make that my problem.

**The creative's job is to speak to that man.** Not the aspirational version, not the victim version, not the cartoon biker version. The actual guy.

## Pitch-Language Summary (for Julian's synthesis)

**For `05_Campaign_Implications.md` to pull from:**

- Primary emotional engine: the **presumption of fault** (Loss 5). Creative exists to refute it without arguing.
- Secondary engine: **recognition of competence.** He's a tradesman; the lawyer he'd hire talks like a tradesman.
- Tertiary engine: **the $35K reframe.** Converting the adjuster's anchor from "better than nothing" to "guaranteed loss."
- Trigger moment: Month 3 evening scroll. Light sensitivity gone, financial pressure high, emotional clarity starting to sharpen.
- Language test: *would Jake (the R1 guy in the canyon crew) respect this creative?* If yes, Ty does too. If it'd embarrass Jake to share — it fails.
