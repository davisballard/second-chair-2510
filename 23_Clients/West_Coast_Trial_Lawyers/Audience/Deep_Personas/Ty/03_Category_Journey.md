# Ty Rivera — Category Lens + Decision Journey
## How He Thinks About PI Lawyers (Before, During, After)

**Prepared:** 2026-04-22
**Session lead:** Richard Shotton (Behavioral Scientist — primary). Contributors: Jon Steel (narrative framing), Adam Ferrier (triggers), Julian Cole (synthesis). Chair: Julian.
**Why this file exists:** The layer most missing from the pre-split deep persona. Without understanding what Ty ALREADY thinks about PI lawyers before our ad hits him, we write creative to an imagined rational audience. This file maps what's actually in his head on day 22 post-crash.

## 1. Category Beliefs — The PI Lawyer Stereotype in Ty's Head

What Ty thinks PI lawyers are, before any WCTL ad reaches him. These are his **pre-existing schemas** — Shotton's key point: the ad doesn't write on a blank page. It has to land *against* an existing belief structure.

**What "PI lawyer" means to Ty:**

1. **The billboard guy.** The I-10 corridor is full of them. Giant faces, thumbs up, "$ONE BILLION RECOVERED," a phone number in 400-point font. To Ty, this aesthetic codes "cheap" — the lawyer equivalent of a used-car dealer in a commercial with fireworks. He doesn't identify. He associates it with the exact insurance-adjuster energy he's already sick of.
2. **The TV commercial lawyer.** Late-night ads, grainy footage, "Were you hurt in an accident? You could be entitled to MILLIONS." Ty's reaction: *this is the guy I'd hire if I wanted less of my settlement and more uncertainty.* He knows the big number is fake and the firm is probably a volume mill.
3. **The lawyer from the commercial for the accident his cousin was in.** Someone in his extended family hired one of these guys 4 years ago. Got a settlement. 40% fees + costs. His cousin got less than he thought he would. Nobody in the family recommends the firm by name — they just said "at least it was something."
4. **A real lawyer a friend of a friend knows.** Jake from the canyon crew mentioned his brother-in-law works at a firm in Phoenix. This is the only non-billboard archetype in Ty's head. Hasn't asked for the number yet. It feels awkward to call the brother-in-law of a guy he rides bikes with on Saturdays.

**What Ty thinks PI lawyers do:**

- Some accurate understanding: they sue on your behalf, they take a percentage, they settle cases before trial.
- Some gaps: he doesn't know what *demand letters, medical liens, policy limits, underinsured motorist stacking, or comparative negligence multipliers* are. He doesn't know that the adjuster's $35K is a statistical opening offer calibrated to case-type averages — he thinks it might be the ceiling.
- Active misconception: **he thinks lawyers cost money upfront.** He grew up in a household where lawyers were for rich people or for trouble. *Contingency fee* is not a concept he's fluent in. The single most important creative line for him is "**No fee unless we win**" — and it has to be *above the fold*, not buried.

**What Ty thinks a GOOD PI lawyer looks like:**

Ty's never hired a lawyer, so his model is built by analogy to tradesmen he respects:
- Talks straight, not in jargon.
- Tells you what's wrong and what it costs before you commit.
- Doesn't upsell.
- Shows up when they say they will.
- Isn't trying to be your friend — is trying to solve the problem.

**This is the register we have to write in.** Anything that sounds like the cousin's billboard lawyer fails. Anything that sounds like the tradesman he respects works.

## 2. The Decision Journey

Mapped against the classic awareness → consideration → decision model, with barriers at each stage.

### Stage 1 — Awareness (Weeks 1–4 post-crash)

**What's happening:** Ty is in survival mode. Hospital, surgery, home, crutches. He's not consciously looking for a lawyer. He's reactive: insurance called, Rachel took the call, we wrote down the number, we haven't called back. Adjuster followed up Day 7. Day 14. Day 21.

**What he's actually seeing:**
- Meta feed starting to show him PI ads (algo detected his "motorcycle accident" search from Day 5). Cardinal's billboard-law-firm ads interleaved with his normal moto content.
- Scrolls past 90%. Stops on maybe 3–4 over two weeks because something caught his eye. Doesn't tap any of them.
- Google search: "how much is my motorcycle accident case worth arizona" late one night, Week 2. Got back 30 near-identical lawyer landing pages. Closed tab.

**Barriers at Awareness stage:**
- **Pattern blindness** — he's seen 50 PI ads in his lifetime. They all blur. New creative has to visually differentiate *in the first second* to interrupt the blur. (This is why M1's identity hook "Riders don't ask for sympathy" works — the first three words signal *this is not for everybody, this is for me specifically*.)
- **Active avoidance of anything that looks like "accident ad."** Crash footage triggers instant scroll. Sad-music-over-family-photo triggers instant scroll. The aesthetic has to signal "not the billboard guy" immediately.

**What moves him forward:** A creative that (a) visually breaks the PI-ad pattern, (b) signals rider-insider, (c) uses language he recognizes as his own. **One ad out of fifty.**

### Stage 2 — Consideration (Weeks 4–12, peak around week 8–10)

**What's happening:** He's walking. Working part-time at 60% pay. Bills are arriving in earnest. The $35K offer has sat on the table for 6–8 weeks. His financial runway is ~4–5 months with Rachel carrying more. **The realization:** he needs someone in his corner. He's starting to actively look.

**What he's doing:**
- **Searching:** "motorcycle accident lawyer phoenix," "motorcycle accident lawyer arizona no fee," "how much is a motorcycle accident settlement worth." He's visiting 5–8 firm websites per search session and closing 7 of them immediately because they look like the billboard.
- **Asking:** Finally texts Jake about the brother-in-law. Brother-in-law refers him to *someone* (not WCTL necessarily) — a 30-second referral text. Ty texts back "appreciate it, will look them up." Doesn't commit.
- **Comparing:** Might have 2–3 firms in the running mentally. Our M3 ad's quiz funnel can be his first firm-level engagement, IF the funnel is frictionless enough that he'd finish it on his phone in the 11PM couch-scroll window.

**Barriers at Consideration stage:**
- **Choice overload.** 5 firms in Ty's head is 4 too many. He's a working-class guy who'd rather pick the first competent option and stop thinking about it than optimize across a field. The firm that makes the DECISION easy wins — not the firm with the best website.
- **Fear of being conned.** The contingency fee is a foreign concept; he has no way to know if 33% is standard or a rip-off. Firms that explain fees simply (chart, plain language, no asterisks) dramatically reduce this barrier. WCTL's landing page + quiz must do this.
- **Spousal alignment.** Rachel is the household operations director right now. She'll have strong input on which firm, and Ty defers because she's carrying the admin. **The creative and landing page need to survive a woman in her late 20s who's not the injured party but IS the decision co-owner.**

**What moves him forward:** A specific, low-friction path to a conversation with a real person at the firm. The quiz does this. Phone tag does not.

### Stage 3 — Decision (Weeks 12–24)

**What's happening:** He's signed an engagement letter with a firm or he hasn't. If he hasn't, the window is closing — either he accepts the $35K + Rachel's parents loan them the gap, or he gets outside help.

**What flips him from Consideration to Decision:**
- A lawyer or intake specialist who talks to him like an adult, not a prospect.
- Concrete next step on the call: *"Here's what I need from you by Friday, here's what I'll do by the following Friday."* Plan-of-action energy, not "let us fight for you" energy.
- No pressure to sign on the first call. He needs to tell Rachel and sleep on it. A firm that pressures him loses him.
- Competence signal: the intake person asking about the specific intersection (Southern & Dobson, left-turn failure-to-yield), referencing his gear, asking about policy limits. Specificity signals *I actually handle cases like yours*.

**Barriers at Decision stage:**
- **Sign-anxiety.** Signing a contract feels like the thing lawyers trick you with. Engagement letters that are 12 pages of dense type kill conversions. Plain-language, pre-explained engagement letter = competitive advantage.
- **Fee anxiety revisited.** Even after he understood contingency fees at the quiz stage, seeing "33%" on paper reactivates the anxiety. The intake specialist has to frame it as *"we don't get paid unless you recover; we work for you, not against you."*

## 3. The Trigger Moments (Ferrier's layer)

What specifically flips Ty from passive to active? Five triggers, ranked by conversion weight:

1. **Day 22: ER bill arrives.** $47,000. First time he sees a five-figure number attached to his own life. **This is when he Googles.**
2. **Month 2: Insurance adjuster's second formal offer.** $35K still on the table, now with a follow-up: "We'd like to close this file." Implication that the offer expires. **This is when he starts to panic.**
3. **Month 2–3: Rachel's parents call.** Offer to loan the wedding fund back if needed. **This is when the social stakes of the crash register.**
4. **Month 3: First time he sees his canyon crew ride without him on Instagram.** Jake posts a Mt. Lemmon photo on a Sunday morning. **This is when the identity loss hits hardest — and when emotional engagement with our Loss hook (M2) is highest.**
5. **The physical therapist's prognosis at Week 12 appointment.** "Expect permanent strength deficit; riding is a personal risk call." **This is when the future permanence becomes real.**

**The campaign should be live during weeks 4–16.** That's the activation window. Too early = creative hits a rider in triage who can't process. Too late = he's accepted the $35K.

## 4. Competitive Set (from Ty's POV)

The alternatives Ty is weighing, actively or passively:

- **Take the $35K.** Easiest option. Carries the hidden "full cost" of covering bills himself for 18 months.
- **Do nothing / don't respond to adjuster.** His default-ish state for the first 4 weeks.
- **Hire a big-name billboard lawyer.** Feels wrong but he knows the name.
- **Hire a friend-of-friend referral** (Jake's brother-in-law). Low social risk; unclear competence.
- **Hire a specialist (trucking, motorcycle-focused).** He doesn't know this category exists until we tell him via creative.
- **Go to small-claims court himself.** He won't. He thought about it once. The amount of paperwork killed it.

**WCTL's job: out-compete the $35K anchor and the billboard lawyers simultaneously.** The anchor is the biggest competitor, not the other firms. A creative that never addresses the $35K-already-on-the-table is missing the real conversation.

## 5. Creative Implications (preview for `05_Campaign_Implications.md`)

- **Opening frame must break PI-ad pattern in <1 second** (Awareness barrier).
- **"No fee unless we win" must be above-the-fold, not buried** (Fee-anxiety barrier).
- **Landing page has to demystify contingency in plain language** (Ambiguity-aversion barrier).
- **Intake call must survive the pre-existing billboard-lawyer schema** (Category barrier).
- **Creative needs to reach him in his 4–16 week activation window**, not earlier/later (Trigger timing).
- **Assume Rachel is co-reading the ad and co-evaluating the firm** (Spousal-alignment barrier).
- **Address the $35K anchor directly, not in subtext** (Competitive-set barrier).
