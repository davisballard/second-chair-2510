## The Archetype (one line)

**Ty Rivera is the working-class rider who did everything right — gear, skill, caution — and still got taken out by a driver looking at her phone. He's not a "biker" and he's not a "squid." He's a guy who rides, a guy who fixes air conditioners, a guy who was twelve months from being a husband at his wedding, and now he's on his back on the asphalt at Southern & Dobson with a femur he can't feel.**

## The Single-Minded Proposition (the "one thing")

> **Ty doesn't want sympathy. He wants the record set straight: he was doing the speed limit, wearing the right gear, in the right lane, and a woman making a left turn didn't see him. He wants someone — *anyone* — to speak to that truth instead of treating him like a statistic.**

This is the SMP. Every hook we write for M1, M2, M3 must trace back to this one idea. If a line of copy could sit comfortably next to "a driver didn't see the rider," it's on strategy. If it implies Ty was reckless, careless, or lucky-to-be-alive because of his own choices, it's off strategy — *even if it would perform*.

## The Core Insight

**Working-class motorcycle riders in Arizona carry a private, unspoken injury on top of the physical one: the social presumption that whatever happened to them was their fault for being on a motorcycle in the first place.** Every neighbor, every cousin at Thanksgiving, every insurance adjuster, every nurse in the ER starts from that assumption. Ty feels it the moment he wakes up in the hospital bed at Banner Desert. He's not just injured. He's been quietly *convicted* by everyone around him of the crime of being a rider.

**The adjuster's call at Day 3 is the moment the presumption gets monetized.** "We'd like to help with your medical bills — $35,000 to close the claim." The number isn't random. It's calibrated to a rider's shame — enough that refusing feels greedy, low enough that accepting means admitting the presumption. The ad that cuts through is the one that flips the presumption: the driver made the decision, not Ty. The record matters.

## The Five Campaign Implications

These are the campaign-shaping calls the persona drives. Details in `05_Campaign_Implications.md`.

1. **M1/M2/M3 must be third-person always.** "Riders don't ask for sympathy." "A driver didn't see the rider." Never "you" — because Ty's relationship with PI advertising is *distrust of being pitched*. Third-person is also AZ Bar ER 7.3 compliant (direct-solicitation protection). Double win.
2. **No crash footage. Ever. (One narrow exception: M2-V3.)** Ty scrolls past wreck imagery. The absence of the bike, the empty parking spot, the helmet on a shelf — those are the creative. The 2026-04-23 Saul-adjudicated exception is M2-V3 (Moment-Before Dramatization): dashcam-POV intersection approach with cut-to-black before impact, counsel-flagged. That single variant lives in `Creative/Week_1_Approval_Package/05_M2_Ty_Loss.md`. All other M1/M2/M3 variants remain no-crash.
3. **No promised numbers.** Ty has already been burned by one number ($35K from the adjuster). Any "get millions!" language reads as the adjuster's cousin. Body copy can reference *what the law says*, never *what the case is worth*.
4. **Tone register: grounded, competent, specific.** "Free case review. No fee unless we win." Not "fight for your rights." Ty responds to competence, not cheerleading. The lawyer he'd hire speaks like a tradesman who respects other tradesmen.
5. **Visual world is locked to supersport, not cruiser.** Full Alpinestars leathers, Shoei helmet, Yamaha R6 — not leather vest, half-helmet, Harley. Every AI generation for Ty's creative must honor the supersport archetype. (Details in `01_Life_World.md` §§1-3.)

## Why the Persona Matters (the commercial argument)

**The supersport demographic crashes 4x more per capita than cruisers** (22.5 vs. 5.6 fatalities per 10,000 registered bikes; see `00_Evidence_Register.md` §14). Arizona is a top-10 US state for motorcycle fatality rates and the most crash-prone demographic is exactly Ty's: 25–35-year-old, Phoenix metro, supersport rider. Targeting this population generates more per-impression leads than any other moto segment WCTL could pay to reach.

**And — crucially — multi-vehicle crashes at intersections (74% of all AZ moto crashes; see Evidence Register §14.3) have the driver at fault 60–66% of the time** regardless of bike type. Under AZ's pure comparative-negligence rule (A.R.S. § 12-2505), even a supersport rider with speed alleged recovers proportionally. The intersection-left-turn case is the clean-liability subset inside a 4x-higher-volume population. WCTL's math works.

## The Voice Test (use this before shipping any Ty creative)

Before any hook, line, or scene ships, ask these three:

1. **Would Ty stop scrolling to read it?** Not "would a 38-year-old middle manager"; specifically *would Ty at 2 AM on the couch with his leg propped up, scrolling past dirtbike videos and post-crash insurance nonsense*?
2. **Would Ty forward it to his riding group chat?** "Dawn patrol — you guys see this one?" Does it read as something a rider respects — not something that pities him?
3. **Does it speak to the presumption?** Does the line, visual, or moment quietly refute the "he was probably speeding" assumption without ever *arguing* the point defensively?

If 3 of 3 = ship it. If 2 of 3 = revise. If 1 of 3 = it's for a different audience.
