## 1. Diana's Category Priors (what she ALREADY thinks about PI lawyers before our ad hits)

Shotton's key point: **the ad doesn't write on a blank page.** For Diana, the page has a specific texture — moderate fluency on legal-process basics (she's signed enough contracts to know what contingency means), strong priors against billboard-firm aesthetics (Phoenix has the most PI billboards per capita in the American Southwest), and a key vulnerability: she tends to over-research and then default to the first option that feels competent.

**What "PI lawyer" means to Diana (based on 20 years of Phoenix-metro exposure):**

1. **The I-10 billboard firms.** Driving to work every day on I-10, she's seen every one — Lerner & Rowe, Phillips Law Group, Husband & Wife Law Team, Gerber & Yeskel, etc. Giant photos of lawyers, thumbs up, phone numbers in 400-point font. To Diana, this aesthetic codes "volume mill." She's never called any of them. She doesn't know anyone who has, though she assumes people do.
2. **The late-night TV commercials.** "Were you hurt in an accident? Call the hammer!" Diana hates these. She scrolls past them on streaming commercial breaks. She associates them with the billboard firms.
3. **"Friend-of-a-friend referrals."** Diana knows 2 people personally who've hired PI lawyers — her cousin (who hired a small Phoenix firm after a minor accident 6 years ago) and a former coworker (who hired a Mesa firm after a trucking accident). Her cousin got a settlement (modest, she thinks maybe $15K); her coworker got a bigger one (she heard maybe $75K) but the process took 2 years and was "exhausting."
4. **The firm from the local news.** She's seen WCTL or comparable firms get quoted in AZCentral articles about trucking accidents or DUI victim compensation. This is the highest-credibility tier in her head — "firms that journalists call for quotes."
5. **Her employer's tenant-dispute counsel.** Her property-management firm uses a general commercial law firm for landlord-tenant issues. She's interacted with them professionally. She knows what a competent lawyer sounds like on the phone, professionally speaking. **She'll pattern-match PI lawyers against this standard.**

**What Diana thinks a GOOD PI lawyer looks like (from her mixed exposure):**
- **Local (AZ, preferably Phoenix-metro).** She won't consider out-of-state.
- **Accessible.** Returns calls within a day, sends updates, communicates proactively.
- **Transparent on fees.** She's a CFO-archetype — she wants to understand the math.
- **Small enough that her case matters.** Not a firm where she's one of 500 active matters.
- **Professional tone.** The "billboard shouting" aesthetic is a disqualifier.
- **Specific to MVA.** She doesn't need a niche specialist (that's Sarah's need for trucking) — a competent general PI firm works.

**What Diana thinks PI lawyers do** (her working mental model):

**Accurate:**
- File suit or negotiate settlement
- Take contingency fee (she knows ~33% + costs is standard — she's read contracts professionally)
- Handle insurance negotiations
- Resolve cases in 12–24 months typically

**Gaps:**
- She doesn't know what a "policy limit demand" is
- She's not sure what the difference is between filing suit and "negotiating" (she thinks one is aggressive, one is not — she doesn't know both happen in sequence)
- She doesn't know AZ's pure comparative negligence rule — she vaguely thinks "if I was partially at fault, I get nothing," which isn't true
- She doesn't know about medical liens on settlements — this is going to surprise her and potentially upset her

**The landing page + intake has to fill Gaps 2 and 4 without making her feel stupid.** Diana hates feeling stupid. She's been a Senior Office Manager for 9 years partly because she's competent — she doesn't want to be educated from scratch. **Meet her at "you probably know X; here's what you might not know about Y" register.**

## 2. The Decision Journey (Diana's 3-stage path to hiring)

Mapped against awareness → consideration → decision. Different structure from Sarah's professional-observer journey.

### Stage 1 — Awareness (Weeks 1–5 post-crash)

**What's happening:** Diana is in physical survival mode. Herniated disc + concussion + three kids + Ben's variable income + a household system that's failing. She's NOT looking for a lawyer actively. She's trying to get through each day.

**What she's seeing:**
- Meta feed starts serving PI ads by Week 2 (algo detected her "herniated disc recovery" + "how long am I off work" searches). Facebook Feed is dominant; Instagram secondary.
- Her Chandler Mom Crew group chat has one mom (Amanda) who casually mentioned last year that she'd been in a fender-bender and her cousin uses a "really good lawyer" — Diana doesn't ask, but she remembers.
- Her property-management firm's coworkers have sent flowers + a "we're thinking of you" card. Karen (her boss) texted twice about the return-to-work timeline. Nobody has mentioned a lawyer.
- An article in AZCentral about "rising commuter injuries on I-10" shows up in her Facebook feed. She reads it. It mentions 2 local PI firms by name as sources — one she recognizes (billboard firm, auto-discount), one she doesn't (which could be WCTL or similar).

**Barriers at Awareness stage:**
- **Pattern blindness (commuter variant).** She's seen thousands of PI ads in her driving career; she's developed selective inattention. New creative has to visually + textually differentiate *in the first 1 second* to interrupt the blur.
- **"I can handle this" identity bias.** She's a pro at admin fights. Her first instinct is to call the insurance company directly and negotiate. **Our ad has to interrupt this self-story** without insulting her competence.

**What moves her forward:** An ad that *sees her system-level loss* — not a generic "injured?" hook. **S5's "Broken bones. Bills don't stop." with the visual of falling envelopes IS this.** The envelopes falling (her kitchen island visual) recognizes her reality. One ad out of 60 breaks through.

### Stage 2 — Consideration (Weeks 5–12, peak around Week 8–10)

**What's happening:** She's hobbling less. She's returned to work on admin-light-only duty (screen time 2 hrs/day max) at Week 10. The $18K adjuster offer has sat for 4 weeks. Ben's summer construction is ramping up but his income fluctuation is still live. **The realization hits at Week 8:** she needs a lawyer.

**What she's doing:**
- **Searching:** "car accident lawyer arizona," "herniated disc car accident settlement," "phoenix car accident attorney reviews," "arizona pure comparative negligence" (she Googled this last one after reading a forum post), "how long car accident lawsuit arizona." She's on her phone at 2 AM + her laptop during 20-minute screen-tolerant windows.
- **Asking:** She DMs Amanda from the Chandler Mom Crew: "Hey, what was the name of your cousin's lawyer?" (Gets a name she doesn't recognize.) She asks Karen: "Does the firm have any PI lawyer recommendations?" (Karen points her to one firm.) She asks Ben: "Your coworker Miguel — didn't his brother use a PI lawyer?" (Gets a third name.)
- **Comparing:** She has 4–6 firms in her head by Week 9. She's doing a spreadsheet-like mental comparison. **This is her peak analysis-paralysis risk moment.**
- **Reading reviews.** Google reviews, Avvo (which she's heard of but not used before), the firm's own website. She will read until her eyes hurt then close the tab.

**Barriers at Consideration stage:**
- **Choice overload, enhanced by analysis-paralysis tendency.** Diana will compare 6 firms; narrowing to one is hard. **The firm that presents its differentiation clearly + gets her on a scheduled call FAST wins against the others.**
- **Fee transparency demand.** If a firm is cagey about fees on the phone or landing page, Diana's done. She's been a contracts-reading professional for 9 years.
- **Concussion-related research fatigue.** She can't research at Sarah's intensity. Her brain gets tired fast. **A landing page that fits on her screen-time tolerance window (under 15 minutes of total engagement from first click to form completion) is critical.**
- **Ben's co-review.** Ben is a co-decision-maker. He'll read the firm's website. He'll have opinions. The creative + landing page must survive a couples' review, not a solo evaluation.

**What moves her forward:** A low-friction, clearly-structured path to a real conversation. Diana is ideal for **Meta lead form ads** (native lead capture without a long landing page) as a first touch. A quiz on a landing page works as long as it's under 4 minutes and accommodates screen-time limits.

### Stage 3 — Decision (Weeks 12–20)

**What's happening:** She's had 1–3 consultations. She's narrowing.

**What flips her from consideration to decision:**
- The firm's intake specialist or lawyer **handles the call efficiently.** Diana does not have time for a 45-minute meandering "tell me about yourself" call. A 20-minute focused call that gets her specific questions answered = she's impressed. 45 minutes of rapport-building = she's annoyed.
- **Clear fee explanation.** Percentage, costs structure, medical-lien-negotiation approach, payment timing.
- **Specific understanding of her case.** The lawyer asks about the F-250 driver's on-duty status (off-duty, she confirms — so personal auto, not commercial). The lawyer asks about her employer's short-term disability + FMLA structure. The lawyer knows that herniated-disc cases sometimes plateau requiring surgery — this matters for case value.
- **No pressure to sign on the first call.** She needs to tell Ben. A firm that pressures loses her.
- **Plain-language engagement letter.** Diana reads contracts professionally. She can smell bad drafting. 12 pages of fine print = she'll hesitate. 5 pages with clear sections = she signs.

**Barriers at Decision stage:**
- **Sign-anxiety (professional-reader variant).** She reads contracts for a living. She'll notice issues a layperson won't. Poor drafting kills conversion.
- **Spousal alignment.** Ben wants to understand what he's signing on to as a household. A firm that proactively addresses "this is a family decision; I'm happy to have a brief call with your husband to address any questions he has" signals respect for her household structure.

## 3. Adam Ferrier's Trigger Moments (Diana-specific)

Ranked by conversion weight:

1. **Week 4: First adjuster offer of $18K arrives.** Diana does the math instantly — her ER bill alone was $5.8K, concussion treatment + PT + missed work = $20K already accumulated. **She realizes the offer is insulting. This is when she decides she needs a lawyer.**
2. **Week 6: Partial short-term disability payment hits** — her paycheck is 60% of normal. **The household budget math breaks.** This is when the financial urgency becomes visible and specific.
3. **Week 8: Ben has a work slowdown week** — his construction subcontractor has a 3-day paid-lower-hours rotation because of a project delay. **Household income dips another 15% for the week.** The stability of Diana's $48K anchor becomes emotionally real for Ben too.
4. **Week 10: First neurosurgeon follow-up appointment.** She's told recovery is slower than hoped; surgery may be on the table by Month 4 if PT plateau holds. **The timeline uncertainty becomes scary.**
5. **Week 12: Return-to-work light duty at 60% of role.** She can sit at the computer 2 hrs/day. The contrast between what she was doing pre-crash and what she can do now is psychologically intense. **Activation is peak.**

**Campaign activation window:** Weeks 5–15 post-crash. Earliest = Week 5 (after first adjuster offer); deepest engagement = Week 8–12; latest save = Week 13–15. **Outside this window: she either defaulted to accepting the $18K by Week 16 or has already hired somebody.**

## 4. Competitive Set (Diana's actual alternatives)

Ranked by her likely preference order:

1. **A mid-size Phoenix PI firm (WCTL's natural position).** Her ideal — professional, specific, local, responsive. If she finds one that makes it easy, she signs.
2. **Amanda's cousin's firm (referral from Chandler Mom Crew).** Low social risk because Amanda vouched. But she'd need to research it anyway.
3. **Ben's coworker Miguel's brother's firm.** Similar dynamic. Referral warmth + unknown quality.
4. **Karen's recommendation (her boss's suggestion).** Probably a commercial law firm that also does some PI — generalist, adequate, not ideal.
5. **The firm from the AZCentral article.** Credible because journalists use them, but she doesn't have the name memorized.
6. **The I-10 billboard firms.** Last resort only. She'd rather accept the $18K than call one of these.
7. **Accept the $18K and end it.** The default if nothing else converts. She'd regret it within 6 months but she might do it out of fatigue.

**WCTL's job:** beat options 2, 3, 4, and 5 via creative + intake + first-call efficiency. Option 1 is the position WCTL is aiming at. The billboard firms (option 6) are not real competition for Diana — but they're WCTL's AESTHETIC competition (if WCTL's creative drifts billboard-y, Diana files WCTL into that category and moves on).

**Time-based competitor (option 7) is the real race.** Every week past Week 15 without conversion is a week closer to the $18K default. **Speed-to-call matters enormously.**

## 5. Creative Implications (preview for `05_Campaign_Implications.md`)

- **Opening frame must interrupt billboard-pattern expectation in < 1 second.** UGC kitchen-table-with-falling-envelopes does this because NO billboard firm would film in this register. The aesthetic itself signals "different tier."
- **"Broken bones. Bills don't stop." hook is filter-sharp.** The first two words screen out soft-tissue self-identifiers. Good.
- **"No fee unless we win" above-the-fold** — necessary but Diana partially understands contingency already.
- **Landing page must respect her CFO fluency + accommodate concussion constraints.** Plain-language, scannable, under 15 minutes of total engagement from click to form.
- **Quiz must fit on her attention budget.** 7 steps max (the current spec per Doc 6 §9). Single-screen per step. No multi-step cognitive loading.
- **Intake must be efficient.** Max 20-minute first call. Structured questions, clear fee explanation, plan-of-action finish.
- **Ben needs to be part of the decision.** Creative + landing page + intake should signal "family-decision respect" explicitly.
- **The $18K anchor (or equivalent from the ad) should be referenced indirectly in Week 8+ creative rotation:** "The first offer is rarely the fair offer" type language.
- **No scare tactics.** Diana scrolls past them. She's already scared enough.
