## Jon Steel's Deprivation Frame — The Six Losses

Diana's deprivation architecture is distinct from Sarah's and Ty's. Hers isn't about professional identity (Sarah) or rider culture (Ty). Hers is about the **operational integrity of a family system she spent 15 years building** — and the fact that she's the only one who saw the system. Losses, layer by layer:

### Loss 1 — Control (the executive function)

Diana's entire identity is organized around being the person who manages things. The office. The household. Three kids' schedules. Her mother's appointments. Ben's variable income. The grocery list. The dog's vet appointments (back when they had a dog). A herniated disc + concussion didn't just injure her body — **they removed the instrument she uses to hold her world together.**

She cannot drive (neck immobility + concussion). She cannot look at screens for more than 10 minutes without a nauseating headache. She cannot sit at her desk. She cannot organize. She cannot manage. **The thing she *is* — the person who holds it all together — she cannot be that right now.** The machine she ran is off.

The specific craft of her control: it's not just task completion. It's *prediction*. She knew on Monday morning that Aiden would forget his soccer cleats on Tuesday; she put them in the car on Sunday. She knew Ben's paycheck would be short in January; she'd set aside $800 extra in November. She knew Mia would get a tummy-ache after eating pizza at her friend's; she'd pack tums in Mia's backpack. **She ran the household on prediction, not reaction.** And now she can't predict anything — including her own recovery timeline.

### Loss 2 — The Invisible Labor (the creative's front-line)

**This is the Diana-specific deprivation that becomes the campaign's emotional engine.** Steel's Doc 2 named it; the tune surfaces it.

Here's what Diana's family discovered in Week 2 — the quiet horror: **nobody knew where anything was.**
- The login for the electric bill autopay
- Which kid has soccer Tuesday versus Thursday
- Where Mia's pediatrician's phone number is stored
- What brand of cat food Pepper eats (Ben bought the wrong one for 4 days)
- When Aiden's dentist appointment was (missed)
- How the chore chart rotation worked
- The password for the school parent-portal
- Whose turn it was to bring snack to Mia's soccer practice
- Where the kids' birth certificates are filed (school needed a copy; Ben spent 45 minutes searching)
- Which of Emma's school activities required "parent volunteer" sign-up

**These are not things Diana did for recognition.** They are things she did because nobody else did them, and now that she can't, the household is in a kind of slow-motion collapse that confirms both her worst fear (*I'm indispensable, nobody sees it*) and her deepest resentment (*I shouldn't have to be indispensable, nobody sees it*). The duality is the lever. Don't choose sides in the creative.

**The creative's translation:** an S5 visual of the frozen calendar + the missed appointment on the dry-erase board + the backpacks on wrong hooks = the emotional payload. It doesn't need explaining. Anyone who's been the hub of a household system feels it in 2 seconds.

### Loss 3 — The Financial Anchor (the household CFO loss)

Diana is the **stable income** of the household — $48K twice-monthly paychecks that arrive reliably. Ben's $44K average construction-foreman income is boom/bust (summer peak $52K equivalent months; winter lull $38K equivalent months). **They planned the household around Diana's paycheck as the anchor.** Mortgage $2,350, car payment $410, credit cards minimums, Mia's after-school program, Emma's phone bill, groceries, utilities — all calibrated to Diana's predictable deposit.

**The mathematical truth post-crash:** her employer offers no short-term disability. She burned her 10 days of PTO in Weeks 1–2. She's on unpaid FMLA for 12 weeks. **The household income drops 52% for Weeks 3 through 15.** The emergency fund was $11K. By Week 8, they've burned through it. By Week 10, the credit card rebuild begins. By Week 12, they're having the "do we tap Ben's 401K?" conversation they swore they wouldn't have.

**This is the rational-brain stake.** The creative doesn't need to spell it out — Diana's audience knows what "bills don't stop" means viscerally. But the *specific mechanism* is her income-stability loss, and the body-copy language should honor that: "Bills don't stop because you can't work" — not "you may have financial hardship."

### Loss 4 — The Commute (her quiet alone-time, now a trigger)

Diana's I-10 drive was — until the crash — **the one time in her day she was alone.** Thirty-five minutes of podcast or NPR, a to-go coffee, the only hours in her life she wasn't accountable to a tenant, a boss, a kid, or a husband. She *liked* the commute. She read it as meditation.

**The crash happened on that road.** When she eventually gets behind the wheel again (clear by Month 2–3 after neck heals), she will flinch every time a vehicle approaches from behind in stop-and-go traffic. **The one sliver of solitude in her day has been converted into a source of anxiety.** She's not sure she'll ever use the commute for podcasts again. It might become the part of her day she dreads.

### Loss 5 — Cognitive Function (the concussion-specific dread)

The concussion is the injury Diana didn't expect to be debilitating. She can follow a conversation with Ben one-on-one, but not with more than two people. She forgets words. She reads a paragraph and can't recall what it said. She tried to help Emma with algebra at Week 2 and couldn't track the steps — she felt her daughter watching her fail at something Emma used to respect her for.

**The concussion terrifies her more than the neck pain**, because the neck will heal, but *what if her brain doesn't come all the way back?* She hasn't said this out loud to anyone. It's too big. She Googled "concussion recovery 42 year old woman" at 2 AM and closed the tab before she finished reading.

**This layer matters for campaign creative because:** Diana's professional competence depends on cognitive sharpness. A Senior Office Manager who can't read a spreadsheet is not a Senior Office Manager. The career threat isn't just "can I go back to work" — it's "will I be the same person who worked there, or a lesser version of her?" **The ad creative must never invoke cognitive clarity issues directly** (too sensitive, too pitying) but the persona's writer should know it's underneath every frame.

### Loss 6 — Marital Balance (the quiet strain)

Diana and Ben's marriage is strong but not bulletproof. Pre-crash: they had a workable division of labor — Diana handled mental-load + schedules, Ben handled physical tasks + construction-income cycles + being the fun-dad. Post-crash: Ben is doing everything. He's the chef, the driver, the kid-wrangler, the household-admin attempted. **He is not Diana, and he doesn't want to be Diana, and he's slowly getting angry — not at her, at the situation.**

Diana can see it. She feels the invisible-labor validation (*see, it IS real labor*) and the guilt (*I never wanted him to resent it*) at the same time. Neither has named it. The marriage is pausing. It's not breaking, but it's pausing. **This is the deepest Loss. It's also the hardest to articulate.** The creative should NEVER reference their marriage directly; but the audience member who identifies with Diana feels this dimension intuitively when the visual is right.

## What She Wants (Explicit — ranked by how consciously she'd name each)

1. **The medical bills covered.** She knows the ER visit was $5,800. She knows the neurosurgeon consult was $1,200. She knows PT is $185/visit uncovered by copay. Running the family CFO math is what she does — and the math is bad.
2. **Her cognitive function fully back.** She won't name this fear but it's primary.
3. **A lawyer who doesn't make her manage another thing.** She has zero bandwidth for a "fight for your rights" process. She wants someone competent to handle it and give her weekly updates.
4. **The peace of knowing her household will be okay in 6 months.** She wants the runway — not the big settlement, the *stability* to not have to think about money until her neck heals.
5. **Ben to not secretly resent her.** She won't say this. But it's in the top 5.

## What She Won't Say Out Loud (The Shadow)

Steel's rule: the payload reaches the thing that isn't said.

- **She's terrified of being seen as weak at her office.** Karen is a friend but also her boss; Karen has already told her "take all the time you need" in a tone that contained the unasked question: *when are you coming back?*
- **She's jealous of the stay-at-home-mom Facebook moms in her neighborhood group.** She used to quietly look down on them. Now she wonders if they had it figured out. The jealousy is humiliating.
- **She's angry at the F-250 driver in a way that surprises her.** Not at his Ford. At his *phone*. She keeps thinking: *he was looking at a screen when he hit me*. She doesn't tell people this because it feels too small.
- **She is scared of the $35K offer** (assuming the adjuster offers one in the same range the campaign persona assumes) — not because it's low, but because she might accept it out of sheer fatigue. She's not sure she has the energy to fight.
- **She resents Ben's construction-income reality.** Not because he made bad choices — because the industry is what it is. She resents the system that made HER income the anchor, because it means SHE has to recover on schedule and the household can't afford her taking proper recovery time.
- **She misses her own mother.** Linda (her mom) had a stroke 18 months ago and hasn't been the same. Diana used to call her when something overwhelming happened. Diana cannot call her now because Linda's short-term memory is impaired and Diana doesn't want to re-explain the crash every time.
- **She's scared Emma is going to lose respect for her.** Emma watched Diana fail at algebra. Emma watched Diana cry at Week 4 when she couldn't find the login for the student parent-portal. Diana has always been Emma's model of competence. Now Emma is seeing the inverse, and Diana is scared Emma will never unsee it.

## The Emotional Arc (Timeline)

### Week 1 — ER + early home / the system starts cracking

**Day of crash (Thursday):** She drives herself home from the I-10 scene (refused the ambulance, Ben followed in his F-150). That night she can't turn her head. She wakes up Friday morning unable to sit up. Ben drives her to Banner Gateway ER. Herniated disc + concussion diagnosis. No screens for 2 weeks. She sits in the parking lot of Banner Gateway and cries for 20 minutes — not for the pain, but because she doesn't know who will pick up the kids.

**Week 1 at home:** She tries to manage verbally — gives Ben instructions. Ben tries. It goes poorly. The kids' Monday morning is a disaster. Diana realizes the system she ran is opaque to everyone but her. The realization is destabilizing.

### Month 1 — The collapse becomes visible

Ben's emergency leave runs out Week 2. He's back at his subcontractor Week 3. **The household's load redistributes to: Diana's mother (Linda, limited by her own stroke recovery), Ben's sister Lien (stretched thin), and Emma (grumpy, 14).** The wall calendar stops updating. The dentist appointment is missed. Mia's school permission slip sits unsigned on the counter. The grocery-pickup app password is unknown to anyone but Diana.

The first adjuster call comes Day 10. The at-fault driver's insurance (USAA for the F-250 driver — he was off-duty from a construction-adjacent job) offers **$18,000 to close**. Diana is on Advil + a cervical collar and doesn't answer the phone. Ben takes the call. He says "we'll get back to you." Diana later asks what they said. Ben tells her $18K. Diana says "that won't even cover PT."

### Month 2 — The decision window

This is the creative's target activation zone. Diana is off the cervical collar. PT three times a week. She can sit at the computer for 30 minutes at a stretch. She's not back to work yet — her employer's short-term disability (partial salary) kicked in at Week 6, dropping her to 60% of regular pay.

**Around Week 6–8, she starts Googling.** "How long does herniated disc recovery take?" first. Then "concussion lawsuit arizona." Then "car accident lawyer arizona." Her Facebook feed starts showing PI ads (algo detected the searches). **This is when S5 hits her.**

### Month 3–6 — Hiring decision or default

If she hires — she signs by Week 10–14. If she doesn't — she accepts the adjuster's increased offer by Month 4 and takes the HELOC hit. **The S5 creative has to reach her in Week 6–14 with a hook that says *we see the invisible labor* — not a hook that says "hurt in an accident?"**

## Adam Ferrier's Motivation Architecture — COM-B Map (Diana-specific)

For Diana to take the action we want (tap ad → complete quiz → book consultation → sign engagement letter), three conditions must be met. Her COM-B profile is distinct from Sarah's and Ty's.

**Capability (does she have the know-how?):**
- ⚠️ *Physical:* limited by concussion — screen time is capped at 30 min before nausea. A long landing-page quiz will lose her. The quiz must fit on her tolerance window.
- ⚠️ *Psychological:* Diana is mid-fluent on contracts and process (she's a Senior Office Manager — she reads lease documents professionally). She's NOT fluent on PI specifics. She doesn't know contingency mechanics, policy-limit demands, or comparative negligence. **The landing page + intake must educate without condescending — Diana's register sits between Ty's (lay) and Sarah's (expert).**

**Opportunity (does her environment allow it?):**
- ⚠️ *Physical:* mobile 4–6 hours/day currently (more than pre-crash). Time is not the constraint; *focused attention* is.
- ⚠️ *Social:* Ben is a co-decision-maker and an active participant. He'll read the firm's website. He'll have opinions. **Unlike Sarah (Marcus is supportive but deferent), Ben is more actively involved in the decision.** The creative + landing page must work for a married-couple co-review, not a sole decision-maker.
- ⚠️ *Environmental:* peak tap-through windows are late-night + early-morning (concussion-insomnia). **2:00 AM – 4:00 AM** is an unusually strong window for Diana because of the concussion-related sleep disturbance combined with anxious mental-load.

**Motivation (does she want to enough?):**
- ⚠️ *Reflective (planned, rational):* She knows she needs a lawyer. She's decided she'll hire one. She's analyzing options.
- ⚠️ *Automatic (emotional):* The $18K adjuster offer is activating — it insults the household's financial reality. **This is the flywheel the creative ignites.**
- ✅ *Activation lever:* specific. She responds to creative that *sees* her invisible labor without ever naming it directly.

## Richard Shotton's Cognitive Barriers (Diana-specific — different from Ty's AND Sarah's)

Five biases actively working against Diana converting:

1. **Analysis paralysis (Diana's #1 bias).** Diana will compare 8 firms. She's an optimizer by temperament — she does this professionally. The firm that **makes the decision easy** — not the firm with the best website — wins. Meta's SAC prevents interest targeting, so the creative must *preempt the comparison* by being undeniably distinctive on first impression.
2. **"I can handle this myself" identity bias.** Diana has managed every major family transaction for 15 years — mortgage, refinance, medical insurance enrollments, kid school transitions, her mom's stroke-recovery admin. She'll initially try to negotiate with the adjuster herself. The creative must signal *why a lawyer adds value even for someone who's handled a thousand admin fights before* — without condescending.
3. **Trust-scarcity around "internet lawyers."** Diana's prior: serious professionals get referrals; Meta ads are the last resort. Our ad must overcome this bias in 2 seconds via production value + copy sophistication + credible specificity.
4. **Loss aversion around time investment.** Diana has zero spare time. Any legal process that signals "depositions, discovery, court dates" reads as more workload. The creative should signal the lawyer *reduces her load*. "We handle the legal fight. You focus on healing." This line is written for Diana.
5. **Sunk-cost fear around the adjuster conversation.** She's had two calls with the adjuster. She might feel, irrationally, that hiring a lawyer "wastes" that engagement. The creative should reframe *the adjuster's offer as the sunk cost*, not the negotiation time.

## The Identity Frame

Diana's self-image, in three sentences she'd never say aloud but would recognize as true:

> I'm the person who makes the household work. I'm the one who knows where things are, remembers the appointments, pays the bills, and predicts what each person needs before they know they need it. The last fifteen years I built a system that runs on my attention — and I built it so quietly that nobody else can see it, and right now the system is failing because I can't be where I was.

**The creative's job:** speak to that woman. Not the aspirational mom, not the victim version, not the cartoon "mom juggling everything" stock persona. The actual woman — mid-collapse, watching her system fail, trying to accept help from people who don't know what they're doing.

## Pitch-Language Summary (for `05_Campaign_Implications.md` to pull from)

**For Julian's synthesis:**

- **Primary emotional engine:** the **Invisible Labor** (Loss 2). Creative visualizes the failing system — envelopes falling, frozen calendar, missed appointment — and lets the viewer read the subtext without ever saying "invisible labor."
- **Secondary engine:** **the stable-income anchor loss** (Loss 3). Diana's $48K is the household's budget anchor. "Bills don't stop because you can't work" reads as lived reality, not slogan.
- **Tertiary engine:** **the $18K (or equivalent) adjuster offer insult.** Diana does the math. She knows what PT costs. The offer's absurdity activates her.
- **Trigger moment:** Week 6–10 evening couch-scroll, 9 PM – 2 AM window (concussion-insomnia zone).
- **Language test:** *would Diana text this to her Chandler Mom Crew group chat with an "is this too real?" caption?* If yes, voice register is right.
- **Binet 40/60 test** (activation-weighted for volume slot): is the creative's first-3-seconds hook filter-sharp enough to screen out soft-tissue self-identifiers? "Broken bones. Bills don't stop." = yes.
