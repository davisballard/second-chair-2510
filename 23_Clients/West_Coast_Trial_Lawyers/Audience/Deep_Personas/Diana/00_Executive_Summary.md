Diana was picked for the S5 slot — the broadest catch-all MVA asset, targeting the 400–600 non-soft-tissue incidents/month in AZ (Evidence Register §3.6). She's the highest-VOLUME slot in the Phase 1 campaign, running UGC Native style (the one UGC test asset; the other five are Elevated Documentary). ## 1. The Archetype (one line)

**Diana Nguyen is the woman who holds everything together and would never describe herself that way. She runs a seven-person office for a property management company, three kids' schedules, her household's budget spreadsheet, her husband's unpredictable construction income, and the thousand small decisions that make a suburban Chandler family function. She was rear-ended on I-10 by an F-250 driver looking at his phone, and for the first time in fifteen years she cannot run anything — and what her family just discovered, in the quiet horror of Week 2, is that nobody else knew the passwords.**

## 2. Why She's the Right Archetype for the S5 Slot (the sourced rationale)

Five reasons. Different load-bearing structure from Sarah's (Sarah was premium-value; Diana is volume-floor).

1. **Broadest demographic catch in the Phase 1 campaign.** S5 has to reach 400–600 monthly non-soft-tissue MVA incidents (Evidence Register §3.6). Diana's profile — 42-year-old suburban mom + office job + daily Phoenix-metro freeway commute — is the statistically modal AZ MVA victim. Any narrower archetype sacrifices the reach S5 needs to carry volume for the entire campaign.
2. **Female form-filler reach** (Creative brief rationale, 2026-04-22 casting). Non-motorcycle PI intake in AZ is filed 55–60% by female family members handling admin for injured relatives. Diana's face pulls both self-identification (injured party) AND form-filler-for-a-spouse archetype in one character.
3. **Office-manager-as-household-manager mirror is strategically poetic.** Her job title *literally* contains the word 'manager.' Her professional identity parallels her household role. The creative's 'she who manages everything can't manage anything right now' arc has professional symmetry baked in. No other persona carries this structural echo.
4. **UGC fit.** Sarah, Ty, and Laura have emotional architectures that read polished — Elevated Documentary. Diana's kitchen-table + falling-envelopes + interrupted-family-system story reads UGC-native. She's the one asset in Phase 1 that tests whether UGC outperforms Elevated on Meta. If UGC wins the A/B test, Phase 2 creative direction shifts.
5. **Severity-filter lean-in.** S5's hook is 'Broken bones. Bills don't stop.' The first two words are the severity filter. Diana's herniated C5-C6 + concussion profile fits the non-soft-tissue threshold exactly (though debatable — the creative's actual targeting leans toward stronger visible injuries; the literal interpretation is 'severe MVA' including fracture patterns. Clarify during Week 1 quiz-data review).

## 4. The Single-Minded Proposition (the "one thing")

> **Diana's entire life is organized around being the person who manages things — for her office, her household, her kids, her parents, her husband, her dog's vet appointments. She's not 'at home on medical leave.' She is a family system that has quietly stopped functioning because the one invisible piece holding it together is on a couch with a herniated disc and a concussion — and everyone else just discovered how much she was actually doing.**

This is the SMP. Every S5 creative must trace to it. If a hook could sit comfortably next to *"Broken bones. Bills don't stop,"* and if the emotional payload quietly names the invisible labor without lecturing, it's on-strategy. If it reduces Diana to 'a hurt person who needs help' — pitying, one-dimensional — it's off-strategy.

---

## 5. The Core Insight (the strategic money)

**The target audience for S5 doesn't recognize themselves as 'injury victims.' They recognize themselves as household systems — the hub of a family's logistics, the person everyone else doesn't realize is doing all the work until suddenly that work isn't getting done.** Diana's specific loss isn't physical pain — it's watching her household slow-motion-collapse in the two weeks after the crash because nobody else knew the Target grocery-pickup app password, which parent-teacher conference was when, whether the 8-year-old's field trip was still on, or where the cat's vet records were kept.

**This is the lever.** A PI firm that recognizes she's not just injured — she's a *family operating system that's been disrupted* — wins her attention. Every other firm in the Meta feed is still treating her as the generic auto-accident victim. WCTL's creative edge is reading her correctly.

The Week-2 realization has a specific emotional texture: it's both Diana's WORST FEAR (*I'm indispensable, nobody sees it*) and her DEEPEST RESENTMENT (*I shouldn't have to be indispensable, nobody sees it*) confirmed at once. The creative that respects this duality — without choosing sides between "you're loved!" and "your family is failing you!" — is the creative that lands.

## 6. The Five Campaign Implications

Details in `05_Campaign_Implications.md`. Top line:

1. **S5 visual must live in the household system, not the hospital or crash scene.** The envelopes falling. The paused laptop. The 8-year-old's backpack on the wrong hook. The grocery list in her handwriting that Ben is trying to decode at 9 PM. **The failure of the system is the creative's real subject.**
2. **Copy register is relatable + specific, not clinical.** Diana is a competent but not credentialed-expert professional. She's not going to parse FMCSA regulations (that's Sarah). She responds to plain-language acknowledgment of her reality. "Bills don't stop because you can't work" — perfect. "Understand AZ's pure comparative negligence framework" — wrong Diana.
3. **UGC style is locked for her.** Per `Creative/Style_Sheets/UGC_Native.md` — handheld iPhone aesthetic, flat phone color, natural daylight through kitchen window, slightly shaky framing. Do NOT let any S5 creative drift Elevated.
4. **No crash footage.** Same SC brand standard as Ty/Sarah. Absence-of-control — the envelopes falling in an otherwise-still domestic frame — is the creative. The F-250 is never in frame.
5. **Left arm sling + right jaw bruise + reading glasses up in hair = locked visual ID.** Per the pre-existing Creative brief and reference portrait prompt. Every S5 frame must honor these side-specific continuity props. Flipping any of them breaks the character lock downstream.

## 7. The Voice Test (use this before shipping any Diana creative)

Three questions. 3/3 = ship. 2/3 = revise. 1/3 = wrong audience.

1. **Would Diana read this line and feel recognized, not pitied?** Not 'would a generic suburban mom' — specifically *would Diana, the 42-year-old office manager who's been reading PI ads on I-10 billboards for 15 years, read this line and think "oh, they actually see me"?*
2. **Would Diana screenshot this and send it to her best friend** with a *"is this too real?"* caption? Does it read as documentary-truthful, or would it embarrass her to share?
3. **Does it name the invisible labor without lecturing?** Does the line or visual signal that WCTL understands what she actually lost — without ever using phrases like "invisible labor," without performative feminism, without pity?

## 8. Why This Persona Matters for the Overall Campaign (volume-slot lens)

**S5 is the volume-floor of the Phase 1 campaign.** Per the Evidence Register + Media Plan: S5 is expected to generate the largest share of delivered leads by count, even though per-case value is lower than Sarah's trucking segment.

Binet's 60/40 framing applied differently to Diana vs. Sarah: Sarah was brand-response hybrid (60/40 toward brand). **Diana is closer to 40/60 toward activation** — volume segments reward short-term direct-response optimization more than long-term brand work. This means:

- **Hook specificity** can be higher-filter ("Broken bones. Bills don't stop.") — we're comfortable losing soft-tissue self-identifiers because volume math still works.
- **Creative refresh cadence** can be faster. Diana's audience has more "mental availability" to our category already (she's seen PI ads her whole life) — we can iterate quicker on hooks without worrying about brand-signal erosion.
- **Direct-response metrics** weight more heavily in S5 optimization — CTR, LPV, quiz-completion-rate — than brand-awareness uplift.

**That said — the UGC test complicates the activation math.** If UGC drastically underperforms Elevated, we've lost not just S5's direct-response but our Phase 2 style-direction signal. S5 carries two loads at once: volume leads AND style learning. Protect the style integrity even while tuning direct-response.
