## Jon Steel's Deprivation Frame — The Six Losses

Sarah's emotional architecture is built on loss. Steel's method: name what's been taken, layer by layer. What she lost is a *stack*, and the stack is what the creative has to speak to without naming any single layer too explicitly.

### Loss 1 — The arm (the instrument of her trade)

Sarah is an ICU nurse. Her left arm is her dominant hand for IV starts, central line manipulation, intubation assists, chest compressions during codes. **The compound mid-shaft humerus fracture isn't just "a broken arm"** — it's a career threat. At 38, she's been using this arm to do high-dexterity life-critical work for 15 years. The external fixator is in for 6 weeks. The internal fixation surgery happens at Week 6. Full range of motion recovery is months out — possibly never 100%.

**For a left-handed ICU nurse, the loss calculus is specific:** she can't start an IV one-handed; she can't do compressions without cross-dominance retraining; she can't adjust a ventilator panel with a compromised supinating motion. She's spent a career being the person the floor trusts on the hard procedures. **Now she is a procedure.**

The surgeon (a Banner-Phoenix orthopedic specialist she's referred patients to) was honest: *"Eighty percent function expected. Possible some strength deficit. Your fine motor will be fine. Endurance work — lifting a 250-lb patient — might be limited."* Sarah heard what he didn't say: *the charge-nurse physical demands of a trauma ICU may not be in your future.*

### Loss 2 — The calm (the identity)

This is the one Steel calls "the architecture loss." Sarah's professional identity is built on being the calm one. She's been the composed voice in other people's emergencies for 15 years. Family members cry; she doesn't. Residents panic; she doesn't. A 22-year-old comes in after flipping his truck on I-17 and the whole ER is scrambling; she's the one who says "breathe, we got him, let's run it" and the room settles.

**Now she's on the gurney, and the nurse at Banner-Phoenix is asking if she needs another warm blanket, and she's saying "no I'm fine" reflexively the way a patient who doesn't want to be a burden says it, and she realizes with quiet horror: *this is the exact answer I've heard ten thousand patients give me, and I have always known it was a lie.***

She doesn't know how to be a patient. She doesn't know how to let people help. She doesn't know how to accept a meal train. She doesn't know how to say "I'm not okay." She's been the other side of this interaction her entire adult life and she does not have the muscle memory for being on this side. **The competence reversal is her identity crisis.** It's not "I lost my job" — it's "I don't know who I am without the role of being the calm one."

### Loss 3 — The breadwinner position (the economic anchor)

Sarah is **71% of the household income** ($82K + $3K outreach vs. Marcus's $33K). This isn't a gender-politics observation — it's a load-bearing family truth. They made mortgage decisions based on her income. They made family-size decisions (one child, not two) partly around her career trajectory. The 2022 Subaru Outback, the Flagstaff house, Oliver's kindergarten, the 401K contributions — all anchored to her being the economic engine.

**When she's on unpaid FMLA at Month 1, then short-term disability at 60% of her salary (if her employer-sponsored STD kicks in reliably, which isn't guaranteed for commercial HVAC — wait, she's healthcare; it typically does, but with delays), the family's income drops to ~$82K combined.** That's a 30% household income cut. The mortgage is $2,150. Oliver's school costs are minimal. But the emergency fund ($18K) burns through in 4 months.

**The financial loss is not dramatic yet — it's slow-bleed.** By Month 3, they've tapped the HELOC. By Month 6, they're having the "do we sell the house?" conversation they swore they'd never have. **This is the creative's target window — Month 2–4, when the bleed is visible and the $35K adjuster offer starts to feel weirdly reasonable.**

### Loss 4 — The drive (the quiet ritual, now hostile)

Sarah's monthly outreach drive on I-17 was — until April 6 — **the one time in her week she was alone.** 1 hour 45 minutes each way, windows up, NPR or a Hidden Brain podcast, a stop at the Sunset Point rest area for coffee and to stretch. She *liked* the commute. It was the only time in her life she wasn't accountable to a patient, a husband, a kindergartener, an intensivist, or a charge nurse above her. Just road and thinking.

**Now the commute is the scene of the crime.** The Sunset Point descent is where the Freightliner hit her. She'll eventually have to drive I-17 again — Oliver's grandmother is in Prescott, her sister is in Peoria, her specialty role requires it. But the first time she's in a passenger seat on that stretch, she knows she'll flinch. **A private sliver of her life has been converted to a trigger.**

### Loss 5 — The future timeline (the upward trajectory)

Sarah is 80% through her CNML (Certified Nurse Manager and Leader) certification. Exam scheduled June 2026. She's been building toward a nurse-manager role — one step above charge — for 3 years. Marcus has been supportive; she bought the study materials in 2023. The certification would position her for a director-of-nursing track at NAH over a 5–10 year horizon — a $120K+ path that could make Marcus's $33K teacher salary sustainable as a supplemental income.

**The CNML exam is now postponed indefinitely.** Can't concentrate on clinical-leadership theory with a leg of bone pinned externally. Can't sit for a 3-hour exam when a 20-minute physical therapy session leaves her exhausted. **Every career milestone she was building toward just got a 12–24 month asterisk.**

And she can feel the professional cost accumulating: colleagues who were 6 months behind her on the CNML track now leapfrog her. Residency-trained nurse managers get promoted off her skill base while she's doing PT. **The crash didn't end her career. It slowed her trajectory — which, in a career game of 5-year compounding, is its own kind of ending.**

### Loss 6 — The certainty (the meta-loss)

This is the one Sarah won't name. Before April 6, Sarah believed — the way every competent professional believes — that *if you make good decisions, you don't get wrecked by random bullshit.* She drove the speed limit. She wore her seatbelt. She had airbags. She had AWD. She trained trauma for 15 years and she knew what bad luck looked like. She thought she was a respectful, calibrated participant in the risk ecosystem.

**Then a tire blew on a truck 100 feet behind her and 1.5 seconds later her arm was in a door frame.** And the truck driver wasn't drunk. The company wasn't a fly-by-night. The weather was perfect. She did everything right. None of it mattered.

**The meta-loss is the loss of "if I do the right thing, I'll be okay."** This is the loss Steel says no ad ever directly names because it's too abstract — but every piece of creative that lands for Sarah has to *respect* the fact that she's grappling with this. Don't treat her like a passive victim. She did everything right. Speak to that.

## What She Wants (Explicit — ranked by how consciously she'd name each)

1. **The medical bills covered.** She knows the ER activation alone was $47K. She knows her OOP max is $8K. She knows surgery 2 is coming. She'd like to not be in medical debt at Thanksgiving.
2. **Her job secured.** She wants her FMC charge role when she recovers. She's worked 15 years to earn that position. She's scared someone else will fill it.
3. **A lawyer who sees the commercial-liability structure she already understands.** "Million-dollar policy, FMCSA logs, maintenance records, vicarious liability for the logistics company" — she's not going to need this explained. She wants it *confirmed*.
4. **Someone to take the legal admin off her plate.** She has PT, a 5-year-old, a recovering marriage, and no cognitive bandwidth to learn personal-injury procedural law from scratch.
5. **Validation that she wasn't the problem.** See Loss 6. She won't ask for this. But she needs it.

## What She Won't Say Out Loud (The Shadow)

Steel's rule: the campaign's emotional payload has to reach the thing that isn't said.

- **She's terrified of being a "patient."** She doesn't want casseroles. She doesn't want sympathy visits. She doesn't want anyone at FMC to see her like this.
- **She's quietly humiliated by the meal train her ICU colleagues organized.** She doesn't want to be the person who needs help. She participated in 40 meal trains over her career for other people. Being on the receiving end feels wrong.
- **She is scared Marcus will resent being the primary caretaker.** He's been a saint. He hasn't complained. She can see him running on fumes. She knows he's angry — not at her, at the situation — and she doesn't know how to acknowledge it without making him feel guilty.
- **She is scared about her Nascar-career.** (Slip: she misspoke once to her sister — meant "nursing" career, said "Nascar." They both laughed. The laugh was relief-laughter, not humor.)
- **She is angry at the trucking company in a way that surprised her.** Not at the driver (who was surely scared too). At the company. Specifically: she thinks about the fleet-maintenance records and she *knows* — from handling truck-crash victims in her ICU — that front-left-tire blowouts aren't just bad luck; they're maintenance history. She is angry in a data-specific way that she's not sure she can explain to a lawyer.
- **She is scared of the $35,000 offer.** Not because she thinks it's too high. Because she's done the math — her ER bill alone is $47K — and she cannot believe an insurance company is offering less than the ER bill and calling it reasonable. **The offer's absurdity is activating her.** This is the trigger.

## The Emotional Arc (Timeline)

### Week 1 — Hospital + early recovery

**Day of crash (Monday April 6):** Banner-University Medical Center Phoenix ICU (she was airlifted). Her own professional community. Intensivists she's worked with on case-collaborations are now her attending physicians. **The humiliation is textual.** Karen flew in from Peoria to be with her; Marcus drove down that night with Oliver and stayed at Karen's. Sarah refuses the ambulance photo one of the ICU nurses tried to take for their "survivor wall." Day 3: adjuster calls. Marcus takes the call because Sarah is on hydromorphone. $35,000 offered. Marcus says "we'll get back to you." Sarah says "no we won't" when she's clear the next day.

### Month 1 — Home / PT start / first real bills

Discharged to Flagstaff Week 2. External fixator in. First medical bills arrive. ER activation: $47K. Surgery: $38K. PT starts 3×/week — it's brutal. She's used to being the one yelling at patients to push through PT. She's crying in her truck after her first session. Hasn't told Marcus. **The adjuster calls again Day 19 with a follow-up: "We can close this out today. $35K still available."** Sarah declines. Marcus suggests maybe they should talk to a lawyer. Sarah agrees. Neither of them knows where to start.

### Month 2–4 — Decision window

This is the creative-activation window. She's returned to work on admin-only light duty at Week 12, making ~60% of her charge-nurse pay. Bills keep arriving. Second surgery happens at Week 6. Recovery trajectory isn't linear. **Marcus is exhausted.** Oliver is acting out at kindergarten in ways he hadn't been. Sarah's CNML study plan is untouched on her kitchen table.

**By Month 2, she's Googled "commercial trucking lawyer arizona."** She's found 4 firms. Two are billboard firms she's tuned out for 15 years. One is a small-print referral from an old NAU nursing classmate (didn't lead anywhere). One is WCTL — she clicked on them after seeing an S1 ad in her Facebook feed at 10 PM one evening, between a post from Flagstaff Trail Runners and a news post about Banner expansion.

### Month 4–6 — Hiring decision

If she hires — if the creative + landing page + intake call pass her clinical-competence filter — she signs by Month 4. If she doesn't, she accepts the $35K offer by Month 5 and takes the HELOC hit. **WCTL's ad spend has to reach her in this 60-day window with a creative that reads as competent, not theatrical.** Miss this window and she's lost.

## Adam Ferrier's Motivation Architecture — COM-B Map (Sarah-specific)

For Sarah to take the action we want (tap ad → complete quiz → book consultation → sign engagement letter), three conditions must be met simultaneously. Her COM-B profile is significantly different from Ty's.

**Capability (does she have the know-how?):**
- ✅ *Physical:* can complete a quiz on her phone with one functional arm. High bandwidth.
- ✅ *Psychological:* **VERY high.** She has medical-legal fluency from 15 years of hospital administration. She understands commercial insurance, policy limits, liability cases (her hospital handles trucking-victim admits weekly), the difference between a defense attorney and a plaintiff's lawyer. She reads documents professionally. **She doesn't need the landing page to educate her. She needs it to RESPECT her.**

**Opportunity (does her environment allow it?):**
- ✅ *Physical:* mobile 6–8 hours/day currently (more than pre-crash). Time is not the constraint.
- ⚠️ *Social:* Marcus is a co-decision-maker but tactical (he's exhausted, he'll defer to Sarah's judgment); Sarah's mother and sister offer input but not weight.
- ⚠️ *Environmental:* the ad has to reach her when she's actually in the emotional-financial window to convert. **Month 2–4 evenings, especially 9–11 PM when Oliver's asleep and she's on her phone.** Too early = she's still in survival mode. Too late = she's accepted the $35K.

**Motivation (does she want to enough?):**
- ⚠️ *Reflective (planned, rational):* She knows she needs a lawyer. She's decided she'll hire one. She hasn't picked one yet.
- ⚠️ *Automatic (emotional):* The adjuster's $35K offer is activating — it insults her professionally. She's angry in a slow-burn clinical way. **The creative's job is to convert that simmering clinical anger into a tap.**
- ✅ *Activation lever:* specific. She's going to respond to creative that names the trucking-insurance asymmetry *in the language she already thinks in*. "A commercial policy covers more than a personal auto policy" — she already knows. "Find out what the law says about your case" — this is the right register.

## Richard Shotton's Cognitive Barriers (Sarah-specific — different from Ty's)

Five biases actively working against Sarah converting:

1. **Expert overconfidence bias.** Sarah has medical fluency and assumes — incorrectly — that medical fluency translates to legal fluency. She may think she can negotiate with the adjuster herself ("I know what my ER bill was worth; I can explain it"). The creative + intake must signal *why a lawyer adds value even for a medical professional* — without condescending.
2. **Authority skepticism.** Sarah's profession is built on medical authority; she's suspicious of competing authority claims. Loud-confident legal marketing triggers her "this person is compensating for something" alarm. **Quiet competence wins.**
3. **Loss aversion around time investment.** Sarah has no spare time. Any legal process that promises "months of deposition prep" reads as more workload. The creative should signal the lawyer *reduces* her cognitive load, doesn't add to it. *"We handle the legal fight. You focus on recovery."*
4. **Analysis paralysis / optimization bias.** Sarah is the kind of person who'd Google 8 firms and Excel-compare their contingency-fee percentages. Meta's SAC prevents interest targeting, but the creative can preempt by *being undeniably better* on the first impression. A specific hook that names the trucking asymmetry rises above the comparative-billboard noise.
5. **Sunk-cost fear around the adjuster negotiation.** She's been talking to the adjuster for 3 weeks. She may feel, irrationally, that switching to a lawyer "wastes" that engagement. The creative should reframe *the adjuster's offer as the sunk cost*, not the negotiation time.

## Les Binet's Effectiveness Lens (why this matters for the S1 slot specifically)

Binet's 60/40 thesis: long-term brand work generates 60% of effectiveness; short-term activation generates 40%. **For premium-value segments like trucking, the balance shifts slightly toward short-term** — because a $500K signed case is immediate revenue that one-time activation can deliver. But there's still a long-term brand signal WCTL has to carry.

**For Sarah's creative, the brand-response hybrid means:**
- **The hook does short-term activation** (specific, filter-hard, quiz-driving).
- **The tone + register does long-term brand work** (signals WCTL as "the firm that actually understands commercial trucking" — a claim that pays dividends even on leads who don't sign in this campaign).

**Load-bearing implication:** don't sacrifice the long-term brand signal for short-term activation aggressiveness. Sarah would dismiss an ad that over-activates (urgency, scarcity, pressure) as billboard noise. The firm that signals *competent calm* wins her AND builds WCTL's long-term trucking reputation.

## The Identity Frame

Sarah's self-image, in three sentences she'd never say aloud but would recognize as true:

> I'm the person the floor trusts when it's hard. I'm the calm voice at the end of the gurney. I made a career out of being the one who holds it together when nobody else can — and right now, the one thing I have never done is hold myself together, and I don't know how to start.

**The creative's job:** speak to that woman. Not the aspirational version (hero nurse). Not the victim version (wounded professional). The actual woman — mid-reversal, clinical about her own crisis, privately undone.

## Pitch-Language Summary (for `05_Campaign_Implications.md` to pull from)

**For Julian's synthesis:**

- **Primary emotional engine:** the **competence reversal** (Loss 2). She's always been the calm one; now she's the emergency. Creative names this without ever saying it.
- **Secondary engine:** **professional respect.** She's treated as the expert she is, not the victim she isn't.
- **Tertiary engine:** **the $35K insult.** The adjuster's offer is so low it activates her clinical pattern-recognition — she knows bad numbers when she sees them, and this one is bad.
- **Trigger moment:** Month 2, late-evening phone scroll, 9–11 PM, between a Facebook post from Flagstaff Trail Runners and an NPR headline in her feed.
- **Language test:** *would Sarah text this to her ICU coworker group chat with a "what do you think?"* If yes, the register is right. If it'd embarrass her to share — it fails.
- **Binet 60/40 test:** does the creative make WCTL a firm Sarah would trust NEXT year, not just today? Brand signal + activation signal in one asset.
