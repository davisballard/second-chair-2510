## 1. SMP → S1 Hook Translation

The SMP from §00: *Sarah knows exactly what a $1M commercial policy is worth, what the FMCSA log entries will show, and what a defense firm's first offer looks like — because she's spent fifteen years reading charts, dealing with adjusters, and talking to families whose loved ones ended up in her ICU after crashes just like this one. She doesn't need to be convinced that trucking is different. She needs someone who speaks that language back to her without condescending.*

**For S1 (video 15–30s, 1:1 + 9:16):**

The S1 asset is Sarah's only creative slot in the Phase 1 campaign. It has to carry more strategic weight than any of Ty's three slots because it's the sole entry point to the premium-value trucking segment.

**Hook direction (from McCabe's 25-option pool in `04_Media_Reach.md` §8):**

- **Lead:** "Hit by a semi on I-17? Commercial insurance is different."
- **Alt A:** "A blown tire. Two seconds. Commercial trucking isn't a car accident."
- **Alt B:** "Million-dollar policies. Different rules. Find out what Arizona law says."

**What each does psychologically:**

- **Lead** activates her pattern-recognition ("commercial insurance is different" = the exact clinical fact she already knows but hasn't heard a lawyer say in plain language). Three-word filter + differentiation signal in one headline.
- **Alt A** leads with crash-mechanics specificity — the kind of 2-sentence factual setup Sarah writes in nursing charts. Signals "this lawyer thinks like me." Higher emotional resonance but less activation.
- **Alt B** is the longest but most information-forward — best for retargeting warm audiences, less ideal for cold top-of-funnel.

**A/B discipline:** Launch all three in Week 1 at equal weight. After 10 days (~150 impressions per creative per week minimum for signal), scale the winner and retire the two losers. Expected: Lead wins on cold; Alt B wins on retargeting warm traffic.

## 2. Body Copy Direction (for Meta feed text)

**Template:**

> Commercial trucks carry $1M+ insurance policies. Their companies have legal teams ready from day one. If an Arizona truck crash left you with broken bones, hospitalization, or surgery, find out what the law says. No fee unless we win.

**Why this template works for Sarah:**

1. **Opens with a fact** ("$1M+ insurance policies") that matches her clinical register. She reads it and the bullshit filter doesn't fire.
2. **Names the asymmetry** ("legal teams ready from day one") — activates her loss-aversion around time and the adjuster's head-start.
3. **Filters hard** ("broken bones, hospitalization, or surgery") — self-qualifies to non-soft-tissue audience.
4. **"Find out what the law says"** is neutral, investigative language — not "fight for your rights." Sarah will tap.
5. **"No fee unless we win"** — necessary, but she doesn't need education on contingency. Brief is fine.

**Alternative body copy angles for A/B testing:**

- *"Commercial trucking companies carry $1M+ policies because federal regulations require it. Your case might be governed by different rules than a typical auto accident. Free case review."* (Heavier fact-weight; her professional voice register.)
- *"Hit by an 18-wheeler on Arizona's highways? Commercial carriers operate under FMCSA regulations. Your recovery depends on counsel who understands both. No fee unless we win."* (Maximum specificity; tests whether FMCSA-level specificity attracts or alienates.)

**CTA text direction:**

- "See what your case is worth." — neutral, investigative
- "Free case review — 60 seconds." — quantified, low-friction
- "Find out what the law says." — no-pressure, information-positioning

**NEVER use:**
- "Call now for your free consultation!" (pushy)
- "You may be entitled to compensation!" (vague)
- "Don't wait — time is limited!" (manipulative urgency — Sarah scrolls past)
- "Get the settlement you deserve!" (pitying + vague numbers)

## 4. Compliance Considerations Specific to Sarah

- **AZ Bar ER 7.3 (direct solicitation):** Hook is third-person. *"Hit by a semi on I-17?"* is an open question, not direct solicitation — compliant.
- **AZ Bar ER 7.1 (truthfulness):** *"Commercial trucks carry $1M+ insurance policies"* is factually accurate (FMCSA minimums for interstate trucking are $750K; most carriers exceed $1M). *"Their legal teams are already working"* is truthful (commercial carriers do have on-retainer defense counsel). **Do not claim specific recovery amounts.**
- **AZ Bar ER 7.5 (firm naming):** WCTL logo + "West Coast Trial Lawyers" text must appear in the end-card.
- **Medical lien considerations:** Banner-University Medical Center will likely file a hospital lien on any settlement. Not an ad concern per se, but the intake team must explain lien negotiation proactively. This is a signal Sarah specifically will look for on the first call.
- **Fault-presumption rigor note** (`00_Evidence_Register.md` §14): If Robert raises "is there fault risk with trucking cases?" — the answer is yes, some, but less than motorcycle. Trucking defendants often focus liability challenges on the injured party's pre-existing conditions or seat-belt compliance rather than speed (which is the sportbike defense angle). Sarah's case has strong liability (tire blowout = maintenance question = company negligence). Brief Robert on this if he asks.
- **TCPA consent language:** Required on quiz-funnel lead-capture. Not our per-ad concern but flagged for intake.

## 5. Creative Acid Tests (before shipping any Sarah creative)

Three questions. 3/3 = ship. 2/3 = revise. 1/3 = wrong audience.

1. **Would Sarah read this line and nod?** Not "would a generic injured person" — specifically *would Sarah, a 38-year-old ICU charge nurse who's seen every PI billboard on I-17 for 15 years, read this line and think "okay, this person actually understands trucking cases"?*
2. **Would Sarah text this to her ICU coworker group chat** with a "what do you think?" — does it read as clinically-adult, or would it embarrass her to share?
3. **Does it name the asymmetry?** Does the creative signal WCTL understands commercial insurance ≠ personal auto? Without lecturing, without condescension, without over-explaining.

## 6. Media Render Acid Tests (Depesh's layer)

Two rules from `04_Media_Reach.md`:

1. **Does it still work sound-off at 9:30 PM on a 6-inch phone screen in FB Reels?** Captions required. First-2-second hook has to land without audio. If you have to unmute to understand it, it's dead.
2. **Would it clear Meta's Special Ad Category review?** No interest-targeting-adjacent copy ("you" or age-specific). No exaggerated injury or outcome claims. No policy-limit guarantees. The *fact* that commercial trucks carry $1M policies is fine to state; *"you'll get $1M"* is not.

## 7. Binet's 60/40 Effectiveness Lens (premium-value segment)

Per `02_Emotional_Architecture.md` §"Binet":

**Trucking is the highest-case-value segment.** One signed trucking case = 20× an average MVA case in revenue. The S1 creative is therefore **brand-response hybrid** — must do both short-term activation (specific hook, hard filter, quiz-driving CTA) AND long-term brand work (signals WCTL as *the* trucking specialist firm in AZ).

**Practical implications:**

- **Don't sacrifice long-term brand signal for short-term activation aggressiveness.** Sarah would dismiss an ad that over-activates (urgency, scarcity, pressure) as billboard noise.
- **The firm name + the trucking-specialist claim are the long-term brand assets.** Every S1 creative must reinforce WCTL = trucking specialist. After 12 weeks of exposure, Sarah's Arizona-trucking-lawyer mental shortlist should contain WCTL even if she doesn't sign this campaign.
- **Measure brand lift** (not just CPA) for the S1 creative. Phase 2 ambition: run brand-awareness surveys among Arizona commuter segments to track unaided recall of "trucking accident lawyer Arizona" — WCTL vs. competitors. This is a Phase 2 measurement, but the creative should be built to support it.
- **Contrast with Ty's M1/M2/M3:** Ty's creatives are higher-volume, lower-case-value, shorter consideration window. The 60/40 balance for Ty's slots skews more 40/60 (activation > brand). Sarah's balance stays at 60/40 or even 70/30 (brand > activation).

## 8. Research Gaps (what this persona hasn't validated)

Items to validate during or after Phase 1 pilot:

- **Marcus's actual role in the decision.** We modeled him as secondary-voice (defers to Sarah's judgment on the lawyer pick but weighs in on financial decisions). WCTL intake data — when Marcus is present on the call, how often does he ask the decisive question — will validate. If Marcus is more decision-lead than modeled, S1 may need a second-voice variant.
- **Meta CPL for the trucking-segment.** Our forecasts in `00_Evidence_Register.md` §§6–8 are internal estimates. Week 1–3 real data will tell us if trucking creative costs more per lead than motorcycle (expected) and whether sign-rate compensates (also expected).
- **"Commercial insurance is different" comprehension.** We assume Sarah gets it fast. A wider audience may not. A/B test whether "Commercial trucks carry $1M policies" is the right top-line (specificity) vs. something softer and more emotional (broader reach, lower specificity).
- **FMCSA vocabulary threshold.** How much specialty jargon ("FMCSA," "commercial policy," "vicarious liability") can we use before the creative alienates non-Sarah-level viewers? This is a real A/B question.
- **The Flagstaff vs. Phoenix framing.** Does the creative benefit from being explicit about Sarah being in Flagstaff (niche geographic filter) or should we keep it AZ-statewide generic? Validate via regional CPL splits.
- **Retargeting pool size for trucking.** Once we have 50+ S1 quiz completers, check LAL expansion economics. Phase 2 consideration.
- **Intake call quality.** Does WCTL's intake team actually have the FMCSA/commercial-trucking knowledge to pass Sarah's first-call clinical-competence filter? If not, the creative is wasted — the intake side is the equally-critical half. Audit before or during Week 1.
