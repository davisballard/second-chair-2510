Sarah was picked for the S1 trucking slot for five sourced reasons (see §2 below). The current version is a **2026-04-22 tune** of the Doc 2 Sarah — same core archetype (ICU nurse, 38, Flagstaff, mixed white-Latina, I-17 crash, left-arm compound fracture), stronger execution layer (traveling trauma specialist title, outreach-specialist commute rationale, 71% household-earner stakes, deeper identity architecture). ## 1. The Archetype (one line)

**Sarah Mitchell is the woman who runs the unit when everyone else is losing their shit. Trauma ICU charge nurse at the only Level I Trauma Center north of Phoenix. The person the hospital calls when a case is too hard for the other rural AZ facilities. The one calm voice on the floor at 3 AM when they code a 22-year-old who flipped his truck on I-17 — which is the exact stretch of road a Freightliner now left-turned her on, and she's the one on the gurney with a compound humerus and a piece of rebar where her calm used to be.**

## 2. Why She's the Right Archetype for the S1 Slot (the sourced rationale)

Five reasons, all load-bearing. If one falls, the archetype weakens.

1. **Professional credibility for the trucking-segment audience.** Trucking victims skew upper-working-class to middle-class professionals (commuters, tradespeople, medical/legal/real-estate pros driving freeways daily). Sarah being an **ICU trauma specialist at a Level I Trauma Center** gives her credibility with the exact audience the S1 hook needs to reach. She's not a "victim" character — she's a professional who processes information clinically and deserves the same respect. The creative's tone register flows from her.
2. **Female form-filler reach** (Creative brief rationale, 2026-04-22 casting summary). Non-motorcycle PI intake in AZ is filed 55–60% by female family members handling admin for injured relatives. A female protagonist reaches the decision-maker even when the injured party is male — Sarah's face is both *injured party* and *form-filler archetype* in a single character.
3. **Left-arm injury as career-ending threat.** ICU nurses are disproportionately left-hand-dominant (dexterity demands of IV starts, ventilator manipulation, bedside code response). A left-arm compound fracture isn't "a bruise" — it's an existential threat to her ability to practice. Stakes read instantly on screen through the arm brace alone.
4. **Mixed white-Latina ethnicity optimizes AZ reach.** 51% white + 32% Hispanic AZ demographic; Sarah pulls both audiences without forcing a casting split. Creative brief locked this.
5. **David-vs-Goliath emotional architecture.** A single charge nurse vs. a Bakersfield logistics company with $1M+ commercial policy is the exact frame S1's hook activates. The asymmetry isn't abstract — it's her body on the pavement vs. their fleet of defense attorneys. That sells.

## 4. The Single-Minded Proposition (the "one thing")

> **Sarah knows exactly what a $1M commercial policy is worth, what the FMCSA log entries will show, and what a defense firm's first offer looks like — because she's spent fifteen years reading charts, dealing with adjusters, and talking to families whose loved ones ended up in her ICU after crashes just like this one. She doesn't need to be convinced that trucking is different. She needs someone who speaks that language back to her without condescending.**

This is the SMP. Every hook we write for S1 must trace to it. If a line of copy could sit comfortably next to *"Truck accidents are not car accidents. The lawyer should be different,"* it's on-strategy. If it sounds like late-night billboard lawyer energy — sympathetic, urgent, emotional, vague — it's off-strategy. *Even if it would perform*.

---

## 5. The Core Insight (the strategic money)

**Sarah's lifelong professional identity is built on one foundation: she is the person who stays calm in other people's emergencies. Now she IS the emergency — and the people around her (family, friends, her coworkers at Flagstaff Medical Center) don't know how to treat her as the patient because they've only ever known her as the rock.** She feels the role-reversal acutely. She's not comfortable needing help. She's not comfortable being pitied. She's not comfortable not being the one in charge.

**This is the lever.** A PI firm that treats Sarah like *another competent professional with a problem she's hiring them to solve* — not like a victim, not like a case number, not like a sympathetic recipient of their heroic advocacy — wins her over in one call. Every other firm in the I-10 billboard belt is doing the opposite. That's the opening.

## 6. The Five Campaign Implications

Details in `05_Campaign_Implications.md`. Top line:

1. **Copy register is clinical, specific, and adult.** Names the trucking-industry vocabulary (commercial policies, FMCSA, driver logs, maintenance records) without over-explaining. Treats her as the expert she is. No "fight for your rights" — she'd cringe.
2. **Numbers only when they're verifiable and specific.** "$1M+ commercial policies" (true for Class 8 trucks) works. "Get millions!" fails. "Free case review" works. "$50M recovered!" fails.
3. **No emotional manipulation.** Sad music, slow-motion crash reconstructions, crying families → Sarah scrolls past instantly. Her professional training is to tune out theatrical emotion and process clinical fact.
4. **The I-17 corridor (not I-10) is her geographic identity.** Hook and visual must honor this. Sunset Point descent between Flagstaff and Camp Verde is where it happened — same stretch where most real AZ trucking fatalities occur annually.
5. **Left-arm brace is the single most important visual ID.** Every frame must show it. The arm is the career. The brace is the career-on-hold. If the brace is missing, the creative loses its primary emotional signal.

## 7. The Voice Test (use this before shipping any Sarah creative)

Three questions. 3/3 = ship. 2/3 = revise. 1/3 = wrong audience.

1. **Would Sarah read this line and nod?** Not "would a generic injured person" — specifically *would Sarah, a 38-year-old ICU charge nurse who's seen every PI billboard on I-17 for the last decade, read this line and think "okay, this person actually understands"?*
2. **Would Sarah text this to her ICU colleague group chat** with a "what do you think?" — does it read as something a clinical professional respects, rather than late-night-TV-lawyer energy that would embarrass her to share?
3. **Does it name the asymmetry?** Does it signal that WCTL understands commercial insurance is different from personal auto — without over-explaining, without lecturing, without condescension?

## 8. Why This Persona Matters for the Overall Campaign (Binet's lens)

**Trucking is the highest-case-value segment in Phase 1** — $90K+ average case, $1M+ policy ceilings (Evidence Register §3.3, §9). One signed trucking case generates 20× the revenue of one MVA case. **The persona depth on Sarah compounds directly into ROAS.**

Binet's 60/40 frame: volume segments reward short-term activation; premium segments reward long-term brand work. Sarah's creative is a **brand-response hybrid** — it has to filter hard (situation-specific hook) AND build long-term trust (clinical register, credibility signals, firm name recall). The Evidence Register has the trucking segment at 91 non-soft-tissue incidents/month — we don't need massive volume here. We need the *right* leads to sign. Sarah's persona depth is exactly that job.
