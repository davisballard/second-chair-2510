## 1. Sarah's Category Priors (what she ALREADY thinks about PI lawyers before our ad hits)

Shotton's key point: **the ad doesn't write on a blank page.** For Sarah, the page is unusually full.

**What "PI lawyer" means to Sarah (based on 15 years of professional adjacency):**

1. **She's watched the category from inside the hospital.** She's been the ICU nurse at the bedside when a family was approached about "hiring a lawyer." She's seen the firms that show up (usually via ER social workers' referral lists), the ones that don't return calls, the ones that sign and disappear for 18 months, the ones that actually communicate. She has a *calibrated* sense of firm quality from professional exposure.
2. **The Phoenix billboard firms are already coded in her head as "volume mills."** She's seen them on I-17 for 15 years. She's seen ICU patients discharged with engagement letters from them and then 6 months later the families complaining that nobody's returning their calls. **This is a significant competitive moat she brings into the decision.** It protects her from the worst firms. It also means any firm that pattern-matches to billboard aesthetic is dead on arrival.
3. **She's been approached by plaintiff's-side investigators.** When she was a resident RN at 25, she gave a statement to a plaintiff's firm about a trucking-accident admission. She remembers the investigator as competent and respectful. She remembers the lawyer (who she met briefly at deposition) as professional. **She has a positive reference-class for good plaintiff's-side lawyers.** This is rare in her demographic.
4. **She does NOT assume all PI lawyers are sharks.** She's more nuanced than Ty. She understands the adversarial structure. She understands the contingency model (she's explained it to patients' families). She understands the tradeoff between firm size and personal attention.
5. **She DOES assume most PI lawyers marketing on Meta are lower-tier.** Her priors: *the firms that are professionally competent don't need to spend on Meta ads because they get referrals from the hospital social workers and existing clients.* **This is a structural challenge for WCTL.** Our ad's first job is to *break the "if they're advertising on Meta, they're not a real firm" prior.*

### What Sarah thinks a GOOD trucking lawyer looks like (from her professional exposure):

- **Specialist.** Knows the FMCSA. Knows vicarious liability for logistics companies. Knows the difference between a trailer owner and a tractor owner.
- **Senior.** Been doing this 10+ years. Not an associate at a volume firm.
- **Communicates clinically.** Sends actual updates, not automated "we're fighting for you" emails.
- **Transparent on fees.** Explains the contingency breakdown (firm %, case costs, medical lien negotiations) on the front end.
- **Small enough that her case matters.** Not a firm where she's one of 400 active matters.
- **Phoenix-based or has Phoenix representation.** She's in Flagstaff; her case is in Maricopa County (where the crash occurred and Banner-Phoenix treated her). She needs local presence.

**This is an enormously specific mental model.** Most PI firms won't pattern-match to it. WCTL has to — or fail.

### What Sarah thinks PI lawyers DO (her working mental model — mostly accurate, some gaps):

**Accurate:**
- File suit or negotiate settlement on behalf of plaintiff
- Take contingency fee (she knows ~33% + costs is standard)
- Handle insurance negotiations; she won't have to talk to the adjuster
- Retain experts (medical, economic, accident reconstruction)
- Typically resolve cases in 12–24 months for non-litigated, longer if trial

**Gaps:**
- She doesn't know the details of AZ-specific pure comparative negligence (though she'd pick it up fast if explained)
- She doesn't know what a "policy limit demand" or "bad faith claim" is — but she'd understand both instantly if named
- She doesn't know about underinsured motorist stacking (which doesn't apply here — commercial policies are robust — but shows her gap level)

**The landing page + intake can speak up to her level** — it doesn't have to educate from zero. It has to *meet* her fluency and extend it.

## 2. The Decision Journey (Sarah's 3-stage path to hiring)

Mapped against awareness → consideration → decision. Significantly different from Ty's.

### Stage 1 — Awareness (Weeks 1–4 post-crash)

**What's happening:** Sarah is at Banner-Phoenix ICU, then discharged to Flagstaff, then at home on external fixator. She's NOT looking for a lawyer actively. She's healing.

**What's she seeing:**
- Meta feed starts serving PI ads by Week 3 (algo detected her Google search for "commercial trucking insurance arizona" from Week 2; plus her hospital geo-data via Banner-Phoenix location). Mostly billboard firms she auto-scrolls.
- Facebook comments from her nursing group ("so sorry to hear..." etc.) — NOT lawyer referrals (her colleagues respect her enough to not be pushy).
- A supplementary ad pool: AARP-adjacent disability insurance ads, medical billing advocacy services. Noise.

**Barriers at awareness:**
- **Pattern blindness (PI variant).** She's seen PI ads on I-17 billboards for 15 years; she's developed selective inattention. Our ad has to break the pattern *professionally*, not aggressively.
- **Dismissive-of-Meta-marketing bias.** Her prior: serious firms don't need to advertise on Meta. She's auto-deprioritizing anything in her Meta feed. **Counter-move:** the ad's production value and copy sophistication have to overcome this bias in 2 seconds.

**What moves her forward:** An ad that *doesn't pattern-match to billboard PI aesthetic.* Clinical register + specific to trucking + visual documentary realism = survives her filter. **This is S1's job.** One ad out of 100 survives.

### Stage 2 — Consideration (Weeks 4–12, peak Week 8–10)

**What's happening:** She's hobbling. Working admin-light. Bills have arrived in earnest. The $35K adjuster offer has sat for 6 weeks. She's mentally committed to "we need a lawyer" by Week 6–8. The question shifts from *if* to *who.*

**What she's doing:**
- **Googling systematically.** Searches: *"commercial trucking lawyer arizona," "trucking accident attorney phoenix," "arizona commercial trucking insurance policy limits," "semi truck accident lawyer no fee."* She opens 8–12 firm websites per session and closes most in under 20 seconds.
- **Asking professionally.** She DMs a former NAU classmate who now works at a medical malpractice firm asking for a trucking PI referral. She asks the Banner-Phoenix hospital social worker (who she knows from case collaborations). Both give her 1–2 firms to research.
- **Reading reviews.** Google reviews, Yelp (less), Avvo (she knows about this — unusual for laypeople but not for medical professionals who're aware of physician-rating sites). She weights Avvo higher than Google reviews.
- **Comparing fee structures.** Sarah is the kind of person who Excel-compares 33% vs. 35% vs. 40% contingency splits. She DOES this.

**Barriers at consideration:**
- **Choice overload, enhanced by analysis-paralysis tendency.** Sarah will compare 5 firms in her head; narrowing to one is hard for her. The firm that *presents its differentiation most clearly in the first 30 seconds of a consultation call* wins against the others.
- **Distance anxiety.** Flagstaff-Phoenix split: she lives in Flagstaff, her case is in Phoenix. She worries about managing the case at distance. Firms that explicitly address "we handle AZ-wide cases; you don't need to come to our office" reduce this barrier.
- **Fee transparency demand.** If a firm is cagey about fees on the phone or landing page, Sarah's done. She expects the same transparency she'd give a patient's family about a procedure's cost.

**What moves her forward:** A frictionless path to a *quality conversation* with a real person at the firm. Not phone tag. Not "we'll call you back." A calendar-scheduled, intake-specialist conversation with someone who can answer her Level 2 questions (policy limits, vicarious liability, typical timeline).

### Stage 3 — Decision (Weeks 12–20)

**What's happening:** She's had 1–3 consultations. She's narrowing.

**What flips her from consideration to decision:**
- The attorney or intake specialist who **asks the RIGHT specific questions about her case** — "What's the Freightliner's DOT number? Do you have the traffic collision report? Was there a fatality-threshold cost analysis?" — signals specialist-tier knowledge. A generalist won't know to ask.
- A clear plan-of-action on call 1: "Here's what I'll do this week, here's what you'll do this week, here's what I expect we'll have by next Friday." Sarah's brain craves structure.
- **No pressure to sign on call 1.** She needs to talk to Marcus. She'll sleep on it. A firm that pressures loses her.
- **The engagement letter is plain-language.** 12 pages of fine print in Times New Roman will kill her conversion. A 4-page letter with clear sections + a fee calculator example = she signs.
- Transparent handling of her Banner-Phoenix medical lien: she knows what a medical lien is (hospitals file liens on PI settlements to recover unpaid treatment costs; her case at Banner generated at least one). A firm that proactively explains lien negotiation as part of their service = massive signal.

**Barriers at decision:**
- **Sign-anxiety (clinical variant).** She reads contracts professionally. She'll see red flags that a layperson wouldn't. Poor drafting kills.
- **Fee-calculator anxiety.** Even with transparent fees, seeing "33% of gross" on a $500K hypothetical settlement triggers the same sticker shock a patient has when they see a hospital bill. The intake specialist should pre-frame: "Our fee includes all case costs up through trial. You don't pay anything out of pocket. Recovery is the only trigger for our compensation."

## 3. Adam Ferrier's Trigger Moments (Sarah-specific)

Ranked by conversion weight:

1. **Day 19: Second adjuster call with $35K anchor repeated.** *"We can close this out today."* Sarah hears the implied urgency and recognizes it for what it is. **This is the cognitive moment where she decides she needs a lawyer.**
2. **Week 6: Second surgery (internal fixation).** Post-op physical therapy begins in earnest. She realizes the recovery is 4–6 months, not 4–6 weeks. **This is when the timeline reality hits.**
3. **Week 8–10: Third and fourth bills arrive.** Banner-Phoenix ER, Banner-Phoenix surgical, orthopedic, PT. Cumulative medical debt visibility exceeds $60K. **She Googles.**
4. **Week 9: Karen (her sister) gently raises the question** — *"have you talked to a lawyer yet?"* Karen's husband is an accountant; he'd asked Karen to ask. **Sarah takes family-weighted input seriously.**
5. **Week 12: Return to work on light duty at 60% pay.** The income reality lands. **Activation shifts from "we'll get around to it" to "we need to hire someone this month."**

**Campaign activation window:** Weeks 6–16 post-crash. Earliest reach = Week 6; deepest engagement = Week 8–12; latest save = Week 14–16. **Outside this window, she's either lost (accepted the $35K) or already hired.**

**WCTL's job: beat options 2–5 decisively via creative + intake + first-call impression.** The $35K (option 6) is the *time-based competitor* — every week that passes without WCTL signing her is a week closer to her defaulting to it.

## 5. Creative Implications (preview for `05_Campaign_Implications.md`)

- **Opening frame must break PI-ad pattern in < 1 second** — specifically the billboard aesthetic. Clinical register, not urgency-aggressive.
- **"Commercial policies carry $1M+" must be in the first 3 seconds of S1.** This is her pattern-match for specialist knowledge. Without it, she scrolls.
- **"No fee unless we win" must be above-the-fold** — lower stakes than for Ty (Sarah understands contingency) but still load-bearing.
- **Landing page must respect her medical-legal fluency.** Don't over-explain. Don't under-signal. Use plain language at a slightly elevated vocabulary level.
- **Intake must demonstrate specialty on first touch.** Ask about the Freightliner. Ask about FMCSA logs. Show she's not talking to a generalist.
- **Assume Marcus reads and weighs in — but Sarah is the primary decision-owner.** Unlike Ty's Rachel-adjacent pattern, Sarah drives this call.
- **Address the $35K anchor in Week 6+ ads directly.** "The first offer is rarely the fair offer" type language. Sarah will understand.
- **Phoenix-based firm signaling matters.** She needs reassurance the firm can operate her case at distance. WCTL's Phoenix presence is a selling point, not a detail.
