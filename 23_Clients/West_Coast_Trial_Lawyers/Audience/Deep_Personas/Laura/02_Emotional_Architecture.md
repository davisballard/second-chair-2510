## Jon Steel's Deprivation Frame — The Five Losses

Laura's deprivation architecture is different from the other three personas. Hers is the only one centered on **PLACE, not body or career or household system.** Ty lost his identity-as-rider. Sarah lost her professional-competence-equilibrium. Diana lost her household-operating-system. **Laura lost her street.** This uniqueness is strategic — it gives Meta's algo a fourth distinct audience profile to identify.

### Loss 1 — The Walk (the 15-year ritual)

Laura has walked the same three-mile morning loop through her Gilbert neighborhood for **15 years**. Not "often." Not "most days." **Every day.** Over 5,400 walks recorded in her Apple Watch. Through pregnancies, through her father's death, through a cancer scare, through every season Gilbert has ever had. **The walk was the most stable thing in her life.**

The loss is not "I can't exercise." The loss is ritual. Structure. The specific emotional function of beginning each day by *claiming her neighborhood through movement*. Without the walk, her morning starts in a void. **She wakes up at 5:45 AM reflexively — the alarm that was set for 15 years — and then there's nothing to do.** Maggie the dog is still there waiting. Caleb has been walking Maggie for her. But the walk isn't Laura's anymore.

### Loss 2 — THE STOLEN NEIGHBORHOOD (the Steel front-line — Laura's unique emotional vector)

**This is the lever. This is what the S2 creative speaks to.**

Laura didn't just lose her morning walk. She lost her *relationship with her street.* For 15 years, Gilbert was Laura's neighborhood in a specific sense — she knew every block, every dog, every family, every HOA violation, every seasonal change. **The quiet contract was: I walk here every morning, and in exchange, I know this place, and in exchange, this place is safe because I've made it mine through repetition.**

**The contract is void.** A driver making a right-turn-on-red at Gilbert & Warner — an intersection Laura had crossed hundreds of times, walking or driving, over 15 years — didn't see her in the crosswalk. The *ordinary* became unsafe. The *familiar* became a trigger.

When Laura is cleared to walk again — in 4–6 months — she will face a question no one else in her family faces: **will she walk the same route?** If yes, she passes the crash site every day. If no, she has to invent a new relationship with Gilbert from scratch, at 42, after 15 years of autopilot. Both options are losses. Both are grief.

**What makes this loss distinctive for creative:**
- It's *place-specific,* not body-specific or economic
- Most PI creative doesn't recognize place-loss as a legitimate injury category
- Women who walk their neighborhoods as identity-ritual (not workout) will recognize it *viscerally*
- The S2 static image — Laura at the crosswalk, looking — holds this loss without needing to explain it

**Steel's translation principle:** Don't name "the stolen neighborhood" in copy. Let the visual carry it. The hook says "Hit while crossing the street?" — the filter. The image says *the street you've crossed ten thousand times is no longer yours* — the emotional payload. The viewer completes it.

### Loss 3 — The Walking Identity (who-she-was)

For 15 years, Laura was "the woman who walks the neighborhood." Neighbors recognized her. New residents learned her name because she'd wave as she walked past. Her kids' friends at Highland Elementary would see her walking and say "there's Mrs. Henderson, she's the walking lady." **Her physical presence in the neighborhood was how people knew her.**

Post-crash, she's the woman on the porch. She's the woman on the couch. The neighbors have been kind — cards, casseroles, a "we're so sorry" committee has formed via Amanda's Gilbert Mom Crew grapevine. But **the identity of "walking Laura" is already being quietly retired** by the neighborhood's muscle memory. She can feel it. No one's rude. But she's being re-cast in a role she didn't audition for: "the woman who was hit at Gilbert and Warner."

### Loss 4 — The Body-Trust (the sudden fragility at 42)

Laura's 42. Until the crash, she thought of her body as fundamentally reliable. She walked 3 miles every day. She had energy. She could carry Olivia's heavy band instrument home from school when needed. Her body was a tool.

Now she can't put weight on her right leg for 6 weeks. The surgery was more invasive than she expected — the hardware is visible on X-rays, plates and screws. The PT is slow. Her right ankle might never be 100%. At 42, she's facing a question women in their mid-40s increasingly face: **was this the moment my body stopped being reliable?**

She hasn't said this to Mike. She hasn't said it to Amanda. She's thought it at 3 AM more than once.

### Loss 5 — The Commute to Work (and thus, a slice of her professional identity)

Laura walked to Highland Elementary 2–3×/week when weather allowed. It wasn't just exercise — it was her transition from *home-Laura* to *work-Laura.* The 25-minute walk let her review her library-day plan, rehearse her lunch-book-club pitch for the 4th graders, draft an email in her head to the PTA.

Post-crash, she can't walk to work. When she eventually returns to Highland Elementary (probably August 2026, after summer break, 4 months post-crash), she'll drive. **She's lost the transitional ritual that helped her be good at her job.** Not a dramatic loss. But a quiet, load-bearing one for her sense of professional flow.

## What She Wants (Explicit — ranked by conscious articulation)

1. **To walk again.** This is her top-line wish. She won't articulate this as a *legal goal* because it's a medical one, but it's the underlying emotional driver behind everything else.
2. **The medical bills covered.** She knows the surgery alone was $18K. She knows her OOP max is ~$6K. She'd like to not drag the household emergency fund through a crash she didn't cause.
3. **Some acknowledgment from the insurance company that this matters.** Not a specific dollar amount — a *tone shift.* Ryan Perez's insurance adjuster is polite but transactional. Laura wants to feel seen, not processed.
4. **Her job secure for August 2026.** Her principal has been supportive ("take the time you need"). She wants this to translate to actual job protection.
5. **Not to dread her own neighborhood.** This is the one she can't consciously articulate, but it's the deepest want.

## What She Won't Say Out Loud (The Shadow)

Steel's rule: the payload reaches the thing that isn't said.

- **She's afraid she'll never walk the old route again.** Not physically — psychologically. She's thought about driving past Gilbert & Warner at dusk and seeing if she feels okay. She hasn't told Mike about this thought experiment because naming it feels like admitting a fear.
- **She feels guilty about taking up her family's attention.** Caleb walking Maggie every morning. Olivia sitting on the couch next to her. Mike doing all the cooking. She doesn't want to be the center of the family's gravity; she wants to be *peripheral,* back to her block-captain-neighborhood-walker role.
- **She's jealous of Amanda** (her best friend, who hasn't been hit by a car, and who casually mentioned "my walking buddy back" in a text last week, referring to her usual walking partner — a friend Laura hadn't met). **Jealousy is an unusual feeling for Laura. It's unsettling.**
- **She's scared the driver's insurance company will lowball her.** She's read enough of Amanda's Facebook group threads about "insurance companies being terrible after crashes" to know this is a real concern. She doesn't know what the math should be — what's a fair settlement for a tibial plateau fracture with surgery + psychological-place-loss that doesn't fit in an actuarial table?
- **She's worried about Maggie's remaining time.** Maggie is 9. Golden retrievers don't live forever. If Laura can't walk with Maggie anymore, and Maggie has maybe 3 years left — those 3 years will be Maggie's last, and Laura will have missed them. This is the quietest, most Steel-level loss under the whole stack.
- **She's angry at the driver** — Ryan Perez, 29, the young man from the Gilbert auto dealership — in a way that's too uncomplicated to voice comfortably. She doesn't want to *hate* him. But she also thinks about him, and she hopes it hurts.
- **She's embarrassed to be "the lady who got hit."** She spent 15 years being the neighborhood walker. That identity is gone. She's scared the new identity — "crash victim" — is the one that sticks.

## The Emotional Arc (Timeline — different from MVA personas because pedestrian grief is mobility-dominant)

### Week 1 — Hospital / surgery / early recovery

- **Day of crash (Friday evening):** 6:30 PM crash at Gilbert & Warner. Ambulance to Banner Gateway. ER. Imaging shows tibial plateau fracture. Admitted. Surgery scheduled for Saturday morning.
- **Day 1 (Saturday):** surgery. Successful ORIF (open reduction internal fixation). Laura awakes post-surgery to Mike + Caleb + Olivia + her mom Karen in the recovery room. Caleb is holding Maggie's leash (Maggie stayed with Karen that night; she's allowed in the hospital parking lot, not the room).
- **Day 2:** discharge home with crutches, pain meds, detailed instructions. Non-weight-bearing for 6 weeks. Laura's first evening home is Sunday. She sleeps on the couch because stairs are impossible.
- **Days 3–7:** acute-recovery mode. Visitors (her cousin Emily from 6 houses down, her mom Karen from Mesa, Mike's sister from Phoenix). Flower deliveries. Get-well cards from Highland Elementary colleagues + her PTA board + cousin Amanda. **She hasn't yet processed any emotional layer beyond "ow."**

### Week 2–4 — The stillness lands

- **Week 2:** pain manageable with OTC NSAIDs + occasional oxycodone. Laura is mentally clear enough to read, watch TV, handle emails one-handed. Caleb is walking Maggie every morning at 6 AM — a quiet ritual he adopted without being asked. **Laura cries privately for the first time one morning when she hears the front door click as Caleb leaves with Maggie.** It hits her: that was her morning. It's his now.
- **Week 3:** the adjuster from State Farm calls. Ryan Perez's insurance wants to settle. Initial offer: **$22,000** (medical costs + some pain and suffering). Laura tells the adjuster she'll review and respond. She hangs up and doesn't know what to think.
- **Week 4:** she tries walking to the front door and back using her crutches. The distance nearly defeats her. She sits on the sectional for the rest of the day, looking out the window at the cul-de-sac. **This is the emotional trough.** The gap between who she was and who she is today is widest here.

### Month 2–3 — Reckoning + decision window

- **Week 5–6:** transition from crutches to cane, starting to bear weight gradually. Her PT is at Week 4 and starting to see progress. She walks from the family room to the kitchen island (6 feet) without the crutches one afternoon. This feels like a victory bigger than the surgery was.
- **Week 7:** first PTA meeting she attends (virtual — she doesn't drive yet). The other board members spend 10 minutes asking her how she is. It's warm. It's also clearly going to be the tone of every interaction for the next 6 months. She's not "Laura the block captain" anymore. She's "Laura recovering."
- **Week 8–10:** **the decision window.** She's walking with a cane. She's cleared to drive again soon. Mike has been saying "we should talk to a lawyer about the insurance thing" since Week 3. She's been putting it off. Around Week 8, she starts Googling.

### Month 4–6 — Return and reinvention

- **Week 12–16:** back at work (probably August 2026 start of school year). Walking with a cane initially, may transition to cane-free by October. She'll need accommodations at Highland Elementary (probably a roll-able book cart, help with book fair setup).
- **Month 4–6:** **the route question.** Her PT will clear her for outdoor walking (short distances first) around Month 4. She has to decide: walk the old route? A new route? Not walk outside at all?
- **The legal decision landing zone:** if she hired a lawyer in the Month 2–3 window, the case is in negotiation by Month 6 — probably a year to resolve. If she didn't hire and accepted an adjuster offer ($22K → maybe $35K escalation), she's "done" by Month 3 but quietly regrets it for years.

## Adam Ferrier's Motivation Architecture — COM-B Map (Laura-specific)

**Capability (know-how):**
- ✅ *Physical:* she can complete a quiz on her phone one-handed even with recovery-mode limits. No concussion-level cognitive restrictions (her Grade 2 concussion resolved around Week 2).
- ⚠️ *Psychological:* Laura is mid-fluent on insurance/legal (more than Ty, less than Sarah). She's the PTA Treasurer — she handles budgets + basic financial admin. She understands contingency at a basic level. She doesn't know AZ-specific PI details. **The landing page should meet her at "you probably understand X; here's Y you might not know."**

**Opportunity (environment):**
- ✅ *Physical:* mobile 4–8 hours/day in recovery mode. No attention-fatigue limits like Diana's concussion.
- ⚠️ *Social:* Mike is a co-decision-maker but deferent — he'd defer to Laura's judgment on which firm. **Different from Diana's more-active-Ben.** Laura mostly drives this decision.
- ⚠️ *Environmental:* peak tap-through is evening after dinner (8–10 PM) and occasionally Saturday morning. **Less insomnia than Diana** (no concussion-related sleep disruption after Week 2).

**Motivation:**
- ⚠️ *Reflective (planned):* She knows she needs a lawyer — or at least to talk to one. She's been procrastinating out of general conflict-aversion.
- ⚠️ *Automatic (emotional):* The $22K insurance offer activated mild irritation but not rage. She's emotionally processing the crash itself more than the insurance math. **Different from Sarah's clinical-anger or Diana's analytical-irritation.**
- ✅ *Activation lever:* specific. A creative that acknowledges her *place-loss* (not just her body-loss) gets her attention in a way that generic PI creative doesn't.

## Richard Shotton's Cognitive Barriers (Laura-specific)

Five biases working against Laura converting:

1. **Conflict aversion.** Laura is a *school librarian + neighborhood block captain* — her entire identity is built on being a peacemaker. Hiring a lawyer feels conflict-forward to her (sue someone? litigate?). The creative + intake must REFRAME legal action as *accountability* and *documented facts,* not as combat. "Finding out what the law says" works for her; "fighting for your rights" fails her.
2. **Fairness-default bias.** Laura assumes most systems work fairly unless proven otherwise. She half-assumes the insurance offer will be fair if she waits long enough. **The creative must quietly signal that the adjuster's offer is statistically low without asking her to adopt a combative posture.**
3. **The "don't make a fuss" suburban-politeness inhibition.** Gilbert-suburb PTA-mom social conditioning: don't complain, don't sue, don't be the neighbor who made a big deal of it. This is a real cultural pressure. Creative tone must never read as "make them pay!" — it reads as *make sure it's handled properly.*
4. **Age-42 "I should handle my own admin" competence bias.** Similar to Diana's "I can handle this myself" — Laura's a PTA Treasurer, she runs spreadsheets. She'll initially think she can negotiate with Ryan's insurance adjuster herself. The creative must signal *why a lawyer adds value for someone who's handled a thousand admin conversations before* without condescending.
5. **Place-trauma avoidance.** Laura doesn't want to re-visit the Gilbert & Warner intersection emotionally. If the creative leans too hard on crash-site imagery, she may avoid it. The S2 dusk-crosswalk frame is a calibrated risk — it's her place, but NOT the impact moment. Keep that line sharp.

## The Identity Frame

Laura's self-image, in three sentences she'd never say aloud but would recognize as true:

> I'm the woman who walks her neighborhood. I know this block. I know which dogs bark and which don't. I've been the quiet custodian of my street for 15 years — not in a showy way, just through repetition and attention. And until April, I thought my place in this neighborhood was something I'd earned permanently through walking it. Now I'm not sure I own any of it anymore.

**The creative's job:** speak to that woman. Not the aspirational-walking-Laura, not the victim-Laura, not the cartoon-suburban-mom. The actual woman — mid-reckoning, neighborhood watching her with quiet sympathy, her identity quietly migrating from "walker" to "former walker" without her permission.

## Pitch-Language Summary (for `05_Campaign_Implications.md` to pull from)

- **Primary emotional engine:** THE STOLEN NEIGHBORHOOD (Loss 2). The creative visualizes the empty crosswalk, Laura standing at the curb, the ordinary place made unfamiliar. Never names "stolen neighborhood" in copy. Visual carries it.
- **Secondary engine:** the walking-identity loss (Loss 1 + 3). The specific texture of a 15-year ritual broken.
- **Tertiary engine:** the adjuster's $22K offer absurdity (softer than Sarah or Diana because Laura is slower-to-anger and financially cushioned). Laura's irritation about the offer is quiet, not loud.
- **Trigger moment:** Week 8–10 evening scroll, when PT progress has started and she's mentally re-engaging with the world but hasn't yet decided the legal-action question.
- **Language test:** *would Laura text this to her Gilbert Mom Crew group chat with a "this made me cry a little but in the good way" caption?* If yes, register is right. If it'd embarrass her to share (too combative, too pitying) — wrong.
- **Static-image discipline:** composition is 90% of the work. One frame, one hook, one emotional truth. Don't try to say too much.
