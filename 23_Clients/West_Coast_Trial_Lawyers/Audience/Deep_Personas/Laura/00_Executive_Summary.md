## Archetype Rationale (why Laura, why THIS version)

Laura was picked for the S2 slot — the broadest pedestrian English-language reach asset. S2 is a **static image creative** (not video) — meaning composition + a 5-word headline does 90% of the work, unlike S1/S5/M1/M2 where 15–30 seconds of scene-building carry the load. The current version is a **2026-04-23 tune** of Doc 2 + Creative brief Laura — same core archetype (42, white, suburban AZ, pedestrian-victim, walker-identity), stronger execution layer (Gilbert AZ specificity, elementary school librarian profession, Intel-engineer husband, 'The Stolen Neighborhood' surfaced as emotional front-line).

## 1. The Archetype (one line)

**Laura Henderson is the woman who, for fifteen years, walked the same three-mile loop through her Gilbert neighborhood every single morning — past the Johnson's Australian shepherd, past the elementary school crossing guard, past the corner house with the Halloween decorations that never come down — and knew every block, every dog, every family, every change. A driver making a right turn on red on her evening walk looked left for a gap in traffic, didn't see her in the crosswalk at Gilbert and Warner, and took the one thing Laura thought belonged permanently to her: the quiet ownership of her own street.**

## 2. Why She's the Right Archetype for the S2 Slot (sourced rationale)

Five reasons. Static-image creative has different strategic demands than video — composition + hook + persona must align tightly in a single frame.

1. **Pedestrian is the 3rd-highest volume case type after MVA and DUI-excluded.** 268 non-soft-tissue pedestrian incidents/month in AZ (Evidence Register §3.4). Substantial pool; broad English-language reach.
2. **Innocence filter is the hardest in the whole campaign.** "Hit while crossing the street" + "you weren't the one driving" — anyone in a vehicle at the moment of crash self-excludes instantly. Highest-filtering hook in Phase 1.
3. **Female form-filler reach** — 55–60% of AZ PI intake is filed by female family members (Creative brief rationale).
4. **The neighborhood-walker identity is strategically distinctive.** None of the other three personas have place-based identity as their emotional core. Ty is rider-identity. Sarah is professional-identity. Diana is household-system-identity. Laura is PLACE-identity. This differentiation gives Meta's algo a fourth distinct audience to find.
5. **Static image format matches her story.** A still frame — Laura standing at the corner where it happened, looking at the empty crosswalk — carries the emotional weight better than motion. The absence of her walking is the creative; a video of her walking (or not walking) would dilute it.

## 4. The Single-Minded Proposition (the "one thing")

> **Laura didn't lose her body — she got most of it back. She lost her relationship with her own street. For fifteen years she was the woman who walked this neighborhood; for the rest of her life, she's the woman who used to walk this neighborhood. The ordinary became unsafe. The familiar became a trigger. And there is no physical therapy for the loss of a place you thought belonged to you.**

This is the SMP. Every S2 design decision must trace to it. If a headline or visual treats Laura as "just another injured pedestrian" — generic, body-centered, recovery-focused — it's off-strategy. If it honors the place-specific loss without getting sentimental about it, it's on-strategy. The static format is a gift here: a single frame can hold place-loss in a way video struggles to.

---

## 5. The Core Insight (the strategic money)

**People who walk their neighborhoods for identity-reasons — not just for fitness, not as a hobby, but as a 15-year daily ritual that defines how they know where they live — experience pedestrian injury as a specific, under-acknowledged loss: the loss of civilian ownership of the place they've spent a decade-plus calling home.** This is not a loss most PI creative names because it doesn't pattern-match to "catastrophic injury" imagery. But for the Laura-type audience, it IS the injury.

The crash at Gilbert & Warner didn't just break her tibia. It broke the quiet, unspoken contract she had with her street: *I walk here every morning. I know this place. This place is safe because I've made it mine through repetition.* **That contract is now void.** And the PI firm that recognizes this specific loss — without forcing Laura to articulate it herself — wins her trust.

The static image creative is uniquely suited to this insight: a single frame of Laura at the crosswalk, looking at the empty painted crosswalk lines, can hold the *absence* of her daily walk in a way video-motion can't. **Stillness is the subject.**

## 6. The Five Campaign Implications

Details in `05_Campaign_Implications.md`. Top line:

1. **S2 composition lives at the intersection, not at the hospital.** The crosswalk is the set. Laura at the curb is the subject. The empty crosswalk markings are the co-star. NOT a hospital bed. NOT the crash moment. NOT a doctor's office. Per SC brand standard (no crash footage).
2. **Hook + visual co-author.** "Hit while crossing the street?" is the current lock (Creative brief). Works. Alternates in `05` §1 — all must honor the pedestrian-innocence filter.
3. **Copy register: warm, domestic, understated.** Laura is neither Ty's lay-rider voice nor Sarah's clinical voice nor Diana's mid-tier-professional voice. She's a school librarian + suburban mom. Her register is *neighborly*. Think NPR + church newsletter.
4. **Cane LEFT hand, ACE wrap RIGHT ankle, wedding band LEFT hand, kitchen scar LEFT forearm.** Every frame honors these continuity props. Lock.
5. **Elevated Documentary style locked.** Not UGC (that's Diana). The S2 frame should have the quiet compositional rigor of an Alec Soth / Nadav Kander portrait photograph. Dusk light + cyan fading sky + amber streetlight per the existing Creative brief.

## 7. The Voice Test (use this before shipping any Laura creative)

Three questions. 3/3 = ship. 2/3 = revise. 1/3 = wrong audience.

1. **Would Laura read this line and feel it in her neighborhood-specific way?** Not "would a generic suburban mom" — specifically *would Laura, the 42-year-old Gilbert school librarian who walked three miles every morning for fifteen years past the Johnson's Australian shepherd, read this and think "yes, they see what I actually lost"?*
2. **Would Laura share this with her Gilbert PTA group chat** with a *"this made me cry a little bit but in the good way"* caption? Does it read as quietly-true rather than performatively-sad?
3. **Does it honor the place-loss without being sentimental about it?** Does the creative let Laura's relationship with her street be the emotional subject — without over-explaining, without voice-over monologue, without forcing her to name it?

---

## 8. Why This Persona Matters for the Overall Campaign (static-image discipline)

**S2 is the only static image in Phase 1.** Every other creative (M1/M2/M3/S1/S5) is video. S2's job is unique: a single frame that stops the scroll AND filters hard AND carries the emotional weight AND signals WCTL specialist-tier-PI.

**Binet's 60/40 frame for static images:** static creative has less room for brand-building motion/narrative, so it tends to skew more toward short-term activation (70/30 activation-heavy). Laura's S2 design must:
- **Filter harder** (because one frame is the only chance to self-qualify the audience)
- **Hook sharper** (5 words must stop the scroll)
- **Activate specifically** (CTA must be crystal-clear in the single frame)

The persona's job is to give McCabe the specificity + register to write a hook that does all three in one line.
