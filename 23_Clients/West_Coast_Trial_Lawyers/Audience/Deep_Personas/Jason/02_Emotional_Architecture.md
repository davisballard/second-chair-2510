## Jon Steel's Deprivation Frame — The Six Losses

Jason's deprivation architecture is distinct from Diana's, Sarah's, and Ty's. Hers (Diana) is about the operational integrity of a household system. Hers (Sarah) is about professional credentialed identity. His (Ty) is about rider identity and the social presumption of fault. **Jason's is about the role inversion of a man whose entire adult life was built on a body that worked and a crew that listened — and who is now, for the first time in twenty-two years, the person being driven, lifted, and worked-around.** Losses, layer by layer:

### Loss 1 — Earning Capacity (the cliff in real time)

Jason is **81% of his household's income**. Kristy's $22K from the Mesa dental office covers groceries and that's it. Workers' comp at 66.7% kicks in only if Hardin's carrier accepts the MVA-on-the-way-home claim — which they're disputing. Without that, he's on his earned PTO (he had 9 days), then nothing.

The household math: $2,180 mortgage. $640 truck payment. $480 health insurance contribution. Kristy's Pilot at $390. Utilities, groceries, three kids' school costs, Tyler's car insurance now that he's driving. **About $5,400 a month before discretionary.** Kristy's $22K covers about $1,800 of that. The $14K emergency fund covers four months at the gap rate. **By Week 16 they're into the credit cards. By Week 20 they're having the 401K conversation Jason swore they'd never have.**

This is not Diana's invisible labor surfaced — *this is the man watching the cliff edge approach in real time and counting the days.* Jason can do this math in his head because he's been running concrete-pour margins for fifteen years. He runs the household budget the same way. He knows exactly when the savings run out.

**The specific texture of his earning-capacity loss:** he isn't worried about losing his career — he's worried about *next Friday's check.* Concrete foremen don't have salary continuity. The day labor stops, the income stops. **Twenty-two years of earned competence converted into an hour-by-hour spreadsheet of a runway disappearing.**

### Loss 2 — The Body That Worked (the asset he sold)

Pelvic fracture (lateral compression, type II). Left femur fracture, mid-shaft, surgical ORIF with intramedullary rod. Three rib fractures, left side (ribs 5–7). Grade 2 concussion. **Walker for five weeks. Cane for at least eight more.** PT three times a week. Surgery scar running along his left thigh. He sleeps in a recliner because the bed is too painful for the first three weeks.

He cannot kneel to set forms. He cannot squat to check rebar tie-offs. He cannot lift a 60-pound bag of cement, which he used to do without thinking. He cannot climb a ladder. He cannot stand on a slab for eight hours. **He cannot do any part of his job, and there is no light-duty office work at Hardin Concrete because Hardin is a concrete company.**

**The body was the only thing he ever sold and it has been taken.** Twenty-two years of physical accumulation — forearm strength, shoulder durability, knees that took the hit — and the asset has been compromised in one second by a guy on a phone. He doesn't know yet how much of it comes back. The orthopedic surgeon said "most patients return to full function within nine months." Jason heard *most.* He has not slept on that word.

### Loss 3 — Crew Authority (the role moving on without him)

Jason's crew is six to eight men. He's run them for nine years. They listen to him; they call him boss; they want him back. **By Week 4, Hardin has promoted Marco — Jason's second, the 31-year-old he himself elevated three years ago — to acting foreman.** Marco is running *Jason's* crew. The Hardin office told Jason it was temporary. Jason knows what temporary means in this business.

The texts come in. Marco asks for advice on a tricky Queen Creek pour. Jason answers. Then Marco stops asking, because Marco is figuring it out. **By Week 6, the crew is running fine without him.** This is what every foreman secretly fears and works against and prepares his second to be capable of. Jason did the work *exactly right* — and the consequence of that work is that the hierarchy that defined him is moving on without him from a recliner.

**The specific texture:** he is proud of Marco, and threatened by Marco, at the same moment. Both are real. Neither cancels the other. Steel's rule: *don't choose sides in the creative.* The duality is the lever.

### Loss 4 — Father-Provider Identity (the man his sons saw)

Tyler is 16. Tyler asks at Week 3 whether the family is going to do the spring break trip to Sedona — they always do; it's the trip; the photo from two years ago is on the wall. Jason has to tell him no. **Tyler sees something in his dad's face that 16-year-old boys aren't supposed to see yet.** Tyler doesn't push it. Tyler goes to football practice the next morning, even though Jason isn't going to make it to the home game on Friday.

Caleb is 9. Caleb used to ask Jason, every weekend, to throw the football in the yard. **By Week 3, Caleb stops asking.** He hasn't said why; he just stops. Jason can see Caleb walk past the recliner with the football tucked under his arm, looking at the sliding door, and not opening it. **Caleb is protecting Jason. A nine-year-old is protecting his father.** Jason has no category for that.

Madison is 13. Madison surprises him by getting closer — she sits next to him on the couch one evening, scrolling her phone, in a way she hasn't done since she was 10. **Jason is grateful and devastated at once.** He doesn't want her getting close to him because he's small. He wants her getting close to him because he's her dad. He can't tell which is happening.

**He used to be the man his sons looked up to. Now he's the man they step quietly around.** This is not a performable loss; it's a watched loss. The creative's emotional payload reaches it without naming it.

### Loss 5 — The Marital Order (eighteen years inverted)

Kristy goes from part-time bookkeeper to: the one who drives him to PT, picks up his prescriptions, schedules his ortho follow-ups, manages the insurance calls, helps him into the shower for the first three weeks, sits next to him at the dental-office bookkeeping job on her lunch break to help her navigate a Hardin workers'-comp form, and now she is **tired in a way he has never seen her**.

Jason has spent 18 years being the strong one. **He doesn't have a category for being on the receiving end of his wife's strength, and neither does she.** They are a team, and the team is functioning, but the geometry is wrong. Every couple's marriage runs on an unstated division of labor; theirs ran on his physical and economic provision and her household-keel. The crash inverted half of that.

**He has not said *thank you* enough.** He doesn't know how to say it without it sounding like a guy who suddenly noticed his wife. He used to know how to say it pre-crash, casually, in a hundred small ways. The vocabulary has gone weird.

**Steel's note:** Kristy is not unhappy. She is *carrying the marriage*, and they both know it, and neither has named it. The campaign creative should never reference the marriage directly. The audience member who identifies with Jason feels this dimension intuitively when the visual is right. (See `01_Life_World.md` §10 visual anchor 12 — "The kitchen counter in the morning, Kristy seen from behind.")

### Loss 6 — The Future He Was Building (the death of the plan)

They were $11,000 from paying off the F-250. They were 14 months from Tyler's college decision (Tyler isn't sure he wants to go; Jason wasn't going to push, but had quietly set aside a small fund). They were three years from a moment Jason had been quietly running the math on: **partnering with Pete to start their own outfit. Reardon & Sons, eventually.** Pete had pulled the truck-up to a Modelo at the back-patio firepit one night last August and said *"three years out, brother."* Jason had nodded.

**The first adjuster offer arrives Day 8.** Hyundai Tucson driver's carrier — AZ minimum policy. **$38,000 to close.** It doesn't even cover the $84K hospital bill that hit the kitchen counter the same week. It doesn't acknowledge the surgery, the rod, the ribs, the concussion, the six months of lost work, the disputed workers' comp, the F-250 totaled, the truck payment still due, the future.

**Jason looks at the $38K and sees the death of the plan.** Not because the number is small — though it is — but because the number is *what they think he's worth*. A guy looking at his phone ran a red light, took out a foreman, and the system has assessed the loss at thirty-eight thousand dollars. **The plan to pay off the truck. The plan to start the outfit with Pete. The plan to be the dad who paid for whatever Tyler decided. All of it.** Thirty-eight thousand dollars.

**This is the activating insult.** Steel's rule for the campaign creative: *the lowball isn't a number, it's a verdict.* The hook discipline traces here.

## What He Wants (Explicit — ranked by how consciously he'd name each)

1. **The hospital bill handled.** $84,000 stamped Banner Gateway Medical Center. He reads it as what he owes — Shotton will deal with that error in §3 of this doc. He wants the bill handled before the next one arrives.
2. **The body all the way back.** He won't say this fear out loud. It's primary.
3. **A check that lets him not panic about the mortgage for six months.** Not a windfall. *Runway.* Same word Diana would use, different texture. Jason's runway is mortgage + truck + kids + insurance, not invisible household admin.
4. **A specialist he doesn't have to manage.** He has zero bandwidth for a "fight for your rights" process. He wants someone competent to handle it and tell him what's needed and when. **The way he'd hire an electrician for a panel he doesn't touch.**
5. **Kristy to not have to keep carrying this.** He won't say it. But it's in the top 5.
6. **The $38K to feel like an insult he doesn't have to swallow.** Reframed: he wants to be *able to refuse the lowball* without running the family into the ground. That's the lawyer's job.

## What He Won't Say Out Loud (The Shadow)

**Steel's rule: the payload reaches the thing that isn't said.**

- **He's terrified Marco is better than him.** Not just a capable second, but actually better. He has watched Marco run the crew over text for six weeks. Some of the calls Marco made were ones Jason wouldn't have made — and the work came out fine. Maybe better. He doesn't know what to do with that.
- **He's terrified Kristy will figure out she's been carrying him for eight weeks.** Not that she'd resent it — she wouldn't — but that *she'd see herself differently afterwards*. That she'd realize she could carry the household alone if she had to, and would never quite see him the same way.
- **He's terrified Tyler saw something in his face he can't take back.** The Sedona moment. The Friday night football game Jason missed. The day Tyler had to drive him home from PT because Kristy was at work. **Sixteen-year-old boys store these images. They don't unstore them.**
- **He's terrified the body never comes all the way back.** Specifically: the pelvis. Kneeling on a slab to check a forming corner is the move that defines his profession. *What if he never kneels right again.* He has not Googled this fear yet. He will, at 2 AM, in Week 7.
- **He resents the man in the Hyundai Tucson in a way that surprises him.** Not at his car. At his *phone*. He keeps thinking: *a guy looking at his phone took my year.* He doesn't tell people because it sounds small. It is not small.
- **He's scared of the $38K offer.** Not because it's low. Because *he might accept it out of sheer fatigue*. Six weeks of pain, six weeks of insurance calls, the workers' comp denial, Kristy tired — the offer is a closed loop, and a closed loop sometimes wins against a long fight that hasn't started yet.
- **He's scared the workers'-comp denial is permanent.** Hardin's carrier is taking the position that an MVA on his commute home isn't a work injury. Jason knows it's a gray area. He doesn't have the energy to fight Hardin and the at-fault driver's insurance and the medical billers all at once.
- **He's scared he'll never run a crew again.** Even if the body comes back. Even if the leg holds. **What if Hardin needs Marco at this point. What if the next foreman job is at a smaller outfit, lower pay, longer commute.** The career math is more fragile than people think.

## The Emotional Arc (Timeline)

### Week 1 — The crash and the early days

**Day of crash (Tuesday):** US-60 + Crismon Rd, ~5:40 PM, eastbound on Crismon, T-boned by a Hyundai Tucson running the red. The driver-side door cave in. Jason is conscious but not moving. EMTs cut him out. Banner Gateway. Surgery on the femur. The pelvic fracture is non-displaced enough not to need surgical fixation, just bedrest. The ribs and concussion are diagnosed in the ER. **Kristy gets the call at 6:10 PM at the dental office.** She drives the Pilot to Banner Gateway, leaves it in the ER lot, doesn't move it for three days.

**Days 2–4 in the hospital:** Jason has surgery Wednesday. He's in for four nights. Kristy stays in the room. Pete drives down from Phoenix Wednesday night and brings Kristy a change of clothes and a sandwich. Marco shows up Thursday on his lunch hour with a hard hat, places it on the foot of the bed, doesn't say much, leaves. **The hard hat sits on the kitchen counter for a week before Kristy moves it to the shop.**

**Week 1 at home:** Jason on the recliner. Kristy on the couch. Cousin Pete drives down from Phoenix on the weekend and helps lift him in and out of the shower for the first three days. Caleb hugs him gently. Tyler keeps to himself, which is how Tyler grieves. **The household is in shock. Nothing about the future is real yet.**

### Month 1 — The collapse becomes mathematical

**Week 2:** the bills start. Banner Gateway sends the $84,000 hospital bill. Banner radiology bills separately at $4,200. The orthopedic surgeon's office sends a $7,800 surgical fee. PT starts at $200/visit. **Jason stares at the kitchen counter stack of envelopes and his stomach drops because he reads it as what he owes.** Kristy says "we'll figure it out, hon" in the voice she uses when she's also scared.

**Day 8:** the at-fault driver's insurance calls. **They offer $38,000 to close.** Jason takes the call from the recliner. He says he'll think about it. He hangs up. He lies still for an hour without moving.

**Week 3:** Hardin's workers'-comp carrier calls. They're disputing whether the MVA on the commute home counts as a work injury. They're not denying outright — they're slow-walking. **Jason understands what slow-walking means.** It means *no income from workers' comp* for now and probably forever. PTO runs out Week 2 (he had 9 days). He's on unpaid leave starting Week 3.

**Week 4:** Hardin promotes Marco to acting foreman. The crew text thread keeps coming in. Jason replies less.

### Month 2 — The decision window (the activation zone)

**This is the creative's target.** Jason is off the walker and on the cane. PT three mornings a week. He can sit at the kitchen counter for 30 minutes at a stretch without his side hurting. The concussion-fog has lifted enough that he can read. **The bills now form a stack two inches thick.** The credit card balance is $4,800 and climbing. Kristy has talked to her boss at the dental office about going to four days a week instead of three; her boss said yes.

**Around Week 6, Jason starts Googling.** First: *"workers comp denial arizona mva."* Then: *"car accident lawyer arizona."* Then: *"how much for broken pelvis settlement."* His Facebook and Instagram feeds start serving him PI ads. Some of them are billboard-mill quality and he scrolls past. **One of them is different.** It's a man in a t-shirt and an unbuttoned Carhartt, sitting at a kitchen counter, looking at a stack of envelopes. The hook says *"Broken bones. Bills don't stop."* Jason doesn't tap. He keeps scrolling. **He thinks about it for two days.**

**Week 7–8:** he taps. Lands on the quiz funnel. Gets to step 3 and bails because Kristy walked in. **Returns Week 8** at 2 AM, finishes the quiz, books the consult.

### Month 3–6 — Hiring decision or default

If he hires — he signs by Week 9–11. If he doesn't — he accepts the adjuster's increased offer (probably $52K–$60K by now) and takes the HELOC hit. **The campaign creative has to reach him in Week 6–10 with a hook that says *we see the man, not the injury* — not a hook that says "have you been hurt in an accident?"**

## Adam Ferrier's Motivation Architecture — COM-B Map (Jason-specific)

For Jason to take the action we want (tap ad → complete quiz → book consultation → sign engagement letter), three conditions must be met. His COM-B profile is distinct from Diana's and Sarah's.

**Capability (does he have the know-how?):**
- ✅ *Physical:* off the walker by Week 6, can sit at a phone or laptop for 30 minutes. The concussion-fog is mostly gone by Week 6. Phone screens are fine; long laptop sessions are still hard.
- ⚠️ *Psychological:* Jason is *highly* fluent on contracts, suppliers, schedules, and operational decisions. He reads structural drawings and supplier MSAs. He is **NOT fluent on contingency mechanics, policy-limit demands, UIM stacking, or the specific economics of an MVA case.** *He doesn't know what he doesn't know, and the gap will read as suspicion if a firm tries to fast-talk him.* The intake must educate without condescending; Jason's register sits closer to Sarah's competent-professional pole than Ty's lay-rider pole.

**Opportunity (does his environment allow it?):**
- ✅ *Physical:* he's home, on the recliner or at the kitchen counter, and his phone is in his hand most of the day. **Time is not the constraint.** Focus is.
- ⚠️ *Social:* Kristy is a co-decision-maker but in a different way than Diana's Ben. **Kristy will defer to Jason on the hiring decision** — it's his case, his body, his career — but she will read the firm's website, ask clarifying questions, and *quietly rule out anything that feels predatory*. She is not a veto, but she is a tone gate. **The creative + landing page must read clean to a 43-year-old AZ working-class wife as well as her husband.**
- ⚠️ *Environmental:* peak tap-through windows are **late evening (8:30 PM – 11:30 PM)** when the kids are settled and Kristy is doing her dental-office bookkeeping at the kitchen table — and **2:00 AM – 3:30 AM** when concussion-related sleep disturbance lifts him out of bed and onto the recliner with his phone.

**Motivation (does he want to enough?):**
- ✅ *Reflective (planned, rational):* He knows by Week 5 that he needs a lawyer. He's in the analysis-then-decision mode by Week 6. The $38K offer activated him.
- ✅ *Automatic (emotional):* The role inversion is intolerable. He wants his life back. *The lawyer is one of the levers that returns it.*
- ✅ *Activation lever:* specific. He responds to creative that *sees the man, not the injury.* If the creative respects his competence, he leans in. If it pities him, he scrolls past.

## Richard Shotton's Cognitive Barriers (Jason-specific)

**Five biases actively working against Jason converting. The four primary ones from §3 of `00_Executive_Summary.md` plus one supplementary that Shotton flagged in the room.**

1. **The Blame Shield (Jason's #1 barrier).** Even though he was T-boned by a driver who ran a red, Jason will replay the intersection in his head asking *was I going too fast through that yellow.* Working-class men hold themselves accountable for outcomes whether the outcomes are theirs or not. **The creative cannot say "it wasn't your fault" — that names the unspeakable.** It must show a guy in his exact situation being treated as obviously not-at-fault — the math handled by a specialist, the question never raised. *Show, don't reassure.*

2. **The Help-Rejection Reflex.** Jason has spent his entire adult life being the person other people call. He is psychologically the helper, not the asker. **Reframe: lawyer ≠ help. Lawyer = specialist.** The way Jason hires an electrician for a panel he doesn't touch, or hires an accountant to do his taxes. **Same logic, same dignity.** The creative copy treatment has to do this conversion work.

3. **Bills-As-Debt Fallacy.** The $84,000 Banner Gateway bill arrives. Jason looks at it and his stomach drops because he reads it as *what he owes*. He doesn't yet understand that medical bills from an at-fault MVA are recoverable damages — against the at-fault party's policy and his own UIM stack — and that medical liens are negotiable post-settlement. **Insight 6 from the audience research applies hard here.** The creative has to decode this without lecturing.

4. **The First-Check Trap.** $38K from the adjuster within 8 days. Mortgage due in 5. **The trap is not subtle and it's not Jason's fault for falling toward it.** The creative has to make the comparison physical: *what fair looks like vs. what fast looks like.* (See `05_Campaign_Implications.md` §3 for the visual treatment.)

5. **(Supplementary) Sunk-cost fear around the workers'-comp dispute.** Jason has spent four weeks in conversation with Hardin's carrier about whether his MVA-on-the-commute counts. He may feel, irrationally, that hiring a PI lawyer "wastes" that engagement. **The creative should reframe the workers'-comp dispute as a parallel, not competing, line of recovery** — and signal that a competent PI firm coordinates with the workers'-comp side, not against it. (Saul: this is also a substantiation point WCTL can defend; their AZ team handles workers'-comp coordination.)

**Phone-pickup window: Week 6 to Week 9.** Earlier and Jason is still in *I'll handle it* mode. Later and the lowball has been increased to $55K and partially cashed. **Media frequency lifts in that window.**

## The Identity Frame

Jason's self-image, in three sentences he'd never say aloud but would recognize as true:

> I'm the man you call when the foundation has to be set right. I'm the dad who shows up to the football game in the same boots he wore to the pour that morning. I built a life on the body that worked and the crew that listened — and I built it so plainly that nobody had to ask whether it was real, and right now the body and the crew and the life are all on a recliner in a living room I don't recognize and I am the man being driven, lifted, and worked-around for the first time in twenty-two years.

**The creative's job:** speak to that man. Not the aspirational Ford-truck-and-sunset version, not the victim version, not the cartoon "tough working-class dad" stock persona. **The actual man** — mid-collapse, watching the role he built moves on without him, trying to accept that he is now the room's quietest member instead of its loudest.

## Pitch-Language Summary (for `05_Campaign_Implications.md` to pull from)

**For Julian's synthesis:**

- **Primary emotional engine:** the **role inversion** — the man whose body worked, whose crew listened, whose plan was real, now being driven, lifted, and worked-around. Creative makes this inversion visible without naming it. The empty truck. The shop calendar frozen. The crew text he hasn't replied to.
- **Secondary engine:** the **breadwinner-down economic gravity** (Loss 1). Jason is 81% of household income; the cliff is mathematical. *"Bills don't stop because you can't work"* lands harder for him than for Diana — he literally is the income.
- **Tertiary engine:** the **$38K verdict** (Loss 6's activating insult). The number isn't low; the number is *what they think he's worth*. Activates his legitimate refusal to swallow the lowball.
- **Trigger moment:** Week 6–10, evening (8:30–11:30 PM) couch-or-recliner scroll, plus the **2 AM concussion-insomnia window** (parallels Diana's, different mechanism).
- **Language test:** *would Jason screenshot this and send it to his crew thread or his cousin Pete with no caption?* If yes, voice register is right. If no — too pretty, too aggressive, too pitying — revise.
- **Binet 40/60 test** (activation-weighted for volume slot): the first-3-seconds hook filter must be sharp enough to screen out soft-tissue self-identifiers. *"Broken bones. Bills don't stop."* — yes; reads especially tight for Jason because he is *literally* the broken-bones case.
