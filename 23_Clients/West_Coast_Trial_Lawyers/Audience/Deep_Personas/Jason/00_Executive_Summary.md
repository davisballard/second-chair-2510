## Archetype Rationale (why Jason, why the AZ male MVA slot needs him)

The four-persona Phase 1 lineup activates female recognition for the largest addressable pool — Diana for general MVA volume, Sarah for trucking, Laura for pedestrian — and reaches male audiences only through Ty (motorcycle, presumption-of-fault). **There is no AZ male persona for non-soft-tissue, non-motorcycle, breadwinner-down MVA.** That gap leaves potentially half the addressable volume of the largest pool (~400–600 incidents/month per Evidence Register §3.6) unactivated.

Jason fills that slot. He is the AZ working-class male equivalent to Diana's volume floor — the man whose body was the only thing he ever sold, who got T-boned at a red-light intersection on US-60 by a driver who ran the light, and whose pelvic + femur surgical injuries have removed him from his crew, his role, and his earning capacity for six to nine months. He is the persona the AZ trades audience looks at and says **"fuck yes, that's me."**

## 1. The Archetype (one line)

**Jason Reardon is the man whose body was the only thing he ever sold. He runs a six-to-eight-man concrete and masonry crew across the East Valley, drives a clean 2019 F-250 with the company decals on the doors, and has spent twenty-two years building a household and a reputation through pure operational competence. He was T-boned on US-60 in Mesa by a driver who ran a red, his pelvis and left femur are surgical, and for the first time in his adult life he is not the man other people call — he is the man on the recliner watching a younger guy run his crew over text message.**

## 2. Why Jason for the AZ Male MVA Volume Slot (the sourced rationale)

Five reasons. Different load-bearing structure from Diana's (Diana is volume-floor through household-system identity; Jason is volume-floor through occupational-identity).

1. **Reaches the unactivated half of the largest addressable pool.** The 400–600 monthly moderate-severe MVA incidents (Evidence Register §3.6) skew slightly male in the 25–44 / 45–54 brackets per `01_AZ_Non_Soft_Tissue_Demographics.md` §6 (males overrepresented across all age groups due to mileage, speed, and seatbelt patterns). Diana converts the female half. Jason is the matching male protagonist that broadens the algorithmic targeting cues across the same single ad set. Same Phase 1 spend; broader reach.
2. **The aspirational working-class hero is a register the campaign hasn't tested.** Sarah is professional-credentialed (ICU charge nurse). Diana is suburban office-manager. Laura is suburban-mom librarian. Ty is rider-identity tradesperson. **None of them activate the East Valley trades-belt male — the foreman, the crew lead, the man whose authority is on-site, not at a desk.** That audience is currently scrolling past every PI ad in the Meta feed because none of them are talking to him. Jason changes that.
3. **Breadwinner-down economics is the strongest variant of the financial-pressure architecture.** Diana is the *stable* anchor of a dual-income household (52% of household income, drops on her loss). Sarah is the *majority* earner (71%). **Jason is the *primary* earner at 81% of household income.** Loss of him equals near-total household income collapse for 6+ months. The financial gravity is the heaviest in the lineup, and the audience reads it instantly. *"Bills don't stop because you can't work"* lands differently for the man who *is* the income.
4. **Non-motorcycle, non-trucking, non-pedestrian fills a clean MVA-archetype slot.** Ty's rider-identity carries presumption-of-fault baggage that doesn't transfer to broader male audiences. Sarah's trucking creative carries trucking-specific liability frames. Laura's pedestrian narrative is location-bound. **Jason is the *standard* MVA — passenger-vehicle T-bone at an AZ intersection — which makes him the most algorithmically-castable male protagonist in the lineup.** His face works for retargeting frames that don't fit the other three.
5. **Severity-filter lean-in clears at the highest threshold.** S5's hook discipline is "Broken bones. Bills don't stop." Jason is *literally broken bones plus surgery plus six months*. He is the persona the hook describes most precisely. His creative carries the campaign's most defensible severity claim and the cleanest substantiation pathway against the $1.7B aggregate.

## 4. The Single-Minded Proposition (the "one thing")

> **Jason Reardon's entire adult identity is built on a body that works and a crew that listens. A non-soft-tissue MVA hasn't just injured him — it has removed him from the role he built every other part of his life around. He's not "in recovery." He's a man watching from a recliner while a younger guy named Marco runs his crew over text, while his wife drives him to PT instead of to a job site, while a $38,000 lowball from an adjuster lands on the kitchen counter five days before the mortgage is due. WCTL's job is to be the one specialist who treats him like he's still the competent man in his own room — because he is.**

This is the SMP. Every general-MVA male creative must trace to it. If a hook could sit comfortably next to *"Broken bones. Bills don't stop,"* and if the emotional payload signals *we see the man, not the injury*, it's on-strategy. If it reduces Jason to a victim being rescued — pitying, defiant, performative — it's off-strategy.

---

## 5. The Core Insight (the strategic money)

**The target audience for this slot doesn't recognize themselves as "injury victims." They recognize themselves as the men other people call — the foreman, the crew lead, the dad, the husband, the guy his neighbor borrows the trailer from.** Jason's specific loss isn't physical pain. It's the *role inversion* — for the first time in his adult life, he is the person being driven, lifted, helped, and worked-around. **And the man he was before the crash has no category for that.**

**This is the lever.** A PI firm that recognizes he's not just a hurt person — he's a *man whose competence has been temporarily relocated to a recliner* — wins his attention. Every other firm in the Meta feed is selling him aggression ("don't let them take what's yours"), pity ("you've been through enough"), or vague urgency ("call now!"). WCTL's edge is reading him correctly: **a specialist hired to handle the room he can't be in right now, the way he'd hire an electrician for the panel he doesn't touch.**

The role-inversion has a specific emotional texture: it's both Jason's WORST FEAR (*the body never comes all the way back*) and his DEEPEST RESENTMENT (*a kid on his phone took my year*) running at the same time. The creative that respects this duality — without choosing sides between "you've earned this fight!" and "you must feel so helpless!" — is the creative that lands.

## 6. The Five Campaign Implications

Details in `05_Campaign_Implications.md`. Top line:

1. **Visual world lives in Jason's working life — pre-crash and the empty version of it post-crash.** The truck in the driveway with the keys still on the hook by the door. The boots by the door he can't bend down to lace. The garage workbench with the paper calendar. The chest freezer Tyler's elk meat is in. **The crew at the jobsite without him is the creative's real subject.** No hospital scenes. No crash footage.
2. **Copy register is competent-to-competent, not advocacy-to-victim.** Jason runs concrete pours, reads spec sheets, negotiates with suppliers. He responds to plain-language acknowledgment of his reality. *"You did the work. Make sure the math is right."* — perfect. *"Don't let them take what's yours!"* — wrong Jason; reads as billboard mill.
3. **Cinematic Documentary primary, UGC variant, copy-led DR static.** Jason's emotional architecture reads polished — Elevated Documentary is his native register. UGC variant exists for pattern break in retargeting; copy-led DR static exists for high-intent search and quiz-funnel close. Do NOT default him to UGC; that's Diana's slot.
4. **No crash footage. No hospital scenes. The empty truck and the empty seat at the kitchen counter are the creative.** Same SC brand standard as the rest of the lineup. The other driver's Hyundai never enters frame.
5. **Walker (Weeks 1–5) or cane (Weeks 6+) on LEFT side, right hand free, Carhartt overshirt unbuttoned, slip-on boots, 5-day stubble, calloused right palm = locked visual ID.** Every frame must honor these continuity props. Flipping the cane to the right side breaks the femur-fracture logic and breaks the character lock.

## 7. The Voice Test (use this before shipping any Jason creative)

Three questions. 3/3 = ship. 2/3 = revise. 1/3 = wrong audience.

1. **Would Jason read this line and feel recognized, not pitied?** Not "would a generic blue-collar man" — specifically *would Jason, the 45-year-old concrete foreman who's been reading PI billboards on US-60 for fifteen years, read this and think "oh, they actually see me"?*
2. **Would Jason screenshot this and send it to his union steward or his crew text thread** with no caption? Does it read as documentary-truthful, or would it embarrass him to share because it's melodramatic or trying too hard?
3. **Does it name the role inversion without naming it directly?** Does the line or visual signal that WCTL understands what he actually lost — without ever using phrases like "primary breadwinner," "lost manhood," "fight for what's yours," or any other on-the-nose register?

## 8. Why This Persona Matters for the Overall Campaign (volume-slot lens)

**Jason shares the volume-floor with Diana** — together they cover the largest addressable pool (400–600 moderate-severe MVA/month) across both major demographic halves. Per-case value sits in the median range ($120K–$180K projected for Jason given pelvic + femur surgical, top of the $75K–$150K moderate-severe MVA range per Evidence Register §9), so he is volume-driven economics, not premium-value.

Binet's 60/40 framing applied to Jason: like Diana, **closer to 40/60 weighted toward activation** — volume segments reward direct-response optimization more than long-term brand work. This means:

- **Hook specificity** can be high-filter ("Broken bones. Bills don't stop." reads tighter for Jason than for Diana — he's *literally* a broken-bones case).
- **Creative refresh cadence** can be faster. AZ trades-male audiences have high mental availability for the PI category (US-60 / I-10 / I-17 billboard saturation has trained them).
- **Direct-response metrics** (CTR, LPV, quiz-completion-rate) weight heavily in optimization.
- **BUT — Jason's slot is the campaign's male-recognition test.** If he significantly outperforms a generic male creative, the four-persona Meta architecture is validated. If he underperforms, we either re-tune the register (probably the trade-slot decision needs another swing) or accept that the AZ male audience converts on a different lever entirely. **Protect the persona integrity even while tuning direct-response.**
