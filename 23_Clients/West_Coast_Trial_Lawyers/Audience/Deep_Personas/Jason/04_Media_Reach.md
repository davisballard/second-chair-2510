## 3. Mood State at Tap Moment

**At 9:30 PM on the recliner:** Jason is tired but alert. Kristy is at the kitchen table doing her dental-office books. Kids settled. He's been thinking about money all day. **He is exactly receptive to a creative that signals competence and respect, and exactly hostile to a creative that signals advocacy-to-victim.** He's running through the day's bills in his head. The phone is a distraction he leans into.

**At 2:30 AM on the recliner:** Jason is awake because the concussion is still messing with his sleep cycles, and because there's a low-grade pain in his left side that won't resolve. **He is more emotional at this hour than at 9:30 PM.** His rational defenses are lower. He is more likely to *tap* an ad here, but also more likely to *bail at quiz step 3* because he loses focus. **Quiz funnel must be short enough to complete in 3–4 minutes maximum.** If the quiz reads as a 7-minute commitment, the 2 AM completion rate collapses.

**The hook test:** the first-3-second hook must work *equally well* at both windows. The 9:30 PM Jason is competence-testing the firm. The 2:30 AM Jason is feeling-testing the firm. **A frame of a man like him in a state like his at a time like this is the universal answer to both modes.**

## 4. Meta Targeting Spec (with Sharp's Aperture Statement)

### Targeting

Per `Audience/05_Creative_Strategy_And_Ad_Set_Architecture.md`: Phase 1 runs a single ad set under Meta's Special Ad Categories (Personal Loans / Employment / Housing — though PI doesn't fall under SAC restrictions, WCTL has historically operated under SAC discipline for compliance conservatism).

**SAC implications for Jason's creative:**
- **No age targeting.** The creative must self-qualify for mid-40s AZ trades male via casting + production register.
- **No gender targeting.** Kristy may be the form-filler in some intake cases; the creative must work for her ear too.
- **No ZIP targeting.** No "Apache Junction" or "Mesa" callouts; the AZ-state level is the geographic anchor.
- **No interest targeting tied to occupation, household income, or education.** The creative's *visual world* (the F-250, the Carhartt, the metal-roof shop, the shop calendar) does the qualification.

### Sharp's Aperture Statement (mandatory inclusion)

**Jason is the *specific protagonist*. The audience aperture is *broader*.**

The visual world specifies: 45-year-old white concrete foreman in Apache Junction with three kids, T-boned at US-60 + Crismon. **The audience aperture is:** AZ trades male, mid-30s through mid-50s, primary or majority earner, family man, hit and out of work. Plumbing foreman in Gilbert with two kids, electrician in Phoenix with one kid, HVAC tech in Chandler whose injury kept him out for three months — **all of these men should see Jason and recognize themselves.** Specificity for casting and recognition; breadth for targeting cues.

**The casting director's audit:** does Jason on screen look like *every AZ trades male* or like *one specific AZ trades male*? If "every," the casting is generic and the recognition collapses. If "one specific," the casting works because the audience reads the specifics as *true* and projects themselves into the truth. **Aim for one specific.**

**The media planner's audit:** does the audience aperture include:
- Construction (general, framing, concrete, masonry)
- Trades (HVAC, electrical, plumbing, mechanical)
- Skilled blue-collar (welding, machining, fleet mechanic)
- Logistics (route delivery, regional driving, light commercial CDL)
- Light manufacturing (warehouse foreman, line lead)

If yes — the aperture is calibrated. If the casting reads narrower than the aperture (e.g., "only the concrete guys see themselves"), the casting needs to back off the trade-specific signifiers.

### Excluded targeting / lookalike vectors

- No lookalikes off the white-collar professional segment. Jason is not a substitute for Sarah's professional-credential audience.
- No exclusion of higher-income brackets (a $200K-earning project manager in commercial construction may still recognize himself in the financial-pressure architecture if the household structure matches).
- **No exclusion of married women** — Kristy is a meaningful form-filler segment.

## 5. Language Patterns (USE / AVOID — 10 categories of his voice register)

### ✅ USE

1. **Specialist framing.** *"Find out what your case is actually worth."* / *"Talk to a specialist."* / *"We handle the legal side; you focus on healing."*
2. **Math language.** *"Make sure the math is right."* / *"More than what insurance first offered."* / *"Free case review — 60 seconds."*
3. **Earned-not-owed language.** *"You did the work."* / *"Collect what's already owed."* / *"What the law actually says."*
4. **Concrete severity language.** *"Broken bones."* / *"Surgery, hospital stays, months of recovery."* / *"Workers' comp dragging?"*
5. **Operational dignity.** *"You ran a crew. Let us run the case."* / *"Hire someone competent."* (Use sparingly — risks try-hard.)
6. **Plain disclosure language.** *"No fee unless we win."* / *"Free case review — no obligation."* (Standard but load-bearing for Jason.)
7. **Documentary specificity.** *"Arizona drivers seriously injured in a crash."* / *"Rear-ended on I-10? T-boned in Mesa?"* (Specificity earns trust.)
8. **Workers'-comp coordination language.** *"We work with workers' comp, not against it."* (Jason-specific differentiator.)
9. **Lien-handling language.** *"We negotiate hospital liens after settlement."* (Decodes the bills-as-debt fallacy without lecturing.)
10. **Low-key competence.** Quiet, declarative sentences. Active voice. Short. *"Bills don't stop. We make them sit down."* — borderline; test.

### ❌ AVOID

1. **Pitying language.** *"You've been through enough."* / *"You deserve to be heard."* / *"We're here for you."* — kills the register on contact.
2. **Aggression / fight metaphors.** *"Fight back."* / *"Don't let them take what's yours."* / *"Go to war for your rights."* — reads as billboard-mill, triggers Travis-the-crew-member prior.
3. **Generic urgency.** *"Call now!"* / *"Don't wait!"* / *"Time is running out!"* — pattern-matches to volume-mill creative; Jason scrolls past.
4. **Big promised numbers.** *"$1 million settlements!"* / *"You may be entitled to MILLIONS!"* — kills credibility.
5. **Patronizing simplification.** *"We'll explain it in plain English."* (Jason reads spec sheets — he doesn't need to be told he can be talked down to.)
6. **Class-coded condescension.** *"We help hard-working families."* / *"Real Americans deserve..."* — try-hard, performative.
7. **Performative masculinity.** *"Get the respect you've earned."* / *"Take back what's yours."* — fight-metaphor-adjacent.
8. **Vague advocacy.** *"Let us be your voice."* / *"We stand by injured Arizonans."* — empty.
9. **Cinematic metaphors.** *"Your second chance starts here."* — try-hard.
10. **Broad emotional claims unsupported by visual.** *"We understand what you're going through."* — Jason will not buy it; the creative has to *show* it.

## 7. Frequency + Fatigue Guardrails

- **Frequency target:** 6–9 impressions over 14 days for prospect cohort. Higher for active retargeting cohorts (12–15 impressions over 14 days).
- **Fatigue signal:** CTR drops below 1.2% on cinematic hero, or quiz-completion-rate drops below 28%. Refresh the creative.
- **Refresh cadence:** every 3 weeks at minimum, every 2 weeks if performance allows. Hook variants are cheaper to test than full creative rebuilds; rotate hooks first.
- **Sound-off requirement:** captions on every video asset. Per `feedback_ad_post_workflow.md`, captions are added in CapCut as the final pass.
- **9:16 + 1:1 production parity:** every cinematic asset ships in both aspect ratios from the start. Don't crop a 16:9 down — frame the shot for both.
