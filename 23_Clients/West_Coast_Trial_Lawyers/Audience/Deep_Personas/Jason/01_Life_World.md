## 1. Professional Role

**Title:** Concrete and Masonry Foreman, Hardin Concrete & Foundation (regional contractor, ~50 employees, ~$12M annual revenue, headquartered in Mesa, jobs across the Phoenix metro East Valley plus occasional Pinal County commercial work).

**Tenure arc:**
- 22 years old: started as a laborer on a four-man framing crew. $13/hr.
- 26: passed his GED (left high school at 17 for his first construction job; finished the GED on his then-girlfriend Kristy's pushing).
- 30: ran his own three-man side crew doing residential foundation pours and retaining walls. Cash flow was thin.
- 36: hired by Hardin Concrete as foreman after his side outfit got too big to run solo and too small to scale. Took the W-2 trade.
- 45 (now): nine years as foreman at Hardin. Runs a six-to-eight-man crew. Pours residential foundations (most volume), retaining walls, commercial slabs, occasional decorative concrete (stained, broom-finished, exposed-aggregate). Signs off on every pour his crew runs.

**Authority profile:** he's not a credentialed expert (no PE license, no engineering background) — he's an *operational* expert. He reads the structural drawings the engineer of record produced, knows when a forming spec is wrong, knows which suppliers cut corners on aggregate, knows which inspectors will pass a marginal slab and which won't. **The crew calls him boss and means it.** His second is Marco Aguilar, 31, a former laborer Jason promoted three years ago.

**Compensation:** $78,000 base salary. Per-job bonuses average $10K–$14K/year tied to crew margin and on-time completion. Most years take-home is $90K–$95K. Health insurance through Hardin (BCBS PPO, $480/mo employee contribution, family covered). 401K with 4% match — current balance ~$94K. **No short-term disability.** Workers' comp covers him at 66.7% of average weekly wage for the first 24 weeks of an off-the-job injury — which an MVA on the way home from a jobsite *technically might* qualify as, but Hardin's carrier disputes it (Steel: see file 03 for the workers'-comp denial subplot, which is a real campaign hook).

## 2. Vehicle + Commute

**Truck:** 2019 Ford F-250 Super Duty, white, single cab with crew extension, 8-foot bed. 84,000 miles. Hardin Concrete decals on both doors, phone number underneath. **Kept washed.** A Yeti 35 cooler strapped in the bed. A locking aluminum tool chest. A stack of forming lumber on a Saturday morning. **The truck is the tool.** Owned with $11,000 left on a $640/mo payment.

Pre-crash daily commute: out of Apache Junction at 5:50 AM, jobsite by 6:45 AM (locations vary — Mesa, Gilbert, Queen Creek, Chandler, occasional Pinal County). Home by 5:30 PM most days. He runs the truck on diesel — an extra cost he absorbs because the Super Duty's torque is what hauls the loaded forming trailer.

**The crash:** Driver-side T-bone at the intersection of US-60 (Superstition Freeway) and Crismon Rd in Mesa. ~5:40 PM, weekday. Coming home from a foundation pour in Queen Creek. The other driver — civilian in a leased Hyundai Tucson — ran the red eastbound on Crismon. Police report cites the other driver. Intersection traffic-cam captured the impact. Clean liability.

**The truck after:** F-250 driver-side door caved, frame bent, totaled. Hyundai driver had AZ minimum carrier policy ($25K BI / $50K aggregate / $15K PD). UIM stack on Jason's Progressive policy is the live recovery vehicle. The truck's status — totaled vs. salvage — is unresolved at Week 6, the moment the campaign reaches him.

## 6. Daily Rhythm

### Pre-crash weekday

- **5:00 AM** — Alarm. Jason gets up before Kristy. Black coffee, pre-set Mr. Coffee.
- **5:15 AM** — At the kitchen counter. Phone with the day's pour schedule, Marco's overnight texts about supplier issues, weather check (concrete pours hate over 100°F). Caleb's lunch packed the night before is in the fridge; he double-checks it.
- **5:35 AM** — Out the door. Boots laced. Truck started. Hardin polo on supplier-meeting days, plain t-shirt under Carhartt other days.
- **5:50 AM** — On US-60 westbound toward whichever jobsite. Country radio (KMLE 107.9) or sometimes silence.
- **6:45 AM** — Jobsite. Walks the perimeter, talks to Marco, runs the morning huddle, signs off on the pour or sets up the day's forming.
- **12:30 PM** — Lunch out of the cooler in the truck cab — usually a leftover Kristy made him, sometimes a ham sandwich. 30 minutes.
- **5:30 PM** — Home. Boots off in the garage, never inside. Kisses Kristy, says hey to whichever kids are around.
- **6:00 PM** — Family dinner if Kristy made one, or grilling on the back patio in summer.
- **8:00 PM** — Football with Caleb in the yard, or watching whatever Tyler has on, or out in the shop fixing something.
- **10:00 PM** — Bed.

### Post-crash weekday (Weeks 1–8)

- **6:30 AM** — Wakes up — concussion is messing with his sleep. Pelvic pain when he tries to roll. Kristy helps him sit up. He hates it.
- **7:00 AM** — Kristy makes coffee. Brings it to him on the recliner in the living room (the bed is too painful for upright; the recliner is where he sleeps Weeks 1–3).
- **7:30 AM** — Caleb leaves for school. Caleb hugs him gently around the chest, careful of the ribs. Madison waves from the door.
- **8:00 AM** — Tyler drives himself to school in the Tacoma. Says "see you, Dad."
- **9:00 AM** — PT three mornings a week (Mon/Wed/Fri). Kristy drives him in the Pilot. The clinic is in Mesa, 18 minutes. He hates that she has to drive him.
- **11:00 AM** — Home. Recliner. The TV is on but he isn't watching. He's looking at his phone, looking at Marco's texts about how the crew did on the Queen Creek pour, telling himself not to text back too much.
- **1:00 PM** — Lunch. Kristy makes him a sandwich. He eats half.
- **2:00 PM** — Phone calls he doesn't want to make. Insurance. Banner Gateway billing. The body shop. The Hardin office about workers' comp. **He runs out of energy at the third call.**
- **4:00 PM** — Tries to read the mail Kristy stacked. The bills are starting to look like a wall.
- **5:30 PM** — Caleb home. Madison home. Tyler in late from football (Tyler has decided not to skip practice because his dad is hurt; Jason knows this and doesn't know what to say about it).
- **6:30 PM** — Family dinner Kristy made. Jason eats it sitting at the head of the table, in the chair he's always sat in. Pretends his side doesn't hurt.
- **8:30 PM** — Recliner. The house quiet. The shop dark across the yard.
- **2:00 AM** — He's still awake. Phone in his hand. He starts Googling.

## 9. The Garage In Detail (THE HERO SET)

**This is Jason's command center, the parallel to Diana's kitchen island. The system that breaks when he can't function lives here.**

- The detached 24x32 metal-roof shop, concrete floor (Jason poured), corrugated metal walls, a single garage door he can pull up from the inside.
- A 12-foot tool wall on the back, organized in a system Jason invented: every tool has an outline drawn in painter's tape, every drawer is labeled in Sharpie. "FORMING" / "FINISHING" / "DEMO" / "TRUCK SPARES" / "ELECTRICAL HE BORROWED FROM HIS BROTHER MIKE AND SHOULD RETURN."
- A workbench he built. A vice. A drill press. A small lathe Pete sold him for $200 ten years ago.
- A paper desk-pad calendar on the workbench, current month visible. **In Sharpie:** pour dates (Jason's blue), Tyler's football games (red, painter's tape star next to home games), Caleb's school events (green), Madison's art-club nights (purple, added by Kristy when she comes out to the shop sometimes). The calendar **stops getting updated at Week 1**.
- The chest freezer with Tyler's elk meat from last fall.
- A second-hand office chair on wheels with a broken-in seat — Pete's old chair from his shop. **After the crash, Jason rolls this chair to the workbench so he can sit and try to work on small repairs without standing.**
- A printed copy of the structural drawings for the most recent foundation pour, marked up in red pencil. He reads them at the workbench when he's thinking through a job.
- A radio that plays KMLE country.
- A small fridge in the back corner with a six-pack of Modelo at any given time.
- The F-250 (when it's there) parked outside the shop bay, washed.

**The crash's domestic visual subject is this shop in the version of itself where Jason isn't there.** The calendar frozen. The chair pulled up but empty. The drawings marked up but not since. **The shop is the kitchen island for an AZ trades male.**

## Casting Notes (for the Higgsfield reference portrait + downstream production)

- **Build:** 5'10"–6'0", ~200–215 lbs, solid working-trade build. Not a gym body and not a soft body — the build of a 45-year-old concrete foreman who pours, lifts, and sets forms for a living. Broad shoulders, thick forearms, thick neck, slight thickening at the waist from middle age but **no beer belly**. The chest and shoulders read as twenty-two years of physical labor. He looked like he could still set forms six weeks ago — until the femur and the pelvis went. *(Recast 2026-04-29 from earlier "soft gut from beer-on-the-patio" framing — risked tilting from "specific" into "schlubby" for the cinematic line; the role-inversion lands harder when pre-crash Jason looks visibly capable.)*
- **Hands:** calloused right palm, scarred knuckles (small white scars from years of forming and rebar work), clean nails (Kristy's influence), wedding band on left ring finger.
- **Face:** weathered, not pretty. Sun lines around the eyes from squinting on jobsites. 5-day stubble on the post-crash frames. Hair short, mid-brown going slightly grey at the temples, slightly receding at the temple corners.
- **Eyes:** brown or hazel. Quiet. **The eye is the audit.** If the casting reads as an actor pretending to be a foreman, kill it. If the casting reads as a foreman who happens to be on camera, ship it.
- **Reading: not Brad Pitt with a tool belt. Not a country-music-video star. A specific, real, regionally-recognizable AZ trades male in his mid-40s.** Sun-darkened skin, especially on the forearms and the back of the neck. **A neck tan-line where his t-shirt sleeve ends.**
