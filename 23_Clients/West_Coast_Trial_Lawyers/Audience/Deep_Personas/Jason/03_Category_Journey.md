## 1. Jason's Category Priors (what he thinks of PI lawyers before the ad reaches him)

The audience for this slot has spent fifteen years driving past PI billboards on US-60 and I-10. Jason is no exception. **His priors are calcified, specific, and unflattering** — and the creative's first job is to displace them.

**Prior 1 — The billboard mill.** Jason has driven past the same dozen PI billboards on US-60 every weekday morning since 2008. Smiling lawyer, large promised dollar amount, 1-800 number, ALL CAPS HEADLINE. Two of them have been there since before Caleb was born. **Jason's read on these firms:** volume operations, fast settlements, bad outcomes for the client, decent business for the lawyer. He's not entirely wrong, and he won't be talked out of this read by another billboard.

**Prior 2 — His buddy who got $7K.** Jason's old crew member Travis (still on the crew, concrete finisher) got rear-ended on the 202 in 2022. Used a billboard guy. Got $7,000 after fees and lien negotiation. Travis was bitter about it for a year. **Jason watched this happen.** When Jason thinks "I should call a PI lawyer," he hears Travis's voice in his head saying *they take it all*.

**Prior 3 — The "professional referral" he doesn't have.** Jason knows that real lawyers — the kind that handle his cousin Pete's commercial business disputes, or that drew up Hardin's contracts with the GCs — are referred to. They don't advertise on billboards. **Jason doesn't have a referral.** He's not in the social network where someone forwards him a name. His cousin Mike in Tucson is an electrical contractor; Pete is a steamfitter; his union steward (Local 502 Cement Masons, not really a "union steward" but the closest analog) is a guy named Ron who's competent but isn't a lawyer-referral source. **Jason has no entry to the world he believes the real lawyers live in.**

**Prior 4 — Insurance company priors.** Jason has dealt with insurance for 22 years — homeowner claims, truck claims, two prior fender-benders. **He knows how insurance works.** He knows the adjuster's job is to close fast and low. He doesn't fully grasp how a PI firm changes that math, but he does grasp that *something* has to change it because his current trajectory is acceptance of the lowball.

**Prior 5 — "If I sue, my insurance goes up."** This is technically wrong (his rates aren't affected by him being a non-at-fault claimant), but it's a real prior that nags at him. **The intake process needs to address it directly without making it the headline.**

**Prior 6 — A vague, half-formed sense that "I'm not the kind of guy who sues."** Jason has never sued anyone for anything. He's never even been to small claims court. **Asking for money for an injury feels foreign to him in a way it doesn't feel foreign to, say, Diana's husband Ben.** This prior is gendered and class-coded; the creative has to overcome it not by lecturing but by reframing — *making the math right is not "suing", it's collecting what's already owed.*

## 2. The Decision Journey (3 stages, calibrated to Jason's recovery + activation arc)

Diana's decision journey runs Awareness Weeks 1–5 / Consideration Weeks 5–12 / Decision Weeks 12–20. **Jason's compresses on the front end and runs slightly later on the back end** because his injury is more acute, his hospital stay was longer, and the workers'-comp dispute eats his early energy.

### Stage 1 — Awareness (Weeks 1–4): "Something happened, the system is moving"

Jason is in shock and pain Weeks 1–2. The household is reactive. **He is not yet thinking about a lawyer.** What's happening:

- Day-of-crash through Day 4: hospital, surgery, recovery beds, drugs.
- Day 5–10: home, recliner, walker, the hospital bill arrives.
- Day 8: the at-fault driver's adjuster calls with **$38,000 to close**. Jason takes the call but says he'll think about it.
- Week 2: Hardin's workers'-comp carrier calls and starts the slow-walk on whether his MVA-on-the-way-home counts.
- Week 3: PTO is gone. Unpaid leave begins. Banner Gateway has now sent two more bills. Kristy's expression has shifted.
- Week 4: Marco is acting foreman. The crew text thread is humming without Jason's input.

**End-of-Stage-1 state:** Jason knows two things: (a) the system as currently configured is going to bury him financially, and (b) the $38K is not enough. **He does not yet know he needs a lawyer.** He still believes he can handle the adjuster himself, the way he handled the homeowner claim in 2019. The creative does not target Stage 1; it builds frequency for Stage 2.

### Stage 2 — Consideration (Weeks 4–9): "Maybe I need a specialist"

This is the activation zone. Jason has stabilized physically — off the walker by Week 6, on a cane, off the heavy meds, the concussion fog clearing. **He has bandwidth for the first time since Day 1.** What's happening:

- Week 5: he calls the adjuster back to negotiate the $38K. **The conversation goes badly.** The adjuster cites his "minor concussion grade" and the fact that "many patients with type II pelvic fractures recover fully without surgery" (Jason's was different — his was the femur that needed surgery — but the adjuster muddies it). Jason hangs up frustrated. **The adjuster comes up to $44K the next day.**
- Week 5–6: he Googles for the first time. *"Pelvic fracture mva settlement arizona."* Then *"workers comp commute home work injury."* Then *"car accident lawyer arizona."* Facebook and Instagram start serving him PI ads.
- Week 6: the first PI ad that doesn't immediately read as a billboard mill. He doesn't tap. He scrolls. **He thinks about it for 36 hours.**
- Week 7: he taps. Lands on a quiz funnel. Gets to step 3. Bails when Kristy walks in (he hasn't told her he's looking yet).
- Week 7–8: he and Kristy have **the conversation** at the kitchen table. Kristy says "we should at least talk to someone." She's been waiting for him to bring it up.
- Week 8: he returns to the quiz, finishes it, books the consult.

**End-of-Stage-2 state:** Jason has identified 2–4 firms he might call. He's read 2 firm websites carefully. He's avoided the obvious billboard-mill ones. **Kristy has read at least one of the firm websites with him.** The decision is between hiring or accepting a counter-offer (probably $52K–$55K by then).

### Stage 3 — Decision (Weeks 9–14): "Sign the engagement letter or take the offer"

- Week 9: consult with WCTL (or another firm). The consult goes one of two ways:
  - **Good consult:** specialist who treats him competently, lays out the case math (UIM stack, medical lien negotiation, lost-wages calculation, pain-and-suffering range), gives a realistic projected range ($120K–$180K against a $38K starting offer), explains the contingency clearly, doesn't pressure. **Jason signs within 48 hours.** Kristy reviews the engagement letter.
  - **Bad consult:** firm that fast-talks him, asks for a retainer, doesn't explain contingency, doesn't address the workers'-comp parallel. **Jason walks. Goes back to scrolling for a few days. May or may not return.**
- Week 10–14: if he hires, he's in. If he doesn't, the adjuster increases to $58K–$62K and **Jason accepts out of fatigue** sometime in Week 12–14.

**End-of-Stage-3 state:** signed engagement or settlement-cashed-and-regretted. **The creative's job is to win Stage 2 → Stage 3 transition with a 9-week-arc audience that is fatigued, financially scared, and proud.**

## 3. Trigger Moments (5 ranked, each a creative target)

Each trigger is a moment where Jason's behavior visibly shifts. The creative should be present on platforms that intersect with these triggers. Ranked by activation power:

### Trigger 1 — The $38K offer arrives (Week 1, Day 8)

The single highest-activation moment in the journey. **Jason reads the $38K not as a number but as a verdict** — what they think he's worth. He doesn't act on it Day 8 because he's still on heavy meds. **The activation lag is 4–5 weeks.** By Week 5–6, the $38K (or its slightly-higher cousin) is the engine driving his Google searches.

**Creative implication:** the campaign reaches him at Week 5–7, but the *substrate* is the $38K. The body copy "more than what insurance first offered" reads as direct rebuttal to the verdict. **This is the body-copy money line for Jason.**

### Trigger 2 — The $84K hospital bill arrives (Week 2)

Banner Gateway's hospital bill arrives Week 2. Then radiology. Then surgical. **The stack is the trigger.** Jason reads the bills as what he owes (the bills-as-debt fallacy from §02). The pile on the kitchen counter accumulates visual weight Weeks 2–6.

**Creative implication:** the visual frame of the unopened-envelope stack on the kitchen counter is the second-highest-activation visual in the campaign. The hook *"Broken bones. Bills don't stop."* names this trigger directly.

### Trigger 3 — Kristy's tone shifts (Week 4–5)

Kristy isn't unhappy. She isn't angry. She is *tired in a new way*, and Jason can feel it without her saying anything. **This is the moment Jason starts seriously considering that "handle it myself" isn't going to work.** Not because his pride breaks. Because his marriage's carrying-load has shifted and he can see it.

**Creative implication:** the visual anchor 12 from `01_Life_World.md` (Kristy seen from behind making coffee, Jason watching her from the recliner) is the trigger-3 visual. The campaign may not feature Kristy directly, but the *reference to her carrying* is in the subtext of every Jason frame.

### Trigger 4 — Marco stops asking for advice (Week 5–6)

The crew text thread keeps going, but the questions to Jason taper. Marco is figuring it out. **This is the Loss 3 trigger** (crew authority moving on without him). The activation logic: Jason recognizes that his return to work is no longer guaranteed, that the longer this drags, the harder the return, and that **financial runway is the only thing buying him time to come back as himself**. The lawyer is part of that runway.

**Creative implication:** trigger 4 is the *competence-to-competence* register's emotional substrate. The hook should respect that Jason is a man whose hierarchy has moved on without him — the firm that talks to him as a peer wins.

### Trigger 5 — The Hardin workers'-comp slow-walk (Week 3–6)

Hardin's carrier doesn't deny outright. They slow-walk. **Jason understands what slow-walking means.** This is one less revenue stream than expected, and it forces him to understand that *parallel recovery vehicles are needed* — he can't rely on workers' comp.

**Creative implication:** this is a substantiation hook for WCTL specifically. WCTL's AZ team handles workers'-comp coordination. The intake or consult can name this as a service distinction. The ad creative can hint at it: *"workers' comp dragging? You may have other options."*

## 4. Competitive Set (7 alternatives, ranked)

What Jason might do instead of hiring WCTL. Ranked by likelihood at Week 6–9, when the campaign reaches him:

1. **Accept the adjuster's increased offer ($55K–$62K) out of fatigue.** Highest probability if no PI firm reaches him. The first-check trap, escalated. He wouldn't be happy. He'd take it.
2. **Hire a billboard-mill firm whose ad he taps because it's the first one with the right severity language.** Travis-the-crew-member outcome. Volume operation, fast cheap settlement, $40–60K net to Jason. He'd regret it within a year.
3. **Handle it himself for two more months, then hire someone in fatigue.** He'd negotiate the adjuster up from $38K to maybe $65K through phone calls, then hire a small-time generalist when the case gets complicated, and end up with $80K–$95K net. Sub-optimal but viable.
4. **Hire a small-town generalist his cousin Mike in Tucson recommends.** Mike knows a lawyer in Tucson who handled his contracting LLC dispute. **Not a PI specialist.** The lawyer would do an okay job, refer it out partially, settle in the $90K–$120K range. Decent outcome, slower than WCTL, less leverage on the medical liens.
5. **Hire WCTL via a Fair Case Meta ad (the campaign target outcome).** Specialist team, projected $120K–$180K range, professional medical-lien negotiation, workers'-comp coordination. **The campaign's optimization target.**
6. **Get referred to a real PI firm by his union (Local 502).** Union steward Ron knows a name. Possible but requires Jason to ask, which his help-rejection reflex resists. Probability low unless Kristy pushes.
7. **Do nothing. Refuse all offers. Wait it out.** Lowest probability. Not Jason's profile. He'll act, he just might act badly.

**The campaign's job is to convert routes 1, 2, 3, and 4 into route 5.**

## 5. Creative Implications (preview — full treatment in `05_Campaign_Implications.md`)

What this Category Journey doc means for the ad creative:

1. **Reach window: Weeks 5–9.** Earlier and Jason isn't ready. Later and the lowball is being cashed.
2. **Hook discipline: severity-filter forward, role-respect register.** "Broken bones. Bills don't stop." filters in his profile. **The body copy and visual respect his competence.** *"Find out what your case is actually worth"* (investigative, peer-to-peer) lands. *"Fight for what you deserve!"* (advocacy-to-victim) doesn't.
3. **Visual trigger: the kitchen-counter stack of unopened bills + the empty truck + the shop calendar frozen.** These three frames are the campaign's emotional payload anchors. Crash footage and hospital scenes are excluded.
4. **Body copy must address the workers'-comp parallel** — not centrally, but in the firm-distinction language. *"We coordinate with workers' comp."* This is a Jason-specific differentiator most billboard mills don't claim.
5. **Kristy is present in the subtext, not the frame.** Visual anchor 12 (Kristy from behind making coffee) can appear in 1–2 cinematic frames as ambient context — never as a speaking character, never as a co-protagonist. **The campaign is for Jason; Kristy is *of* him, not *with* him in frame.**
6. **The 2 AM kitchen-counter scroll is a viable cinematic frame** (visual anchor 10 from `01_Life_World.md`). It's the activation moment. The phone glow on his face is the audience's POV — they're watching him watching the scroll that becomes them.
7. **The consult call must work for Kristy's ear.** Not because she's on it — she may or may not be — but because Jason will summarize it for her afterward, and **what he summarizes is what closes the case**. The intake script and consult choreography matter for retention from book → sign.
