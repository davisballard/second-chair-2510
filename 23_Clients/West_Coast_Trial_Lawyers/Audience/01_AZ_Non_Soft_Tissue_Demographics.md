## 1. Arizona State Overview

### Population

Arizona's population reached approximately **7.58 million** in 2025, growing at a 1.3% annual rate driven by net migration from higher-cost states (primarily California). This places Arizona as one of the fastest-growing states in the nation.

### Age Distribution

| Age Group | % of Population | Estimated Count |
|-----------|----------------|-----------------|
| Under 18 | 21.3% | ~1.61M |
| 18-24 | ~9% | ~682K |
| 25-34 | ~14% | ~1.06M |
| 35-44 | ~13% | ~985K |
| 45-54 | ~12% | ~909K |
| 55-64 | ~12% | ~909K |
| 65+ | 19.3% | ~1.46M |

The state has a growing elderly population (19.3% are 65+), which is relevant for pedestrian crash exposure, and a substantial working-age population (25-54) that drives the bulk of non-soft-tissue crash volume.

### Ethnicity & Race

| Group | % of Population |
|-------|----------------|
| White (Non-Hispanic) | 51.2% |
| Hispanic or Latino | 32.1% (~2.43M) |
| Black or African American | 4.6% |
| Native American / Alaska Native | 4.2% |
| Asian | 3.6% |
| Two or More Races | 4.7% |

**Critical finding:** Nearly one-third of Arizona's population is Hispanic. Among children under 5, over 46% are Hispanic, while only 11% of those 75+ are Hispanic -- indicating a young, growing demographic with increasing accident exposure over time.

The largest ancestry group is Mexican (25.8%), followed by German (16.5%), Irish (10.9%), and English (10.3%). Arizona's Hispanic population is 89% Mexican-origin, reflecting deep historic and geographic ties to Mexico.

### Income

- **Median household income (2024):** $79,964
- **Poverty rate:** 11.7% (lowest since ACS tracking began in 2010)
- Poverty disproportionately affects Hispanic, Black, Native American, and Pacific Islander populations
- Native Americans have the highest poverty rate and traffic fatality rate per capita in the state

### Urban vs. Rural Split

| Area | Population | % of State |
|------|-----------|------------|
| **Maricopa County (Phoenix metro)** | 4.73M | 62% |
| **Pima County (Tucson metro)** | 1.09M | 14% |
| Pinal County | ~480K | 6% |
| All other counties (rural) | ~1.28M | 17% |

Arizona is overwhelmingly urban. Maricopa + Pima counties account for **76% of the state population** and an even higher share of crash volume. Phoenix alone logged 37,472 crashes with 265 fatal crashes and 10,449 injuries in 2024.

### Crash Overview (2024)

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Total crashes | 121,107 |
| Fatal crashes | 1,117 |
| Injury crashes | 37,376 |
| Total fatalities | 1,228 |
| Total injuries | 54,000+ |
| Daily average | 331 crashes; 3 fatal per day |

Arizona produces **~925-1,233 non-soft-tissue incidents per month**, creating a cumulative unrepresented pipeline of 2,775-7,400 people at any given time.

## 2. Motorcycle Crash Victims

### Incident Volume

| Metric | 2023 | 2024 |
|--------|------|------|
| Total motorcycle crashes | 3,165 | 3,036 |
| Fatalities | 258 | ~245 (est.) |
| Injuries | 2,571 | ~2,450 (est.) |
| Daily average | ~9 crashes/day | ~8.3 crashes/day |

A **36.6% increase in crashes since 2020**, driven by a 33% increase in motorcycle registrations (209,719 to 279,569 since 2019). Arizona's year-round riding climate makes this a uniquely active state for motorcycle use.

### Demographic Profile

**Age:**

| Age Group | % of Crashes | % of Fatalities | Key Fact |
|-----------|-------------|----------------|----------|
| 15-24 | 16% | 13% | Young riders, less experience |
| **25-34** | **17.4%** | **24%** (63 deaths) | **Highest fatality rate. 628 injured.** |
| **35-44** | **15.9%** | **21%** (55 deaths) | Second-highest fatality rate |
| 45-54 | 13.1% | 14.8% | Established riders |
| 55-64 | ~10% | ~12% | Higher fatality-to-crash ratio |
| 65-74 | 6.8% | 6.1% | Aging riders with slower recovery |
| 75+ | 1.3% | 6.2% | Very high fatality rate when involved |

The 25-44 age range dominates both crash volume and fatalities, accounting for **33.3% of all motorcycle crashes and 45% of all motorcycle fatalities** in Arizona.

**Gender:**
- **94% of fatalities are male**
- **87%+ of injuries are male**
- Women riders are growing (19% of owners nationally, 26% of Millennial owners) but remain a small fraction of crash victims

**Income:**
- National median household income for motorcycle owners: $62,500
- Harley-Davidson owners (dominant brand in AZ): median ~$90,000, average $85,300
- Many riders are not blue-collar as commonly assumed -- 72% have at least some college education, 71% are employed, 15% are retired
- The "professional who rides" profile dominates: lawyers, doctors, tradespeople, retirees

**Ethnicity:**
- Historically White male dominated, but Hispanic, Black, and Asian American riding communities are growing and skewing younger than the traditional rider base
- Arizona's substantial Hispanic population means significant and growing Latino motorcycle participation

### Geographic Distribution

| Area | Crashes | % of State Total | Fatalities |
|------|---------|-----------------|------------|
| Maricopa County | 2,019 | 64% | 156 |
| Pima County | 294 | 9.3% | 37 |
| Pinal County | 186 | 5.9% | — |
| Yavapai County | 162 | 5.1% | — |
| Mohave County | 157 | 5.0% | — |

**Maricopa + Pima = 74.4% of all motorcycle fatalities.**

**Urban vs. Rural:**
- Urban: 2,490 crashes (79%), 181 deaths (70% of fatalities)
- Rural: 675 crashes (21%), 77 deaths (30% of fatalities) -- disproportionately fatal

### Lifestyle & Behavioral Traits

- **Year-round riders.** Arizona's riding season never truly ends. Clubs schedule elevated rides during summer months. This is not a seasonal hobby -- it is a transportation mode and identity.
- **Club culture is active.** The Arizona Motorcycle Clubs Council (AZCMC) coordinates dozens of clubs -- cruiser, sport bike, dirt, co-ed, veteran-affiliated, brand-specific. The Katz MC (co-ed, Phoenix) is the largest in the state. Military/veteran clubs (Arizona Leathernecks) are prominent.
- **Helmet use is optional and low.** Only 63.9% of Arizona riders wear helmets. No helmet = 21% TBI rate vs. 10% with helmet. Among 85+ riders, helmet use drops to 49%.
- **Alcohol involvement is significant.** 6% of motorcycle crashes, but 20% of motorcycle fatalities, involved impairment. The 35-49 age group has the highest impairment rate in fatal motorcycle crashes nationally (34%).
- **Speed is the primary factor.** 22% of motorcycle crashes involved speeding; speeding accounted for 28% of fatalities.

### Media Consumption

- Skews toward Facebook (ages 30-54, daily habit) and Instagram (25-44)
- YouTube is the dominant video platform for motorcycle content (reviews, ride footage, gear)
- Motorcycle community has strong Facebook Group culture (club pages, event coordination, ride scheduling)
- Reddit's r/motorcycles and brand-specific forums are active but not ad-targetable

### Creative Self-Qualification Signals

Because we cannot target by demographics under Special Ad Categories, creative must make riders self-identify. Effective signals include:
- Motorcycle imagery (road scenes, handlebars, gear)
- Language: "If you ride..." / "Fellow riders..." / "Motorcycle crash in Arizona?"
- Reference to year-round riding culture, desert roads, specific corridors
- Helmet/no-helmet scenarios (resonates with AZ's no-mandate culture)

## 3. Trucking / Commercial Vehicle Victims

### Incident Volume

| Metric | 2024 |
|--------|------|
| Total large truck/bus crashes | 2,750 |
| Fatal crashes | 118 |
| Injuries | 1,090 |
| Monthly volume | ~229 crashes |

Arizona is a major freight corridor state. I-10 (Phoenix-Tucson-CA border), I-40 (Flagstaff-New Mexico), and I-17 (Phoenix-Flagstaff) carry thousands of commercial trucks daily.

### Who Are the Victims? (Not the Truck Drivers)

This is critical: **the people we are targeting are NOT truck drivers.** They are passenger vehicle occupants, pedestrians, and cyclists struck by large trucks.

**National data (2023 NHTSA):**
- **73.4% of fatalities** in large truck crashes were people in passenger vehicles (3,787 of 5,158 people killed)
- Only 19.5% were truck occupants
- 7.1% were non-occupants (pedestrians, cyclists)

The typical trucking crash victim in Arizona is a **commuter or traveler in a passenger vehicle** who was struck by or involved in a collision with a large truck on a major highway corridor.

### Demographic Profile

**Age:**
- Mirrors the general Arizona commuter population
- 25-54 is the core driving-age commuter demographic
- 25-34 age group has the highest overall crash involvement rate in Arizona (11,032 injuries statewide in 2024)

**Gender:**
- Roughly proportional to driving population (slight male skew due to higher VMT)
- Male victims may skew slightly higher due to construction/trade occupations requiring highway commutes

**Income:**
- Varies widely by corridor:
  - I-10 (Phoenix-Tucson): Middle-income commuters, logistics workers, travelers. Phoenix median household income: $81,332.
  - I-40 (Northern AZ): Mix of travelers, reservation communities, rural residents. Mohave County had 227 truck crashes in 2024.
  - I-17 (Phoenix-Flagstaff): Mix of Phoenix suburban commuters (north Valley), weekend travelers, Northern AZ residents.

**Ethnicity:**
- Phoenix workforce is 42% Hispanic, 40.6% White, 7.4% Black
- Interstate corridors serving the Phoenix metro reflect this diversity
- I-40 corridor through Navajo and Apache counties intersects tribal land -- Native American populations are significantly overrepresented in traffic fatalities (74.3 per 100K vs. state average of 21.3)

### Geographic Distribution

| Corridor | 2024 Fatal Truck Crashes | Key Demographics |
|----------|------------------------|------------------|
| I-10 (statewide) | 32 | Diverse commuters; freight corridor connecting CA-AZ-NM |
| I-40 (Mohave County alone) | 4 fatalities, 74 injuries, 227 crashes | Rural, tribal communities, travelers |
| I-17 | Data not isolated | Phoenix north Valley suburban commuters |

### Lifestyle & Behavioral Traits

- **Car-dependent commuters.** 64.2% of Phoenix workers drive alone to work. Average commute: 25.6 minutes. These are people on the road every day, sharing lanes with semis.
- **2 cars per household average** in Phoenix -- high vehicle miles traveled (VMT) per capita.
- **Frequent highway users.** The Phoenix metro's sprawl means commuters regularly travel 20-40 miles each way on interstates.
- **Working families.** Many are dual-income households where a serious injury creates immediate financial crisis.

### Media Consumption

- Facebook is the dominant platform for 30-54 commuter demographics
- Radio and podcast listeners (commute time = audio consumption)
- Local news consumers (AZFamily, Arizona Republic, Fox 10 Phoenix)

### Creative Self-Qualification Signals

- Imagery: Large trucks on Arizona highways, I-10 desert corridor scenes, aftermath scenes
- Language: "Hit by a truck?" / "Injured in a crash with a commercial vehicle?" / "Driving on I-10 when..."
- Reference specific corridors Arizona residents will recognize
- Emphasize that truck crashes involve commercial insurance policies (higher coverage = higher compensation)

## 4. Pedestrian & Cyclist Crash Victims

### Incident Volume

| Metric | 2024 |
|--------|------|
| Pedestrian crashes | 2,079 |
| Pedestrian fatalities | 263 (one every 33 hours) |
| Pedestrian injuries | 1,912 |
| Bicycle crashes | 1,379 (5-year high, 21% increase over 2023) |
| Bicycle fatalities | 44 |
| Bicycle injuries | 1,300+ |
| **Combined monthly volume** | **~288 crashes** |

Arizona has one of the highest pedestrian fatality rates in the nation. Pedestrian crashes represent only 1.72% of all crashes but account for **21.42% of all traffic fatalities** -- a staggering overrepresentation.

### Demographic Profile -- Pedestrians

**Age:**

| Age Group | Fatalities | Injuries | Key Fact |
|-----------|-----------|----------|----------|
| 25-34 | — | **313 (most injured)** | Peak injury age group |
| 35-44 | — | High | Working-age, urban |
| 45-54 | — | High | Increasing fatality risk |
| **55-64** | **62 (most killed)** | High | **Highest fatality count of any age group** |
| 65+ | ~19% of fatalities | Moderate | Slower mobility, higher mortality |

The split between "most injured" (25-34) and "most killed" (55-64) is important for creative: younger victims survive but with devastating injuries; older victims die at higher rates.

**Gender:**
- Men are significantly overrepresented in pedestrian fatalities, especially ages 25-44
- Men in their late 20s to mid-40s are most frequently involved in pedestrian crashes

**Income:**
- Pedestrian crash victims disproportionately come from lower-income areas
- Phoenix's High Injury Network is concentrated in west-side neighborhoods (Maryvale, South Phoenix, West Phoenix) that are lower income and underserved
- People in these neighborhoods rely more heavily on walking due to limited vehicle access
- Low-income areas see slower infrastructure improvements (fewer crosswalks, fewer streetlights)

**Ethnicity:**
- Maryvale (West Phoenix): **75.1% Hispanic**, 16.7% poverty rate, 61.8% of households speak a non-English language at home
- South Phoenix: Majority-minority, high Hispanic concentration
- These are the same neighborhoods with the highest pedestrian crash rates

### Demographic Profile -- Cyclists

**Gender:**
- **86% of cyclist fatalities are male** (more than 6x female fatalities)

**Age:**
- 25-44: Most injuries
- 35-54: 31% of deaths (largest fatality group)
- 55-74: Highest fatality rate per crash (survive less often)

**Geographic Distribution:**
- Maricopa County: **70% of all fatal bicycle crashes** and 68% of all bicycle crashes statewide
- Urban intersections without dedicated bike infrastructure are the primary crash locations

### High-Injury Geography

**Dangerous corridors (pedestrian):**
- Grand Avenue (SR-60): Highest pedestrian fatality rate in Phoenix
- McDowell Road: Consistently among top crash corridors
- Indian School Road: Multiple high-injury intersections (67th Ave & Indian School: 263 crashes)
- Thomas Road: High crash concentration
- 51st Avenue corridor: Multiple dangerous intersections

**Dangerous intersections (combined):**
- 67th Ave & Indian School Rd: 263 crashes (2017-2022)
- 67th Ave & McDowell Rd: 251 crashes
- 75th Ave & Indian School Rd: 249 crashes

These are all in **west Phoenix / Maryvale** -- the highest-Hispanic, lowest-income neighborhoods in the city.

### Lighting Conditions

- **76% of pedestrian fatalities occur in dark conditions**
- Crashes in darkness have a fatality rate **6.6x higher** than daylight crashes
- This correlates with infrastructure gaps in lower-income neighborhoods (fewer streetlights)

### Time Patterns

- Pedestrian/bicycle crashes peak between **3 PM - 4 PM and 6 PM - 7 PM** (commute windows)
- Fatal pedestrian crashes concentrate at night, outside marked crosswalks

### Lifestyle & Behavioral Traits

- **Transit-dependent or car-free.** Many pedestrian victims are people without reliable vehicle access who walk to transit stops, work, or errands
- **Service workers.** People working late shifts (restaurant, retail, warehouse) who walk or bike to/from work in darkness
- **Cyclists may be commuters or recreational.** But serious injury crashes overwhelmingly involve commuter cyclists on arterials, not recreational riders on paths
- **66.9% of killed or injured pedestrians were crossing at the time** -- these are people doing normal things (crossing a street) in an environment not designed for them

### Media Consumption

- Pedestrian/cyclist victims skew younger (25-44 for injuries) and lower income
- Facebook remains dominant for 30+ demographics; Instagram for 25-34
- Spanish-language social media is critical for west Phoenix/Maryvale audience
- Local community Facebook groups (neighborhood-specific) are active

### Creative Self-Qualification Signals

- Imagery: Crosswalks, intersections, bicycles, urban streets (not rural)
- Language: "Hit while crossing the street?" / "Injured while biking in Phoenix?" / "A driver hit you -- now what?"
- Bilingual creative essential for Phoenix west-side reach
- Emotional resonance is high: pedestrians are unambiguously innocent victims
- Nighttime imagery reflects the reality (76% of deaths in dark conditions)

## 5. DUI / Alcohol-Related Crash Victims

### Incident Volume

| Metric | 2024 |
|--------|------|
| Alcohol-related crashes | 5,520 |
| Fatalities | 347 |
| Injuries | 3,576 |
| Monthly injury volume | ~298 crashes with injuries |

Alcohol-related crashes account for approximately **28% of all fatal crashes** in Arizona. Victims in DUI crashes suffer more severe trauma than average, including traumatic brain injuries, internal organ damage, spinal cord injuries, and long-term disability.

### Who Are the Victims?

The "victim" in a DUI crash is NOT the impaired driver. Victims include:

**National breakdown (2022 NHTSA):**
- Passengers riding with the impaired driver: 12% of fatalities
- **Occupants of OTHER vehicles: 16% of fatalities** (the "innocent bystander on the road")
- **Non-occupants (pedestrians, cyclists): 12% of fatalities**
- The impaired driver themselves: 60% of fatalities

For our campaign, we target the **28% who are truly innocent victims** -- people in other cars, pedestrians, and cyclists struck by drunk drivers, plus passengers who were riding with an impaired driver.

### Demographic Profile

**Impaired Driver Demographics (context for understanding when/where victims are at risk):**
- **71.08% of alcohol-related crashes involve male drivers**
- **31.40% of drivers involved are ages 25-34** (largest single age group)
- Peak crash time: **2 AM - 3 AM** (bar closing hours)
- **43.6% of alcohol-related crashes occur on nights and weekends**

**Victim Demographics:**
- Male victims aged 15-34 are the most represented group, comprising **36.7% of fatalities and 35.7% of serious injuries** in alcohol-impaired crashes
- Pedestrian victims of DUI crashes skew older (55+) and lower-income (walking in areas with limited lighting)
- **25% of children under 14 killed in traffic crashes** died in alcohol-related incidents; approximately half were riding with the impaired driver

**Age of innocent victims (other vehicle occupants, pedestrians):**
- Mirrors the general driving/walking population with a skew toward 25-54 (working hours overlap with late-night impaired driving hours)
- Late-night drivers (shift workers, rideshare drivers, people returning from events) are overrepresented

### Geographic Distribution

- **Maricopa County: 3,544 of 5,520 alcohol-related crashes** (64.2%) -- proportional to population
- Entertainment districts (Scottsdale Old Town, Mill Avenue in Tempe, Downtown Phoenix, 4th Avenue in Tucson) generate disproportionate DUI activity
- Late-night highway segments connecting entertainment areas to residential areas are highest-risk corridors

### Time Patterns

| Time Period | Risk Level | Context |
|-------------|-----------|---------|
| Friday-Saturday nights | **Highest** | Bar/club closing hours |
| 2 AM - 3 AM | **Peak hour** | Post-bar-close departure |
| Sunday mornings | High | Lingering Saturday night impairment |
| Holiday weekends | Elevated | New Year's, July 4th, Labor Day, Super Bowl Sunday |
| Weekday nights | Moderate | Restaurant/happy hour departures |

### Lifestyle & Behavioral Traits

- DUI victims are defined by **wrong place, wrong time** -- they are often commuters, late-shift workers, or people out on normal errands
- **Shift workers** (healthcare, hospitality, logistics) driving home after midnight are particularly exposed
- **Rideshare drivers and passengers** are at risk during peak impaired driving hours
- The emotional resonance of DUI victimhood is very high: pure innocence against clear negligence

### Media Consumption

- Mirrors general Arizona population demographics (25-54, Facebook/Instagram dominant)
- Victims are not identifiable by platform behavior -- they are defined by the incident, not pre-existing traits
- Post-incident, DUI victims and their families consume legal content heavily via Google Search and local news

### Creative Self-Qualification Signals

- Language: "Hit by a drunk driver?" / "A DUI driver changed your life" / "You were doing everything right. They weren't."
- Emotional framing: innocence vs. negligence is the strongest creative angle in all PI advertising
- Nighttime imagery, headlights, shattered glass
- Reference to punitive damages (drunk driving often triggers punitive damage eligibility, increasing case value)

## 6. Moderate-to-Severe MVA Victims

### Incident Volume

This is the **largest category** -- the broad pool of people in car crashes with injuries more serious than soft tissue (whiplash, minor sprains). Includes broken bones, herniated discs, concussions/TBI, surgeries, internal injuries, long-term disability.

**Estimated monthly volume: 400-600 qualifying incidents**

(Derived from: 37,376 injury crashes/year in 2024 x ~15-20% meeting moderate-to-severe threshold = 5,600-7,475/year = 467-623/month)

### Demographic Profile

**Age:**

| Age Group | Fatalities (2024) | Injuries (2024) | Key Fact |
|-----------|------------------|-----------------|----------|
| 15-19 | 88 | 5,398 | Young, inexperienced drivers |
| 20-24 | 119 | 6,843 | High crash rate, lower seatbelt use |
| **25-34** | **210** | **11,032** | **Highest fatality AND injury count** |
| **35-44** | **197** | **8,396** | Second-highest fatalities |
| 45-54 | 154 | 6,643 | Established careers, high lost wages |
| 55-64 | 133 | 5,792 | Longer recovery, higher medical costs |
| 65+ | 175+ | 4,000+ | Highest fatality rate per crash |

**The 25-44 age group is the core.** They account for the most fatalities (407 combined), the most injuries (19,428 combined), and represent the highest-value cases due to significant lost wages, family dependents, and long recovery trajectories.

**Gender:**
- Males are overrepresented in fatal crashes across all age groups
- Males drive more miles, at higher speeds, with lower seatbelt compliance
- Teen males (15-24) have the lowest seatbelt use rate of any age-gender group

**Seatbelt Use:**
- Only 70% of vehicle occupants in trauma crashes were belted
- Unbelted death rate: **4.8%** vs. 1.4% for belted occupants
- American Indian/Alaska Native: 59% seatbelt compliance (lowest by race)
- Teen drivers (15-24): lowest compliance by age

**Income:**
- Spans the full income spectrum but middle-income ($50K-$100K) households are the core
- Phoenix median household income: $81,332
- These are people with enough economic activity that a serious injury creates real financial distress (mortgage, car payments, childcare) but without enough savings to absorb months without income

**Ethnicity:**
- Reflects Arizona's general population: ~51% White, ~32% Hispanic, ~5% Black, ~4% Native American
- Native Americans have a fatality rate of **74.3 per 100K** vs. the state average of 21.3 -- 3.5x overrepresentation
- Hispanic drivers are well-represented in the 25-44 crash-heavy age bracket due to Arizona's young Hispanic population

### Geographic Distribution

| Area | Total Crashes (2024) | Fatal Crashes | Injuries |
|------|---------------------|--------------|----------|
| Phoenix | 37,472 | 265 | 10,449 |
| Mesa | 8,022 | 46 | — |
| Tempe | 6,637 | 29 | — |
| Maricopa County (total) | 88,094 | 594 | — |
| Pima County | 8,867 | 182 | — |
| Pinal County | 5,702 | 93 | — |

**Most dangerous day:** Friday
**Most dangerous month:** April
**Peak fatal hour:** 6-7 PM
**Uninsured motorist rate:** ~12% (near national average of 13%)

### Lifestyle & Behavioral Traits

- **Commuters.** The average Phoenix resident drives alone (64.2%) with a 25.6-minute commute. The sprawling metro and wide, fast arterials create constant exposure.
- **Family-oriented.** The 25-44 core are raising children, managing households, carrying mortgages. A serious injury is a family crisis, not just a personal one.
- **Underinsured.** Arizona requires only minimum liability ($25K/$50K/$15K). Many at-fault drivers carry minimum coverage, leaving victims underinsured when medical bills exceed policy limits.
- **Working class to professional.** The spectrum is wide -- construction workers, nurses, teachers, small business owners, office workers. What unites them is economic vulnerability to a sudden income disruption.

### Media Consumption

- **Facebook:** Dominant for 30-54 age range. 80% adoption among 30-49 year olds; 58% daily use.
- **Instagram:** Strong for 25-34 age range. 50% of US adults use Instagram.
- **YouTube:** High reach across all age groups (85% of US adults).
- Local news apps and websites (AZ Central, AZFamily) are heavily consumed post-accident for information.

### Creative Self-Qualification Signals

- Broad injury language: "Seriously injured in a car accident?" / "Broken bones, surgery, or worse after a crash?"
- Reference to financial distress: "Bills piling up while you can't work?"
- Arizona-specific context: wide roads, sun glare, intersection crashes
- The creative has to exclude soft tissue without saying "soft tissue" -- injury severity language does the filtering

## 7. Rideshare Accident Victims

### Incident Volume

Rideshare-specific crash data for Arizona is not separately tracked by ADOT. However:

- The introduction of rideshare services nationally correlated with a **3% annual increase in vehicular fatalities**, translating to approximately 987 additional roadway deaths per year
- Accident rates per 100,000 people increased from 2.0 to 3.5 after Uber/Lyft market entry
- Phoenix is one of the top 10 Uber/Lyft markets in the US by ride volume

**Estimated monthly volume for Arizona:** Small but growing. Most rideshare accidents are MVAs that happen to involve an Uber/Lyft vehicle; they are not a separate crash type but a separate liability structure (access to Uber/Lyft's $1M commercial policy).

### Demographic Profile

**Users (potential passengers involved in crashes):**

| Demographic | Rideshare Usage Rate |
|-------------|---------------------|
| Age 18-29 | 45-51% have used rideshare |
| Age 30-49 | 36% |
| Age 50-64 | 23% |
| Age 65+ | 13% |
| Income $75K+ | 53% |
| Income <$30K | 24% |
| Urban | 45% (19% weekly) |
| Suburban | 40% (6% weekly) |
| Rural | 19% (5% weekly) |

**The typical rideshare user is 18-34, college-educated, urban, with household income of $75K+.**

**Drivers (who may be victims or at-fault):**
- Average age of Lyft driver in Arizona: 35
- 60% male, 40% female
- Majority ages 25-44
- Concentrated in Phoenix metropolitan area

**In Arizona specifically:**
- Uber holds ~76% of the rideshare market; Lyft holds ~24%
- Most Lyft drivers are concentrated in the Phoenix metro
- Usage skews heavily toward Scottsdale, Tempe, Downtown Phoenix, and airport corridors

### Lifestyle & Behavioral Traits

- **Young, urban, social.** Rideshare users are going to/from bars, restaurants, events, airports, and work
- **Late-night usage peaks** overlap with DUI crash risk hours -- rideshare passengers are exposed to impaired drivers on the road even though they themselves chose not to drive
- **ASU students and young professionals** in Tempe/Scottsdale are heavy rideshare users
- **Airport corridor (Sky Harbor)** generates high rideshare volume with distracted driving risk

### Media Consumption

- **Instagram-dominant** for the 18-29 core user base
- **TikTok** has high penetration in this demographic (38% of 18-24 users)
- **Facebook** for the 30-49 secondary segment
- This is the most digitally native of all case type segments

### Creative Self-Qualification Signals

- Language: "Injured in an Uber or Lyft?" / "Your rideshare crash isn't just a car accident"
- Emphasize the $1M commercial policy angle (unique to rideshare -- passengers don't know this)
- Urban, nightlife-adjacent imagery
- App-interface visual cues (phone with ride request)

## 8. Arizona Hispanic Population Deep-Dive

### Why This Matters

At **32.1% of the state population (~2.43M people)**, Arizona's Hispanic community is not a sub-segment -- it is a core audience. Any PI campaign in Arizona that does not actively address Hispanic demographics is leaving a third of the addressable market untouched.

### Population Distribution

## 11. Bilingual & Multicultural Creative Implications

### The Strategic Imperative

With 32.1% of Arizona's population being Hispanic and an even higher concentration in crash-heavy neighborhoods, bilingual creative is not optional -- it is a competitive advantage. Most PI firms running Meta ads in Arizona use English-only creative. Spanish-language and bilingual ads will:

1. **Reach an underserved audience.** The 29% of Hispanic social media users who primarily use Spanish are a large, undercompeted audience for PI advertising.
2. **Build trust through cultural resonance.** Campaigns using Spanish or Spanglish tend to create a stronger sense of belonging among Hispanic audiences.
3. **Self-qualify under Special Ad Categories.** Since we cannot target by ethnicity, Spanish-language creative naturally self-qualifies -- only Spanish speakers will engage.
4. **Access the highest-crash neighborhoods.** Maryvale (75.1% Hispanic) and South Phoenix (65-70% Hispanic) have the highest pedestrian crash rates and the highest Spanish-language household rates.

### Recommended Approach

| Creative Version | Language | Target Segment | Rationale |
|-----------------|----------|---------------|-----------|
| **Primary English** | English | All segments | Reaches ~60% of Hispanic audience + all non-Hispanic |
| **Primary Spanish** | Spanish | Pedestrian/cyclist, MVA | Reaches the 29% Spanish-dominant, particularly Maryvale/South Phoenix |
| **Bilingual/Spanglish** | Mixed | Motorcycle, DUI, MVA | Resonates with the 25% bilingual segment; signals cultural competence |

### Key Creative Considerations

- **Do not translate word-for-word.** Spanish creative should be culturally adapted, not translated. "Abogado de accidentes" carries different connotations than "accident lawyer."
- **Family framing resonates.** Hispanic cultural values emphasize family -- creative showing impact on family (not just the individual victim) will perform better.
- **Trust signals are critical.** Hispanic audiences show higher skepticism toward advertising. Testimonials, firm credentials, and "no cost unless we win" messaging are particularly important.
- **WCTL should confirm Spanish intake capability.** If WCTL's intake team cannot handle Spanish-language calls, we need a bilingual handoff process or we risk burning leads.
- **Univision/Telemundo visual language.** Hispanic audiences are familiar with the visual language of Spanish-language television; creative that mirrors this aesthetic (warm tones, family imagery, direct address) will feel native.

### Multicultural Considerations Beyond Hispanic

- **Native American population (4.2%):** Dramatically overrepresented in traffic fatalities (74.3 per 100K vs. 21.3 state average). However, tribal communities are difficult to reach via Meta ads due to lower social media penetration in rural reservation areas and complex jurisdictional issues with on-reservation crashes.
- **Black population (4.6%):** Proportionally represented in crash data. No specialized creative approach required beyond inclusive imagery.
- **Asian population (3.6%):** Lower crash involvement rate. Not a priority segment for the pilot.
