## Governing Principle (Hegarty)

**Every hook filters before it persuades.** The first three words carry the severity gate. If word three hasn't closed the door on the wrong person, the hook is doing the quiz funnel's job for it, and we're paying Meta to attract leads we'll reject.

Every asset below is scored against:
- **Filter strength** — does the hook repel soft-tissue / low-severity self-identifiers?
- **Persona fit** — does this hook speak to the emotional truth of the named persona?
- **Compliance** — AZ Bar ER 7.1 (truthfulness), ER 7.3 (no direct solicitation), Meta SAC, TCPA one-to-one.

## Legend — Fields Per Asset

- **Hook (text-on-screen):** The 3–7 words that open the scroll-stop moment.
- **Primary text (Meta feed body):** The 1–3 sentence body copy that appears above / around the creative in the feed.
- **CTA button:** Meta's standard button (Learn More / Get Quote / Apply Now / etc.) + supporting text.
- **Visual direction:** All AI-generated. No stock footage, no live shoots. **Each asset gets a consistent protagonist character** (one AI-generated face maintained across all assets for that character), built from a single reference image per character. **4 locked protagonists for Phase 1:** Marco (M1/M2/M3), Sarah (S1), Laura (S2), Diana (S5). Full character briefs in the Protagonist Character Briefs section below — paste each verbatim into every prompt for that character. The protagonist is the emotional anchor — viewers see themselves or someone they know, not an abstraction.
- **Persona vs. casting (Steel).** The personas in Doc 2 are the psychological / behavioral truth — emotional profiles, not demographic mandates. Ethnicity and specific appearance of the on-screen protagonist is a casting decision optimized for reach under Meta Special Ad Categories. The persona emotional profile stays; the face reflects AZ's 51% White / 32% Hispanic / 4.6% Black / 4.2% AI-AN / 3.6% Asian composition for the broadest-net assets, with Spanish-specific casting (Miguel) held tight for Spanish-audience resonance.
- **Indistinguishable-from-real is the SC brand standard** — no "AI slop" aesthetic. Per Davis's note: the campaign is using ChatGPT Images 2.0 (or equivalent current-gen stack) and accuracy is not a concern; prompts can be as detailed as the briefs below specify.
- **Production feel:** Barry Hott Ugly Ads Doctrine — iPhone-feel, text-on-screen primary, sound-off comprehension mandatory.
- **Self-qualification mechanics:** Which of Doc 5 §A's four filter strategies are working (severity language / situation specificity / visual qualification / negative self-qualification).
- **Persona fit:** Which persona in Doc 2 this is written to.
- **Rationale:** Why this hook, not another.

### How to use these briefs

1. **Generate 3–4 candidate portrait images per character** using the brief verbatim. Neutral background, medium shot, even lighting. Davis picks one per character. That's the locked reference.
2. **Prop + side consistency matters — every identifying mark must persist across every frame:**
    - **Marco:** small scar above **right eyebrow** (from dirtbike spill), 3-day stubble, weathered brown leather vest, silver wedding band on **left hand**, forearm tan line where riding gloves stop, scarred knuckles
    - **Sarah:** black canvas arm brace on **LEFT forearm** (Velcro), pale-blue hospital scrubs visible under open beige jacket, thin white-gold wedding band, subtle hint of early-second-trimester pregnancy, small scar on chin (childhood)
    - **Laura:** cane in **LEFT hand** (struck on right side), soft ACE wrap visible at **right ankle** beneath jean cuff, old scar on **left forearm** (kitchen accident), plain white-gold wedding band left hand, small stud earrings, thin silver collar chain
    - **Diana:** black medical-grade fabric arm sling cradling **RIGHT arm** (strap crosses body from left shoulder), faint yellowing fading bruise along **right jawline** (old, not fresh), reading glasses pushed up into hair, thin silver necklace, simple wedding band, small mole above left lip
    - These side-specific details must stay constant across every generation for that character — a flipped prop, missing scar, or changed side will read as two different people to the audience and break the character-consistency lock.
3. **Scene-specific prompting:** once the reference is locked, use it as the character reference in ChatGPT Images 2.0's character-consistency mode, then write the scene prompt (garage / intersection / kitchen / etc.) with the character-specific brief pasted in. Do not re-define the character per scene — reference the locked image.
4. **Continuity check before launch:** line up all 3 Marco assets side-by-side. Same person? Same scar? Same vest? Same hands? If any frame reads as "a different guy wearing the same vest," regenerate from the locked reference.

## Motorcycle Creatives (M1–M3) — Identity-Based Self-Qualification

> **Architecture note:** All 6 creatives (M1–M3 and S1/S2/S5 below) run in **a single Meta campaign, single ad set** (see Doc 6 §2). The motorcycle grouping and severe-injury grouping below are for readability — they are NOT separate ad sets. ### M1 — Identity (Video · 15–30 sec · 1:1 + 9:16)

**Persona:** Marco Reyes — 38, Mesa, HVAC technician, Harley Street Glide, intersection crash, mid-shaft femur fracture. Loss: identity as a rider. *(Doc 2 §1 shows age 35 — the on-screen casting brief is 38 per the character brief above ["weathered outdoor-AZ rider with faint gray at temples"]. Persona age ≠ casting age per the persona-vs-casting principle.)*

**Hook (text-on-screen):**
> **"Riders don't ask for sympathy. They ask for what's fair."**

**Primary text (Meta feed):**
> A driver didn't see you. An insurance company's first offer isn't designed to. For Arizona riders seriously injured in a motorcycle crash, the law says more. No fee unless we win.

**CTA:** Learn More → lands on quiz. Supporting line: *"See what your case could be worth — 60 seconds, free."*

**Visual direction (all AI, Marco protagonist):**
- 0:00–0:04 — Medium shot, Marco sitting on a low wall or garage stool in jeans and a weathered leather vest, Street Glide visible at rest beside him. Desert golden hour light on his face. He's looking off-camera, not at us — thoughtful, not sad. Quiet strength. Text overlay begins: *"Riders don't ask for sympathy."*
- 0:04–0:10 — Slow push in on Marco. He's weighing something. Not acting, not posing. Just a rider sitting with what happened. Text completes: *"Riders don't ask for sympathy."*
- 0:10–0:16 — Match cut to garage interior. Marco in the background out of focus, foreground: keys on a hook, helmet on a shelf with visible dust, empty parking spot where the bike used to be. Text overlay: *"They ask for what's fair."*
- 0:16–0:22 — Marco's hands — working hands, scarred knuckles — resting on his knees. Held for 3 seconds. Text fades to CTA: *"Arizona riders — free case review. No fee unless we win."*
- 0:22–end — Logo / disclaimer frame. "Advertising. For Arizona riders injured in a motorcycle accident."

**Production feel:** Marco's face is the emotional anchor. Don't cast him as "injured" — cast him as a rider who has been through something. Natural light only. No score. Ambient desert wind / a single distant engine in the opening shot, then silence in the garage. Text serif or slab — weight matters. Handheld micro-movement acceptable on the push-in. **Character reference image for Marco: establish in prompt once, re-use across M1/M2/M3.**

**Self-qualification mechanics:**
- Situation specificity: "motorcycle crash" in body. Strong.
- Negative self-qualification: "seriously injured" in body — filters minor spills.
- Visual qualification: Absence of the bike > any injury imagery.
- Severity language: Light in the hook itself. Carried by body.

**Persona fit:** Marco said out loud what he wanted — "not to feel like a number." Hook is identity-based third-person. "Riders" is the word he uses. "What's fair" is the word he'd use if someone asked him what he wanted. Nothing here promises millions.

**Rationale:** This is the 5/5 creative-distinctiveness asset from Doc 3. The hook doesn't scream severity because the whole creative speaks to identity — and identity-based creative self-qualifies by refusing to land on non-riders. Motorcycle audience reads "riders" as "me." Non-riders read it as "not me." Hegarty's first-three-words principle is met by word one.

**Rationale:** Loss hook is the deprivation-thinking twin to M1's identity hook. Where M1 speaks to community and fairness, M2 speaks to the slow accumulation of loss — which is Marco at the point where he's actually ready to hire someone. Paired with M1 (via algorithm allocation, same ad set), the two hooks cover the emotional range of the motorcycle audience without either cannibalizing the other.

## Production Standards (Hott)

Every asset must pass:
1. **Sound-off comprehension test.** Mute the video. If the text-on-screen doesn't carry the hook + filter + CTA, rework.
2. **3-second rule.** First 3 seconds must contain the hook. Not the setup — the hook.
3. **Protagonist anchor test.** Within the first 5 seconds of any video, the persona's protagonist must be in frame (even briefly). The face is the emotional anchor. No pure b-roll opens.
4. **Indistinguishable-from-real test.** If any AI shot reads as "generated" (uncanny face, off-texture skin, weird hand geometry), re-prompt or re-generate. SC's brand standard is that the viewer cannot tell it's AI.
5. **iPhone-feel test.** Does it look organic in a Facebook feed, or does it announce "ad"? If it announces, soften the production.
6. **First-three-words test (Hegarty).** Underline the first three words of the text-on-screen. Do they carry severity, situation, or identity? If no, rewrite.
7. **Resonance test (Steel).** Read the hook aloud while imagining the named persona reading it at 11pm scrolling. Do they stop? If no, rewrite.
8. **Character consistency check.** Before Week 1 launch, line up all of Marco's three assets (M1/M2/M3) side-by-side. Is it visibly the same person? If not, re-generate from the locked Marco reference image.
