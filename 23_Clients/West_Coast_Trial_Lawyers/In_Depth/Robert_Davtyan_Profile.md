## The Ego Management Problem

Unlike Tommie (who was secure enough to say "I don't know" and respect straight-shooters), Robert is **structurally insecure** about his vendor decisions.

- Every Socratic question must be designed so Robert "discovers" the problem without being told it — because being told makes him defensive, and defensiveness kills the deal.
- He needs to feel smart for seeing what's wrong with the current setup, not stupid for having missed it.
- **The pivot phrase across the whole call:** *"Yeah — and nobody gets shown that by their current vendor. That's just how the industry works. You're not supposed to know."*
- That phrase takes the blame off Robert and puts it on the industry. Use it after any Socratic question that surfaces a gap in his current setup.
- Related variants:
  - *"Most marketers in your seat have never had a vendor volunteer that."*
  - *"The industry trains you to watch CPL because CPL is the thing vendors can compete on. They're not going to give you CPSC — it's the number that would let you compare them."*
  - *"You're doing the job the way the job is built. The fact that you're asking about SB-37 right now tells me you're already thinking further ahead than most people in your seat."*

## Neama Rahmani — The Absent Buyer

**Who he is:**
- Co-founder, President, and the firm's public face
- Nationally recognized trial lawyer
- TV legal analyst, media commentator
- Has handled thousands of civil cases, won over $1B personally
- Has built a carefully polished trial-lawyer brand over a decade

**Why Neama matters for this deal:**
- **He's the check-signer.** Robert is the marketing guy, but Neama signs the vendor contract (or approves the marketing director to sign).
- **He's a trial lawyer — he understands liability.** SB-37 statutory damages, private right of action, and vendor liability attribution are the exact type of risk language Neama is fluent in. He won't dismiss it.
- **His personal brand IS the firm's asset.** Anything that runs under his name threatens his personal equity directly. He is *personally* exposed, not just legally.
- **He would be furious if he saw the current vendor ads.** This is the emotional stake Robert can bring upstairs. If Robert walks into Neama's office with "here's what's running under your name right now and we can't even pull it on demand," Neama reacts.

**The mental model for the call:** Activate Neama as an imaginary third party in the room. Every time Robert describes his current setup, the Socratic follow-up is some variant of:
- *"If Neama saw that, what would he say?"*
- *"How would Neama react to that answer?"*
- *"What would Neama want you to do about that?"*

This does three things at once:
1. Gets Robert to apply Neama's trial-lawyer risk instinct to his own operation
2. Lets Robert externalize the blame ("Neama would be pissed" ≠ "Robert screwed up")
3. Creates the reason for Call 2 — the answer to every "what next?" is "let's get Neama in the room"

## Behavioral Framework — How to Approach Robert

| Principle | Application |
|---|---|
| **Pratfall effect (Aronson 1966)** | Voluntarily acknowledging SC's newness *increases* credibility on every other claim. Don't hide it — lead with it. See `Trust_Experience_Reframe.md`. |
| **Loss aversion (Kahneman)** | Frame SB-37 exposure as a loss Robert is currently unaware of. Loss aversion is 2.25x stronger than gain appeal. |
| **Cognitive ease** | Robert is not an engineer. Stories and anecdotes beat tables. Reserve tables for pricing only. |
| **Self-concept protection** | Every question is designed so Robert is the one who *discovers* the problem. This lets him frame the insight as his own when he walks it upstairs. |
| **Commitment & consistency (Cialdini)** | Get him to say small "no"s throughout the call. "Is it crazy to think your current vendors might be running crash footage?" → "Well, yeah, maybe." Small commitments compound. |
| **Reactance (Brehm 1966)** | Don't push. Don't close. Don't ask twice. The moment Robert feels sold-to, reactance kicks in. Give him space. |

## What NOT to Do With Robert

- **Don't name his current vendors.** Even if he mentions them — redirect: *"I'm not here to talk about any specific vendor. Every vendor in this space has a hard time with the same problem."*
- **Don't critique decisions he's already made.** Critique the industry he operates in.
- **Don't show up with slides.** This is a conversation. Share files on screen only if he asks to see them.
- **Don't pitch Neama through Robert.** ("When Neama sees this...") Instead, equip Robert to pitch Neama. ("What would Neama need to see to feel confident?")
- **Don't try to close Robert.** He can't close. The close is "what does your next step with Neama look like?"
- **Don't treat him as peer-to-Tommie.** He's not running P&L. He's running campaigns. Speak his language: creative, messaging, brand, compliance, speed-to-lead.
- **Don't dwell on SC's Disney/Google/NFL history like a résumé.** Use it in service of the trust reframe only (see `Trust_Experience_Reframe.md`), not as general name-dropping.
- **Don't ask him to commit to anything.** Call 1 ends with "I'll send you material for Neama." That's the only ask.
