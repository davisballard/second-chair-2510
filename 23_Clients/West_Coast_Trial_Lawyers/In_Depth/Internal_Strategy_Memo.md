## The SB-37 Wedge — Why This Call Even Exists

Sasha's SB-37 email landed this meeting. Robert responded within 20 minutes. That's a buying signal. The SB-37 language hit something — either an existing internal conversation Robert has been having internally, or a gap in the current vendor oversight, or both.

**SB-37 is context, not a quiz.** The call references it in the opening frame of Phase 2 — as part of why SC shows clients creative — but it never becomes an interrogation topic.

**Critical rules:**
- **Do NOT ask Robert if he's familiar with SB-37.** Assume he is. He's a marketing exec at a California PI firm — if he didn't know SB-37, he wouldn't have taken this meeting. Asking gives him a chance to feel dumb or patronized.
- **Do NOT lecture Robert on SB-37.** No "statutory damages $5K-$100K per violation" facts dump unless he asks for specifics.
- **Do NOT front-load Neama.** Robert hasn't mentioned his boss. Keep the discovery peer-to-peer, not "imagine your boss walking in."
- **Frame SC's position declaratively, then ask neutral present-tense questions about how Robert's current setup works.** The wedge isn't "when's the last time you failed" — it's "here's how we work and why, how does your setup handle that?"

**The golden sequence** (from Discovery Call Script Phase 2) — **two questions, not five**:

**Opening frame (Davis-declarative):**
> *"One thing that's central to how we work — showing clients the actual creative before it runs, and on an ongoing basis. It's non-negotiable for us. Partly for brand coherence, partly for SB-37 compliance. So I'm curious how things work on your side."*

1. **Q1 (neutral present-tense):** *"Are your current lead vendors showing you the ad creative they're running for WCTL?"* → he says no / only at kickoff / hedges
2. **Q2 (denial-escape close):** *"For all we know your vendors are running completely on-brand clean creative — that's possible, right? Okay — so the real question isn't whether it's clean, it's how you'd actually know. If someone walked in tomorrow and asked you to show them everything running under your firm's name this week, could you pull it together?"* → silence, let it land

**Between and after: the industry-blame release valve.** *"Yeah — and that's honestly the industry norm. Most vendors keep the creative in a black box. Nobody in your role gets shown the creative as a matter of course."*

Robert exits Phase 2 having mentally agreed to three things without being told any of them: (1) he doesn't know what's running under his firm's name, (2) SB-37 creates real personal exposure for Neama, (3) he has no documentation to prove compliance.

**That's the entire wedge.**

## Call Approach — The Ego Management Protocol

Robert is junior and structurally defensive about his vendor choices. Every technique must account for that.

1. **Never critique his vendors directly. Critique the industry.** Every time Robert surfaces something uncomfortable, take the blame off him and put it on how the industry is built.
2. **Never tell him things. Ask questions that let him discover them.** 70% questions / 30% statements.
3. **Always give him an out.** Pivot phrase: *"Yeah, most marketers in your seat don't get shown that by their vendors. That's just how the industry works. You're not supposed to know."*
4. **Always arm him for Neama.** Every objection handler ends with "what would help you bring this to Neama?"
5. **Protect his self-concept.** Every insight he arrives at should feel like his own, not something Davis taught him.
