**He's junior and defensive.** Not because he's weak — because his structural position (he owns the current vendor relationships) means every question about those vendors is implicitly a question about his judgment.

**Every Socratic question needs an "out" built in.** When Robert realizes something uncomfortable about his current setup, Davis immediately takes blame off Robert and puts it on the industry: *"Yeah — and nobody gets shown that by their current vendor. That's just how the industry works."*

**He won't fill silence comfortably.** Tommie was comfortable with silence. Robert will fill the void. That's where the best information comes from. **Count to 4 after every calibrated question. Don't rescue him.**

**He's a Communications major.** Speak his language — messaging, tone, positioning, brand. Don't speak in spreadsheets.

**He's a performer for Neama.** Everything Robert says is being pre-rehearsed for an imaginary future conversation with Neama. Use that. Give him lines he can take straight upstairs.

## PHASE 1: OPENING (3 minutes) — Gentle disarm + conversational frame-set

### Davis opens — DIRECTION, not verbatim

**This is a direction, not a script. Don't read it. Feel it.** Casual, conversational, low-key. The goal is to set a relaxed frame where Robert's guard doesn't go up. Do NOT use the words "trash your current setup" (too loaded) or "flip the normal dynamic" (too meta, reads as a sales tactic).

**The three beats:**

**Beat 1 — Appreciate the time + gentle disarm:**

Something like: *"Robert, appreciate you making the time. You obviously get pitched by lead gen companies all the time, and you've got relationships that are working or they wouldn't still be there — so just want to say upfront, we're not here to critique your current setup. More just wanted to share something you might not have seen yet and see what you think."*

*(pause — let him respond or nod)*

**Beat 2 — Name the SB-37 thread that got the meeting:**

Something like: *"Sasha mentioned SB-37 in the email — honestly, I think it's the most interesting thing happening in California lead gen right now, and the part nobody's really talking about. Wanted to dig into that with you."*

**Beat 3 — Soft frame-set on conversation style:**

Something casual and low-key. Example: *"And one thing before I start asking some questions — on initial calls like this, we like to run it less as us telling you about what we do, and more as figuring out exactly what you want and what's going on on your end. Then we can talk about how we do things or answer any questions from there. Sound good?"*

*(pause, let him answer)*

**Why this works:**
- Casual tone keeps his guard down — no "I know you're thinking X" performative accusation audit
- "Not here to critique your current setup" is the load-bearing line that de-fangs vendor-protection defensiveness — much softer than "trash"
- The conversation-style frame is explained as *how we usually run initial calls*, not as a deliberate technique ("flip the dynamic") — that keeps it from reading as a sales move
- Asking for buy-in via "Sound good?" is low-stakes and gives him a soft yes without committing to anything

**Tone notes:**
- Old friend calling, not vendor pitching
- Conversational, not performed
- "Sound good?" / "Cool with you?" — either works. Pick what's natural.
- If Robert pushes back or seems guarded, drop another layer of casual — don't escalate.

## PHASE 2: CREATIVE DISCOVERY — the heart of the call (8–10 minutes)

**This is a brand-quality conversation, not a compliance conversation.** Robert is a Communications major who serves as WCTL's press contact. He thinks in messaging, tone, positioning. Speak his native language — creative, brand, on-brand, off-brand, proud, aligned.

**Three critical rules for this phase:**

1. **Do NOT ask Robert if he's familiar with SB-37.** Assume he is. He's a marketing exec at a California PI firm — if he didn't know SB-37, he wouldn't have taken this meeting. Asking reads as either patronizing or as a quiz that gives him a chance to feel dumb. SB-37 gets referenced as context alongside brand coherence — never as a test.

2. **Do NOT front-load Neama.** Robert hasn't mentioned his boss. Name-dropping his boss early reads as "we're big-leaguing you" and makes him defensive. Neama comes in Phase 7.

3. **Frame SC's position declaratively, then ask neutral present-tense questions.** The wedge is "here's how we work and why — how does your current setup handle that?" — not "when's the last time you failed to do this?" Robert is the brand person. Talk to him as a peer.

**Psychology of this sequence (for your mental model, not for Robert):**

- **Opening frame** establishes SC's declarative position (we show the creative, it's non-negotiable, here's why) and plants SB-37 as context — not as a quiz
- **Q1** asks a neutral present-tense peer-to-peer question about how Robert's current setup works. Robert answers honestly because it's not accusatory. His answer IS the wedge — delivered in his own voice.
- **Q2** gives him the denial out ("maybe they're fine") and immediately closes it with the documentation question
- **Q3** (optional) makes Robert the brand evaluator — asks him to imagine if the creative would pass his own brand taste test. He's evaluating vendor work as a peer, not defending himself.
- **Q4** (optional) surfaces past pain or latent fear

**None of these questions treat Robert like he's failed at his job.** They treat him like the brand person he is — asking neutral questions about his setup, asking him to evaluate vendor work from his own professional sensibility. That's the register that opens him up.

**No SB-37 quiz. No Neama pressure. No "when's the last time" gotcha.** SB-37 gets referenced once in the opening frame as context. Neama is absent. The questions are all present-tense and neutral.

**By the end of Phase 2, Robert has mentally agreed to three things without being told any of them:**
1. He doesn't actually know what's running under his firm's name
2. SB-37 creates real personal exposure for Neama
3. He has no documentation to prove compliance even if it exists

**That's the entire wedge.**
