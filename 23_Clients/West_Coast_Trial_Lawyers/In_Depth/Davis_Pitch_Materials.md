## 4. How Compliance Works — SB-37 + FCC 2025

**The California piece — SB-37:**

SB-37 went into effect January 1, 2026. It changed who carries the liability for lead vendor advertising in California. Under SB-37, the firm is responsible for what the vendor runs under the firm's name — not just what the firm runs directly. If a vendor is using crash footage, promising settlement amounts, skipping mandatory disclosures, or running prohibited content, that exposure lands on the firm. Statutory damages from $5,000 to $100,000 per violation. Private right of action — meaning anyone can sue, not just the state bar.

- No crash footage (real or simulated)
- No settlement amount promises
- No "get more than you already settled for" framing
- Trusted-advisor tone (not ambulance-chaser)
- Mandatory disclosures on every placement
- Firm disclosure on every funnel
- Everything documentable and auditable

Every single ad is visible to you in the dashboard before it runs. If you want approval-before-launch as a standing requirement, we set that up.

FCC 2025 changed everything in lead gen consent. The old way — a hyperlink that says "see our 500 marketing partners" and a single checkbox — is now illegal. Every lead we generate has one-to-one explicit consent.

## 9. How We Measure Performance — Projected Attorney Fee → Projected ROAS

**The metric we lead with: Projected Attorney Fee per Lead → Projected ROAS on the cohort.** Computed on day one from the Fit Score × sign probability × median attorney fee by case type. Zero data required from the firm. Canonical source: `01_Identity/Projected_Attorney_Fee_Model.md`.

This is what sets Second Chair apart from every other vendor in the space. Most vendors compete on CPL (a vanity metric) or report CPSC 6-12 months after the fact (a lagging metric that averages case values and hides the mix). Projected Attorney Fee is the only measurement that gives the firm a real read on the value of the pipeline on day one, before any case has signed.

### The Formula

```
Projected Attorney Fee per Lead = Expected Attorney Fee × Sign Probability

  Expected Fee = f(injury severity, case type, treatment, fault, recency)
  Sign Probability = f(Lead Grade: A/B/C/D)

Projected ROAS = Σ(Projected Fee) / Total Spend
```

### The Measurement Stack

**Day One — Projected Attorney Fee + Projected ROAS**
The primary metric. Computed from the Fit Score × sign probability × base fee model. Zero data required from the firm. This is what lives on the dashboard.

**Ongoing (as signs come in) — Sign Rate Validation**
A simple yes/no per lead — did this sign. Not protected by privilege or NDA because it's a conversion event, not case data. Validates the projected model; tunes it if sign rate deviates from grade distribution.

**Long-term (6-24+ months, optional) — Actual ROAS Refinement**
Only if the firm can share aggregate revenue attribution without violating NDAs or Rule 1.6 confidentiality. Most PI settlements carry NDAs, so aggregate is the realistic ceiling for most firms. Totally acceptable if the firm can't share even at the aggregate level — the projected ROAS and sign rate remain the honest measurement floor.

### CPSC — The Alongside Metric

Cost Per Signed Case tracks alongside Projected ROAS as a secondary confirmation metric. Both numbers improve together. CPSC is NOT the primary metric because:

1. It's lagging — requires 6-12+ months of case resolution data to be meaningful
2. It averages case values across types, hiding the mix
3. It creates comparison shopping — firms benchmark vendors on "lower CPSC" without asking what's in the mix

**If the firm can't share any data at all:** Projected ROAS still works. That's the whole point. The measurement floor is solid from day one regardless of what the firm can or cannot share.
