## WHY FAIR CASE

Three naming rounds produced one clear survivor.

**"Fair" is the claimant's own word.** Research across both rounds confirmed that accident victims don't say "justice" or "claim" when talking to a friend or their spouse. They say "I just want things to be fair." "Fair" requires zero decoding — it's already in her vocabulary before she sees the ad.

**"Case" is accessible lay language.** "Claim" is what adjusters say. "Case" is what people say — "you might have a case," "what's my case worth?" It's lower-commitment, lower-litigation-signal, and not yet owned in the B2C lead space the way "Claim" is.

**Together they have a secondary meaning.** Not just a fair case *evaluation* — a case worth making. There's an inherent argument for fairness in the name itself.

---

## THE URL DECISION

**getafaircase.com** over **faircase.net**

.com authority wins for a consumer trust brand. The "get a" prefix functions as a CTA baked into the URL — in direct response advertising, that's a structural asset, not a liability. The brand name in all copy and creative is **Fair Case**. The URL is the action wrapper.

Where "get a fair case" reads awkwardly as a phrase, the fix is always in the surrounding copy: *"Find out what your case is worth at getafaircase.com."* The brand is not the URL — the brand is Fair Case.

## NIGEL'S CLOSE

*"Three rounds. Twelve territories. Fifty-plus names generated, filtered, debated. The name that survives all of it is Fair Case — because it's already the claimant's vocabulary before we showed up. That's the highest bar in naming: you're not teaching anyone a word. You're using the word they were already thinking.*

*getafaircase.com. Visual identity opens next."*
