# Fair Case — Brand Overview

**Status:** Active — confirmed March 12, 2026  
**Domain:** getafaircase.com  
**Parent company:** Second Chair  
**Audience:** Personal injury claimants (consumer-facing)  

---

## The Name

**Fair Case.** Two words that do the work before the brand has to.

"Fair" is the claimant's own word. Consumer research across three naming rounds confirmed that accident victims don't say "justice" or "claim" when talking to a spouse or a friend. They say *"I just want things to be fair."* The word requires zero decoding — it is already in her vocabulary before she sees the ad.

"Case" is accessible lay language. "Claim" is what adjusters say. "Case" is what people say — *"you might have a case," "what's my case worth?"* It carries lower litigation signal and is not yet owned in the B2C lead space the way "Claim" is.

Together, there's a secondary meaning: not just a fair case *evaluation* — a case worth making. The name carries an inherent argument for fairness.

**The URL:** getafaircase.com — .com authority over faircase.net. The "get a" prefix is a CTA embedded in the URL itself. In all copy and creative, the brand is **Fair Case**. The URL is the action wrapper.

## Brand Position (Consumer-Facing)

Fair Case helps injured people understand their rights and connect with attorneys who can fight for them — at no upfront cost.

**Tone:** Clear, plain-spoken, on the claimant's side. Never lawyerly. Never clinical. Never corporate.

**Promise:** You deserve to know what your case is worth.

**Core messages:**
- No upfront cost — attorneys work on contingency
- Time limits exist — don't wait
- Insurance companies have lawyers. You should too.
- Find out if you have a case — it only takes a few minutes

## What Fair Case Is (and Isn't)

| Fair Case IS | Fair Case IS NOT |
|---|---|
| The consumer intake brand | The law firm product |
| What injured people see | What attorneys see |
| The top-of-funnel entry point | A law firm or legal advice provider |
| An intake qualification service | A lead marketplace (never mentioned) |

Second Chair is invisible to the consumer. Fair Case is invisible to the firm. They never cross.

## The Funnel

Claimants arrive via paid ads → land at getafaircase.com → complete a 6-question quiz:

1. Type of accident (car, truck, motorcycle, pedestrian, rideshare, other)
2. When it happened (within 30 days / 1–6 months / 6–12 months / over a year)
3. Medical treatment status (ongoing / completed / planning / none)
4. Fault (not my fault / unsure / partially my fault)
5. Whether they already have an attorney
6. Injury severity (minor / moderate / severe / life-altering)

After the quiz: name, phone, email, TCPA consent.

## Target Audience

**Primary:** Motor vehicle accident victims (car, truck, motorcycle, rideshare, pedestrian)  
**Timeframe preference:** 30–90 days post-accident (sweet spot for firm partners)  
**Age range:** 25–65  
**Geography:** Initially Colorado, expanding to CA, NY, FL, TX  

**Mindset at the moment of contact:**
- Stressed and overwhelmed
- Dealing with insurance companies
- Unsure if they have a case or how the process works
- Cost-sensitive ("I can't afford a lawyer")
- Looking for someone to handle it for them

**The flooding problem:** Once a user engages with one PI ad, Meta floods them with competitor ads. Fair Case has about one moment to stand out before the feed moves on. Creative does the qualifying in the first 3 seconds.

## Brand Voice (Consumer-Facing)

| Do | Don't |
|---|---|
| Use "you" — always direct | Use "clients" or "claimants" |
| Say "case" not "claim" | Say "lawsuit" or "litigation" |
| Say "no upfront cost" | Say "contingency fee" |
| Be plain and human | Be clinical or lawyerly |
| Reference the insurance company as the adversary | Make it feel like a legal transaction |
| Use short sentences | Use long explanatory paragraphs |

**Critical compliance note:** In Meta ads specifically, "you" is radioactive when paired with injury/condition language. "Are you injured?" gets ads rejected. In paid ad copy, use third-person language: "Accident victims may be entitled to compensation." See `02_Social/COPY_VOICE_GUIDE.md` for the full rules.

## The Consumer Empowerment Mandate

This is what Fair Case advertising owes the person who sees it. Not a brand statement — the emotional substrate of every creative decision.

The accident victim is inside a claims system built by the insurance company, running in the insurance company's favor. The adjuster calls within hours of the accident. Their frame is established before the claimant has any competing information. The process was built by the entity with the power, and it runs in that entity's favor. The claimant is alone in that system unless someone shows them they are not.

**What the ad must make that person feel:**

*"I'm not being played. These people see me. I'm going to be connected with the right person, someone who knows what I'm dealing with, someone who's going to fight. And I deserve this."*

The emotional targets, in order: **heard → fired up → clear-eyed → ready to act.**

Not fear. Not desperation. Not the lowest-common-denominator urgency that the rest of the PI category runs on. Genuine empowerment — the feeling that fighting back is worth it, that they are not greedy for wanting what they are owed, that the justice system is one of the few places where a regular person can actually get what's theirs.

**The founding conviction behind this:**

These founders have been inside bureaucratic systems built by power and running in power's favor. Davis's girlfriend navigating US immigration. Sasha's family surviving systems designed to exclude them. They understand at a biographical level what it means to be in the claimant's position — talented, legitimate, working hard, and still losing because the system wasn't built for them. That is not metaphor. It is the direct emotional source of what Fair Case advertising must do.

## The B2C Invitation Line (Locked)

*"Before you decide anything, make one call. Ten minutes. Free. Here's what you should know."*

This is the Fair Case posture in consumer advertising. Not a tagline — the posture. The specific emotional and informational position Fair Case holds in the claimant's world: one fact, one low-barrier action, before the insurance company's frame is permanently established.

---

## The Honest Advertising Posture

Fair Case advertising is honest not because it was decided to be ethical, but because the business model cannot be profitable through dishonesty. What this means in practice:

- **No manufactured urgency** — the SOL deadline is real; state it. An artificial "act now!" where none exists is manipulation.
- **No implied settlement values** — the quiz estimate is a display tool only; never claim a specific dollar outcome.
- **No actors presenting as real clients** — ever.
- **No fear as the primary motivator** — information is the motivator. The insurance company's tactics, the settlement gap, the deadline — all real. State them specifically. Don't amplify them into dread.
- **The ad earns the response** — the claimant calls because something true moved them. Not because they were tricked.

---

## The "Advertising Does Intake Work" Hypothesis

The central strategic claim that makes Fair Case commercially valuable — not just ethically preferable:

*The claimant who arrives through a Fair Case funnel was honestly persuaded. They arrive with accurate expectations, ready to engage, without the attorney having to undo anything first.*

Most PI lead gen advertising promises the claimant millions, creates rage, and delivers someone who expects a six-figure settlement from a fender bender. The intake call then becomes damage control — managing down expectations the advertising inflated. That is not a lead. That is a problem.

Fair Case's honest advertising means the claimant arrives knowing what they have. They didn't call because they were told they'd get $500K. They called because a specific, true piece of information — the $33,700 gap, the SOL deadline, the fact that the insurance company already has lawyers — made a brief, free call the obvious next step.

That is a different product. Not a different marketing strategy. The ethical choice and the commercial choice are the same.
