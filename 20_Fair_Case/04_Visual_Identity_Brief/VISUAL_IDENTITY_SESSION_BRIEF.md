# Fair Case — Visual Identity Session Brief

**Purpose:** Decision-framing document for the upcoming Fair Case branding session.  
**Date:** March 2026  
**Status:** Pre-session prep. The decisions below are open — this document frames the question, not the answer.

---

## What This Session Decides

1. Does Fair Case get a distinct visual identity from Second Chair's B2B brand?
2. If yes — what is the visual direction? (See B2C visual direction, below.)
3. What are the conversion-safe color and design requirements for the getafaircase.com funnel?
4. What is the relationship between Fair Case's visual identity and the ad creative visual style?

---

## The Current Situation

Second Chair's B2B brand identity is locked: **oxblood and old gold, Tiempos/Caslon typography, formal institutional aesthetic** — a deliberate zag against the tech-startup look that dominates legal marketing.

Fair Case, as the consumer-facing brand, currently uses the same visual DNA by default. The question is whether it should continue to, or whether consumer context requires a different identity.

---

## The Case for a Distinct Fair Case Identity

**The audience is different.** Second Chair speaks to a PI attorney who evaluates vendors through institutional credibility signals. Fair Case speaks to a working adult in a moment of stress and confusion who needs to immediately trust a brand they've never heard of.

**The form factor is different.** The primary Fair Case experience is a mobile quiz funnel. Conversion on forms is heavily affected by visual trust signals — color, layout, perceived legitimacy. The oxblood/old gold palette that reads as authoritative and serious in B2B may not produce the highest conversion rate in a consumer form context.

**The tone is different.** Second Chair is weighted, institutional, peer-to-peer with attorneys. Fair Case is human, plain-spoken, on the claimant's side. The visual identity should reinforce the voice — not contradict it.

**The connection is invisible.** The claimant never sees Second Chair. The attorney never sees Fair Case. There is no reason for the visual identities to match — and there is a reason they might not: they serve different emotional needs.

---

## The Case for Consistency

**Brand coherence at the company level.** Second Chair and Fair Case are the same company. If Fair Case ever becomes a known brand to partners or investors, visual consistency simplifies the story.

**The oxblood/old gold zag may still work in B2C.** The conventional wisdom is that consumer PI advertising should look clean and approachable (blues, whites, greens). Fair Case could distinguish itself by looking institutional and serious — the visual equivalent of "we're not trying to manipulate you."

**Less production complexity.** One identity system, applied in two contexts, is simpler to maintain than two full identity systems.

---

## The Conversion-Safety Constraint

Whatever visual direction is chosen, it must be tested against form conversion. The following are the highest-stakes design decisions for conversion rate on getafaircase.com:

**Color:**
- Blues and greens have the strongest documented trust signal in consumer form research
- Oxblood and old gold are associated with institutional authority — this may or may not transfer positively to a claimant who needs to feel safe submitting personal information
- This is a testable hypothesis: run the quiz with the institutional palette and a trust-optimized palette, measure completion rate

**Typography:**
- Tiempos/Caslon reads as formal, editorial, old-money. In a consumer context, this could read as trustworthy or as off-putting (cold, corporate).
- A more approachable serif or a clean sans-serif may produce higher completion rates on the form
- Again, testable

**Layout:**
- The quiz should feel more like a service than a form. Progress indicators matter. The less it looks like a legal document and the more it looks like a tool the claimant is using for their own benefit, the better.

**Recommendation:** The branding session should separate the identity question (what does the brand look like?) from the conversion question (what does the quiz form look like?). These can be different — a distinct, bold brand identity on the advertising and profile, with a conversion-optimized, cleaner form on the quiz itself.

## B2C Visual Direction (Ad Creative) — CORRECTED

**⚠️ Important clarification:** The visual direction below supersedes any previous framing that characterized the B2C visual approach as "ordinary is the point" or purely naturalistic. That is not the locked direction.

---

### What the Direction Is

**Cinematic heightened realism.** Not "ordinary." Not "looked found." Not "low-fi."

"Ordinary" describes the *subject matter* — real people, real environments, real situations that the target audience recognizes. "Cinematic" describes the *execution* — intentional framing, deliberate camera movement, precise casting, specific light, controlled color grade.

The distinction matters because:
- **Scroll-stopping requires visual contrast.** Ordinary-looking content does not stop the scroll. Cinematic execution does, even when the subject is mundane.
- **Heightened realism conditions the viewer to read the moment as meaningful.** People have been trained by film and television to associate cinematic visual language with emotional weight and significance. That association transfers to the ad.
- **Character specificity is the content.** As with the Luca Maxim style, the character IS the insight — before a word is spoken. Overly ordinary casting produces forgettable generic content. Precisely cast, cinematically composed characters produce immediate recognition.

**In practice:** A kitchen counter with the adjuster's business card sitting on it, shot with depth, correct light, and a specific face reacting to it — that is not ordinary. That is cinematic about something ordinary. The distinction is everything.

---

### Graham Fink's B2C Mode (Territory D) — Correct Interpretation

Territory D defined B2C visual direction as: *"The specific human in the specific moment."* The environment should be recognizable. The face should be specific. The emotional weight should be real.

The key phrase is: *"Cinematic quality that doesn't read as production."* This means the production is intentional but not visible as such. The skill is in making the cinematic feel found — not in making it look like a commercial. The viewer should feel they are watching something real and specific, not a staged scenario.

This is NOT "ordinary is the point." This is "the ordinary made cinematic through the specific."

---

### The Two Testing Styles (For Ad Production — See Also: B2C_CREATIVE_PRODUCTION_BRIEF.md)

Two styles will be tested in Fair Case paid advertising:

**Style A — Full Luca Maxim Cinematic (Dramatic)**  
Maximum visual drama. Saturated contrast. Archetypal characters with starter-pack precision (the insurance villain, the working-class hero). Exaggerated motion. The emotional dial at 10. Every frame a potential movie poster. The cinematic that reads as cinematic, without hiding its intentionality.

**Style B — Normalized Cinematic (Restrained)**  
Same camera discipline, same character precision, same deliberate framing — but with the drama dialed back. Naturalistic color grade. Quieter motion. The same production care, applied to a register that reads as observed rather than performed. The cinematic that hides the production.

Both styles use the same message architecture. Both serve the same three tiers. The question is which produces higher conversion and lower CPM in the Fair Case context. This is an empirical question, not a creative preference — test it.

## Pre-Session Decision Framework

Before the session, answer these:

1. **Identity question:** Should Fair Case have its own logo, color palette, and typography? Or does it share Second Chair's system?
   - If own identity: what emotional territory should it occupy? (Trust? Empowerment? Clarity?)
   - If shared: how is it adapted for consumer context?

2. **Conversion question:** What should the quiz form look like? (Color, typography, layout — separate from brand identity)

3. **Ad visual question:** Is the Luca Maxim cinematic direction right for Fair Case, or does the consumer context require a different approach? (Already addressed above — two styles will be tested.)

4. **Consistency question:** Does Fair Case need visual consistency with Second Chair at all? Or are they independent enough that the consumer brand can go wherever the conversion data points?
