## PHASE 1 — STRATEGIC ANCHORING

### Julian Cole — The Strategic Frame

I want to start by being honest about what kind of brand problem this is, because I think there's a risk we walk into this session with the wrong ambitions.

Fair Case is not a brand-building exercise. It is a paid acquisition machine with a brand face. The funnel is: ad → landing page → quiz → lead → law firm. Every brand decision exists to support that funnel, not to build long-term preference. The person who sees Fair Case is not going to come back. She is not going to become a "brand fan." She is not going to tell her friends unless they've also just had an accident — which is rare, and when it happens, she'll share the lawyer's name, not the form she filled out.

So the strategic question I want us to hold in the room before we debate the logo: what does this brand identity actually need to do?

My answer: one thing. Pass the gate. When Maria Rodriguez sees the Fair Case brand for the first time — probably as a watermark in a video ad, or as a profile photo on an Instagram page she checks before submitting her information — the brand needs to produce one response: *this is legitimate. I'm not going to get scammed.*

That's the brief. Everything else — distinctiveness, brand love, visual craft — is in service of passing that gate. If the identity doesn't pass the legitimacy gate for a stressed 38-year-old woman on her phone, it has failed, regardless of how well it performs on every other metric.

Now — I want Byron to push back on that. Because I think there's a version of this where that framing undersells what the brand can do. Byron.

### Byron Sharp — The Distinctiveness Argument

Julian's framing is partly right and partly dangerous.

He's right that we're not building long-term loyalty — the Ehrenberg-Bass data is clear that in one-time-purchase categories, penetration is everything. You get one shot. The question isn't "how do we get her to come back?" It's "how do we reach enough of the right people the first time?"

But here's where Julian undersells it: distinctive brand assets aren't just for long-term memory. They're the mechanism for mental availability at the moment of need. And in this category, the moment of need is highly specific and emotionally intense. When someone has been in an accident and is being called by an insurance adjuster, and they Google "do I have a case" at 11pm — do they recognize Fair Case? Have the three or four exposures they may have had to our ads left a memory structure that makes Fair Case come to mind?

That's where distinctiveness earns its money. It's not about brand love. It's about recognition at the right moment. A distinctive mark — something that breaks from the category — is what creates that recognition.

So let me give you the category mapping. What does every PI legal brand look like?

Scales of justice. Blue. White. A courthouse. A handshake. Stock photography of a confident lawyer in a suit. Corporate sans-serif. The message: "we are legitimate and serious." Every single one of them says the same thing, looks the same way, and is forgotten the moment you close the tab.

Fair Case's distinctiveness opportunity is wherever that isn't. And I want to say something that's going to sound counterintuitive: the Roman torch that's already on the table might be the right call, not because it "looks good," but because nothing in this category looks like it. A flaming torch is distinctive in PI legal advertising in the way that a black sheep was distinctive when we first used it at BBH. It doesn't follow the category convention. It will be remembered precisely because it refuses to look like a legal brand.

### Adam Ferrier — The Behavioral Frame

I want to add something before we get to the icon debate.

The distinctiveness argument is correct in principle. But in this specific context, there's a behavioral tension I need to flag.

The consumer's primary barrier is not "do I know this brand?" It's "is this a scam?" The internet is full of lead gen forms that look legitimate and aren't. PI legal lead gen in particular has a reputation for exactly that — a form you fill out and then get called by ten different law offices for six months. Maria Rodriguez knows this. She has been burned or knows someone who has. Her brain is running a scam-detection protocol before she reads a word.

What passes the scam-detection protocol? Authority cues. Fluency. The visual signals that say "this was built by someone who invested in looking real." These are not the same as distinctive.

A highly distinctive brand identity — unexpected colors, an unusual icon — can actually trigger the scam-detection heuristic. "Why does a law firm have a torch? This seems off." Unusual is sometimes unfamiliar, and unfamiliar is sometimes unsafe.

So I want to put a constraint on the distinctiveness argument: the identity must be distinctive within the range of what reads as legitimate. We can't be so distinctive that we read as suspicious. The torch might thread that needle — a historical symbol of justice and light is meaningful and specific without being random. But I want us to test that assumption before we commit.

Shotton — you have specific heuristics on trust and authority. What's the behavioral science on this?

### Richard Shotton — Trust Heuristics

Ferrier's right to flag it. Let me give you three specific heuristics that will constrain the design decisions.

**1. The fluency heuristic.** Processing ease produces positive affect. When something is easy to process — clean layout, familiar visual cues, readable type — the brain interprets that ease as quality and trustworthiness. This doesn't mean conventional — it means not visually cluttered or cognitively demanding. A distinctive mark can have high fluency if it's simple. A complicated one loses the heuristic even if it's meaningful.

**2. The authority heuristic.** We read authority from specific visual signals: formal typography, muted or institutional color, vertical hierarchy in layout, the visual grammar of things that have existed for a long time. This is Ferrier's scam-detection point made mechanically. The authority heuristic is why Second Chair's oxblood/old gold palette works in B2B — it signals "we have been here for decades." The question for Fair Case is whether a more distinctive palette can still carry those authority signals, or whether authority requires institutional visual conventions.

**3. The familiarity heuristic.** Things that look familiar feel safe. This is different from the fluency heuristic. Familiarity is categorical — "this looks like other things I've trusted before." If Fair Case looks too different from every other legal service, it fails the familiarity test even if it passes the fluency test.

Here's where I land: the authority and familiarity heuristics push toward institutional visual language (which is the oxblood direction). The distinctiveness argument and fluency heuristic push toward something cleaner and more memorable. The design has to find a way to carry all three. A well-executed historical symbol — the torch — can do that if the treatment is simple and the surrounding visual system is clean. It's unusual enough to be distinctive, historical enough to trigger authority, and simple enough to pass fluency.

The color is where the heuristics fight hardest. I want to revisit that in the color debate.

### Jon Steel — The Emotional Truth

I want to take the temperature down a bit and ask what everyone in this room is forgetting.

What is the human thing happening when Maria Rodriguez encounters this brand?

She didn't ask to be in this situation. She got hit by someone else's car. She's in pain, probably managing insurance calls while also dealing with medical appointments and work and her kids. She doesn't know if she has a case. She doesn't know how any of this works. She's scared that she's going to make the wrong decision — sign something she shouldn't, miss something she should have caught, end up with nothing.

That's the natural habitat. Not "consumer seeking legal services." A person in an unexpected, unfair situation who doesn't know what they're entitled to.

What does "Fair Case" mean in that moment? It means: *someone is offering to show me what I actually have. Someone is on my side before I commit to anything.*

That's the emotional truth the brand has to carry. Not authority. Not legitimacy, even though Ferrier and Shotton are right that it's a gate. Under the legitimacy gate, there's a more human thing: this brand is on my side. It's not just legal. It's fair.

The torch, to me, reads exactly right. A torch doesn't mean law. It means light on a situation that was in the dark. It means: here is what you actually have. You have a right to see it. That's "fair case" — a fair look at what's in front of you.

The question I want Hegarty to answer is whether there's an idea here worth building. Not a strategy — an idea. John.

### John Hegarty — The Idea Test

Right. Steel's made the right move. Let's stop talking about heuristics for a second and ask the fundamental question: is there an idea here?

Because I've heard a lot of good strategic argument in the last twenty minutes and almost no creative thinking. Sharp's right about distinctiveness. Ferrier's right about the scam-detection problem. Shotton's right about the heuristics. Steel's absolutely right that we need to find the human truth. But none of that tells me whether there's an idea at the center of this brand.

An idea is not a positioning statement. An idea is the thing you can build a visual world from. The thing you can build a sentence from that someone will repeat without being told to.

I want to test the torch against that standard.

The torch is ancient. It has carried meaning across two and a half thousand years — in marathon races, in the Olympics, on the Statue of Liberty. What does it actually mean? Not "law" — Steel's right to push back on that. It means: I carry the light so you can see the path. It means: this truth has been kept alive, passed from hand to hand, not extinguished.

For Fair Case: the adjuster has information the claimant doesn't have. The gap between what the insurance company knows and what the claimant knows is what's costing her. The torch is: here is the light. Here is what you couldn't see. Now you can see it.

*That's* an idea. It's not "we're trustworthy." It's not "we're different." It's: *we bring the light to what was in the dark.*

### Les Binet — The Investment Question

Before we get too excited about the idea, I need to put the uncomfortable argument on the table. Peter and I have been quiet because Julian set it up correctly and I want to honor the full complexity of it.

*Does brand investment matter for this business?*

The data on one-time purchase categories is nuanced. You're right that there's no loyalty here. That's a weak but real argument for brand investment.

The stronger argument, though, is this: Fair Case's ads ARE the brand. The claimant encounters Fair Case for the first time through an ad. The brand identity — the mark, the color, the feeling — is a signal that persists across the ad, the landing page, the quiz, and the Instagram page they check before they submit. If all four of those touchpoints look like a coherent, intentional brand, that coherence is itself a trust signal. The investment in the identity isn't for long-term memory. It's for in-moment coherence — which is what closes the legitimacy gate.

So: invest in the identity, but calibrate the ambition to the function. This isn't Nike. This isn't building forty years of brand equity. This is building enough brand coherence that every touchpoint says the same thing at the same moment. That's achievable. It also means the identity doesn't need to be heroically ambitious — it needs to be precisely right.

Peter.

## PHASE 3 — IDENTITY DEBATES

### Debate 1: The Icon / Mark

**Massimo Vignelli opens:**

The torch is on the table. Before we debate treatment, I need to establish the semantic territory it occupies, because the form follows the meaning.

## Post-Session Addendum — Form-First Constraint

**Added:** March 2026, after session

### The Correction

The session produced the right brand identity direction. One thing was wrong: the order of operations. The session treated the brand as the primary artifact and the form as a downstream application. That's backwards.

Fair Case's funnel is: **ad → landing page → quiz form → lead.** The claimant clicks from the ad directly into the form. The form IS the primary brand experience — not a downstream application of the brand. 99–100% of Fair Case's brand encounters happen on that funnel. The Instagram page, the social posts, the logo on the landing page — all secondary. The form is first.

The brand identity must be built to work in the form, not the other way around. The form's conversion requirements set the constraints; the brand identity fills the space within them.

### What This Changes in the Brief

Two decisions from the session are locked at the brand level but **subject to form validation** before they govern form design:

**1. Typography — Tiempos (locked for brand; form uses its own standard)**

Tiempos (humanist serif) is locked for:
- The wordmark at the top of the form
- Social posts
- Homepage and landing page headlines
- Any brand-facing printed or digital material

The form itself uses a **clean sans-serif** as its primary typeface. The session's research (Wroblewski's form mastery, mobile-first framework) is unambiguous: serifs increase cognitive load on mobile forms. High legibility at mobile scale requires a humanist sans-serif — Inter, Plus Jakarta Sans, or equivalent. The form has its own typographic standard. These are not in conflict — they are different contexts.

**2. Color — Warm amber brand palette, but form background and layout are conversion-first**

The brand color (warm amber / firelight gold) is locked and correct. How it appears in the form is a testing question, not a design decision:

- **Form background:** Near-white or warm off-white. Not amber. Backgrounds in conversion-optimized forms are white or very close to white. The question of whether a barely-there amber tint outperforms pure white is testable.
- **CTA button and accent:** Warm amber as the primary button color is a strong hypothesis — it's distinctive, it's the brand, and it creates visual contrast against a white form. This should be the default direction and tested against a neutral high-contrast alternative.
- **Progress bar:** Warm amber progress fill is appropriate — it's a trust and momentum signal, not a conversion friction point.
- **Logo at top of form:** The mark (torch + "Fair Case" wordmark) in warm amber or dark tone on white. Not a navigation link, or if it is, it goes to the homepage and the path back is clear.

### What Massimo Should Know for Session 2

The mark and wordmark must be evaluated in one additional context beyond the three already specified: **on a white or near-white form background, at the top of a mobile screen.**

The mark appears in the upper portion of the form (logo area, not primary action area), small but present — a quality signal that says this was built by someone who cared. Massimo should produce one mockup showing the combination lockup (symbol + wordmark) at this scale and in this context. If the warm amber mark has contrast issues on white at small sizes, the dark-anchor version of the mark (deep warm charcoal, not amber) may be the form-context standard.

## Color Correction Addendum — Navy + Amber System

**Added:** March 2026, after form-first constraint was applied

### The Problem with Amber as Primary

The session locked warm amber / firelight gold as the primary brand color. Applied in context, this fails on one critical surface: the logo on the form.

The form is white. The logo sits on white. Warm amber on white is low contrast, reads as washed-out or cheap at small logo sizes, and does not pass WCAG AA at the scale the mark appears in the form header. This is not a design failure — it's a practical constraint the session didn't test against.

Amber as a primary logo color requires a dark background to work. The form has a white background. The constraint is non-negotiable: the logo must read on white.

### The Revised Color System

The palette is corrected to a two-color role system. The colors don't change — their roles do.

**Roman Navy (Primary / Anchor)**
`#0D1F3C` — deep authoritative navy. This is the logo color, the wordmark color, the torch body. It reads at any size on white. It carries the authority signal Shotton's heuristics require. Navy + gold/amber is a documented legal combination — law school seals, bar association marks, established firm letterheads. The authority heuristic is satisfied from the moment the mark appears on screen.

**Torch Amber (Accent / Fire)**
`#C8821A` — warm firelight amber. This is the torch flame. The CTA button. The progress bar fill. The loading screen accent. Torch Amber appears where action and warmth are the signal — not where legibility and authority are required.

**The torch mark makes the system coherent.** A torch has a dark body and a bright flame. The color system IS the icon. Roman Navy handle, Torch Amber flame — the two colors describe the object that is the mark. This is not two colors in conflict; it's one object rendered correctly.

### What This Fixes

| Surface | Previous direction | Corrected direction |
|---|---|---|
| Logo on white form | Amber mark (contrast failure) | Roman Navy mark (`#0D1F3C`) |
| Logo on dark background (ads) | Amber mark on dark | Roman Navy mark + Torch Amber flame |
| CTA button | Amber fill, white text | Torch Amber fill (`#C8821A`), Roman Navy text (`#0D1F3C`) — white fails contrast at 2.95:1 |
| Progress bar | Amber fill | Torch Amber fill — unchanged |
| Loading screen bg | Dark charcoal (TBD) | Roman Navy `#0D1F3C` |
| Wordmark | Amber or dark | Roman Navy — same anchor as mark |

### What Massimo Should Know for Session 2 (Updated)

The mark is a two-color object: navy body, amber flame. Both versions of the combination lockup (mark + wordmark) should be produced in this palette. The "dark-anchor version" referenced in the form-first addendum is now the primary direction — navy is the anchor.

The warm amber palette that was "primary" is now specifically the accent. Massimo's color exploration in Session 2 should show the full range of the amber accent in its form contexts: CTA button, progress bar, loading screen fill. The mark itself is navy.
