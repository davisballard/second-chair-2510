# Fair Case — Brand Identity Session Agenda

**Session type:** Round table / Research + Decision session
**Scope:** Fair Case brand identity only — logo, mark, color palette, typography, and visual feel
**Not in scope:** Ad video style (see `B2C_CREATIVE_PRODUCTION_BRIEF.md`), campaign art direction (Graham's domain post-session), website build (Chester's domain)
**Status:** Pre-session document. All items marked as open are open. Decisions made in session update this document.
**Date:** March 2026

---

## The One-Line Purpose

Decide what Fair Case looks like — the mark, the color, the type — so Massimo has a specific brief and can begin design exploration.

Everything else in this document serves that purpose.

## The Room

### Chair
**Nigel Bogle — Account Management**
Holds the room. Keeps it on brief. Manages time. Makes sure the session produces decisions, not just opinions. Calls the vote when the room circles.

### Creative Leadership
**John Hegarty — Executive Creative Director**
The north star. Evaluates every direction against one question: is there an idea here? Does this brand identity express something true and specific about Fair Case, or is it just a design? The session ends when Hegarty can answer yes or when the room knows what's still missing.

**Massimo Vignelli — Brand Identity Designer**
The executor. Massimo is not here to decide — he's here to hear the brief well enough to design. His output from this session is a written design brief and, if the room produces enough clarity, an initial direction. His input during the session: what is and isn't designable, what has been tried in this category, what the distinctiveness territory looks like.

**Graham Fink — Senior Art Director**
Campaign application lens. Graham's job in this session is to flag whether each identity direction translates — to social, to the quiz funnel, to the Instagram page, to the environments where claimants will actually see this brand. If a direction looks great as a logo but disappears in a Reel thumbnail, Graham says so here.

**Ed McCabe — Senior Copywriter**
Voice and name. The brand name Fair Case is a working title — McCabe is in the room to confirm or challenge it. More importantly: what does the brand say about itself? The tagline, the tone, the one-line brand promise. Identity is not just visual. McCabe ensures the session doesn't lock a visual identity that contradicts or outpaces the verbal one.

**Giuseppe Karma — AI Creative Director**
Production feasibility. Giuseppe answers the question: can this be generated at the quality level we need, and what does it cost? He is a fast check on ideas that look good on a deck but break in AI-assisted production. He's also the bridge between brand identity and ad creative — what the brand establishes, he has to execute.

### Strategy
**Julian Cole — Head of Strategy**
Opens the session with the strategic frame. What does this brand need to do? For whom, in what moment, through what channel? Julian's brief to the room establishes the design constraints before any design decisions are made. He stays in the room as the strategic checkpoint — when the debate gets too aesthetic, Julian pulls it back to what the brand is trying to achieve.

**Jon Steel — Narrative Strategy**
The emotional truth. What story is Fair Case telling? Not the positioning statement — the human story underneath it. The fairness story. The "you deserved a fair shot at this" story. Steel's job is to make sure the identity is grounded in something emotionally true, not just strategically sound. The torch means something — what does it mean here, and does the design express that?

**Adam Ferrier — Behavioral Strategist**
Consumer psychology and trust signals. Ferrier briefs the room on what visual and emotional signals produce trust and propensity to act in a stressed, skeptical consumer who is about to submit their personal information into an unfamiliar form. This is not abstract — it directly constrains the color and typography decisions.

**Richard Shotton — Behavioral Scientist**
Behavioral economics. Shotton addresses the specific heuristics at play when a claimant encounters the Fair Case brand for the first time: authority cues, social proof signals, fluency and processing ease, the effort heuristic. His input on color and typography is behavioral research, not aesthetic preference.

**Byron Sharp — Brand Growth Scientist**
Distinctiveness science. Sharp makes one argument and makes it well: the job of the Fair Case identity is to be recognizable and memorable in a category where no brand is recognizable or memorable. He has a view on the category conventions (what everyone else looks like) and on what Fair Case needs to look different from. He will push Massimo toward genuine distinctiveness and away from "best-in-category convention."

**Les Binet & Peter Field — Effectiveness Researchers**
The uncomfortable question. Binet and Field address the brand investment question directly: does brand building matter at all for a business with one-time customers, high purchase-decision stakes, and a paid acquisition model? Their answer shapes how much creative ambition the identity deserves. This is the session's necessary friction before anyone gets too excited about logo design.

### Performance / Execution
**Indigo Sato — Performance Creative**
Social translation. Indigo's job: for each identity direction, assess how it plays in paid social — as a profile picture on a mobile screen, as a watermark on a video ad, as a brand signal in the first frame before the hook lands. Performance creative is where most brand identities fail. Indigo surfaces those failure modes in this session, not after production.

**Simon Croft — Head of Media Buying**
Media environment. Croft briefs the room on the actual media contexts where claimants will see this brand: Instagram feed, Stories, Reels, Facebook. He addresses what the brand needs to look like at the sizes, speeds, and scroll environments where it will live. A brand that looks right in a PDF and wrong in a Reel is the wrong brand.

## Session Structure

### Phase 1 — Strategic Anchoring (30 minutes)
*Julian Cole chairs. No design discussion in this phase.*

**1a. What the brand needs to do (Julian Cole, 10 minutes)**
The single strategic brief to the room: Fair Case is a one-time-customer B2C acquisition brand. The claimant will see it once, in a moment of stress, on a mobile screen. They need to feel: safe, legitimate, on my side, worth ten minutes of my time. The brand identity must produce that feeling before a word is read.

Design constraint from strategy: the identity must work at thumbnail scale, must not look like a legal services brand, and must not look like a tech startup. Those two directions are the category convention and the category anti-convention. The distinctive space is somewhere else.

**1b. The emotional truth (Jon Steel, 5 minutes)**
What's the story underneath Fair Case? Not the positioning — the human thing. A fair case. The idea that everyone who was hit by someone else's negligence deserves to know what they actually have before they give anything away. The torch doesn't mean law. It means: light on a situation that was in the dark. It means: the fire of something that was unjust, now recognized.

Is that the right story? Steel confirms or challenges it.

**1c. Consumer psychology brief (Adam Ferrier + Richard Shotton, 10 minutes)**
What visual signals produce trust and propensity to act in the Fair Case consumer? The combination of Ferrier's behavioral strategy and Shotton's behavioral economics gives the room the psychological constraints before the aesthetic debate begins.

Key questions:
- What colors produce the trust + authority combination this consumer needs?
- What does "processing ease" look like in this context — and does a bold, distinctive identity sacrifice it?
- What brand identity signals tell the claimant "this is legitimate" vs. "this is a scam form"?

**1d. The distinctiveness argument (Byron Sharp, 5 minutes)**
Sharp makes the category-mapping argument: here is what every PI legal brand looks like (scales of justice, blue, white, corporate serif, stock photo of a handshake). Here is what Fair Case needs to not look like in order to be remembered. The distinctiveness territory is wherever the category isn't.

Sharp's question to Massimo: what's the single most distinctive thing the Fair Case identity can do that no one else in this category is doing?

---

### Phase 2 — Research Review (20 minutes)
*Nigel chairs. Fast, factual, no debate.*

The room reviews three specific research findings before the design debate begins. These are facts, not opinions. They constrain the debate.

**Finding 1 — The category looks like this:**
Blues, whites, greens, scales of justice, courthouse steps, or a lawyer in a suit. Stock photography. Corporate sans-serif. The "trust us" color palette. The conversion-optimized, soul-free design language of every legal services form on the internet.

**Finding 2 — The claimant's mindset at the point of encounter:**
Stressed. Skeptical. Not looking for a brand — looking for a signal that this is legitimate enough to give ten minutes to. The visual trust signal is the first gate. If the identity fails the "is this a scam?" check, nothing else matters.

**Finding 3 — The conversion-safety constraint:**
Blues and greens have the strongest documented trust signal in consumer form research. Oxblood and old gold (Second Chair's B2B palette) are associated with institutional authority — this may or may not transfer positively to a claimant who needs to feel safe submitting personal information. This is a testable hypothesis — but the design should account for the risk before testing.

---

### Phase 3 — Identity Debates (90 minutes)
*Massimo facilitates each debate. Nigel keeps time. Hegarty calls the question.*

#### Debate 1 — The Icon / Mark (25 minutes)

**What's decided:** A flaming Roman torch is the leading icon candidate. The creative and strategic instinct for this mark has been established.

**What's open:**

*The semantic question (Jon Steel + Hegarty):*
What does the torch mean in the Fair Case context? Justice? Light on injustice? The fire of something unjust now named? The torch as symbol is rich — but symbol richness is only useful if the specific meaning is right and the treatment expresses it. Is the torch the right icon for a brand whose primary job is "this is legitimate, give us ten minutes"? Or does it carry too much gravitas for the consumer moment?

*The treatment question (Massimo):*
Three treatment directions for Massimo to present options against in subsequent design work:
1. **Illustrative** — a drawn torch with detail and character; warmer, more human, more craft
2. **Geometric/abstract** — a simplified mark derived from the torch form; scales to any size, reads as modern and intentional
3. **Typographic integration** — the torch form embedded into or alongside the wordmark rather than standing alone

The session decides which treatment territory Massimo explores first.

*The function question (Graham + Indigo):*
Does the mark work at 40px (Instagram profile picture)? Does it read in a Reel watermark? Does it survive on a dark background? Graham and Indigo each evaluate the three treatment directions against the actual media environments. The mark that fails at 40px is not the mark.

*The lock-up question (Massimo + Ed McCabe):*
Does the icon stand alone, or does it live within a wordmark lock-up? What is the wordmark — "Fair Case" in full, or a shorter form? McCabe weighs in on the name itself here.

**Decision target:** Settle the treatment direction. Massimo takes a specific brief into design exploration, not an open field.

---

#### Debate 2 — Color Territory (30 minutes)

Three directions. The room debates, then decides which one or two Massimo explores.

**Direction 1 — Institutional Authority (Stay adjacent to Second Chair)**
Stay in the oxblood and gold territory — adapted for consumer context. The argument: this is the visual equivalent of honest advertising. No one in PI consumer advertising looks institutional. The zag is looking serious and trustworthy rather than approachable and friendly. The claimant who sees Fair Case and thinks "this looks like a real institution" has received the right signal.

The risk: oxblood/old gold may read as cold or off-putting in a mobile form context where the consumer needs to feel safe and on-side. Testable, but the test costs conversion data.

Shotton's view: authority cues are powerful trust signals for unfamiliar brands. The institutional palette may produce the "legitimate" read the consumer needs at the gate.

**Direction 2 — Conversion-Optimized Trust**
Blues, neutrals, whites. The palette that consumer form research says converts. Trustworthy, clean, approachable. The visual language of: we're not going to harm you.

The risk: every legal services form on the internet already looks like this. The Fair Case form looks like every other PI legal services form. It doesn't get dismissed — but it doesn't get remembered either. Sharp's distinctiveness argument weighs heavily against this direction.

Ferrier's view: processing ease favors familiar visual languages. If the claimant's brain processes "this looks like a legitimate form" quickly and without friction, conversion may be higher than with a more distinctive palette.

**Direction 3 — A Distinct Third Territory**
Neither institutional nor trust-palette. Something that emerges from what "Fair Case" actually means: fairness, balance, the honest light on a situation. Colors that are warm, human, and specific — not because they convert best in form research, but because they express the brand idea.

This direction is higher risk and higher reward. It requires Massimo to find a palette that is simultaneously distinctive, warm, trustworthy, and specific to this brand. It might not exist. If it does, it's the right answer.

Hegarty's call: is there a color story here that's brave and right? Or is this category too functional for creative ambition in the palette?

**Decision target:** Settle on one or two directions for Massimo to design against. The testing question (which palette converts best) is answered by the quiz, not by this room — but this room decides what gets built.

---

#### Debate 3 — Typography (20 minutes)

**The current default:** Tiempos/Caslon — the Second Chair B2B system. Formal, editorial, institutional.

**The debate:**

*Does Tiempos work in consumer context?*
It reads as authoritative and serious in B2B. In a consumer mobile context, the question is whether "authoritative and serious" is the right register — or whether it reads as cold, formal, and intimidating to someone who is already stressed and skeptical.

Shotton: formal typography increases perceived expertise and authority, which is a trust signal. The argument for keeping Tiempos.

Ferrier: processing ease in a mobile form context favors clean, readable, contemporary type. The argument for a more approachable serif or a clean sans-serif.

Sharp: what does the category use? If the category is all sans-serif, Tiempos is a distinctiveness play. If the category is all formal serif, it's convention.

*Three directions to evaluate:*
1. **Stay with Tiempos/Caslon** — adapted weight and sizing for mobile. Consistent with Second Chair. Institutional authority read.
2. **Contemporary serif** — something warm and human, still trustworthy but not cold. The "friend who happens to be a lawyer" typographic register.
3. **Clean sans-serif** — maximum legibility at mobile scale, conversion-optimized, lowest-friction read. The risk is category generic.

**Decision target:** A typographic direction for Massimo to work with. Not the specific font — the register and character. Massimo makes the font call; the room sets the brief.

---

#### Debate 4 — Brand Feel / Emotional Register (15 minutes)
*Hegarty chairs.*

The identity debates above can each be answered wrong if the room doesn't agree on what a claimant should feel when they encounter the Fair Case brand.

**The question:** When Maria Rodriguez sees the Fair Case logo on her screen — before she reads a word, before she clicks anything — what should she feel?

The room proposes and debates. Some candidates:
- "This is legitimate. I'm not going to get scammed."
- "This is on my side. Someone built this for people like me."
- "This is serious. This isn't a gimmick."
- "This is calm. I can make a decision here."

The answer to this question is the brief for the entire identity. Massimo designs toward the feeling; Hegarty evaluates whether the design lands there.

**Hegarty's test:** Does the identity produce that feeling? Not "is it good design?" — does it produce the specific feeling this brand needs to produce in this specific consumer in this specific moment?

## The Binet / Peter Field Provocation

Before Phase 3 begins, Binet and Peter Field make one argument. It is uncomfortable and necessary.

**The argument:** Brand-building investment — the kind that produces a distinctive, memorable identity that earns place in long-term memory — is most valuable for repeat-purchase categories where the consumer will encounter the brand many times over years. Fair Case's consumers are one-time customers who may never see the brand again after they submit a form. The textbook Binet/Field model says: for a direct response acquisition brand with a one-time customer, the identity's job is conversion, not memory. Build for the moment, not the mental model.

**The counter-argument:** This is only true if Fair Case stays a one-time-customer business. Referral value — a claimant who shares the brand with someone who was just in an accident — depends on the brand being memorable enough to share. "I used this form" is not memorable. "I used Fair Case" might be.

**The provocation:** How much creative ambition does this identity deserve? Is this a conversion-optimization exercise with a brand face on it? Or is there a genuine brand here that deserves genuine distinctiveness investment?

The room answers this before Massimo starts designing. The answer calibrates how much creative ambition the brief carries.
