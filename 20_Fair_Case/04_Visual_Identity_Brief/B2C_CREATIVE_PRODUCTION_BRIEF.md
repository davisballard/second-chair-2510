# Fair Case — B2C Creative Production Brief

**Version:** 2.0 — March 2026
**Status:** Framework document. Specific hooks, narratives, and format decisions to be built out before first production run.
**Purpose:** Define the three testing styles, establish the research agenda for hooks and narratives, and provide the operational framework for Fair Case ad creative production.

**Change from v1.0:** Expanded from two styles to three. Style B is now a distinct pole (raw phone footage) rather than a restrained version of Style A. The former Style B (Normalized Cinematic) becomes Style C — explicitly positioned as the in-between.

---

## The Three Styles Being Tested

Fair Case will test three distinct visual executions of the same message strategy. All three use the same three-tier message architecture (`01_B2C_Strategy/B2C_MESSAGE_ARCHITECTURE.md`). All three target the same personas (`01_B2C_Strategy/CONSUMER_PERSONAS.md`). The question is which execution — or which execution per tier — produces the best conversion in the Fair Case consumer context.

The three styles form a deliberate spectrum:

```
STYLE A                    STYLE C                    STYLE B
Full cinematic             The in-between             Raw phone footage
Sweeping drama             Hollywood but real         No production tells
Dial at 10                 Dial at 5–6                Dial at 1
```

Test A vs. B first — the poles. When one wins or the data splits by tier, C becomes the intelligent in-between hypothesis.

**How testing works operationally:** All style variants run as separate ads within ONE ad set per Market. Meta rotates and optimizes delivery across them simultaneously. Do NOT create separate ad sets per style — at SC's volume (1-3 leads/day per Market), splitting across multiple ad sets prevents any of them from getting enough conversion events to exit learning. One ad set, 3-5 creatives, let Meta find the winners. 100 leads per Market minimum before expanding to a second ad set. See `09_Operations/HOW_META_CAMPAIGNS_OPTIMIZE.md` for full campaign structure.

### Style A — Full Luca Maxim Cinematic (Dramatic)

**Reference:** `02_Visual/Luca_Maxim_Format.md`

**What it is:**
Maximum cinematic drama applied to PI consumer advertising. Movie-trailer execution. Every frame a potential still. Archetypal characters built with starter-pack precision — the insurance villain rendered in full cartoonish malevolence, the working-class hero rendered with recognition-triggering specificity.

**Visual signature:**
- High visual contrast (cold fluorescent for villain scenes, warm golden hour for hero/resolution)
- Exaggerated motion — nothing is subtle, nothing is slow
- Characters with 10+ specific visual details that make the viewer say "I know that person"
- Dramatic camera movements: sweep, arc, push, dive — never passive
- Color grade: desaturated with lifted blacks, subtle film grain, shallow depth of field
- The "God View" shot, the absurdist object, the crowd of conformity — dramatic visual tropes applied to the insurance system

**Emotional register:**
The emotion dial is at 10. The villainous adjuster scene is theatrical. The hero moment is aspirational. The visual language of a serious film applied to the claimant's real situation.

**Tone:**
Heightened realism. The subject matter is ordinary (a kitchen, a phone call, a decision). The execution is cinematic (specific light, specific face, specific movement). The viewer reads the situation as weighty because it looks weighty.

**Best use cases:**
- Hook-heavy Reels and video ads where scroll-stopping is the primary challenge
- Tier 1 and Tier 2 — active decision and accumulation phases
- Any format where the character IS the insight before a word is spoken
- Premium placements where production investment is justified

**Production considerations:**
AI-assisted cinematography (Nano Banana / Kling). Character sheets required before generation — no generic prompts. Full villain archetype pre-defined in `02_Visual/Luca_Maxim_Format.md`. Hero character built per market using the Character Sheet Template in the same document. Higher production investment: ~$2–3 per video for AI generation at this quality level.

### Style B — Raw Phone Footage (Naturalistic)

**What it is:**
The opposite pole from Style A. Zero production tells. This is not "low-fi aesthetic" — it is the complete absence of a produced aesthetic. The video looks as if someone picked up their phone and filmed what was happening in front of them. No sweeping angles, no deliberate framing, no color grade that reads as chosen, no cinematic depth of field. The camera moves because hands move.

**Visual signature:**
- Completely naturalistic color — no grade, no lift, no intentional warmth or coolness
- Handheld shake — not stylized handheld, just the way people actually hold phones
- Framing that feels discovered, not placed — slightly off-center, sometimes cut off, sometimes too close
- Characters who look like real people, not cast for a role
- Backgrounds that are actually in the shot, not set-dressed
- Audio that sounds like real audio, not cleaned for production

**Emotional register:**
Unmediated reality. This is not performed. This is not an ad. The viewer doesn't read it as a brand — they read it as a person.

**The critical distinction:**
Style B doesn't hide production quality. It eliminates production entirely. The goal is not "looks found" — it's "looks like it was filmed by someone's cousin." Where Style A is a Netflix drama, Style B is a text message video. The viewer's brain should not register "ad" at all.

**Best use cases:**
- Any context where the audience has strong ad fatigue or ad skepticism
- Tier 2 and Tier 3 — claimants who have already been marketed to and are filtering
- TikTok (native format expectation is raw footage)
- UGC-style creative that can run as dark posts from personal accounts
- Contexts where authenticity is the primary conversion driver

**Production considerations:**
Lower production investment — intentionally. Shoot on actual phone. No lighting rig. Real locations with ambient sound. The skill is in casting and scripting, not in cinematography. The character must still be specific and recognizable (see personas), but the camera can't do the work — the situation has to do it.

**The risk:**
Style B is easy to confuse with bad Style A. The difference: bad Style A looks like a produced ad that lost its production value. Style B looks like something that was never trying to be produced. If a Style B execution makes the viewer think "this is an ad with a phone filter on it," it has failed. Only run Style B when it would genuinely fool someone into thinking it wasn't produced.

### Style C — The In-Between (Manufactured Real)

**What it is:**
The same production discipline as Style A — intentional framing, precise casting, deliberate camera movement, controlled color grade — but with the dramatic register dialed back significantly to land between A and B. This style is cinematic but not theatrical. Hollywood but real. Beautiful but not performed. The viewer feels they are watching something observed, not staged — but it is staged, with care.

**Visual signature:**
- Naturalistic color grade — warm but not heightened, colors that read as "real life" rather than "film"
- Quieter motion — handheld observation rather than dramatic sweep; the camera chooses where to be but doesn't announce that it chose
- Camera placement that feels found, not selected for drama
- Characters who read as precise and specific but not archetypal or exaggerated
- Emotional register of: the weight of a real moment in an ordinary day

**Emotional register:**
Cinematic intimacy. The difference between a prestige drama (AMC, HBO) and a movie trailer. The subject matter matters. The person matters. The camera is a witness, not a narrator.

**The key distinction from Style A:**
Style C does not remove the cinematic quality — it conceals it. The production care is the same. The apparent production is lower. The viewer should feel they are watching something specific and real, not watching an ad. Style A's production says "this is a story." Style C's production says "this is a moment."

**The key distinction from Style B:**
Style C is still produced. A trained eye can see the framing decisions, the casting precision, the color grade. Style B would show none of that. Style C hides production from a casual viewer; Style B eliminates production entirely.

**From Territory D (Graham Fink):**
*"Cinematic quality that doesn't read as production. The skill is in making it feel found, not filmed."*

This is Style C. Territory D's B2C visual mode is the reference for this style.

**Best use cases:**
- Any format where authenticity and trust are the primary conversion drivers but scroll-stopping visual quality still matters
- Tier 2 and Tier 3 — accumulation phase and SOL deadline awareness
- Contexts where Style A feels too produced and Style B feels too rough
- Meta feed placements where content competes with personal posts

**Production considerations:**
Same AI-assisted production as Style A, but different prompting philosophy. The goal is to produce something that looks like naturalistic drama or documentary footage — not like a produced ad. Color grade notes: naturalistic, not lifted; motion notes: subtle handheld, deliberate stillness. No dramatic camera sweeps.

## The Testing Framework

### Phase 1: Poles First

Test Style A against Style B. The two poles define the range. Run the same message concept against both styles — identical copy, identical CTA, identical landing page. Variable: visual style only.

**Why poles first:**
Style C is only meaningful relative to A and B. Testing the middle before you know where the extremes land produces ambiguous data. If A clearly outperforms B, Style C is not the hypothesis — scaling A is. If B clearly outperforms A, Style C is still not the hypothesis — the data is telling you something about the audience's relationship to production, and that signal should be understood before introducing a third variable. Style C enters the test when A and B split by tier, or when neither clearly dominates.

### What We're Measuring

| Metric | What It Tells Us |
|---|---|
| Click-through rate (CTR) | Which style stops the scroll more effectively |
| Quiz completion rate | Which style maintains engagement through the funnel |
| Lead form submission rate | Which style produces motivated, action-ready claimants |
| Cost per submitted lead | Efficiency by style |
| Intake call conversion rate | Which style produces leads who are genuinely ready to engage |

### How to Test

**Structure:**
Run Style A and Style B against the same message concept (e.g., the same Tier 1 hook about the adjuster's call). Identical copy, identical CTA, identical landing page. Variable: visual style only.

**Budget allocation at launch:**
60% to Style A (strongest scroll-stopping hypothesis for cold traffic). 40% to Style B. If neither dominates by week 3, introduce Style C as a third cell at 20% (rebalancing from the lagging style).

**Per-tier testing:**
Test independently by tier — Style A may outperform in Tier 1 (dramatic hook needed to stop the scroll) while Style B may outperform in Tier 2 (accumulation fatigue, needs authenticity). Style C is the Tier 2/3 hypothesis if B proves too raw for mid-funnel. Don't collapse tier data.

**Market-specific considerations:**
Colorado launch market. Running all relevant styles in the same geo. When expanding to CA, NY, FL, TX — test whether style preference varies by market before scaling.

## Hook Research Agenda

*This section documents what needs to be built out before first production. These are open research questions, not settled decisions.*

### What We Know About Hooks

From behavioral copy research, the highest-performing hook structures for PI consumer advertising are:

1. **The Insider Revelation** — "There's a reason the adjuster calls you within hours. And it's not to help you."
2. **The Specific Fact** — "68% of accident victims accept the first offer without consulting an attorney."
3. **The Incredulous** — "I cannot believe insurance companies get away with this."
4. **The Myth Buster** — "Most people think they can't afford a lawyer. That's exactly what the insurance company wants you to believe."
5. **The Stop** — "Before you respond to the insurance company — hear what you actually have."

**Research question:** Which hook types perform best for Fair Case's honest advertising posture? Fair Case's ethical constraint is that the ad must be genuinely true and non-manipulative. Some hook types (especially "The Incredulous") can slide into manufactured outrage. The research question: which hook types are simultaneously compelling and compliant with the honest advertising posture?

**Style interaction:** Hook type and visual style interact. "The Incredulous" hook may work best in Style A (where theatrical emotion is the register). "The Stop" may work best in Style B or C (where quiet directness is more credible). Research both the hook and the style × hook combination.

**Research needed:**
- A/B test hook types within each style
- Monitor for "I feel manipulated" response signals (low-quality leads, intake drop-off)
- Identify which hooks attract claimants who arrive with accurate expectations vs. inflated ones

## Narrative Research Agenda

### Three Narrative Territories to Test

**Narrative 1 — The Information Gap**
Theme: "You don't know what you have. Here's how to find out."
Structure: The adjuster has more information than you. The gap between what they know and what you know is what's costing you. One call to close the gap.
Tone: Informative, calm, not fear-based. The claimant is intelligent and just needs the information.

**Narrative 2 — The System Reveal**
Theme: "The claims process was built by the insurance company. Here's what you can do about it."
Structure: Not adversarial anger — structural clarity. The system runs this way. Here's how to navigate it.
Tone: Authoritative, matter-of-fact. Like a knowledgeable friend explaining how something actually works.

**Narrative 3 — The People Who Called**
Theme: Implicit social proof — the people in similar situations who got a Fair Case.
Structure: Specific, recognizable situations. "The call from the adjuster. The bills arriving. The decision before anything is signed." Observational.
Tone: Documentary. Seen and recognized, not manufactured.

**Style fit hypothesis:**
- Narrative 1 (Information Gap) × Style A or C — the informational gap is a dramatic reveal; cinematic register amplifies the stakes
- Narrative 2 (System Reveal) × Style C — authoritative but not theatrical; the calm cinematic register matches the tone
- Narrative 3 (The People Who Called) × Style B — this is the naturalistic narrative; real people in real moments, filmed as if found

**Research question:** Confirm style × narrative fit with data. The hypotheses above are directional, not settled.

## Length Research Agenda

### What We Know

Meta video ad length research across direct response industries shows:
- **7–15 seconds:** Best for cold audiences. Hook must work in 3 seconds. No time for narrative arc — one fact, one CTA.
- **15–30 seconds:** Best for warm audiences (retargeting). Room for a two-beat narrative — the problem and the action.
- **30–60 seconds:** Works for high-intent audiences who are actively researching. More context, more trust-building. Risk: drop-off if the hook wasn't strong enough.

### Fair Case Hypotheses to Test

**Hypothesis 1:** For Tier 1 (Days 0–30), 15-second Style A (Luca Maxim cinematic hook) outperforms longer formats because the claimant hasn't established intent yet — they need to be stopped, not persuaded.

**Hypothesis 2:** For Tier 2 (1–6 months, retargeting), 30-second Style C (in-between cinematic) outperforms because the claimant needs context — the "it's not too late" narrative requires some space to land, and Style C's naturalistic register is better suited to a claimant who has already seen several ads.

**Hypothesis 3:** For Tier 3, a Style B or text-based minimal-production format may outperform both A and C — the SOL deadline is doing the work, and production quality is less important than factual clarity. The raw phone footage register may feel more urgent and real than cinematic execution.

**Test:**
- 15s vs. 30s by tier, controlling for style
- Measure drop-off at 3s, 7s, 15s intervals
- Completion rate as proxy for narrative engagement

## Production Workflow (First Run)

### Before First Ads Are Produced

- [ ] Hero character sheets built for Colorado market (per Luca Maxim Format character sheet template)
- [ ] Style A visual prompts drafted and test-generated
- [ ] Style B shot list drafted (actual phone, real location — no AI generation for first test)
- [ ] Style C visual prompts drafted and test-generated
- [ ] Hook copy drafted for each tier (minimum 3 hooks per tier per style)
- [ ] Landing page (getafaircase.com quiz) verified live and TCPA-compliant
- [ ] TrustedForm and Jornaya active
- [ ] Meta Business Manager account connected to Fair Case Instagram
- [ ] Fair Case Instagram page warmup complete (see `02_Social/INSTAGRAM_WARMUP_PLAN.md`)

### Ad Production Order (Phase 1)

1. **Tier 1, Style A, 15s** — highest-priority test (active decision window, maximum drama, scroll-stopping)
2. **Tier 1, Style B, 15s** — pole comparison; raw vs. cinematic on the same hook
3. **Tier 2, Style C, 30s** — accumulation phase, in-between cinematic; the "it's not too late" narrative
4. **Tier 3, Style B or text/minimal, 15s** — SOL deadline, fact-first, naturalistic urgency

Phase 1 gives data on the style question (A vs. B), the length question (15 vs. 30), and all three tiers. Style C enters Phase 2 as the informed hypothesis from Phase 1 data.

## Quality Floor

Every piece of Fair Case creative must pass this test before it goes live:

**The honest advertising test:** Would a reasonable person, after seeing this ad and calling Fair Case, feel that the ad accurately represented what they were getting into? If yes — the ad can run. If no — it cannot.

**The specificity test:** Is the creative specific enough that a person who did NOT have an accident in the relevant window would scroll past without registering it as relevant to them? If yes — the self-selection mechanism is working. If no — the ad is too generic.

**The quality test (style-specific):**
- Style A: Does it look like someone who cared made it? If the details are wrong, the production is not complete.
- Style B: Does it look like no one tried to make it? If a trained eye can see the production decisions, it has failed. Only runs when it would genuinely fool someone.
- Style C: Does it look intentional but not produced? The production should be invisible to a casual viewer but present to someone who knows what to look for.
