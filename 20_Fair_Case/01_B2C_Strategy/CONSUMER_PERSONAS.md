## The $33,700 Gap (The Central Data Point)

In California, accident victims who consulted an attorney before accepting a settlement averaged **$47,300**. Those who didn't averaged **$13,600**. Same injuries, same accidents.

- **68%** of accident victims accept the first offer without consulting an attorney
- **91%** of quick-settlers later found their compensation was insufficient
- **90%+** of all injury victims absorb losses without ever claiming

The gap is not primarily the money. It is being moved through a system that has every incentive not to treat them fairly, with no one genuinely in their corner.

## The Three Behavioral Barriers to Action

These are what every Fair Case ad must overcome. Know them before writing a line of copy.

**1. The Minimization Instinct**  
*"I don't want to be the kind of person who sues."*  
Identity conflict with the plaintiff role is the primary barrier — not information deficit, not cost, not access. The claimant has internalized a cultural message that suing is greedy, and they do not want to see themselves that way.  
**What breaks it:** Repositioning action not as "suing" but as "finding out what you have" — one call, ten minutes, free, no commitment. The minimum viable action creates no identity conflict.

**2. Optimism Bias About Insurers**  
*"They'll be fair. I was in the right."*  
The adjuster calls first — often within hours. At peak financial stress, before the adjuster's strategy becomes visible, the claimant trusts that the insurance company is on their side.  
**What breaks it:** The specific, true fact: the insurance company's job is to settle your claim for as little as possible. Not antagonism — information. One fact, stated plainly.

**3. Present Bias**  
*"I'll deal with it later."*  
The adjuster establishes their frame before the claimant has any competing information. Delay defaults to acceptance.  
**What breaks it:** Tier-appropriate urgency. Real deadlines (SOL), real costs of waiting (evidence degradation, the gap between first offer and what the case is worth). Never manufactured.

## Four Named Personas

These are research-composite personas, not demographic segments. Each represents a real behavioral cluster from consumer research. Use these when writing ad copy, concepting creative, or making content decisions.

---

### Maria Delgado — The Working Parent in Crisis

**Age:** 34  
**Situation:** Two kids, works full-time, household income ~$58K. Got rear-ended on the way to pick up her kids. Car is in the shop. She's been dealing with a sore neck and some back pain but hasn't gone to the doctor because she can't take time off.  
**Current belief:** *"The insurance company said they'd cover the car. I don't know if the rest is worth making a big deal about."*  
**What she's facing:** The adjuster already called and was friendly and helpful. She's about to accept whatever they offer because it feels like the easiest path forward and she doesn't have the bandwidth for anything else.

**Behavioral profile:**
- Tier 1 — Days 0–30
- Highest conversion likelihood
- Trust is the primary barrier: she doesn't know who is legitimate
- She won't call a law firm. She will take a "quick quiz to see if she qualifies"

**Creative signals that work for Maria:**
- Situations she recognizes (the call from the adjuster, the insurance forms on the kitchen table)
- Nothing that feels legal or complicated
- The specific fact that makes action obvious: before you respond to anything, one call to hear what you actually have
- Minimum-barrier CTA: a quiz, not a consultation

**What Maria never needs:**
- Dollar promises she can't evaluate
- Fear about her health or her case
- Anything that asks her to identify as a "victim"

### DeShawn Williams — The Self-Employed Operator

**Age:** 41  
**Situation:** Runs a small landscaping business. Got hit by a delivery truck that ran a red light. He was driving his work truck. He's limping and working through it because he can't afford to stop. Worried about his equipment. Has not seen a doctor.  
**Current belief:** *"I don't have time for this. If I stop working, I lose clients. And lawyers drag things out forever — I can't afford to be in a lawsuit for two years."*  
**What he's facing:** He is most at risk of lapsing into Tier 2 (1–6 months) because the minimization instinct and time pressure will push him past the critical early window.

**Behavioral profile:**
- Tier 1 — Days 0–30 (at risk of sliding to Tier 2)
- Loss aversion is the primary lever — framing "one call" as protecting what he's built
- He responds to efficiency: this process is fast, this is not a commitment, this does not disrupt operations

**Creative signals that work for DeShawn:**
- The specific math: what his case could be worth vs. what one call costs (ten minutes)
- Efficiency framing: "ten minutes" is the barrier, not "a legal engagement"
- Peer identity: the working person who got knocked down and is fighting to stay up
- The specific truth about his situation: medical documentation early = stronger case

**What DeShawn never needs:**
- Drama or emotional overreach
- Legal jargon
- Anything that implies the process will take over his life

### Jennifer Torres — The Young Digital Native Without Rights Awareness

**Age:** 26  
**Situation:** Rideshare passenger. Uber driver rear-ended someone. Jennifer got whiplash, some bruising. Uber's insurance company sent a claim form. She has no idea what any of it means. She's never dealt with insurance before.  
**Current belief:** *"I don't think I have a case? I don't even know what that means. Uber said they'd handle it. I just want to know if I have to do anything."*  
**What she's facing:** Uber and their insurer are betting on her compliance. She doesn't know she has separate legal options. She doesn't know she can talk to an attorney for free.

**Behavioral profile:**
- Tier 1 — Days 0–30
- Awareness gap is the primary barrier — she doesn't know the option exists
- Digital-native: she'll discover Fair Case through video (Instagram, Facebook Reels)
- High curiosity, low commitment threshold — she'll take a quiz

**Creative signals that work for Jennifer:**
- Educational frame: "Here's what they don't tell you about rideshare accident claims"
- Social proof: "What rideshare passengers are doing after accidents"
- Short video, direct, authoritative voice, fast-paced
- The one fact: Uber's insurance is designed to protect Uber, not you

**What Jennifer never needs:**
- The implication that she's signing up for a lawsuit
- Complexity
- Anything that sounds like an old-school PI firm

### Robert Holtz — The Retired Patient

**Age:** 67  
**Situation:** Retired, fixed income. T-boned at an intersection 8 months ago. He's had ongoing back pain, two doctors' visits. He received a settlement offer that felt too low, but he's been putting off dealing with it because it's stressful and he doesn't want to get into "all that."  
**Current belief:** *"It's probably too late to do anything about it now. I accepted part of the offer already. And honestly, I just want to be done with the whole thing."*  
**What he's facing:** He may still have a viable case. His medical bills have continued to arrive. The settlement he accepted may not have covered the full picture. He's in Tier 2, possibly approaching Tier 3.

**Behavioral profile:**
- Tier 2 — 1–6 months (accumulating situation)
- Trust is the highest barrier: he's been trained by advertising to distrust PI firms
- The "it's too late" belief is often wrong — breaking it with a specific fact is the lever
- He moves on information, not emotion — no pressure, no drama

**Creative signals that work for Robert:**
- The specific, true correction: most people believe it's too late; some still have options
- Low pressure: one call to find out where you stand. No commitment
- Peer-appropriate voice: this doesn't feel like it's built for someone 30 years younger
- The bills angle: the settlement he accepted may not cover what arrived later

**What Robert never needs:**
- Urgency that feels fake
- Anything that reads as a scam
- Complexity that requires him to do a lot of work to understand

## The Three Timing Windows

**Tier 1 — Days 0–30 (Active Decision Window)**  
- Claim is unsigned. Adjuster has called. Claimant is still in the window.  
- Highest conversion likelihood.  
- Primary message: "Before you respond to anything — one call."  
- Primary channel: Meta (Facebook/Instagram) creative self-selection.

**Tier 2 — 1–6 Months (Accumulation Phase)**  
- Bills are arriving. Offer may be on the table. Claimant is living with an unresolved situation.  
- Moderate conversion likelihood, HIGH case value for serious injuries.  
- Primary message: "The bills keep arriving. One call to find out if you still have options."  
- Primary channel: Meta retargeting (they've been to sites by now).

**Tier 3 — 6+ Months to Statute of Limitations (Deadline Approaching)**  
- Claimant believes it's too late. Often wrong.  
- Lower conversion volume, very high individual case value.  
- Primary message: "In [state], you have [X] years. If you haven't acted — find out if your window is still open."  
- Primary channel: Meta, state-specific copy variables.

## The Minimum Viable Action

Across all personas, all tiers, all situations — one action that overcomes all three behavioral barriers:

> **"One call. Ten minutes. Free. No commitment. Before you say yes to anything."**

This language works because:
- It is not a lawsuit — it is a phone call
- It is not a commitment — it is information
- It is not expensive — it is free
- It has a time cost of ten minutes — not two years
- It preserves the claimant's identity — they are not "suing"; they are "finding out"

Every Fair Case CTA should point at some version of this action. The quiz on getafaircase.com is this action in digital form.
