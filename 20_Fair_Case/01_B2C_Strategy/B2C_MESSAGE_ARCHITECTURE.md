## The Core Message

The insurance company's job is to settle your claim for as little as possible. Before you respond to anything — one free call to hear what you actually have.

**Why this is the message:**  
It delivers one specific, true fact. It makes a minimum-barrier action the obvious next step. It does not sell legal services, does not promise an outcome, does not require the claimant to identify as a plaintiff. It meets them exactly where they are — before the adjuster's offer has established the frame.

---

## The 99%/1% Mandate

At any given moment, approximately 1% of people in a market had a recent accident constituting a viable PI case. The advertising reaches all of them.

**Wrong approach:** Emotional manipulation (crash footage, scare language, manufactured urgency) that briefly captures everyone's attention and sustains no one's.

**Right approach:** Creative so specific to the claimant's situation that the 1% immediately recognize themselves — and the 99% self-select out because the ad doesn't register as relevant to them.

**The self-selection test (apply to every piece of copy before use):**  
*Would a person who did not have an accident in the relevant window scroll past this without registering it as relevant to them?*  
If yes — the mechanism is working. If no — the copy is too generic. Make it more specific.

## Three-Tier Message Framework

### Tier 1 — Days 0–30 (Active Decision Window)

**Who they are:**  
A working adult who had a car accident in the last 30 days. They may have received a call from the adjuster. They haven't signed anything. They don't know if they have a case. They are not thinking of themselves as a claimant — they are thinking of themselves as a person whose life got disrupted who wants to resolve this and move on.

**Dominant emotion:** Confusion + low-level anxiety + desire to resolve and move on.

**What they believe right now:**  
*"I don't know if I have a case. I don't want to deal with legal stuff. I just want this to be over. The insurance company already called me. They seem like they have this handled."*

**What the message must accomplish:**  
Create a moment of genuine recognition — *"that's exactly my situation"* — followed by a single, low-barrier action that feels like getting information, not signing up for anything.

**The message (Tier 1):**  
*"The insurance company's job is to settle your claim for as little as possible. Before you respond to anything — one free call to hear what you actually have."*

Or stripped further:  
*"Before you respond to the insurance company — hear what you actually have. Free. Ten minutes."*

**CTA:** Get a Fair Case / See if you qualify / Take the quiz (60 seconds)

**What this message never does:**
- Claims the person definitely has a case
- Promises a dollar amount
- Uses fear of the insurance company as the primary emotion (information is the motivator)
- Creates artificial urgency where none exists
- Talks down to the claimant

---

### Tier 2 — 1–6 Months (Accumulation Phase)

**Who they are:**  
The claimant who is 1–6 months past their accident. Either they accepted a first offer and are living with it, or they're undecided or in informal negotiation. Medical bills are arriving. Financial reality is setting in. The situation is heavier now than it was in the immediate aftermath.

**Dominant emotion:** Accumulation — managing an ongoing problem under financial pressure.

**What they believe right now:**  
*"It's too late to do anything. I already accepted the offer / I waited too long / this is just how it goes. The bills are coming in but there's nothing I can do about it now."*

This belief is often wrong. Many claimants still have viable cases and remaining time. The ad provides the specific, true information that corrects it.

**The message (Tier 2):**  
*"Most people who accepted a first offer didn't know they had other options. Some still do. One call to find out where you stand."*

For the undecided claimant still in the window:  
*"The bills keep arriving. The offer might not be enough to cover them. One call — find out if you have options."*

**CTA:** Find out if you still have options / See if there's still time / Get a Fair Case

**What this message never does:**
- Tells claimants they definitely have a case they don't
- Manufactures urgency where none exists
- Positions the insurance company as evil — positions the claimant's information deficit as the problem

---

### Tier 3 — 6+ Months to Statute of Limitations

**Who they are:**  
The claimant who has not acted. They may not know there is a statute of limitations. They may have assumed it was too late long ago. They may have a real, viable case approaching the window where it permanently closes.

**Dominant emotion:** Resignation — the ship has sailed.

**What they believe right now:**  
*"It's definitely too late. I didn't do anything. That ship has sailed."*

Often incorrect — depending on state SOL, they may still have significant time.

**The message (Tier 3):**  
*"In [state], you have [X] years from the date of your accident to file a claim. After that, you can't. If you're not sure whether you still have time — one call to find out."*

The specific deadline is the ad. It does not need emotional framing. The fact is the creative.

**State-specific production requirement:** Each Tier 3 ad must carry the correct SOL for the state it runs in. This is a production workflow requirement — templates with state-specific copy variables.

**CTA:** Find out if your window is still open / Get a Fair Case

**What this message never does:**
- Manufactures dread around the deadline ("ACT NOW BEFORE TIME RUNS OUT!")
- Promises the claimant has a case
- Uses generic urgency language

## Tone Rules

**Universal across all tiers:**

1. **One specific fact. One minimum-barrier action.** Not a sales pitch. Not a story. One thing they didn't know. One thing they can do about it.

2. **Never manufacture urgency beyond what the situation genuinely contains.** The SOL deadline is real — state it precisely. The value of consulting early is real — state it specifically. Manufactured urgency is manipulation. It also gets ads flagged.

3. **The claimant is a person making a real decision — not a victim who needs rescue.** Write to their agency. "Know what you have before you decide" — not "don't let them take advantage of you."

4. **Tier-specific always.** Tier 1 copy is wrong for Tier 2. Tier 3 copy is wrong for Tier 1. Every piece of ad copy must know which tier it's speaking to.

5. **Specificity is targeting.** Under Meta Special Ad Categories, there is no demographic targeting — only geographic + creative self-selection. The more specific and true the ad is to the claimant's situation, the stronger the self-selection. Vague ads waste spend.

## Brand Character (Applied to Consumer Context)

The B2B brand character applies in B2C with a different register. Same traits, different manifestation.

**Expert, not arrogant — B2C version:**  
Fair Case speaks with authority because the information is real, not because it's trying to impress. It delivers one specific fact and gets out of the way.

**Honest in the specific — B2C version:**  
No "you may be owed thousands." No implied settlement calculations. One true thing. If it can't be stated specifically, it's not in the ad.

**Respectful of intelligence — B2C version:**  
The claimant is addressed as an intelligent adult making a real decision. Not a demographic. Not a victim to be mined for conversion. A person in the middle of a real situation with real consequences.

**Weighted, not heavy — B2C version:**  
The register carries weight because the situation has weight — a person's decision about a life-changing claim. It is not stiff, clinical, or formal. It is not overdramatic or fear-based. The specific feeling of standing at a fork in the road and someone giving you the information that clarifies it.

## Prohibited Language (B2C)

These are permanent. Not "guidelines" — prohibitions.

| Prohibited | Why | What to Use Instead |
|---|---|---|
| "You could be owed THOUSANDS" | Guarantee + manufactured excitement | "$33,700 — that's what the average claimant leaves on the table" (documented fact) |
| "Don't let them get away with it" | Antagonism without substance | "Before you respond to anything — hear what you actually have" |
| "You may have a case!" (+ exclamation) | The lowest form of PI advertising | "Find out if your situation qualifies" |
| "Call now before it's too late!" | Manufactured urgency without real deadline | "In [state], the deadline is [X] years" (only where real) |
| "We'll fight for you!" | Promise the attorney makes, not the ad | "Connect with an attorney who will" |
| Specific dollar amount without documentation | Promise of outcome | "Settlements in similar cases have ranged from..." |
| "Are you injured?" | Meta Personal Attributes violation | "Accident victims may be entitled to..." |
| "Were you in a crash?" | Meta Personal Attributes violation | "Car accident cases are being accepted" |
| "You deserve compensation" | Guarantee + personal attribute | "Compensation may be available for qualifying cases" |
| "sue" / "lawsuit" / "litigation" | Identity barrier activation | "case" / "claim" / "your options" |

See `02_Social/COPY_VOICE_GUIDE.md` for the full "YOU" ban compliance rules and the complete banned phrases list.

## The Fair Case Funnel — Message to Action

| Touchpoint | Message Layer | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Ad (Meta) | Tier-specific message — one fact | Stop scrolling |
| Landing page / Quiz entry | Trust establishment + minimum-barrier CTA | Start the quiz |
| Quiz Q1–Q4 | Commitment ladder — each question builds investment | Complete 4 questions |
| Loading screen | Anticipation — "reviewing your answers..." | Wait |
| Quiz result + lead capture | Earned access — "based on what you've shared, here's what's next" | Submit name/phone/email + TCPA consent |
| Intake call | Attorney conversation — expectations set by honest advertising | Schedule / retain |

The ad's job ends at "stop scrolling." The funnel's job is to take someone from stopped to submitted. Every step must reduce friction, not add to it.

## What Fair Case Never Does in B2C Advertising

1. Promises a dollar amount it cannot guarantee for that specific situation
2. Uses crash footage or dramatized accident imagery
3. Claims the claimant "definitely has a case"
4. Creates fake urgency where no genuine deadline exists
5. Uses fear of the insurance company as the primary motivating emotion
6. Runs any copy that a reasonable person would call manipulative after reading it
7. Uses actors presenting as real clients
8. Targets by demographic — relies exclusively on creative self-selection + geographic targeting
