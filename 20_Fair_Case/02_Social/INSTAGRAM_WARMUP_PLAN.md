## Why Warmup Matters

When someone sees a Fair Case ad and taps the profile name to check us out, they're doing a basic credibility audit in under 10 seconds:

1. Does the profile look complete and real?
2. Do they post? (Or is this a ghost account?)
3. Do people follow them?
4. Does the content match what the ad promised?

A brand-new account with zero posts and 12 followers fails this audit. The ad spend that drove that click is wasted — not because the creative didn't work, but because the page killed the trust.

This plan solves that. It is entirely about **visual credibility for ad visitors** — not organic reach, not building an engaged community, not gaming the algorithm.

---

## The Two Systems You Need to Understand

Most advice online conflates two completely separate systems. Knowing the difference is the whole game.

### System 1: Instagram's Organic Algorithm

This system controls who sees your posts in the feed, Explore, and Reels. It rewards authentic engagement — likes, comments, saves, shares from real followers. Fake followers **destroy this system**: posts to 1,000 fake followers get 8–15 real interactions, Instagram reads this as low-quality content, and suppresses reach even to real accounts.

**Does this matter for Fair Case?** No. We do not care about organic reach. Posts exist only to fill the page for ad visitors to see.

### System 2: Meta Ads Auction

This system controls whether your paid ads get delivered, at what cost, and to whom. It runs on: **Bid × Estimated Action Rate × Ad Quality**. Your Instagram page follower count is **not a direct input** to this formula. Meta's ad auction does not know or care if you have 50 or 50,000 Instagram followers.

**The actual risk to your ads:** If Instagram restricts or flags your account for inauthentic behavior (sudden mass follower acquisition), it can affect the connected Facebook Business account and ad delivery. This is the risk to manage — account restriction, not auction performance.

**Bottom line:** Buying followers at a controlled rate is a tolerable risk for our use case. The goal is to avoid account flags, not to avoid buying followers entirely.

## Step 2: Content — What to Post and Why

### The Only Goal

Content on this page serves one purpose: **when an ad-driven visitor lands on the profile, the page looks like a legitimate brand that's been around a while.**

This means:
- At least 9–12 posts in the grid before ads launch (aim for 12–14)
- Posts should look professionally designed (on-brand colors, clean typography)
- Mix of formats (static images, carousels, a Reel or two) — variety signals legitimate activity
- Captions should be written but not optimized for hashtags or reach

### What Not to Do

- Do not use generic stock courtroom photos, gavels, or scales of justice
- Do not write captions full of hashtags — it looks spammy to page visitors
- Do not post the same format repeatedly — visual monotony reads as low-effort
- Do not use all the same copy angles — mix education, emotion, and social proof

### Posting Cadence

- **Before ads launch:** 1 post per day for 14 days (see `CONTENT_CALENDAR.md` for specifics)
- **After ads launch:** Drop to 3–4 posts per week — enough to show the page is active

You do not need to post every day forever. You need the page to not look dormant. 3x/week sustained is more than sufficient.

### Hide Likes

Turn on "Hide Like Counts" on every post. This is available post-publication in post settings. Removes the low-engagement signal from visible view.

### Stories

Post 1–2 Stories per week during warmup. They don't need to be elaborate — repurpose content from posts. Stories show account activity to Instagram's trust system even if no one watches them.

## Step 3: Follower Acquisition

### The Case For Doing It

You need the page to not look brand new. 50 followers reads as: *brand new account, probably a scam.* 1,000+ followers reads as: *this brand exists.* The follower count is a trust signal for humans browsing the page — not a technical input to your ad performance.

Buying followers is against Instagram's Terms of Service. It is also extremely common. Instagram's enforcement is detection-based, not intent-based — the question is whether you trigger their automated detection systems, not whether you're technically in violation.

Done correctly (slowly, with quality accounts), the risk of account restriction is low. Done incorrectly (buying 10,000 overnight from a $5 bot farm), the account can be flagged within hours.

### Safe Follower Acquisition: The Rules

**Rule 1 — Start late, not day one.** Wait until the account is 3–5 days old and has at least 3–5 posts published. An account with 0 posts and 500 followers acquired in 24 hours triggers every detection flag.

**Rule 2 — Slow drip, not bulk.** Safe rate: **100–300 followers per day** maximum. At 200/day, you reach 1,000 followers in 5 days, and 2,800 by the end of 2 weeks. This is the target range — do not exceed it.

**Rule 3 — Quality over volume.** The difference between safe and flagged is follower quality. Safe services deliver accounts that look real: profile photos, some posts of their own, real-looking usernames, geo-distributed. Unsafe services deliver bot accounts: generic usernames, no profile photos, 0 posts. Instagram's detection is more sophisticated at identifying bot characteristics than it is at catching the buying behavior itself.

**Rule 4 — Never give them your password.** Any service that asks for your login credentials is a security risk. Reputable services deliver followers by targeting your account from their side — they never need your password.

**Rule 5 — Do not run follower campaigns simultaneously.** Don't run Instagram's own "profile visits" ad objective at the same time as buying followers. The combination of paid profile traffic plus unusual follow activity looks synthetic.

### Target Follower Count

For a regional PI consumer brand like Fair Case — starting in Colorado, running lead gen ads — the right credibility range is **3,000–4,000 followers** at launch.

| Milestone | What It Signals to an Ad Visitor |
|---|---|
| Under 500 | Brand new account — trust kill for a legal brand asking for personal info |
| 1,000 | Passes the sniff test, but reads as early-stage and unproven |
| 3,000–4,000 | **Target.** Looks like a legitimate regional service brand that's been operating for a year or two |
| Over 10,000 | Backfires in a trust-sensitive category — a CO injury page with 15K followers and 11 likes per post is an obvious red flag |

**The math at a safe rate over 2.5–3 weeks (21 days):**

| Daily Rate | Day 21 Total |
|---|---|
| 150/day | ~3,150 followers |
| 200/day | ~4,200 followers |

**Recommended target before ad launch: 3,000–4,000.** Start buying on day 4–5 (once 3–5 posts are live), run at 150–200/day, taper slightly in week 3. That number paired with 14 posts and a complete profile is more than enough to pass the trust audit of any ad-driven visitor.

Don't chase 10K. The credibility ceiling for a regional brand in a sensitive category is lower than you think — too many followers with no engagement is worse than the right number.

## Step 4: Ad Account Connection and Pixel Warmup

Before running lead gen campaigns, run a low-budget video view or traffic campaign to warm the Pixel.

**Why:** Meta's lead gen algorithm performs better when the Pixel has existing conversion data. Cold Pixel = expensive early CPLs. Warmed Pixel = cheaper leads faster.

**Warmup campaign setup:**
- Objective: Video Views or Traffic (not Lead Gen yet)
- Budget: $10–20/day
- Duration: 5–7 days before switching to lead gen
- Creative: Top-performing ad concept (the one you're most confident in)
- Targeting: Colorado, age 25–65, broad (let Meta find the audience)

After 5–7 days, switch the campaign to Lead Generation objective. The Pixel now has data on who engaged with the content and can optimize toward similar people.

## Step 5: Ongoing Social (Post-Launch)

Once ads are running, the page only needs to look active — not perform organically.

**Sustainable cadence:** 3 posts/week, mix of content types  
**Stories:** 2–3x/week (repurpose post content)  
**Engagement:** You don't need to chase comments or DMs at launch. If you get them, respond — but don't spend time soliciting it.

**Chopping from ad creative:** When a paid ad performs well, repurpose the concept as an organic post. Indigo's Chop Matrix applies here. The winning ad hook → carousel post → Story → Reel. Maximum output from existing production.
