## Channel and Platform

**Primary channel:** Meta (Facebook and Instagram) — 95–100% of B2C ad spend.

**Platform constraint — Special Ad Categories:**  
Personal injury legal advertising operates under Meta's Special Ad Category designation. This means:
- **No demographic targeting** — no age, gender, household income
- **No interest targeting** — no behavioral or psychographic stacks
- **No lookalike audiences** built from demographic seeds
- **Geographic targeting only** — DMA / city / zip level
- **Creative is the only targeting tool**

This is not a limitation. It is the mechanism that makes creative quality the entire competitive advantage. The ad that stops the right person mid-scroll while being invisible to everyone else is not built through an algorithm — it is built through craft.

**The self-selection mechanism:**  
The ad must be specific enough to resonate immediately with a person who had an accident and be completely uninteresting to someone who didn't. Precision through creative, not precision through data. The more specific and true the ad is to the claimant's situation, the stronger the self-selection effect. Vague ads waste spend.

---

## The 99%/1% Mandate

At any given moment, approximately 1% of people in a market have had a recent accident that constitutes a viable PI case. The other 99% have not. The advertising reaches all of them.

**Wrong approach:** Emotional manipulation (crash footage, scare language, manufactured urgency) that briefly captures attention from everyone and sustains attention from no one specific.

**Right approach:** Creative so specifically true to the claimant's situation that it resonates instantly with the 1% — and so specific that the 99% don't register it as relevant to them. The goal is not to avoid annoying the 99%. The goal is to be so specific to the 1% that the 99% self-select out.

**How each tier solves this:**
- **Tier 1:** The specificity of the situation ("you received a call from their insurance company" — only someone who did receives this as relevant)
- **Tier 2:** The specificity of the consequence ("your bills are adding up" — only someone living with an unresolved claim registers this)
- **Tier 3:** The specificity of the deadline ("in [state], you have X years" — only someone who had an accident and hasn't acted registers this as urgent)

## Tier 1 — Days 0–30 (Active Decision Window)

### Who We're Reaching

The working adult who had a car accident in the last 30 days. They may have received a call from the insurance adjuster. They may have already accepted a first offer or started negotiating. They may not have taken any action yet.

They are not "a personal injury claimant" in their own mind. They are a person whose life got disrupted. Their dominant emotion is confusion + low-level anxiety + a desire to resolve and move on. They are not looking for a lawyer. They are looking for information that helps them make the right decision.

### What the Ad Must Accomplish

Create a moment of genuine recognition — *"that's exactly my situation"* — followed by a single, low-barrier action that feels like getting information, not signing up for anything. The ad does not sell legal services. It provides the one fact that makes a brief, free phone call the obvious next step.

### Core Message

*"The insurance company's job is to settle your claim for as little as possible. Before you respond to anything — one free call to hear what you actually have."*

Or stripped:  
*"Before you respond to the insurance company — hear what you actually have. Free. Ten minutes."*

**CTA:** Get a Fair Case / See if you qualify (quiz entry)

### Visual Direction (Tier 1)

**The moment:** The decision moment before an action. Not the accident. Not the aftermath. The specific moment — the phone in hand, the pause before answering, the kitchen counter where the adjuster's card is sitting.

**The register:** Cinematic. Intentionally produced. The claimant's world made specific and real — not staged, but directed. The light is right because someone cared about the light. The face is specific because the casting was specific. The environment is recognizable because the detail was chosen.

**What it does NOT show:**
- The crash or accident
- A lawyer in a suit
- Scales of justice or legal imagery
- Dollar amounts
- Crying or dramatic pain performance
- Actors who read as "typical PI advertising"

**The emotion being evoked:** The weight of a real decision. The specific feeling of standing at a fork in the road and not being sure which path is right — and then someone gives you the information that clarifies it.

**Production approach:**
- AI-assisted cinematography — intentionally cinematic, not trying to look found
- Controlled environments: kitchen, parked car, waiting room, evening light through a window
- Movement is deliberate — not handheld verité, not locked-off static. Camera moves with a point of view.
- Sound design: minimal, diegetic, real
- Color grade: warm but not cheerful. The emotional register of a significant moment in an ordinary day.

## Tier 2 — 1–6 Months (Accumulation Phase)

### Who We're Reaching

The claimant who is 1–6 months past their accident. Either they accepted a first offer and are living with it, or they're still undecided. Medical bills are arriving. Financial reality is setting in. Their situation is heavier now.

### Core Message

*"Most people who accepted a first offer didn't know they had other options. Some still do. One call to find out where you stand."*

For the undecided claimant still in the window:  
*"The bills keep arriving. The offer might not be enough to cover them. One call — find out if you have options."*

**CTA:** Find out if you still have options / Get a Fair Case

### Visual Direction (Tier 2)

**The moment:** The consequence, not the event. The kitchen table with bills on it. The person on the phone with the insurance company, expression unreadable. The late-evening calculation — the specific look of someone doing math in their head that doesn't add up.

**Shift from Tier 1:** Slightly heavier register. The decision was made — or is being made under financial pressure. Same visual language and production quality as Tier 1, but the specific imagery shifts from the pre-decision moment to the living-with-it moment.

---

## Tier 3 — 6+ Months to Statute of Limitations

### Who We're Reaching

The claimant who has not acted. They may not know there is a statute of limitations. They may have assumed it was too late long ago. They may have a real case that is still viable — approaching the window where it permanently closes.

### Core Message

*"In [state], you have [X] years from the date of your accident to file a claim. After that, you can't. If you're not sure whether you still have time — one call to find out."*

The specific deadline is the ad. It does not need support. The fact is the creative.

**CTA:** Find out if your window is still open / Get a Fair Case

**State-specific production requirement:** Each Tier 3 ad must include the correct SOL for the specific state it runs in. Build templates with state-specific copy variables. Produce at scale across target markets.

### Visual Direction (Tier 3)

**The moment:** The calendar. The clock. Not scary or ticking — in the way that a specific date on a page means something. The visual language of time-awareness without manufactured dread.

Can be the most visually stripped of the three tiers. The fact is doing the work. The visual serves the fact.

## What All Three Tiers Share

**Visual language:**
- Cinematic production quality — same level of care, same color temperature, same attention to environmental detail
- The claimant's world, made specific — their environments, their objects, their light
- No lawyer imagery in B2C
- No crash imagery — the accident is past; the decision is present
- Motion is intentional — camera placement and movement signal that someone made a choice about where to look

**Tonal language:**
- One specific fact
- One minimum-barrier action
- No manufacturing of fear or urgency beyond what the situation genuinely contains
- The claimant addressed as an intelligent adult making a real decision

## Production Workflow

**Volume requirement:**  
Fair Case's competitive advantage includes hyper-localized creative production at scale. The same campaign framework runs across multiple DMAs with market-specific elements (city/state, local details, SOL by state for Tier 3).

**AI-assisted production:**  
The cinematic quality required is achievable at scale through AI-assisted creative production — but the quality threshold must be maintained. The test for every piece of produced creative: does it look like someone who cared made it? If the details are wrong, the production is not complete.

**The quality floor:**  
Fair Case advertising is the primary trust signal for the brand. If the creative falls below the quality threshold, the trust signal fails before the claimant ever calls. Every piece of creative must be reviewed against this brief before it goes live.

**The feedback loop:**  
CPSC tracking per creative concept, per market, per tier. What worked for whom, in which markets, at which stage of the claim cycle. This data feeds back into the creative iteration cycle.

## Visual Style — Cinematic Direction

**⚠️ Note:** This section establishes the confirmed cinematic direction. The specific testing framework (Style A vs. Style B) is documented separately in `04_Visual_Identity_Brief/B2C_CREATIVE_PRODUCTION_BRIEF.md`.

The B2C visual direction for Fair Case is **cinematic heightened realism** — not "ordinary is the point."

"Ordinary" describes the subject matter: real people, real environments, real situations. Cinematic describes the execution: intentional framing, deliberate movement, precise casting, specific light, controlled color.

This matters because:
1. Scroll-stopping requires visual contrast — content that registers as different from the surrounding feed
2. Cinematic execution conditions the viewer to read the situation as meaningful and weighty
3. The Hollywood register creates aspirational realism — people have been trained by film and television to read cinematic imagery as "heightened real life," which is more compelling than literal reality

**What cinematic means in practice:**
- Camera placement and movement signal deliberate intention
- Faces are chosen for specificity, not generic attractiveness
- Environments are chosen for recognizability and detail, not because they were convenient
- Color grading is intentional — the emotional temperature of the frame is controlled
- Every frame could be a still that communicates something

**What cinematic does NOT mean:**
- Over-produced, glossy, ad-looking
- Generic stock footage aesthetic
- Drama for drama's sake — every element serves the emotional moment

## What Fair Case Never Does in B2C Advertising

1. Promises a dollar amount it cannot guarantee for that specific situation
2. Uses crash footage or dramatized accident imagery
3. Claims the claimant "definitely has a case"
4. Creates fake urgency where no genuine deadline exists
5. Uses fear of the insurance company as the primary motivating emotion
6. Runs any copy that a reasonable person would call manipulative after reading it
7. Uses actors presenting as real clients
8. Targets by demographic — relies exclusively on creative self-selection + geographic targeting

## Launch Constraint

**At launch, Fair Case has no real claimant testimonials.** They do not exist until first cases resolve (Phase 2). At launch, B2C creative is built around:
- Publicly sourced data: the $33,700 gap, the 68% who accept first offers
- The honest positioning — information, not manipulation
- The cinematic visual language described in this brief

**No actors presenting as real clients. Ever.**
