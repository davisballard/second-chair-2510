## Governing Principle

*"Second Chair must feel trustworthy before it feels impressive."*

This sentence from the Attorney Buying Voice research governs every section of this document. The category is full of impressive claims. The attorney has heard them all and doesn't believe them. What moves an attorney to try a new vendor is the feeling that this company understands their world and isn't trying to manipulate them. Every brand decision — message, character, voice, proof — is evaluated against this standard before anything else.

---

## Section 1 — Positioning Statement (Locked)

**For whom:** PI attorneys who control their firm's lead generation spend — typically the managing attorney or sole practitioner, the primary decision-maker even in firms with marketing staff — who have bought leads before and been burned by advertising that exploited the claimant's vulnerability, and who are now looking for a vendor they don't feel they need to be defended against.

**Who find that:** Every major PI lead gen vendor generates leads from advertising that manipulates the claimant's vulnerability to produce volume — which means the claimant arrives at the intake call with inflated expectations the attorney must manage down before the real conversation can begin, eroding conversion rates and eroding the attorney's sense of the practice they built.

**Second Chair is:** The only PI lead gen company that builds advertising that honestly persuades claimants, producing leads who arrive with accurate expectations and the readiness to engage — because the advertising earned their response rather than manufacturing it.

**Because:** Davis Ballard's 18 years of brand strategy and creative direction, Alex DuBelko's compliance-grade product engineering, and Sasha Zinshtein's Fortune 500 programmatic media expertise and financial-fluency sales practice are integrated from founding into the same company — which means the creative, the intake pipeline, the compliance architecture, and the attorney relationship are all calibrated to the same single outcome: a claimant who was honestly won and who arrives ready.

**So that:** The attorney can evaluate the advertising before they commit, trust that every claimant was served honestly, report on cost-per-signed-case as the primary relationship health metric, and finally work with a lead gen vendor they don't need to verify every month.

## Section 2 — Single-Minded Proposition (Locked)

*"The leads PI attorneys buy should be as honestly won as the cases they take."*

**What "honestly won" means in Second Chair's vocabulary:**
A case is honestly won through genuine advocacy — the attorney believed in it, worked it, earned the outcome. A lead is honestly won through advertising that earns the claimant's response: specific, accurate information about what they have, emotional resonance that is true to their real situation, no manufactured urgency, no false claims about outcome. The claimant responded because something genuine moved them. That is the standard. The attorney who receives that lead gets someone who came for the right reasons — and arrives with accurate expectations.

**The claim chain underneath the SMP:**
Honestly won → claimant arrives with accurate expectations → claimant is emotionally ready to engage → converts at a higher rate than the manipulated claimant → the ethical choice and the commercial choice are the same

This is the commercial proof that makes "honestly won" a performance argument, not just an ethical one. Honesty is not a constraint on results — it is the mechanism that produces them.

## Section 3 — B2B Message Architecture

### Brand Belief
*(One thing. Never changes. The conviction that makes everything else coherent.)*

**"The attorney who fights for people deserves leads that were fought for honestly."**

---

### Primary Message
*(What the attorney hears first. The door-opener. Specific, verifiable, low-barrier to evaluate.)*

**"Second Chair builds advertising you can evaluate before you commit — because we built it to be worth showing."**

This message works because:
- It makes an offer no other PI lead gen vendor has made (show the advertising before commitment)
- It implies confidence in the product without stating it
- It requires zero trust to engage — the attorney evaluates, they don't believe in advance
- It is verifiable in the first conversation, which reduces perceived risk immediately

---

### Supporting Messages
*(Three only. In priority order. Each tied to a verifiable proof point.)*

**1. The performance proof:**
*"Claimants who were honestly persuaded arrive with accurate expectations and convert at a higher rate. The ethical choice and the commercial choice are the same."*

Why it works: It reframes honesty as a performance argument, not a moral one. The attorney doesn't have to care about ethics to care about conversion rates. The claim is testable and measurable — which means it builds credibility over the relationship, not just at the point of sale.

Proof point: The intake call from a manipulated claimant vs. an honestly persuaded one. The difference is visible, specific, and felt immediately by any attorney who has managed a bad intake call.

**2. The metric proof:**
*"We give you your cost-per-signed-case — not CPL. CPL tells you what you paid to talk to someone. CPSC tells you what you paid to sign them. Those numbers are different. Most attorneys don't know how different."*

Why it works: It educates without condescending. The attorney who doesn't know their CPSC is invited to find out, not made to feel ignorant. The attorney who does know it is being offered a smarter comparison tool.

Proof point: Second Chair reports CPSC as the primary metric on every account by default. No other PI lead gen vendor does this without being asked.

**3. The infrastructure proof:**
*"The creative, the compliance infrastructure, and the media execution are run by the same founding team. There's no vendor handoff — no layer where the claim breaks down."*

Why it works: Directly addresses the most common way lead gen vendors fail — they outsource one piece (usually the creative or the compliance) and the hand-off is where quality collapses. Second Chair's founding structure eliminates this.

Proof point: The three founders' specific roles — Davis (creative and strategy), Alex (product and compliance infrastructure), Sasha (media execution and attorney sales) — are operated from the same company with shared accountability to the same metric (CPSC).

### The Four Buying Moments — How Second Chair Is Present at Each

The Attorney Buying Voice research identifies four specific moments when PI attorneys actually become buyers. Second Chair's sales and content strategy is built around being present at all four.

**Moment 1: The 2 AM Ceiling Math**
The managing partner alone with the numbers — cases in, revenue out, the math getting frightening. Maximum buying readiness, zero defenses. Second Chair's presence: podcast content, digital touchpoints, LinkedIn presence that speaks to this specific private anxiety without manufacturing it. Not "buy now" — "here's how to understand your case acquisition cost."

**Moment 2: The Peer Hallway Recommendation**
"I've been using Second Chair and it's actually working." This is the most efficient buyer conversion available. Second Chair's strategy: every satisfied attorney is a systematically activated referral source. Not "do you know someone?" but "here's how to introduce them, here's what to tell them."

**Moment 3: The Switching Trigger**
The attorney who discovered their "exclusive" leads were sold to three other firms. The one who got a bar association compliance warning. The Vaziri Law moment. This attorney is an active buyer for 2–4 weeks. Second Chair's strategy: content and digital presence that surfaces when attorneys are searching in crisis mode — comparison searches, competitor alternatives, quality complaints.

**Moment 4: The Sales Call That Understands the Practice**
Sasha's call. The one that demonstrates Second Chair knows what a contingency fee is, knows why Q1 is slow, knows what "soft tissue" means in the case mix, knows the difference between pre-lit and lit. The attorney who gets this call doesn't experience it as a vendor pitch. They experience it as a peer conversation.

## Section 4 — B2C Message Architecture

*Messages are differentiated by timing tier. The claimant in Days 0–30 is in a completely different emotional and decision state from the claimant approaching their SOL. The advertising must speak to who they are in that specific moment — not to a generic "accident victim."*

---

### Tier 1 — Days 0–30 (Immediately Post-Accident, Decision Fully Open)

**Who they are:** The working person who had an accident, received a call from the insurance company, and isn't sure what to do. They haven't accepted or declined. They may not know they have a case. They're not "a personal injury claimant" in their own mind — they're someone who got hurt and is trying to figure out what happens next.

**Recognition message (what stops them mid-scroll):**
*"If you were in an accident recently — you'll want to hear this before you respond to the insurance company."*

or more specific:
*"The insurance company called. Here's what they're not telling you."*

**Evidence message (the specific fact that creates belief):**
*"People who consulted an attorney before accepting an offer received significantly more than those who didn't. One conversation. Ten minutes. No cost."*

**Action message (minimum viable ask):**
*"Before you respond to anything — one free call."*

**What this creative must NOT do:**
- Manufacture urgency that isn't real ("Act now before it's too late!")
- Overclaim what a lawyer can do ("We'll get you what you deserve!")
- Talk down to them ("Did you know you could be owed THOUSANDS?")
- Make them feel like they're being sold something

**What this creative must DO:**
- Show them something specifically true about their situation
- Give them a reason to make one small, free, reversible action
- Respect their intelligence and their right to make their own decision

---

### Tier 2 — 1–6 Months (Accumulation Phase — Bills Arriving, Situation Unresolved)

**Who they are:** The claimant who accepted a first offer and is now wondering if they made a mistake, or who is still negotiating and watching the bills accumulate. Their anxiety is financial, not procedural — they're living with the consequences now.

**Recognition message:**
*"Your bills are still coming in. The settlement you received might not cover them."*

or:
*"You accepted a settlement. But medical bills don't stop."*

**Evidence message:**
*"Most accident victims who accept the first offer don't know they had other options. Some did. Some still do."*

**Action message:**
*"Find out where you stand. One call. No commitment required."*

---

### Tier 3 — 6+ Months to SOL (Late Stage, Deadline Approaching)

**Who they are:** The claimant who hasn't acted, who may not know there is a deadline, and who may still have a viable case they've never pursued. The advertising is informational, not emotional — this person needs a fact, not inspiration.

**Recognition message:**
*"There's a deadline on your right to pursue a claim. Most people don't know when it is."*

**Evidence message (must be state-specific):**
*"In [state], you have [X] years from the date of your accident to file. After that, the option closes. The clock is running."*

**Action message:**
*"If you've never spoken to an attorney about your accident — find out if you still can."*

**Critical note for Tier 3:** This creative must be genuinely informational, not manufactured urgency. The deadline is real. State the deadline. State it specifically. Do not manufacture fear around it.

## Section 5 — Brand Character

*Four traits. Each defined by what it IS and what it IS NOT. The "is not" definition is more useful than the "is" — it prevents the character from collapsing into generic brand language.*

---

### Trait 1: Expert, Not Arrogant

**Is:** Confident in the craft because the craft is real. Speaks with specific authority drawn from verifiable experience. Names what has been done and why it matters to the attorney sitting across from Sasha, not to a general audience. Doesn't claim to be the best — demonstrates something specific that competitors can't match.

**Is not:** Chest-thumping. Does not invoke "industry-leading," "best-in-class," or "cutting-edge" language. Does not dismiss competitors — makes them irrelevant by demonstrating something they can't replicate. Does not ask for trust in advance of earning it.

**Sounds like:** *"Here's the advertising. Tell us what you think."*

**Does not sound like:** *"We are the premier PI lead generation solution in the market."*

---

### Trait 2: Honest in the Specific, Not the Abstract

**Is:** Uses exact numbers, real situations, specific processes. When something is uncertain or not yet proven, says so. Does not reach for abstract commitments ("we're committed to quality") — reaches for concrete evidence instead. Makes the claimant's situation and the attorney's situation visible in specific, recognizable detail.

**Is not:** Values performance. Does not gesture at commitment without showing the evidence underneath it. Does not say "we're transparent" — is transparent, specifically: shows the advertising, shows the compliance record, shows the CPSC number.

**Sounds like:** *"Your cost-per-signed-case from our leads last quarter was $X. Here's the breakdown of what drove that number."*

**Does not sound like:** *"We're dedicated to delivering transparent, accountable lead generation solutions."*

---

### Trait 3: Respectful of Intelligence — Theirs and Ours

**Is:** Treats the attorney as a capable evaluator of a business investment who has seen every vendor pitch and isn't impressed by any of them. Speaks the language of case acquisition economics, not marketing. Does not explain the value of lead gen to a PI attorney — they know. Explains specifically what Second Chair does differently, with evidence.

**Is not:** Selling. Not a pitch. Does not use urgency tactics, competitor fear, or scarcity mechanics. Does not explain things the attorney already knows. Does not treat the claimant as a demographic — treats them as the specific person who is in the middle of a real situation that has real consequences for their life.

**Sounds like (B2B):** *"What's your current cost-per-signed-case from your existing vendors? We'd like to understand the baseline before we make any proposals."*

**Does not sound like:** *"Act now — limited markets available!"*

**Sounds like (B2C):** *"Before you respond to anything, make one call. Ten minutes. Free. Here's what you should know."*

**Does not sound like:** *"You could be owed THOUSANDS! Call now!"*

---

### Trait 4: Weighted, Not Heavy

**Is:** Serious in register without being stiff. Carries weight because what it's talking about — a person's decision about a life-changing claim, an attorney's professional identity — actually has weight. Does not perform warmth or perform seriousness. Is what it is because the work is done carefully.

**Is not:** Corporate. Not full of legal or marketing jargon. Not formal for formality's sake. Not a startup talking about "disruption." Not an ad agency talking about "storytelling." Not a law firm talking about "zealous advocacy." Something specific that the category has never produced: a vendor that speaks like a principal, not a salesperson.

**Sounds like:** *"The intake call is the first impression an attorney makes on a potential client. We try to make sure it starts right."*

**Does not sound like:** *"We're disrupting the PI lead acquisition ecosystem with innovative, data-driven solutions that leverage our proprietary technology platform."*

## Section 6 — Tone of Voice

*Written by Ed McCabe. Reviewed by Jon Steel. Not a list of adjectives — a set of principles with real examples.*

---

### The Core Principle

The attorney who hears Second Chair speak should feel, before they consciously recognize it: *this person understands my world and is not trying to manipulate me.*

Every word that undermines that feeling must be cut. Every sentence that performs confidence instead of demonstrating it must be rewritten. The register is earned peer, not vendor pitch.

---

### B2B Voice — Speaking to the Decision-Maker

**Principle 1: Use the fewest words that contain the full weight of the idea.**
Every extra word is debt. If the sentence can be cut in half and the meaning sharpens, cut it in half.

*Heavy:* "We believe that PI attorneys deserve to have access to leads that have been generated through the highest possible standards of ethical and transparent advertising practice."
*Right:* "The leads PI attorneys buy should be as honestly won as the cases they take."

**Principle 2: The sentence should do one thing. When it's doing two, split it or cut one.**

*Two things:* "Our compliance infrastructure is best-in-class and our leads convert at higher rates because of our honest advertising methodology."
*One thing each:* "Here's the compliance infrastructure — TrustedForm on every lead, fail-closed DNC screening, no shared leads." Pause. "Here's what that produces in the intake room."

**Principle 3: Specificity is credibility.**
"Our leads convert better" is not credible. "The claimant who was honestly persuaded arrives with accurate expectations. They don't require damage control. That difference is visible in the first five minutes of the intake call." — that's something the attorney can evaluate.

**Principle 4: Write for the attorney who has already bought and been burned.**
Don't explain why lead gen matters — they know. Don't explain what CPSC is — either they know or the explanation is one sentence. Start from the assumption that they've been disappointed before and show them specifically what's different.

**Principle 5: Never end with a question in B2B copy.**
Questions position Second Chair as seeking permission. State. Don't ask.
*Wrong:* "Would you like to see how we can help your firm?"
*Right:* "Before we go further — here's the advertising we're running. Take a look."

**What Second Chair never says in B2B:**
- "We're committed to..."
- "We're dedicated to..."
- "We're passionate about..."
- "Best-in-class" / "industry-leading" / "cutting-edge" / "innovative"
- "Solution" (as a standalone noun)
- "Ecosystem" / "synergy" / "leverage" (as a verb)
- "Game-changing" / "revolutionary" / "transformative"
- "We get results" (without immediately specifying what results, for whom, and how)

---

### B2C Voice — Speaking to the Claimant

**Different person. Different state. Different register entirely.**

The B2C claimant is not reading marketing copy — they're in the middle of their life. Something happened. They're confused, possibly in pain, probably worried about money. The advertising meets them there. It doesn't try to inspire them. It gives them a specific fact and a minimum-barrier next step.

**Principle 1: One specific fact. One specific action.**
Not a sales pitch. Not a story. One thing they didn't know. One thing they can do about it. That's the ad.

**Principle 2: Never manufacture urgency that isn't real.**
The SOL deadline is real — state it precisely. The value of consulting an attorney early is real — state it specifically. "Act NOW before it's too late!" for a case that is nowhere near its deadline is manipulation. The advertising must never cross this line.

**Principle 3: The claimant is not a victim who needs rescue. They are a person making a real decision.**
Write to their agency, not their vulnerability. "You deserve to know what you have before you decide" — not "don't let the insurance company take advantage of you." One is respectful. The other is fear.

**Principle 4: Tier-specific. Always.**
Tier 1 language (they haven't decided anything yet) is different from Tier 2 language (they're living with an unresolved situation) which is different from Tier 3 language (they have a deadline). Each ad must know which tier it's speaking to.

**What Second Chair never says in B2C:**
- "You could be owed THOUSANDS" (caps, implied amount the ad can't guarantee)
- "Don't let them get away with it" (antagonism without substance)
- "You may have a case!" (may + exclamation point = the lowest form of PI advertising)
- "Call now before it's too late!" (for any situation where there is not a genuine, specific, imminent deadline)
- "We'll fight for you!" (promise the attorney makes, not the advertising)
- Any dollar amount Second Chair cannot specifically substantiate for that specific situation

## Section 7 — Proof Points

*These are the verifiable facts that support every brand claim Second Chair makes. Pre-launch proof only — specific, concrete, statable without qualification. Post-launch format to be populated with performance data.*

### Pre-Launch — Provable Now

**The primary proof mechanism:**
Second Chair shows the advertising it runs to generate leads before any attorney commits to a partnership. This is not available from any other PI lead gen vendor. It is the most disarming and differentiated move in the category.

**Compliance architecture:**
- TrustedForm full stack (certify + verify + retain) on every lead: court-admissible consent record, advertising origin documentation, full audit trail
- Fail-closed DNC/litigator screening: compliance is architectural, not opt-in — leads that fail screening are not delivered
- No shared leads: a lead generated for one attorney is not sold to any other attorney

**Reporting and transparency:**
- CPSC reported as the primary account metric by default — no other PI lead gen vendor does this without being specifically requested
- Dual scoring engine: Conversion Score and Case Value Score visible to the attorney, not just internally tracked

**The founding team's specific credentials (verifiable by any attorney who investigates):**

- Alex DuBelko: Mobile platform product architecture at TradeStation (FINRA/SEC-adjacent compliance infrastructure, crash-free performance at scale), CXL/Reforge certifications, Summit Analytics consulting
- Sasha Zinshtein: 14+ years programmatic media, programmatic buying for 18+ Fortune 500 brands at 33across (ASOS, Bose, Diageo, H&R Block, Pepsico, Tinder, and others); Director of Growth at The Flower Agency (restricted-category advertising with no standard platform access — direct operational analog to PI Special Ad Categories); financial underwriting at Riverpoint Capital (company financials, capital placement, payback modeling)

## Section 8 — What Second Chair Never Does

*The brand's non-negotiables. Specific enough to be actionable. Permanent regardless of competitive pressure or revenue opportunity.*

**1. Never runs misleading advertising.**
The advertising must be honest in every material claim about what a claimant has, what an attorney can do, and what the process involves. This means: no fake urgency, no false guarantees of settlement outcome, no deceptive testimonials, no misleading implications about the legal process. If the advertising would embarrass the attorney whose name will appear on the intake call, it doesn't run.

**2. Never shares leads between attorneys.**
A lead is generated for one attorney. It is delivered to one attorney. It is never resold, re-routed, or shared with another firm — regardless of market coverage considerations or revenue opportunity.

**3. Never hides the advertising from attorney partners.**
Any attorney who asks to see the advertising that generated their leads gets it — immediately, in full. This includes the creative, the landing page, the campaign parameters, and the TrustedForm record on any specific lead.

**4. Never reports CPL as the primary metric.**
CPL tells an attorney what they paid to talk to someone. CPSC tells them what they paid to sign a case. These numbers are different in ways that matter. Second Chair always leads with CPSC. CPL may appear in reporting but is never presented as the primary success metric.

**5. Never takes an attorney partner whose intake practices contradict what the advertising promises.**
The advertising creates an expectation in the claimant. If the attorney's intake process fails that expectation — through deceptive, high-pressure, or exploitative intake tactics — the advertising's work is destroyed and the claimant is harmed. Second Chair reviews the intake practices of prospective attorney partners before signing them, and reserves the right to end partnerships where the intake reality doesn't match what the advertising set up.

**6. Never pitches volume as the primary value.**
Volume is a function of the market, the budget, and the campaign. It is a means, not an end. The end is signed cases at a cost that works for the attorney's business. Second Chair leads every conversation with quality and CPSC, not lead count.

**7. Never uses fear, artificial urgency, or manufactured scarcity in B2C advertising.**
The claimant's situation has enough real urgency (a real deadline, real bills, a real decision to make). The advertising does not need to manufacture more. When a genuine deadline exists (SOL approaching), it is stated specifically and factually. When it does not exist, it is not implied.

**8. Never claims to be something it isn't — yet.**
If Second Chair does not yet have data in a specific market, it says so. If a capability is planned but not built, it is described as planned. If a claim requires qualification, the qualification is included. The brand's long-term credibility depends on not overpromising at launch.

## The Architecture in One Paragraph

Second Chair is the PI lead gen company built for the attorney who entered the profession to fight for people and who has spent years managing down the expectations that misleading lead gen advertising created. The brand's commercial claim is that honestly won leads — claimants who were genuinely, skillfully persuaded — convert at higher rates than manipulated ones, which means the ethical choice and the commercial choice are the same. The proof is immediate and specific: here is the advertising Second Chair runs. Evaluate it before committing to anything. The voice is earned peer — specific, weighted, without performance. The character is expert without arrogance, honest in the specific, respectful of the attorney's intelligence. The visual work is cinematic — intentionally produced, emotionally precise, honest in its emotional logic. The non-negotiable at the center of all of it: Second Chair is the first vendor in the PI lead gen category that the attorney doesn't feel they need to be defended against.
