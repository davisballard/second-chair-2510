## Single-Minded Proposition

*"The leads PI attorneys buy should be as honestly won as the cases they take."*

---

## Who Are We Talking To

**B2B — The Decision-Maker:**

Jennifer Walsh. Managing attorney of a 3–6 person PI firm. She runs the practice, controls the marketing spend, and makes vendor decisions herself. She may have a paralegal who filters her calls — but if someone gets through, it's her on the other line.

She entered PI law because she believed in fighting for people. She has spent years buying leads from vendors whose advertising she would never show to a client. She knows the category is broken. She has said so — privately, to a colleague over drinks. She has never found the alternative.

She is not looking for inspiration. She is not looking for a brand that shares her values. She is looking for someone who can deliver what they promise without requiring her to constantly verify they haven't lied to her. She is looking for a vendor she does not feel she needs to be defended against.

She has been burned before. She needs to see proof before she believes anything.

**B2C — The Claimant:**

Maria. Early 30s. Had an accident two weeks ago. Received a call from the insurance company. Hasn't responded. Not sure what she has or what she should do. She's not "a personal injury claimant" in her own head — she's someone whose life got disrupted and who doesn't want to make a mistake.

She is not looking for a lawyer. She is looking for information. She wants to know what she actually has before she decides anything. She is not afraid of making a call — she's afraid of being taken advantage of.

---

## What Do They Currently Think

**Jennifer (B2B):**
*"Every vendor says they're different. None of them are. I don't know what this company is actually doing to generate their leads, and I've learned not to ask because the answer is either evasive or something I don't want to know. The best I can do is track my conversion rate and fire them if the math stops working. This is the part of running a practice I hate."*

**Maria (B2C):**
*"I don't know if I have a case. I don't know if I'm the kind of person who sues. I don't want to deal with a lawyer. I just want to know what I'm entitled to before I say yes or no to anything."*

---

## What Do We Want Them To Think

**Jennifer:**
*"This is the first vendor who showed me what they do before asking me to sign anything. I can see how they reach people. I can see that the advertising is something I could stand behind. I want to try it."*

One behavior change: she agrees to see the advertising before she commits to anything.

**Maria:**
*"That ad was talking directly to me. I made the call because it felt like the right thing to do — not because I was scared into it."*

One behavior change: she makes one call before she makes any decision.

---

## Why Should They Believe Us

**For Jennifer:**
We show her the advertising before she signs anything. No other lead gen vendor in the PI category does this. The act of showing it — before any commitment — is the proof. She doesn't have to believe our claim about quality. She evaluates it herself, in real time.

**For Maria:**
The advertising does not tell her she has a case. It tells her she has the right to find out. The accuracy of that claim — that she deserves information before she decides — is something she can evaluate immediately and verify when she calls.

---

## The Creative Mandate

**Make advertising the claimant would thank Second Chair for running.**

Not advertising that manipulates. Not advertising that performs honesty. Advertising that gives the claimant something genuinely useful — a specific piece of information, a minimum-barrier action, a reason to make a call that is actually in their interest — and that the attorney who sees it in the pitch meeting thinks: *"I want my name associated with this."*

The B2C ad is the B2B proof. They are the same creative act. The ad that reaches Maria is the ad that Jennifer evaluates before she commits. If the ad passes both tests simultaneously — Maria calls because it gave her something real; Jennifer wants to show it — the creative mandate is met.

If it passes one test but not the other, it's not ready.

---

## Visual Direction

**Cinematic. Not naturalistic.**

The claimant's world — a kitchen, a car, an evening — elevated to the emotional register of the decision being made. Intentionally produced. The craft is visible. The care is visible. The emotional logic is honest even though the production is deliberate.

This is not found footage. It is not AI-generated content that tries to look like found footage and fails in the details. It is cinematic work that looks like someone who cared made it — because someone who cares did.

**The test for B2C creative:** Could the person who saw this ad and made the call — and had a good outcome — say "that ad was the reason I called"? If yes: the visual execution is right. If the creative depends on manufactured fear, scale-of-justice imagery, or exaggerated claims, it is wrong regardless of production quality.

**The test for B2B visual:** When the attorney sees this creative in the pitch meeting, do they think "this is advertising I can be associated with"? If yes: the B2B use of the B2C creative is working.

---

## What We Never Do

- Run advertising that exaggerates what the claimant has or what an attorney can deliver
- Use fear, artificial urgency, or manufactured scarcity ("Act now before it's too late!")
- Claim a dollar amount we can't substantiate for that specific situation
- Tell the attorney our leads are exclusive and then sell the same lead to another firm
- Pitch volume as the primary value
- Report CPL as the success metric when CPSC is what matters
- Describe our own advertising without showing it

---

## Hegarty's Test — Applied

*"Would a PI attorney show an ad produced from this brief to another PI attorney, unprompted?"*

**Answer:** Yes — if the creative mandate is met. The ad from this brief is the kind of thing an attorney who has been burned by the lead gen category would forward to a colleague with the message: "This is what it should look like." That is the test. That is the brief.
