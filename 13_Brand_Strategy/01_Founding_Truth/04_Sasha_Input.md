**Your answer:**

People think discovery is about asking the right questions. It's not. It's about understanding the economics of the other person's world faster than they expect you to. Because once someone realizes you actually understand the game they're playing, the conversation stops feeling like a sales call and starts feeling like a strategy session. That's when things actually open up.

Every first call I take, I'm trying to figure out three things immediately: how they actually win business driving revenue right now, where their pipeline breaks down and stops scaling, and what the real economics of their problem are. Because of that, I almost never open with a pitch. I don't leave discovery just because a timer went off. **Objection handling and closing — your style:**

Are you a closer by pressure (assumptive language, direct asks, urgency) or by education (teaching them something that makes saying yes the logical conclusion)? When do you lean into each?

**Your answer:**

Most people think closing is about pressure. It isn't. Closing is about clarity and timing. If someone fully understands the problem, believes the solution works, and feels safe making the decision, the close happens naturally. If one of those pieces is missing, no amount of clever language fixes it. The job in a sales conversation is simply to remove the friction that's stopping the decision from happening.

My default approach is education, not pressure. Not in the sense of giving lectures, but in the sense of helping the person see their situation clearly enough that the decision becomes obvious. Most objections aren't resistance; they're confusion. They happen when the prospect can't yet answer one of three questions: Will this actually work? Is the risk worth it? What happens if it doesn't? When those questions are answered clearly, people usually move forward without needing to be pushed.

But pressure certainly has its place. The time to use it is when someone already believes in the solution but is hesitating solely because of inertia. That hesitation shows up in phrases like, *"This makes sense,"* or *"Let me circle back internally."* At that point, the issue isn't belief; it's momentum. That's where a more direct close works: *"Sounds like the real question isn't whether this works—it's whether now is the right time to test it."* When someone already believes in the outcome, a clear ask helps them make the decision they were already leaning toward.

You can usually hear when someone is ready to close before they even say it. The conversation shifts from evaluation questions to operational ones. Instead of asking how it works, they ask how quickly we could start, how many leads it would generate, or what onboarding looks like. That shift is huge. It means their brain has moved from "Should we do this?" to "How would we implement this?" Another major signal is a change in tone—they stop debating the idea and start imagining the outcome, saying things like, *"If we added 20 more cases a month, our intake team would need to adjust."* When someone is mentally planning the future with your solution in it, the close is already halfway done. That's the moment I just ask: *"Would it make sense to move forward and test this?"* No theatrics. Just a clear next step.

When someone says, *"I need to think about it,"* it usually means one of three things: they're unsure about something specific, they don't feel enough urgency yet, or they're trying to end the conversation politely. The first move is never to push. It's to understand what's actually behind it. The easiest way is to acknowledge the hesitation directly: *"Totally fair. Usually when someone says that, it's because there's one part of this that doesn't feel fully clear yet. What's the piece you'd want to think through?"* That question shows I'm not trying to corner them, and it gives them permission to say the real concern out loud, which allows me to actually address it. Sometimes it reveals a solvable issue; sometimes it reveals that the timing truly isn't right. Both outcomes are useful, because a delayed decision that's honest is far more valuable than a rushed "yes" that falls apart a week later.

The truth is, most objections are not barriers. They're signals. They tell you exactly where the prospect still feels uncertainty or risk. If you treat objections like resistance, you are fighting the prospect. If you treat them like information, you learn how to move the conversation forward.

There's really only one objection that is genuinely hard to handle: a dial tone. The single hardest part of my job is when someone hangs up the phone, and I have to figure out how to call them back and get them to change their mind. Everything else is just a conversation.

**Measurement — adapting to CPSC:**

Second Chair measures success by cost-per-signed-case, not cost-per-lead. You've measured CPL throughout your career. What changes operationally when the primary success metric is downstream of the lead — in how you bid, how you target, how you optimize, how you report to the team?

What does the reporting dashboard you need to make good allocation decisions look like? What data, at what cadence?

**Your answer:**

If the goal is Cost Per Signed Case, the entire reporting system has to be built backward from that outcome. Most dashboards in legal marketing stop at cost-per-lead or cost-per-call. That's useful for media optimization, but it's completely incomplete. You can drive CPL down all day long and still destroy the economics if the cases don't retain. The dashboard that actually drives good allocation decisions connects the media, the lead, the intake, and the signed case in one continuous chain. Without that chain, you're flying blind.

Pushing CPSC as our primary focus requires intense alignment with the law firm. They have to share their data with us as efficiently as possible. We might have to build the technical bridge to make that happen, which is definitely in our wheelhouse, but their willingness to cooperate is the key. Our reporting dashboard needs to be as real-time as possible, and it has to answer one fundamental question: which dollars are producing signed cases, and which dollars are just producing activity?

To actually do that, we have to track four layers simultaneously. The first layer is top-of-funnel media performance—spend, impressions, clicks, CPL. That's where we get the daily tactical signals to kill bad creatives or shift budgets, but it doesn't determine allocation. The second layer is lead quality. We need to know our lead-to-qualified rate and our cost-per-qualified lead based on the intake disposition, like whether an accident actually occurred or an injury is present. The third layer is intake performance, which most marketing dashboards ignore. Two identical campaigns can produce wildly different results depending on the firm's intake execution. Measuring speed to first contact, call answer rate, and consult-to-retainer rate answers the uncomfortable question of whether the marketing is bad or the intake system is just leaking cases. Many "bad leads" are actually just slow follow-up.

Finally, the fourth layer is the real scoreboard: case outcomes. This is where we track signed cases, CPSC, and estimated case value so we can calculate the true return on ad spend in law firm economics. If our CPSC is $3,000 and the average case value is $12,000, we have a healthy acquisition model.

The ultimate dashboard flows top-to-bottom through the funnel. It lets me see instantly if Campaign A has a $90 CPL and a $4,000 CPSC, while Campaign B has a $180 CPL but a $2,200 CPSC. Most marketers would kill Campaign B based on the high lead cost, but a proper dashboard shows it's actually the better campaign. The more budget we put through this model, the faster these patterns emerge. Higher spend produces the signal density we need to make confident decisions instead of speculating on small datasets. Once we reach meaningful volume, the system stops being guesswork. It becomes an allocation engine that tells us exactly which channels produce the lowest CPSC, which campaigns produce the highest value cases, and precisely where the next dollar should go.
