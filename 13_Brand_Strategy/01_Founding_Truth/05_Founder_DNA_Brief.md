## What This Document Is

The Founding Truth Brief captures what Second Chair is strategically. This document captures something different: who the founders are emotionally, and why that emotional reality is the invisible substrate everything Second Chair does is built on.

It does not appear in advertising. It is not a pitch. It is not published externally. But every piece of creative, every client conversation, every product decision, every sales call, every policy choice should be filterable through it: *does this reflect who these founders actually are and what they actually believe?* If yes, it's Second Chair. If no, it's just another lead gen company with better positioning.

**How to use this document:**

- Strategy team: reference before and after every brand architecture decision
- Creative team: read before beginning any campaign concepting — this is the emotional brief that lives underneath the strategic brief
- Sales (Sasha): this is the document that articulates what you already know intuitively — read it and let it sharpen what you say in the first 30 seconds of every cold call
- Full agency: this is the reason Second Chair is worth building something real for, rather than just technically correct work

## Section 1: The Structural Parallel

**The pattern the founders have all lived.**

Davis built the Vacation® brand from zero. He did not get equity because he left before the vesting cliff. That is not a villain story about the Vacation founder — it is a structural story about how employment works. The person creating the value and the person controlling the economics are separated by design. The value does not flow back. Davis understood this from the inside.

Alex built a 0-to-1 mobile trading app at TradeStation. A million users. 99.96% crash-free. 100% FINRA/SEC compliant — a technical achievement that people who have not done it cannot fully appreciate. Then the role ended. Not because his work wasn't exceptional. Because TradeStation moved in a different direction, and the institutional scorecard — the thing that registers career outcomes for parents, for family, for the world — didn't register what he actually built. It registered the layoff.

Sasha built his career outside the family default path — deliberately, against expectation, with nobody handing him a blueprint. Every job was a bet: a $15-an-hour ad-tech internship in New York, a pawnshop, an architecture firm, financial sales. His parents came from Kyiv with nothing. His mother couldn't attend the colleges she earned because her family wouldn't allow it. The system he grew up inside measured success on a specific, narrow scoreboard — one that didn't recognize what he was actually building in himself.

The three of them share a specific experience: doing something genuinely extraordinary inside a system whose scoreboard doesn't reflect it.

**The same pattern in the PI lead gen category:**

The vendor earns on lead volume. The attorney absorbs quality risk. The claimant sees advertising that was designed to exploit their vulnerability, not inform their decision. The person creating the value — the attorney fighting a consequential case, the accident victim whose claim is legitimate — and the person controlling the economics — the lead gen vendor, the insurance adjuster — are structurally separated. The value does not flow back.

This is not a metaphor. It is the same mechanism the founders have each personally navigated. Davis's Vacation equity structure. Alex's TradeStation layoff. Sasha's parents' stolen educational opportunities. The PI lead gen category's misaligned vendor incentives. The insurance company's claim settlement machinery. Same structure. Different participants.

**Why this makes the founders uniquely credible:**

These founders have not read about this dynamic in a business school case study. They have been inside it. When a PI attorney in a first sales call detects — in Sasha's tone, in the product architecture Alex built, in the advertising Davis is designing — that the people across from them actually understand what it's like to do extraordinary work inside a system that doesn't give it back, that is a different kind of trust than any proof point can produce.

They are not building Second Chair because they identified a market opportunity. They are building it because they have spent their careers on the wrong side of the structural separation they are now designing their company to refuse to reproduce.

## Section 2: The Vocation Statement

**Why Second Chair is not a business venture.**

Davis put it plainly: he wants to build something where he is in control of his own destiny and his hard work pays off accordingly. He wants to build something he is genuinely proud of — not proud of in a pitch deck way, but proud of in terms of what it looks like, what it says, what it does in the world. And he wants it to put real fear into insurance companies. Not symbolically. Actually — through the cases it generates, through the attorneys it empowers, through the claimants it gets in front of real fighters.

Sasha put it plainly too: the deepest driver is not the success. It's the proof. Every swing he took, every bet he made on himself when other people doubted his decisions, led exactly here. He is building this because he possesses a singular, relentless urge to prove that taking the hard road was the right call. The attorneys Second Chair serves know exactly what that feels like. They chose a hard, stigmatized profession and have to prove themselves every time they walk into a courtroom or negotiate with a Goliath insurance carrier. The chip on the shoulder is shared. And that shared drive to prove every doubter wrong is, in Sasha's own words, the reason this company is going to win.

**The unified conviction — what is true for all three founders simultaneously:**

Second Chair exists because all three founders have been on the wrong side of a system that separates the people creating value from the people controlling the economics — and they are building a company that structurally refuses to reproduce that separation. For Davis, that means building the advertising he actually believes advertising should be, without a client brief filtering the vision. For Alex, that means building a product he controls from the architecture through the economic model. For Sasha, that means selling something he believes in with no incentive to oversell. For the first time, all three are on the right side of the incentive structure they have each, in different ways, been on the wrong side of.

**What Second Chair lets its founders do that they couldn't do anywhere else:**

Davis can build advertising that reflects what he actually believes advertising should be — without asking permission, without a client's risk tolerance filtering the vision. Alex can build product infrastructure he controls from the architecture through the business model. Sasha can sell something with genuine conviction, because the product is actually aligned with the attorney's outcomes rather than the vendor's revenue. None of this was available inside an employer's structure. All of it is now available — and all of it is their responsibility.

## Section 3: The Consumer Empowerment Mandate

**What the founders believe Second Chair's B2C advertising owes the accident victim.**

Davis has been watching in real time what it means to be inside a bureaucratic system that moves on its own timeline regardless of fairness, where power sits entirely on one side. His girlfriend — from a family of doctors and dentists, talented, hardworking — cannot get into the United States because she is an artist. He has pulled every string he has access to. He has reached the right people. The answer is still no, because the system is not designed for her. It is designed for people with land, capital, the right documentation of wealth.

That is the same experience the accident victim is having with the insurance adjuster. It does not matter how much they have paid in, how clearly they were wronged, how legitimate their claim is. The process was built by the entity with the power, and it runs in that entity's favor. The claimant is alone in that system unless someone shows them they are not.

Sasha carries the same understanding from a different biography. His grandparents survived the Holocaust and the Soviet Union. They came to America and were protected by a community — including immigrant attorneys who built practices to serve people exactly like them. When he thinks about an accident victim in the critical window after a crash, facing an adjuster who is betting on their complacency, he thinks about what that community meant. About what it meant to have people who knew how the system worked standing in your corner before the other side established the frame.

**What Davis wants that person to feel when they see a Second Chair ad:**

Heard. To see that someone actually looked at who they are — their market, their life, their neighborhood — and took the time to understand them before saying a word to them. Not to manipulate them. To see them.

Fired up. That fighting back is worth it. That they are not greedy for wanting what they are owed. That they are not crazy for thinking the system has not been fair to them. Because it hasn't. These are people who work multiple jobs, who can't afford a house no matter how hard they grind, who have paid into insurance systems every month just to be lowballed and dismissed when something actually happens. The justice system is one of the very few places where a regular person can get what's theirs.

*I'm not being played. These people see me. I'm going to be connected with the right person, someone who knows what I'm dealing with, someone who's going to fight. And I deserve this.*

**The consumer empowerment mandate in one sentence:**

Second Chair's B2C advertising exists to create the moment — before the insurance adjuster's offer has established their frame — when an accident victim realizes they do not have to navigate this alone, and that fighting back is not greedy, it is what they are owed.

**Why this is different from how every other lead gen vendor approaches B2C advertising:**

The category's B2C advertising is optimized for conversion through manipulation: settlement fantasies, implied government programs, urgency tactics built on manufactured fear. The creative decisions made by every PI lead gen vendor are made by people who have no specific knowledge of the human being they are trying to reach, no respect for that person's intelligence, and no alignment between what the ad promises and what the attorney can deliver.

Second Chair's founders are building advertising from a fundamentally different orientation: these are people like us, fighting inside systems that have power over them, and they deserve the truth and the conviction that they can win. That different orientation produces different creative choices at every step — in the research done before a line is written, in the specificity of what is shown, in the honesty of what is claimed, in the emotional state the ad is designed to leave the viewer in.

**The advertising model — what this looks like operationally:**

All ad spend goes to B2C — no paid B2B advertising. Attorneys are reached entirely through Sasha's cold outreach. The consumer advertising is 100% direct response performance advertising; there is no pre-accident brand awareness budget. Accident victims are episodic, one-time consumers — brand awareness built before an accident will not survive to the moment of need. The strategy reaches people when they have an active need, across three distinct timing tiers:

- **Tier 1 (Days 0–30):** Claimant has not yet signed. The adjuster has called. They are scrolling. Meta (FB/IG) is the mechanism — creative self-selection. The ad is specific and honest enough that someone in this exact situation recognizes themselves and stops. Message: "Before you sign anything." Calm, specific, no dollar promises.
- **Tier 2 (1–6 months):** Stalled soft tissue claimant with mounting bills; or serious-injury claimant still in treatment who hasn't retained anyone. Meta retargeting is now operational — they have already visited sites. Message: "It's not too late. The gap between what you were offered and what you're owed is real and it's documented."
- **Tier 3 (6+ months to SOL):** Serious-injury claimant (spinal, TBI, surgical) who believes their window has closed. It hasn't. SOL education is the barrier to break. Lowest conversion volume, highest individual case value.

Because personal injury advertising falls under Special Ad Categories on every major platform, no demographic, interest, or behavioral targeting is available. Geographic targeting and creative self-selection are the only precision tools. The specificity of the creative — the market, the language, the details of a real person's real situation — is what functions as targeting. We don't reach accident victims by finding them. We reach them by making ads honest and specific enough that accident victims find themselves in the ad.

## Section 4: The Freedom Statement

**What founder ownership — no PE, no stockholders — specifically enables.**

Two specific things Second Chair can say or do in advertising, in sales conversations, in policy decisions, that a PE-backed competitor cannot, because that competitor's investors would not allow it:

**First: publish CPSC data with full methodology.** A PE-backed lead gen company cannot publish its actual cost-per-signed-case data with the methodology used to calculate it, because the data would be auditable and the methodology would be criticizable. Their investors require the ability to control the narrative around performance. Second Chair publishes the data because its revenue model is aligned with the outcome the data measures. Transparency is not a risk here — it is the product.

**Second: walk away from revenue that violates the model.** A PE-backed company with quarterly targets cannot walk away from an attorney account because the attorney wants volume over quality, or because the intake structure isn't set up to handle leads responsibly. Sasha's non-negotiable — "I will be the one who tells them no" — is only possible because no one is pressuring Second Chair to protect quarterly returns. Founder ownership is what makes this operationally real rather than aspirationally stated.

**What this means for the brand's credibility:**

The brand's honest advertising claim is structurally enforced by the CPSC model — the revenue mechanism makes dishonesty unprofitable. The brand's freedom to be honest is structurally enforced by founder ownership — the governance structure makes dishonesty unconstrained by external pressure. Second Chair is not being honest because it decided to be ethical. It is being honest because every structural element of how it operates — revenue model, ownership structure, founder conviction — makes honesty the only viable path.

This is not a differentiation strategy. It is an architectural fact.

## Section 5: The First-Generation Thread

**The founders' proximity to the immigrant experience.**

Davis is navigating the US immigration system for his girlfriend in real time. It is personal, live, and specific — not a sociological observation. He knows exactly what it feels like to be inside a bureaucratic system where power sits entirely on one side, where the outcome depends on whether you know how to navigate the rules, and where merit and fairness are not the same thing as outcome. That experience directly informs how he thinks about the accident victim in the critical window after a crash: inside a claims system that was designed by the insurance company to serve the insurance company, not knowing whether they have the right to fight back, not knowing whether fighting is worth it.

Sasha's grandparents came from Ukraine. As Jews, they survived the Holocaust and the Soviet Union — the lived experience of being on the wrong side of a system with absolute power. His wife came from Venezuela and navigated the US immigration system directly. His father immigrated from Kyiv. When Sasha describes seeing immigrant attorneys build practices to protect future immigrants and their families, he is not describing a demographic observation. He is describing the community that protected his own family.

**The PI attorney demographic observation:**

Many PI attorney firms are founded by first and second generation immigrants. It is visible in the firm names on billboards — the Ukrainian names, the Eastern European names, the names that tell you these are people who had to build it themselves, who came here for opportunity and then created it rather than inheriting it. They looked at a stigmatized, brutal profession and said *I can build an empire here* when the safe path would have been corporate law. They bet on themselves when the conventional scoreboard would have called it a mistake. That is the fighter identity. That is the David vs. Goliath orientation that drew people to PI law specifically. It is the same DNA Sasha's family has.

**The B2C audience connection:**

The accident victims Second Chair will reach are often from the same demographic — working class to middle class, first and second generation, navigating a claims system that was designed by the insurance company to serve the insurance company. The founders' proximity to this experience is not coincidental. Davis understands what it is like to watch someone exceptional be denied by a system that doesn't care about their merit. Sasha understands what it is like to have nothing except the people who choose to stand in your corner. Both of them understand what it is like to be told, implicitly or explicitly, that the system was not built for you.

**How this manifests — not explicitly, but everywhere:**

In the advertising register: the voice that speaks to claimants as intelligent people who deserve the truth, not as victims to be mined for conversion. The visual specificity — the landmarks, the neighborhoods, the details of real lives — that communicates genuine research into who the viewer actually is.

In how sales conversations are run: Sasha's ability to meet a first-generation attorney firm owner where they actually are — recognizing the chip on the shoulder, the built-it-themselves origin story, the skepticism of anyone who sounds like they're pitching rather than understanding — because he is a version of that person.

In product decisions: the firm portal's real-time lead age counter, designed to respect the attorney's most scarce resource — time. The scoring model's transparency, designed to give the attorney the same information Second Chair has so they are not trusting a claim. The full audit trail, designed to give the attorney visibility into every decision made about every lead, because they deserve to see the work.

In what Second Chair refuses: every exploitation tactic is something the founders recognize as the same mechanism that has been used against people they know or are. It is not a policy decision. It is a biographical one.

## Section 6: How the Founder DNA Manifests in the Brand

**Specific. Not philosophical.**

**In advertising:**

Second Chair ads are built on research into who the viewer actually is — not demographic research, but human research. What does a person in this market, in this neighborhood, in this specific moment of vulnerability need to hear to feel like fighting back is worth it? The specific visual references (landmarks, interiors, faces that belong to this community) communicate that the work was done before a word was written. The honesty of what is claimed — no implied settlement values, no actors pretending to be clients, no manufactured urgency — comes directly from the founders' conviction that the claimant deserves the truth. The emotional target (fired up, clear-eyed, ready to move) comes directly from what Davis wants his own advertising to do: not manipulate someone into filling out a form, but genuinely empower them to exercise a right they probably didn't know they had.

**In sales conversations:**

Sasha will acknowledge the risk out loud before the attorney does. He knows the pattern — burned before, defensive, in risk management mode — because he understands exactly how trust gets destroyed in a category where every vendor has overpromised and underdelivered. He will say: "Most firms get burned by bad exclusivity or recycled leads. I want to show you how ours works differently before you decide anything." He hands leverage to the attorney rather than manufacturing urgency. He will tell a firm Second Chair is not the right fit rather than take the money and underdeliver. These are not sales tactics. They are expressions of a conviction he carries personally: you cannot sell something you don't believe in and do it honestly. He believes in this. It comes through in a way that cannot be manufactured.

**In client relationships:**

When Second Chair is underperforming, the attorney knows before they ask. The performance data in the firm portal — CPSC, lead grade distribution, volume vs. signed case ratio — is visible in real time because the founders built a product that reflects what they believe a vendor-client relationship should look like: no information asymmetry, no hiding behind a dashboard, no managed narrative around numbers that aren't moving. This is a direct expression of the founding conviction: the person doing the work (the attorney fighting cases) deserves to see what the person controlling the economics (Second Chair) actually has.

**In product decisions:**

Alex built the scoring model's weights into the database rather than hardcoding them — so they can be adjusted in 30 seconds via admin UI without a code deployment. This is not a technical convenience. It is an architectural expression of alignment: when an attorney's feedback reveals that a scoring weight is producing the wrong outcome, the model changes fast. The product is built to respond to the people it serves, not to protect its own methodology. The TrustedForm integration was not built because it is legally required — it was built because the founders believe the attorney deserves court-admissible consent documentation for every lead they receive. That is a product decision that costs money to implement and produces no additional revenue. It is there because it is the right thing to build.

**In what Second Chair refuses:**

No shared leads — because treating a claimant like a commodity is the same thing the insurance adjuster does when they make the first call. No implied settlement values — because telling someone what they want to hear rather than what is true is exactly what the system they are fighting against does to them. No actors presenting as real clients — because manufactured authenticity is a form of the same manipulation the category runs on. No hiding pricing — because information asymmetry is how power stays with the vendor and stays away from the attorney. No taking clients Second Chair cannot genuinely serve — because the worst thing you can do to someone who has been burned before is burn them again.

Every refusal on this list is something the founders have personally experienced being on the wrong side of.

## Section 7: The Unified Founder Story

**One paragraph. The honest version.**

Three people building Second Chair have each, in different ways, been inside a system that separates the people creating value from the people controlling the economics — and have felt what it is like to have that structure work against them. Davis built a brand that became a cultural phenomenon without capturing the equity that represented. Alex built a million-user financial platform at FINRA/SEC compliance standards and then watched the role end when institutional priorities shifted. Sasha built his career entirely outside the path that was handed to him, betting on himself in a world that measured him against a scoreboard he couldn't win on yet. They came together to build Second Chair because the PI lead gen category runs on the same structural logic they have each been failed by — vendors who profit on lead volume while attorneys absorb quality risk and claimants absorb advertising that was designed to exploit rather than inform — and because they are, collectively, the only team in this market with the specific combination of advertising pedigree, product engineering, sales conviction, and personal biography to dismantle it. Second Chair is not a business built on a market opportunity. It is a business built by three people who looked at a system that had failed them and the people they come from, and decided they were done participating in it from the wrong side.
