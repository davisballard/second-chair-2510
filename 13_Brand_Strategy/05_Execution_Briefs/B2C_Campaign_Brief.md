## Channel and Platform

**Primary channel:** Meta (Facebook and Instagram) — 95–100% of B2C ad spend.

**Platform constraint — Special Ad Categories:**
Personal injury legal advertising runs under Meta's Special Ad Category. This means:
- **No demographic targeting** — no age, gender, household income targeting
- **No interest targeting** — no behavioral or psychographic stacks
- **No lookalike audiences** built from any seed
- **Geographic targeting is available** — Market/city level
- **Creative is the only targeting tool**

This is not a limitation. It is the mechanism that makes Second Chair's creative capability a competitive advantage. The ad that stops the right person mid-scroll while being invisible to everyone else is not built through a targeting algorithm — it is built through craft. That is what Second Chair offers that competitors cannot replicate.

**Campaign structure:** 1 ad set per Market at launch, 3-5 creatives inside (different angles, hooks, formats). Meta rotates and tests them simultaneously, shifting delivery to the winners. All conversion events feed one learning model. Second ad set (Spanish, retargeting) launches after first exits learning (~3-6 weeks at SC's volume of 1-3 leads/day). 100-lead minimum per Market before expanding. See `09_Operations/HOW_META_CAMPAIGNS_OPTIMIZE.md`.

**What IS available under SAC:** Retargeting — website visitors, video viewers (50%+), lead form openers, customer list uploads. These become valuable after the first 100 leads build enough site traffic.

**The self-selection mechanism:**
The ad must be specific enough to resonate immediately with a person who had an accident and be completely uninteresting to someone who didn't. This is precision through creative, not precision through data. The more specific and true the ad is to the claimant's situation, the stronger the self-selection effect. Vague ads waste spend. Specific ads find the right person.

## The 99%/1% Mandate

**The fundamental challenge of PI advertising:**
At any given moment, approximately 1% of people in a market have had a recent accident that constitutes a viable PI case. The other 99% have not. The advertising reaches all 99% while being financially justified only by the 1%.

The wrong approach: use emotional manipulation (dramatic crash footage, scare language, manufactured urgency) that briefly captures attention from everyone and sustains attention from no one specific.

The right approach: use creative that is so specifically true to the claimant's situation that it resonates instantly with the 1% — and is so specific that the 99% don't register it as relevant to them. The goal is not to avoid annoying the 99%. The goal is to be so specific to the 1% that the 99% self-select out.

**How each tier solves this:**
- Tier 1: The specificity of the situation ("you received a call from their insurance company" — only someone who did receives this as relevant)
- Tier 2: The specificity of the consequence ("your bills are adding up" — only someone living with an unresolved claim registers this as addressed to them)
- Tier 3: The specificity of the deadline ("in [state], you have X years" — only someone who had an accident and hasn't acted registers this as urgent)

## Tier 1 — Days 0–30 (Active Decision Window)

### Who We're Reaching

The working adult who had a car accident in the last 30 days. They may have already received a call from the insurance adjuster. They may have already accepted a first offer or started negotiating. They may not have taken any action yet.

They are not "a personal injury claimant" in their own mind. They are a person whose life got disrupted. They may not know what they have. They may not know they can do anything about it. They may not know there is a deadline.

Their dominant emotion is not anger — it is confusion + low-level anxiety + a desire to resolve and move on. They are not looking for a lawyer. They are looking for information that helps them make the right decision.

### What They Believe When We Reach Them

*"I don't know if I have a case. I don't want to deal with legal stuff. I just want this to be over. The insurance company already called me. They seem like they have this handled."*

### What the Ad Must Accomplish

Create a moment of genuine recognition — *"that's exactly my situation"* — followed by a single, low-barrier action that feels like getting information, not signing up for anything. The ad does not sell legal services. It provides the one fact that makes a brief, free phone call the obvious next step.

### What It Says (One Thing)

*"The insurance company's job is to settle your claim for as little as possible. Before you respond to anything — one free call to hear what you actually have."*

Or, stripped further:

*"Before you respond to the insurance company — hear what you actually have. Free. Ten minutes."*

**What "what you actually have" does:** It acknowledges the claimant may not know if they have a case — and makes finding out the action, not filing a lawsuit. Lower barrier than "call a lawyer." Higher information value than "click here."

### Visual Direction (Tier 1)

**The moment:** The decision moment before an action. Not the accident. Not the aftermath. The specific moment — the phone in hand, the pause before answering, the kitchen counter where the adjuster's card is sitting.

**The register:** Cinematic. Intentionally produced. The claimant's world made specific and real — not staged, but directed. The light is right because someone cared about the light. The face is specific because the casting was specific. The environment is recognizable because the detail was chosen, not filled in generically.

**What it does NOT show:**
- The crash or accident
- A lawyer in a suit
- Scales of justice or legal imagery
- Dollar amounts
- Crying or dramatic pain performance
- Actors who read as "typical PI advertising"

**The emotion being evoked:** The weight of a real decision. Not fear. Not anger. The specific feeling of standing at a fork in the road and not being sure which path is right — and then someone gives you the information that clarifies it.

**Production approach:**
- AI-assisted cinematography — intentionally cinematic, not trying to look found
- Controlled environments (kitchen, parked car, waiting room, evening light through a window)
- Movement is deliberate — not handheld verité, not locked-off static. The camera moves like it was placed there by someone with a point of view.
- Sound design: minimal, diegetic, real
- Color grade: warm but not cheerful. The emotional register of a significant moment in an ordinary day.

### What It Does Not Do

- Does not say "you could be owed THOUSANDS"
- Does not say "don't let them get away with it"
- Does not use "may have a case!" language
- Does not create artificial urgency that doesn't exist
- Does not talk down to the claimant
- Does not promise an outcome the attorney can't guarantee

## Tier 2 — 1–6 Months (Accumulation Phase)

### Who We're Reaching

The claimant who is 1–6 months past their accident. They either accepted a first offer and are living with it, or they're still undecided or in informal negotiation. Medical bills are arriving. The physical consequences are more real than they were in the immediate aftermath. The financial reality is setting in.

Their situation has changed since the accident — it's heavier now. They're not processing shock. They're managing an ongoing problem.

### What They Believe When We Reach Them

*"It's too late to do anything. I already accepted the offer / I waited too long / this is just how it goes. The bills are coming in but there's nothing I can do about it now."*

(This belief is often wrong — many claimants still have viable cases and/or remaining time. The advertising provides the information that correct this belief.)

### What the Ad Must Accomplish

Break the "it's too late" belief with a specific, true piece of information — and make a brief, free phone call feel like finding out, not committing.

### What It Says

*"Most people who accepted a first offer didn't know they had other options. Some still do. One call to find out where you stand."*

Or for the undecided claimant still in the window:
*"The bills keep arriving. The offer might not be enough to cover them. One call — find out if you have options."*

### Visual Direction (Tier 2)

**The moment:** The consequence, not the event. The kitchen table with bills on it. The person on the phone with the insurance company, expression unreadable. The late evening calculation — the specific look of someone doing math in their head that doesn't add up.

**Shift from Tier 1:** Slightly heavier register. The decision was made — or is being made under financial pressure. The emotional weight is accumulation, not immediacy. The visual language should carry that shift without becoming melodramatic.

**Production note:** Tier 2 creative can run in the same visual language and production quality as Tier 1 — same color grade, same production care — but the specific imagery shifts from the pre-decision moment to the living-with-it moment.

### What It Does Not Do

- Does not tell them they definitely have a case they don't
- Does not manufacture urgency where none exists
- Does not position the insurance company as evil — positions the claimant's information deficit as the problem

## Tier 3 — 6+ Months to Statute of Limitations (Deadline Approaching)

### Who We're Reaching

The claimant who has not acted. They may not know there is a statute of limitations. They may have assumed it was too late long ago. They may have had a real case that is still viable — and is approaching the window where it permanently closes.

This is the informational tier. The claimant needs a fact, not an emotional appeal.

### What They Believe When We Reach Them

*"It's definitely too late. I didn't do anything. That ship has sailed."*

(Often incorrect — depending on state SOL, they may still have significant time.)

### What the Ad Must Accomplish

Deliver the state-specific deadline accurately and make a one-call action feel like the obvious response to finding out they might still have time.

### What It Says

*"In [state], you have [X] years from the date of your accident to file a claim. After that, you can't. If you're not sure whether you still have time — one call to find out."*

The specific deadline is the ad. It does not need support. It does not need emotional framing. The fact is the creative.

### Visual Direction (Tier 3)

**The moment:** The calendar. The clock. Not in a scary, ticking way — in the way that a specific date on a page means something. The visual language of time-awareness without manufactured dread.

**Production note:** Tier 3 can be the most visually stripped of the three tiers. The fact is doing the work. The visual serves the fact rather than preceding it. A simple, clear, specific piece of information delivered with the visual weight it deserves.

**State-specific production requirement:** Each Tier 3 ad must include the correct SOL for the specific state it runs in. This is a production workflow requirement, not a creative one — templates with state-specific copy variables, produced at scale across target markets.

### What It Does Not Do

- Does not manufacture urgency that isn't there ("ACT NOW BEFORE TIME RUNS OUT!")
- Does not promise the claimant has a case — offers them the ability to find out if they still have time
- Does not use generic legal advertising language ("don't wait another day!")

## What All Three Tiers Share

**The consistent visual language:**
- Cinematic production quality across all tiers — the same level of care, the same color temperature, the same attention to environmental detail
- The claimant's world, made specific — their environments, their objects, their light
- No lawyer imagery in B2C (the attorney is the product; the ad doesn't show the product)
- No crash imagery — the accident is past; the decision is present
- Motion is intentional — camera placement and movement signal that someone made a choice about where to look

**The consistent tonal language:**
- One specific fact
- One minimum-barrier action
- No manufacturing of fear or urgency beyond what the situation genuinely contains
- The claimant addressed as an intelligent adult making a real decision

**The creative self-selection test (applied to every ad before production):**
*"Would a person who did not have an accident in the relevant window scroll past this without registering it as relevant to them?"*
If yes: the self-selection mechanism is working. If no: the ad is too generic and needs to be more specific.

## Production Workflow

**Volume requirement:** Second Chair's competitive advantage includes hyper-localized creative production at scale. The same campaign framework runs across multiple Markets with market-specific elements (city/state, local details, SOL by state for Tier 3).

**AI-assisted production:** The cinematic quality Second Chair requires is achievable at scale through AI-assisted creative production — but the quality threshold must be maintained. The test for every piece of produced creative: does it look like someone who cared made it? If the details are wrong, the production is not complete.

**The quality floor:** Second Chair's advertising is the primary proof of the brand's claim ("honestly won advertising"). If the creative falls below the quality threshold, the proof fails before the claimant even calls. Every piece of creative must be reviewed against the brief before it goes live.

**The feedback loop:** CPSC tracking per creative concept, per market, per tier. What worked for whom, in which markets, at which stage of the claim cycle. This data feeds back into the creative iteration cycle. The council (Davis's AI-assisted strategic and creative advisory system) evaluates performance data and generates hypotheses for the next creative iteration.

## What Second Chair Never Does in B2C Advertising

1. Promises a dollar amount it cannot guarantee for that specific situation
2. Uses crash footage or dramatized accident imagery
3. Claims the claimant "definitely has a case"
4. Creates fake urgency where no genuine deadline exists
5. Uses fear of the insurance company as the primary motivating emotion (information is the motivator, not fear)
6. Runs any copy that a reasonable person would call manipulative after reading it
7. Targets by demographic — relies exclusively on creative self-selection for precision
