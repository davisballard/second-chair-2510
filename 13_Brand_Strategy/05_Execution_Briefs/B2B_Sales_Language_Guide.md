## What This Document Is

This is not a script. It is a guide to the language — the actual words — that produce the outcomes Second Chair needs in a sales conversation. Every line in this document should be usable by Sasha in a real conversation with a real attorney without sounding like a script.

The governing principle from the Attorney Buying Voice research: *"Second Chair must feel trustworthy before it feels impressive."*

The attorney who receives Sasha's outreach has heard every lead gen vendor pitch. They're not looking for impressive. They're looking for a reason not to delete this. The language that earns attention is specific, honest, and demonstrates that Second Chair understands their world before asking for anything.

---

## The Opening Line

**The one sentence that makes an attorney stop and keep reading.**

The opening line must do one thing: demonstrate that Second Chair knows something specific about the attorney's situation that a generic vendor wouldn't know. Not their name. Not their firm name. Something about their practice reality that signals: this person has done their homework and understands my world.

**Structure:** [Specific observation about their practice] + [Something true about what that produces] + [The minimum ask]

**Examples (by situation):**

*For a solo or small firm attorney who does MVA work:*
*"Most PI firms running lead gen are buying CPL — but the metric that actually matters to a practice like yours is cost-per-signed-case. Most vendors don't report it. We make it the primary metric. Worth a 30-minute look at what we're running in [city]?"*

*For an attorney Sasha has researched who has commented publicly about lead gen quality:*
*"You've mentioned that lead quality is the core problem with how PI firms currently source cases. We built Second Chair specifically around that complaint — and we show every attorney the advertising we run before they commit to anything. Thirty minutes. I'll show you what we're running in your market."*

*For a firm coming off a bad vendor experience (switching trigger moment):*
*"I saw that [competitor] has been getting some attention lately for [issue]. We built Second Chair specifically to fix the things that make PI attorneys distrust the category. Before I explain what we do — can I show you the advertising we run to generate our leads?"*

**The test for any opening line:**
*Does it make the attorney feel like Sasha knows their world — not like Sasha is pitching them?*

## Cold Email

### Subject Line Logic

The subject line must do one of three things:
1. Name something specific to their practice or market
2. Raise the metric question that reframes the conversation ("What's your cost-per-signed-case from your current vendor?")
3. Make an offer they haven't been made before ("See the advertising we'd run in your market — no commitment")

**Subject line candidates:**

- *What's your cost-per-signed-case from [competitor/current vendor]?*
- *The advertising we run to generate PI leads in [city] — want to see it?*
- *Before you commit to any lead gen vendor — ask to see the advertising.*
- *[City] PI market — what we're running and what it's producing*
- *Your intake calls start before you answer. Here's why.*

**The test:** Would an attorney who gets 20 vendor emails a day read this? If the subject line sounds like a vendor email, rewrite it until it sounds like a peer who knows something.

---

### Email Structure

**Opening paragraph — demonstrate you know their world:**

Sasha's opening paragraph references something specific — the attorney's practice area, their market, a public statement they've made, a pattern Sasha has observed. It is not generic. It takes 2 minutes of research to write and it shows.

*Example:*
*"I've been looking at PI lead generation in the Atlanta market and I noticed that most of what's being run — across all the major vendors — is either aggregated search intent or social advertising that doesn't hold up to scrutiny. Attorneys who look closely at what's generating their leads usually find they'd rather not know. We built Second Chair because that's the wrong way to run a practice."*

---

**The proof move — show before you claim:**

The second paragraph does not pitch the product. It makes the one offer that no other lead gen vendor has made.

*"Before I explain what Second Chair does or how it works — I'd like to show you the advertising we run to generate our leads in your market. No commitment, no demo, just: look at this, tell me what you think. If it's not a better standard than what you've seen before, we're probably not the right fit."*

---

**The evidence — one specific fact:**

One piece of evidence that the claim is real. Not a list. One thing.

*"Claimants who were honestly persuaded — who responded to advertising that gave them accurate information about their situation — arrive at the intake call differently. They don't require damage control. They convert at a higher rate. The ethical choice and the commercial choice are the same thing. That's what we're built around."*

---

**The offer — minimum viable ask:**

*"Thirty minutes. I'll show you the advertising we'd run for your market, walk you through the compliance record on a sample lead, and show you the cost-per-signed-case reporting format. If none of that is better than what you're seeing now, we're not the right fit and I'll tell you so."*

---

**The CTA — specific, not generic:**

*"If that's worth 30 minutes, reply with a time that works and I'll send a calendar link. If you'd rather just see the advertising first — reply and I'll send you what we're running in [city]. No call needed."*

**The test for the full email:** Read it as the attorney receiving it. Does it sound like a vendor email or like a peer who knows something? If it's vendor email, cut the claims and add specificity.

## The First Call

### The Opener

Sasha does not open by pitching. Sasha opens by demonstrating he knows the attorney's world.

*"Before I explain what we do — can I ask you one question? What's your current cost-per-signed-case from your existing lead gen vendors?"*

Most attorneys won't know this number. Sasha's response when they don't:

*"That's the most common answer I get — because most vendors don't report it. They report CPL, which tells you what you paid to talk to someone. CPSC tells you what you paid to sign them. Those numbers are usually pretty different. I'd like to show you ours — but first, I want to show you the advertising we run to generate our leads. Is that okay?"*

**Why this sequence works:**
1. The CPSC question reframes how the attorney evaluates the conversation — they're now thinking in the metric that actually matters
2. The offer to show the advertising shifts from pitch mode to evaluation mode — the attorney is evaluating, not being sold to
3. Sasha hasn't claimed anything yet. The attorney is already in a different cognitive frame.

---

### Showing the Advertising (The Primary Proof Move)

*"Here's the advertising we'd run in your market — [share screen or send the creative]. Take a look. Tell me what you think."*

Pause. Let them look. Let them respond. Do not explain the advertising while they're looking at it.

If they respond positively:
*"That's the standard we hold ourselves to. Every attorney who works with us has evaluated this before they committed to anything. What you see is what your potential clients saw before they called."*

If they respond neutrally or skeptically:
*"Good — skepticism is the right response. The claim is that this advertising honestly persuades people, which means the person who calls you arrives differently than someone who was manipulated into a funnel. I'd like to show you what that difference looks like in the intake call."*

---

### Moving Through the Product

After the advertising has been evaluated, Sasha explains the system in three minutes or less:

*"The advertising gets people there honestly. The product qualifies what arrives — TrustedForm compliance record on every lead, DNC screening, the dual scoring that tells you which leads to prioritize before you pick up the phone. And we report on cost-per-signed-case, not CPL, so you can see whether the relationship is working."*

Then, introducing premium tiers (optional, based on client sophistication):

*"We also offer premium qualification tiers — totally optional. Standard tier is what I just described: we deliver the web lead, you call them. But we can also qualify leads for you. AI-Qualified tier: an AI voice agent calls within 90 seconds, completes our 7-question intake script, delivers full transcript and structured notes. That's an extra $200 per lead. Or Human Live Transfer: Sasha or I personally call, qualify, and if they have a case, we transfer them to you live — while they're on the phone and warm. That's $1,200 more. Live transfers convert at 40-60% vs. 10-15% for web leads. It's for firms that want fewer leads at higher conversion, or firms with 24/7 intake capability like Jacoby & Meyers."*

Then: *"I want to show you what the reporting actually looks like — because that's usually the thing that changes how attorneys think about evaluating any vendor."*

---

### Handling the "We Already Have Vendors" Conversation

This is not an objection. It is an invitation to reframe.

*"Good — that means you have a baseline to compare against. What's your current CPSC from those vendors?"*

If they don't know: Back to the metric reframe.
If they do know: *"That's the number we'd need to beat to be worth your time. Here's how we'd approach that in your market."*

## Objection Responses

*Mapped from the Attorney Buying Voice research. These are the responses to what the attorney is actually afraid of — not what they said.*

---

**"I've been burned before."**

*What they're afraid of:* Losing money again.

*Sasha's response:*
*"Tell me what happened."*
[Listen. Specifically.]
*"Here's what we do differently about that specific thing: [address the exact issue they named]. And before you commit to anything — month-to-month, no contracts, bad leads get replaced. The structure is designed for people who've been burned before."*

---

**"$1,500 per lead is too expensive."** (Premium tier objection)

*What they're afraid of:* The premium price doesn't justify the ROI.

*Sasha's response:*
*"Let me show you the math. Standard leads at $315 convert at around 12% — that's $2,625 per signed case. Live transfers at $1,515 convert at 40-60%, let's say 50%. That's $3,030 per signed case. You're paying 15% more per case but you need 75% fewer leads to hit your case volume target. Your intake team is spending 6+ hours less per week chasing dead ends. And you're the only firm in your DMA getting qualified live transfers. The question isn't whether it's expensive — it's whether $400 more per signed case is worth having your phone ring with someone who's already qualified and ready to sign."*

---

**"Why not just use Settlement Allies?"** (CPA alternative)

*What they're asking:* CPA vendors only charge per signed case — why pay per lead at all?

*Sasha's response:*
*"Settlement Allies charges $2,500-$6,500 per signed case. Our premium tier costs $3,030 per signed case. The difference is: you pay SA 30-60 days after the case is signed. You pay us weekly. Your cashflow is 4-6 weeks faster. Second, SA doesn't give you DMA exclusivity — they'll sell the same market to 3-5 firms. You get one firm per DMA with us. Third, with SA you have no control over which cases you sign — they send them, you take them or lose the relationship. With us, you're getting the lead at intake stage and you decide. You pay 20% more than SA's low end but you get faster payment, exclusivity, and control. For firms that value those three things, we're worth it."*

---

**"How is this different from [competitor]?"**

*What they're asking:* Show me the difference. Don't tell me.

*Sasha's response:*
*"Can I show you the advertising we run, and you can evaluate the difference yourself? I'd rather you see it than hear me claim it."*

---

**"How many leads can you get me in my market?"**

*What they're afraid of:* Volume promises that don't materialize.

*Sasha's response:*
*"I'd rather start with a volume that lets you verify the quality before we talk about scaling. The goal for the first 90 days is to get you to CPSC clarity — to know whether the math works for your practice. Once it does, we scale. What volume would give you enough signal in 90 days?"*

---

**"Your leads are probably garbage like everyone else's."**

*What they're asking:* Do you generate leads honestly?

*Sasha's response:*
*"Fair. Here's what I'd suggest — look at the advertising we run, look at a sample lead with the TrustedForm record, and tell me if that's the same as what you've seen before. I'm not going to argue against your experience. I'm going to show you something and let you evaluate it."*

---

**"I need to know it works before I commit."**

*What they're asking:* Give me proof without risk.

*Sasha's response:*
*"Here's the closest thing to that: a named attorney in a comparable market you can call, the CPSC data from their last 90 days, and a pilot structure that starts at a volume designed for you to verify the quality before you decide whether to scale. Does that work?"*

---

**"It's too expensive."**

*What they're asking:* Does the ROI math work?

*Sasha's response:*
*"What's your current CPSC from your existing vendors? Let's do the math. At [price per lead], with [their practice's expected conversion rate], the CPSC is [X]. Compare that to what you're paying now per signed case."*

---

**"I need to talk to my partner first."**

*What they're saying:* I'm not ready to decide.

*Sasha's response:*
*"Absolutely. Here's what I'd suggest sending them — it has the advertising, the compliance record, and the CPSC reporting format. That's usually what a partner needs to see before a conversation makes sense. What's the right follow-up time once you've had that conversation?"*

## The Close

**The offer Sasha makes when the attorney is interested:**

*"Here's what I'd suggest: a 50-lead pilot. Month-to-month. We show you the advertising before you commit — you've already seen it. We run for 90 days. At day 45 we review your CPSC together. At day 90 you make a call based on real data."*

**The test for closing:**
The close is not a pitch. It is a logical next step for an attorney who has already seen the advertising, evaluated the compliance infrastructure, and understands the CPSC metric. The close should feel like what they were already thinking.

**If they're not ready:**
*"That's fine. What would need to be true for this to make sense in the next 30-60 days? I'd rather come back when the timing is right than push now."*

## The CPSC Frame

The single most powerful reframe in the sales conversation. Deploy it early, before any other claim is made.

**The question:**
*"What's your current cost-per-signed-case from your existing vendors?"*

**If they don't know it:**
*"Most attorneys don't — because most vendors don't report it. They report CPL, which tells you what you paid to have a conversation. CPSC tells you what you paid to sign a case. Those numbers are usually very different. Let me show you ours."*

**If they do know it:**
*"Good. That's the number we'd need to improve on to be worth your time. Here's what we're producing in comparable markets."*

**The power of this frame:**
Every other vendor in the category reports CPL. The moment Second Chair introduces CPSC as the evaluation metric, every other vendor's reporting becomes incomparable — the attorney can't compare apples to apples with their current vendors anymore. This is not a trick. It is the correct metric. The attorney benefits from knowing it regardless of whether they work with Second Chair.

## What Sasha Never Says

- "We're the best / the leading / the premier" — anything superlative without immediate proof
- "You could be losing cases to [competitor]" — fear-based competitive language
- "Act now — we only have [X] markets available" — artificial scarcity
- "I think you'd love what we do" — premature enthusiasm before the attorney has evaluated anything
- "Our proprietary technology / innovative platform / cutting-edge solution" — vendor language
- "Most of our clients see great results" — vague without an immediately available specific
- "Let me know if you have any questions" — email close that signals low commitment and invites no response

**The one thing Sasha always says** before anything else in a first conversation: *"Before I explain what we do — can I show you the advertising we run to generate our leads?"*
