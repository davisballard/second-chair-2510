## Purpose

**What this page must accomplish:**
Turn a skeptical attorney — one who arrived because a cold email said something specific — into someone who books a call to see the advertising Second Chair runs for their market.

That is the one job. Not explain the product. Not build the brand. Not demonstrate thought leadership. Get the attorney who arrived skeptical to book thirty minutes to see the advertising before they commit to anything.

---

## Audience State on Arrival

The attorney who lands on secondchair.com arrived from one of two places:
- **Sasha's cold email or LinkedIn outreach** — something was specific enough to warrant a click. They are investigating, not buying. They are actively looking for a reason to close the tab.
- **A peer recommendation or referral** — someone they trust said "look at this." Slightly warmer. Still skeptical. Still has been burned before.

Either way, they arrive with the same mental model: *"Another lead gen vendor. They'll claim to be different. Let me see if they are."*

They have 90 seconds before they decide whether to engage or close the tab. The page does not have time to build a relationship. It has time to do one thing that makes them stay: show them something they have never seen from a lead gen vendor.

## The Persuasion Sequence

*Section by section. What each section does — not what it says.*

---

### Section 1 — The Pattern Interrupt (Above the fold)

**What it does:** Creates immediate cognitive dissonance. The attorney lands and sees something from a lead gen vendor that no lead gen vendor has ever shown them.

**How:** Show the advertising. Not a description of the advertising. Not a claim about the advertising's quality. The actual ad — a frame, a still, a clip — visible above the fold with minimal context.

**The headline (supporting the ad, not explaining it):**
*"This is the advertising we run to generate your leads. Before you learn anything else about us — see what we show the people who call you."*

**Why this works:** The attorney was prepared to evaluate claims. We've shown them something to evaluate instead. The pattern is broken. They are now in evaluation mode, not skepticism mode. The advertising has to carry the weight here — which is why the quality of the B2C creative is the website's most important asset.

**What this section is not:** A hero banner. A tagline. A benefit claim. A rotating carousel. A stock photo of a handshake or a gavel.

---

### Section 2 — The Proof Chain (Below the fold, immediately)

**What it does:** Explains what they just saw and what it produces. Makes the commercial argument that the advertising shown is not just ethical — it performs better.

**The specific sequence of arguments:**

1. *"That advertising doesn't manipulate. It honestly persuades."*
   What that means: specific information, real emotional resonance, no manufactured urgency. The claimant who sees it responds because something true moved them.

2. *"Which means the person who calls you arrives differently."*
   The contrast: the claimant from manipulated advertising arrives with inflated expectations and requires damage control. The claimant who was honestly persuaded arrives with accurate expectations and the readiness to engage. The intake call starts from a different place.

3. *"Honestly persuaded claimants convert at a higher rate. The ethical choice and the commercial choice are the same thing."*
   This is the claim that makes everything else make sense. The page doesn't ask the attorney to care about ethics. It shows them that the ethical approach produces better commercial outcomes.

**Tone:** Direct, specific, no flourish. The language of a peer making a business case, not a vendor making a pitch.

---

### Section 3 — The Product (Plain English)

**What it does:** Converts the attorney who is now curious about the advertising into someone who understands the full system behind it. The product section must be specific enough to be evaluable — not a list of features, but a description of what the attorney actually receives and how it works.

**Four things, plain English:**

1. **The advertising gets them there honestly.** We build creative in-house — the strategy, the visual direction, the copy — and run it on Meta (FB/IG). We show you the advertising before you commit to anything. You can evaluate it yourself.

2. **The system qualifies and scores what arrives.** Every lead arrives with a compliance record (TrustedForm — court-admissible consent and origin documentation). Every lead is run through DNC/litigator screening automatically — if it fails, it doesn't get delivered. The two scores (Conversion Score + Case Value Score) tell you how to prioritize your intake queue before you pick up the phone.

3. **We don't just deliver leads. We qualify them (optional upgrade).** Standard tier: we deliver the web lead, you call them. AI-Qualified tier (+$200): we call within 90 seconds using AI voice, complete intake script, deliver transcript and structured notes. Human Live Transfer (+$1,200): Sasha or Davis calls within 90 seconds, qualifies, and transfers live to your intake team. Live transfers convert at 40-60% vs. 10-15% for web leads. One vendor for lead gen + intake + transfer.

4. **You see the metric that matters.** We report on your cost-per-signed-case — not CPL. CPL tells you what you paid to talk to someone. CPSC tells you what you paid to sign them. We make that number visible so you can evaluate whether the relationship is working. Standard leads: $315 at 12% = $2,625 CPSC. Live transfers: $1,515 at 50% = $3,030 CPSC. You pay 15% more per case but need 75% fewer leads.

**What this section is not:** A feature list. A spec sheet. A comparison table. Technical language the attorney doesn't need.

---

### Section 4 — The Team (Specific Credentials, Not Bios)

**What it does:** Answers the implicit question every attorney is asking by this point: *"Who built this and why should I believe they can do what they're claiming?"*

**The structure:** Not bios. Specific, verifiable credentials tied directly to what Second Chair does.

**Davis:** The advertising you just saw was built by someone who has spent 18 years building advertising for global brands. The craft that makes the B2C creative work — specific, emotionally precise, honest without being flat — is not generated by a template. It is built by someone who has done this across categories far harder to advertise in than personal injury.

**Alex:** The compliance infrastructure behind every lead — TrustedForm full stack, fail-closed screening, the dual scoring engine — was built by someone who built financial products at FINRA/SEC compliance standards. The same engineering discipline that makes a financial platform audit-ready makes Second Chair's compliance architecture non-negotiable rather than aspirational.

**Sasha:** The advertising reaches people who had accidents on Meta — the most restrictive ad environment in digital marketing — because Sasha has run restricted-category advertising at Fortune 500 scale for 14 years, including at The Flower Agency where standard platforms were entirely unavailable. He knows how to build reach and performance without demographic targeting, because he's done it before in a different restricted category. He also ran programmatic buying for 18+ global brands at 33across, and underwrote company financials at Riverpoint Capital — which is why his sales conversations with managing partners sound like capital allocation conversations, not vendor pitches.

**The "only us" line (can be a sidebar or visual callout):**
*"Three companies would struggle to assemble this combination. We built it into one from the start."*

---

### Section 5 — The Model (Three Points)

**What it does:** Addresses the "what's the catch" moment. Every attorney who has gotten this far is looking for the trap. This section removes it.

1. **Month-to-month. No contracts.** If the CPSC math doesn't work after 90 days, you leave. No penalty.

2. **No shared leads.** The lead generated for your firm is delivered to your firm. It is not re-routed to another attorney in your market.

3. **You see the advertising before you commit.** We start every relationship by showing you what we're running. Not after you sign. Before.

---

### Section 6 — The Offer (Exit Action)

**What it does:** Converts the engaged attorney into a scheduled conversation.

**The offer:**
*"See the advertising we'd run in your market. Thirty minutes. No commitment required."*

Not "request a demo." Not "talk to sales." Not "get a quote."

*See the advertising we'd run in your market.*

This is the correct offer because:
- It is specific (your market, not generic)
- It requires no commitment
- It delivers something the attorney actually wants to see (the creative)
- It puts Second Chair in the position of demonstrating rather than pitching

**The CTA button:**
*"See the advertising for [city] — Book 30 minutes"*

If possible, the city auto-populates from the attorney's location. If not, the form asks for their market as the first field.

**Secondary action (for attorneys not ready to book):**
*"Get the Second Chair market report for [state]"* — a one-page document showing lead volume data for their Market, the scoring breakdown, and a sample of the creative direction. Email capture. No call required.

## Tone

The website sounds like the Brand Architecture's B2B voice: earned peer, not vendor. Specific, not aspirational. Demonstrates, does not claim.

One test for every line of website copy: *would a PI attorney believe a vendor said this, or would they believe a peer said it?* If it reads like a vendor claim, rewrite it until it reads like a peer who knows something.

The website never:
- Claims to be "the best" or "the leading"
- Uses the word "solution" or "ecosystem"
- Promises volume without immediately qualifying it with the CPSC frame
- Tells the attorney what they should feel
- Ends a section with a question

---

## What It Is Not

- Not a features page. Features belong in the pitch document. The website converts, it doesn't inform.
- Not a brand manifesto. The purpose sub-line ("Second Chair exists so the attorney who needs clients and the client who needs an attorney can finally find each other honestly") lives in the brand architecture. It may appear on the website as a secondary element, but it is not the lead.
- Not a case study carousel. Case studies require case data that doesn't exist pre-launch. The offer to show the advertising is the pre-launch proof mechanism.
- Not a blog or content hub. Content comes later, if at all. The website's job at launch is conversion, not authority-building.

## Hegarty's Test

*"Would a PI attorney forward this URL to a partner at their firm to justify looking at Second Chair?"*

**Answer:** Yes — if Section 1 (the advertising shown above the fold) does its job. The attorney who sees the creative and finds it genuinely different from everything the category has produced will forward the URL because it is the fastest way to make the case to a partner without a phone call. The URL becomes the pitch. That's the bar the site must clear.
