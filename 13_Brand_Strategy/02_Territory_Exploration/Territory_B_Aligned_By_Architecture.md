# Territory B — Aligned by Architecture
## Session 1 Territory Document

**Session:** 1 — Territory Exploration

## Territory Name
Aligned by Architecture

## Single-Minded Proposition
*The only PI lead gen company whose business model cannot be profitable through dishonesty — because our success is measured by the same metric as yours.*

## What This Territory Claims
Every other vendor can decide to be honest. Only Second Chair has a business model that structurally enforces it. CPSC alignment means a bad lead costs Second Chair exactly as much as it costs the attorney. The compliance infrastructure (TrustedForm, fail-closed DNC/litigator screening, full audit trail) makes that honesty verifiable and durable. The attorney isn't trusting Second Chair's values. They're evaluating Second Chair's business model — and the model makes trust structurally rational, not a leap of faith.

## Proof Points
- CPSC as success metric: Second Chair only succeeds when the attorney succeeds on the metric that matters to them
- TrustedForm full stack: certify + verify + retain — court-admissible on every lead
- Fail-closed DNC/litigator screening: compliance policy is architectural, not optional
- No shared leads: enforced by business model design, not just stated policy
- No lock-in: month-to-month terms signal that Second Chair is betting on its own performance

## What This Territory Sounds Like
- "You're not trusting our word. You're trusting a business model that can't be profitable if we lie to you."
- "Our compliance infrastructure was built to FINRA/SEC standards. Because honest advertising requires honest infrastructure."
- "We don't succeed unless you do. That's not a marketing line — it's how the model works."
- "You can verify every claim we make. We built the infrastructure to make that possible."

## What This Territory Looks Like
Formal, institutional, trust-conveying. The visual language of a firm that would survive regulatory scrutiny — the architecture of compliance made visible through design. Typography weighted toward authority. The advertising shown alongside the infrastructure, so the attorney sees the whole chain: this is the ad → this is the intake record → this is the compliance documentation. The creative and the compliance in the same visual frame. No stock images. No abstract trust metaphors. The actual system — visible, legible, auditable.

## Who This Territory Speaks To
The attorney who has had a compliance event — or who fears one — from a vendor running misleading advertising. Also: the attorney who has been named in a bar complaint related to a lead vendor's advertising practices. Also: the marketing-director tier who needs to justify vendor selection to managing partners and wants to show a system, not just a claim.

## Research Alignment
- Category Consumer Synthesis Tension 3: Trust vs. Category-Wide Credibility Crisis
- Consumer Synthesis: the six cardinal sins of lead gen; attorney trust signal hierarchy (transparent verifiable data ranks #2)
- Founding Truth Brief Section 3: compliance infrastructure (TrustedForm, DNC/litigator, audit trail)
- Three Cs Master Synthesis: proof requirements — "named qualification criteria, visible advertising, named clients = specificity no competitor can match"

## Risk Factors
- "Aligned by Architecture" is technical language. The emotional translation is strong in conversation but doesn't carry as a brand territory on its own.
- If CPSC becomes a category norm — which it should if Second Chair succeeds in redefining category standards — the structural alignment claim becomes table stakes rather than a differentiator. The territory is time-bounded in its exclusivity.
- A well-resourced competitor could build a similar CPSC dashboard and compliance stack. The structural claim would not be permanently exclusive.
- The territory addresses trust but not identity. The ambulance chaser wound is not resolved here.

## Hegarty's Test
Weak. An attorney would be persuaded by this territory but would not share an ad produced from it unprompted. The territory closes deals; it does not build culture. Persuasive, not remarkable.

## Roma's Test
Very strong — strongest of all five territories. Sasha opens with: *"What's your current cost-per-signed-case?"* That question alone changes the evaluation frame. The attorney has never been asked it by a lead gen vendor. The territory gives Sasha the most powerful cold-call opener in the category: a metric reframe that makes every other vendor's offering suddenly incomparable on the terms they've been using.

They belong inside Territory D as the "this is why you can trust the claim" layer. The CPSC sales opener belongs in the B2B Sales Language Guide regardless of which territory wins.
