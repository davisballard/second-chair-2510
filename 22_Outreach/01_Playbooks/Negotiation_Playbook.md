## The 10 Golden Rules {#the-10-golden-rules}

1. **Slow down.** Late-night FM DJ voice. Calm = control.
2. **Ask, don't tell.** Target 70% questions / 30% statements. When in doubt, ask another question.
3. **Critique the industry, never their specific vendors.** Every time the prospect surfaces something uncomfortable, put the blame on how the industry is built, not on them or their vendor choices.
4. **Give the prospect the insight, not yourself.** Every realization should feel like theirs — not something Davis taught them.
5. **Silence 4+ seconds after every Socratic question.** Count internally. The silence-filler is the best data of the call.
6. **Accusation audit at every transition.** Pre-empt what the prospect is thinking before they say it.
7. **Arm them for the decision-maker at every objection.** The real buyer usually isn't in the room. Every answer ends with *"what would help you bring this to [decision-maker]?"*
8. **Precise numbers.** $5,000-$100,000, not "five figures." $360, not "around $350." Precision = credibility.
9. **Fairness trigger for pricing.** *"I want this to feel fair to both sides."*
10. **Don't compete on risk.** Refuse guarantee requests with Socratic questions, not counter-offers.

## Accusation Audits {#accusation-audits}

Pre-empt defensive thinking at every major transition. Patterns that work across any call:

### Opening gentle disarm
> *"You obviously get pitched by lead gen companies all the time, and you've got relationships working or they wouldn't still be there — so just want to say upfront, we're not here to critique your current setup."*

**Note:** Keep this casual. Do NOT use "trash your current setup" or meta-frames like "flip the normal dynamic" — they read defensive and put a junior marketer's guard up.

### Before the SB-37 reveal (CA prospects)
> *"You're probably wondering why the lead gen guy is talking to you about state bar compliance. Feels off-topic until it isn't."*

### Before pricing
> *"When I show you the pricing, you're probably going to think we're expensive compared to what you're used to. That's fair — we are, on a cost-per-lead basis. The question is what you're actually buying."*

### Before the trust / newness topic
> *"You're probably already thinking, 'these guys have been doing this four months, why should I trust them with [market]?' That's the exact question I'd ask in your seat."*

### Before the decision-maker handoff
> *"You're probably already thinking about how to pitch this to [decision-maker] and wondering if I'm going to make that easy or hard. I'm going to make it easy."*

## Mirrors {#mirrors}

Mirror the prospect's exact words back. Never translate, never upgrade to fancier language. Marketers will speak in their own vocabulary: *quality, CPL, sign rate, volume, attribution, brand-safe, speed-to-contact, intake, compliance, creative, funnel, conversion.*

### The pattern
**Them:** *"The attribution is a nightmare."*
**You:** *"Nightmare?"*
*(silence 4+s)*
**Them (fills):** *"Yeah, we've got three vendors firing pixels and every platform is claiming credit. I can't actually tell what's working."*

Each mirror extracts deeper information without asking a direct question. Prospects are talkers when they feel heard.

## Labels {#labels}

**Wrong:** *"It sounds like your vendors are failing you."* (Attacks their judgment — they defend.)
**Right:** *"It sounds like the current setup works for volume but maybe not for the brand-safety piece."* (Gives past judgment an out — acknowledges what works.)

**Wrong:** *"It seems like you haven't been tracking case value projection."* (Shaming.)
**Right:** *"It seems like CPL has been the metric your vendors report, and projected attorney fee per lead is the one you might want to see."* (Future-oriented — not their fault they haven't had it.)

### Layered label pattern
**Surface:** *"It sounds like the headline problem is volume."*
**Deeper:** *"Underneath that — the thing that would actually make [decision-maker] uncomfortable — is not knowing what's running under the firm's name."*

### Key openers
- *"It seems like..."*
- *"It sounds like..."*
- *"It looks like..."*

**NEVER use:**
- *"I understand..."* (patronizing)
- *"I hear you..."* (implies dismissal)

## Calibrated Questions {#calibrated-questions}

### Discovery
- *"What does [decision-maker] care about most when it comes to the firm's advertising?"*
- *"How would you know if a vendor was running non-compliant ads right now?"*
- *"What would have to be true for you to walk into [decision-maker]'s office tomorrow and say 'we need to change vendors'?"*
- *"How are you currently measuring whether lead spend is actually working — the full picture, not just CPL?"*
- *"What's the biggest problem you're carrying that [decision-maker] doesn't know about yet?"*
- *"What do your vendors' ads even look like — have you seen them?"*
- *"Who at the firm signs off on vendor contracts?"*
- *"How does your intake team handle leads that arrive with bad expectations?"*
- *"What would a great lead look like when it arrives at intake?"*

### Objection-handling
- *"How am I supposed to guarantee outcomes in [market] without cutting corners on compliance?"*
- *"What would have to be different for us to make this work?"*
- *"How does that feel compared to what [decision-maker] would want?"*
- *"What would the ideal vendor look like to you, honestly?"*

### Implementation
- *"How would you want to start — one Market or a few?"*
- *"How would success look in 90 days from your perspective?"*
- *"What would you want to see in the proposal to [decision-maker]?"*

## "No"-Oriented Questions {#no-oriented-questions}

Prospects feel safer saying "No" than "Yes." Use that.

| Instead of | Try |
|---|---|
| *"Are you interested in starting with California?"* | *"Would it be crazy to test California in your three biggest markets and see what the data actually looks like?"* |
| *"Can I send you a proposal?"* | *"Is it a bad idea for me to send over a pricing sheet and a one-pager you could share with [decision-maker]?"* |
| *"Should we set up a follow-up?"* | *"Would you be opposed to getting a second call on the calendar with [decision-maker] on it?"* |
| *"Does this all make sense?"* | *"Is it ridiculous to think the $5K-$100K per-violation exposure is something worth bringing to [decision-maker]?"* |

**Why "No" works:** Junior contacts don't want to commit to "Yes" without the decision-maker's blessing. "No" gives them protection — and paradoxically makes them more likely to agree.

## Getting To "That's Right" {#thats-right}

After discovery, summarize what the prospect said and wait for "that's right":

> *"So let me make sure I've got this. You've been running with your current vendors, getting some level of volume, measuring mostly on CPL. You've never actually seen the creative they're running. [Compliance issue] landed and nobody's walked you through what your actual exposure looks like. [Decision-maker]'s brand is the firm's main asset. And what would help you most right now isn't another vendor pitch — it's something you can bring to [decision-maker] that makes the case for them. Is that right?"*

**If they say "That's right" or "Exactly":** Proceed to the SC story phase. They're ready to listen.

**If they say "You're right":** That's NOT the same thing. "You're right" means "shut up and move on." Go back to discovery — something didn't land.

## The Fairness Trigger {#fairness-trigger}

> *"Listen, I want a pricing structure that feels fair to both sides. California is where you have the most exposure, the most offices, and the most work for us to do — that's why the lockdown discount exists. One compliance framework under SB-37, one creative system. Real operational efficiency I can pass through. Other states are list price because they're independent work — different compliance rules, different creative, different campaigns. That's fair, right?"*

Once the prospect nods at "fair," they've agreed to the structure. Then it's harder to argue about specific line items.

## Black Swans {#black-swans}

Every negotiation has 3-5 pieces of hidden information that would change everything. Universal watch-list:

1. **Does the decision-maker actually care about lead gen?** If they've delegated it entirely, the contact has more authority than you think.
2. **Who actually signs vendor contracts?** Could be the contact, marketing director, ops, decision-maker directly, or CFO. Ask: *"Who'd actually be on the contract if we move forward?"*
3. **Is the firm under any state bar scrutiny?** Don't ask directly. Listen for: *"We've been thinking about this a lot lately"* / *"Our compliance person has been asking questions"* / *"It's timely that you reached out."*
4. **Does the contact secretly hate their current vendors?** Listen for: *"I didn't pick them originally"* / *"They were here when I got here"* / *"I've been wanting to audit them."*
5. **Sister firms / related entities.** If the prospect mentions another firm unprompted, they may be thinking about cross-firm wins for their own career.
6. **Other media channels (TV/radio/OOH).** High-volume firms often run multiple channels. Each has its own compliance exposure.

### How to surface Black Swans
- Unexpected questions: *"What surprises people about how [firm] handles lead gen?"*
- Listen for anomalies — things that don't fit the expected shape
- Note what they emphasize AND what they avoid
- *"If you could change one thing about your current setup, what would it be?"*

## Voice Tone Allocation {#voice-tone}

- **Late-night FM DJ voice:** 80-85% — calm, slow, downward inflection, authority without pressure
- **Positive / playful voice:** 10% — opening rapport, arming-for-decision-maker phase, small moments of connection
- **Direct / assertive voice:** 5% or less — ONLY for: pricing anchors, the "no guarantee" moment, the "2 weeks is my normal" reveal. Overuse kills credibility.

**The opposite rule:** If you ever catch yourself using assertive voice more than twice in the same phase, slow down and switch back to late-night DJ.

## Strategic Silence {#strategic-silence}

Their silence-filler is the most valuable data of the call — the thing they didn't mean to say, the concern they hadn't planned to voice, the Black Swan they didn't know they were sitting on.

**Target:** 4+ seconds after every calibrated question.

**How to stay silent:** count internally. Look at notes. Take a sip of water. Write something down. Don't look at the camera waiting — that adds pressure.

## The Roma Layer {#roma-layer}

Roma (Glengarry Glen Ross) is the closer who finds the wound and moves the buyer *gently*. He doesn't "always be closing" crudely — he finds the intimate truth of the prospect and speaks to it.

- **Speak like you're already their trusted advisor**, not a vendor auditioning
- **Don't sell — discover together.** Treat the call as *"let's figure out what's actually happening in your world"*
- **Name the emotional truth.** Every contact is carrying something — anxiety about compliance, frustration with current vendors, pressure from leadership, career ambition for a win
- **Build one specific moment of connection** — the thing in their world you can actually acknowledge (background, city, industry context) — briefly, without fanfare
- **Close softly, not hard.** Close is always *"what would help you bring this to [decision-maker]?"* — never *"are you ready to move forward?"*

### The closing verbatim (soft ask)

> *"I think there's something real here. What would be the most useful next step from your perspective? And be direct with me — if the right next step is 'send me material and I'll float it with [decision-maker],' that's fine. If it's 'let's get a second call with [decision-maker] on it,' that's fine. If it's 'I need to think about it for a few days,' that's fine too. What actually works for how you operate?"*

*(silence — let them answer)*

Then give them exactly what they asked for. No more. No less.

## Guarantee Reframe — The Full Socratic Ladder {#guarantee-reframe}

> **When to use:** Only if the prospect brings up guarantees. Do NOT raise the topic yourself.

### The frame

Competing vendors pitch *"we'll guarantee CPSC."* Saying "no, we don't guarantee" sounds like cowardice.

**Goal:** Lead the prospect to the insight, through their own logic, that vendors willing to guarantee CPSC are hacks competing on risk because they cannot compete on quality — and the corners they cut to make a guarantee safe for themselves are exactly the SB-37 exposure wedge the call already walked through.

**Master principle:** Every sequence ends with the prospect able to walk into the decision-maker's office and say *"I asked them about guarantees and here's what they explained — and honestly, it's the first time I've heard a vendor say no to a guarantee in a way that made sense."* Not you closing them; them closing the decision-maker.

### Framing principle

The guarantee isn't the enemy. The *vendor who offers it* is the enemy — because the only way to safely guarantee a CPSC number in a top-tier market is to:
- Share leads across multiple firms (destroys exclusivity)
- Use formulaic creative pre-run with other firms 100 times (destroys specificity)
- Strip creative production costs to the bone (destroys quality)
- Skip compliance review (creates SB-37 exposure)
- Hide data from the client (destroys trust)
- Control which leads "count" as signed cases (controls the black box)

The sequences walk them to each realization on their own.

### Sequence 1 — "What Would Have To Be True"
> *"What would have to be true about how those leads are produced for someone to guarantee the outcome?"*
*(silence)*
> *"Like, what corners would they have to cut to de-risk it on their side?"*
*(silence)*
> *"If you were running that vendor's business and you had to guarantee $3,000 CPSC in [market] — what would you do to protect yourself?"*

### Sequence 2 — Risk-Doesn't-Disappear
> *"When a vendor takes all the risk on themselves, where do you think that risk actually goes?"*
*(silence)*
> *"Does it just disappear? Or does it show up somewhere else you're not looking?"*

### Sequence 3 — Most-Competitive-Market
> *"[Market] is one of the most competitive PI markets — [context: firm count, CPC, ad saturation]. Right?"*
*(let them confirm)*
> *"So if a vendor walks into that market and guarantees signed-case cost on their first week with [firm] — what does that tell you about how they're sourcing those leads?"*
*(silence)*
> *"Is there any way to guarantee that outcome in [market] without running the exact same playbook 100 times before with other firms?"*

### Sequence 4 — Creative Implication
> *"Say a vendor guarantees $3,000 CPSC in [market]. What do you think the creative looks like?"*
*(silence)*
> *"Are they spending two weeks researching [local-specific angle]? Or are they running the same template they used for a firm in another state?"*
*(silence)*
> *"And if they're running the template — what's the template have to say to convert? Is it the 'you deserve more' kind of copy? The big settlement numbers? The crash footage?"*

**Connects the guarantee directly to the SB-37 prohibited-content wedge.**

### Sequence 5 — What They're Commoditizing
> *"When vendors compete on risk guarantees instead of quality — like 'we'll eat the downside if the CPSC is over $3,300' — what are they telling the market about their product?"*
*(silence)*
> *"Is it that their leads are so good they can guarantee it? Or that their leads are so commoditized they have to compete on something else?"*

### Sequence 6 — Who Else Have They Guaranteed To
> *"If a vendor's guaranteeing [firm] $3K CPSC in [market] — how many other firms have they guaranteed the same thing to?"*
*(silence)*
> *"And if the answer is 'lots,' what does that tell you about exclusivity?"*

### Sequence 7 — SB-37 Bridge (ties it all together)
> *"So everything we talked about earlier — SB-37, vendor liability, crash footage, disclosures — does a guarantee-offering vendor have the cost structure to run SB-37-compliant creative in California?"*
*(silence)*
> *"Compliance costs money. Custom creative costs money. Deep market research costs money. If a vendor is absorbing downside risk on a guarantee, where is that budget coming from?"*

### Sequence 8 — Asymmetry
> *"A $3K CPSC guarantee on a $25K settlement generating ~$8K in fees — what's the max downside the vendor is absorbing, really? Even in the worst case."*
*(silence — let them do the math)*
> *"And what's your downside if the leads are non-compliant and you eat statutory damages of $5K to $100K per violation across a hundred ads?"*
*(silence)*
> *"Who's actually carrying the bigger risk in that deal?"*

### Sequence 9 — Would [Decision-Maker] Take This Deal
> *"If [decision-maker] sat in this chair and heard a vendor say 'we'll guarantee $3K CPSC in [market]' — what's their first question going to be?"*
*(silence — let them channel the decision-maker)*
> *"Knowing [decision-maker] as a trial lawyer who understands liability — does they take the guarantee at face value, or ask how they're sourcing the leads?"*

### Sequence 10 — The Ask-Back
When they ask *"Do you guys offer guarantees?"*:
> *"What do you think I'm about to say?"*
*(silence — let them finish the thought)*

**Expected:** *"You're not."* or *"Probably not."*
> *"Yeah. And after everything we just talked through — does that make sense now?"*

### Sequence 11 — Constructive Close (reframes "no guarantee" as strength)
> *"So we don't guarantee. But here's what we do instead."*
> *"One — every lead we deliver has a Projected Attorney Fee attached. On day one, before any case has signed, you can see the projected ROAS of the cohort. No other vendor in this space projects per-lead value like that. You can audit the math line by line. That's the opposite of a guarantee — it's transparency in both directions, visible from the first lead."*
> *"Two — we show you every ad before it runs. So if the creative isn't working, you don't need a guarantee, you need better creative, and you can demand it directly."*
> *"Three — we give you territory exclusivity. No other firm is racing you to the same lead. That alone lifts your conversion rate from the 3-5% shared range into the 15-25% exclusive range."*
> *"Four — we measure ourselves on projected attorney fee value and projected ROAS, not on CPL. So the incentives are aligned with yours from day one — we get paid to deliver leads with real projected value, not just phone numbers."*
*(pause)*
> *"Is that a stronger product for [firm], or a weaker one than a guarantee?"*

### Sequence 12 — Nuclear Fallback
If they're still pushing hard:
> *"Let me ask it this way. If I told you right now I could guarantee $3K CPSC on your first 100 leads in [market] — knowing everything we just talked about, would you actually trust that guarantee? Or would it make you more suspicious of me?"*

**If they say "I'd still want the guarantee":** Black Swan. Push up to decision-maker: *"What would make [decision-maker] feel like the guarantee was actually protective, rather than a cost the vendor is going to bury somewhere else?"*

### Do Not Say
- "We don't believe in guarantees." (Defensive.)
- "Our leads are so good we don't need to." (Arrogant.)
- "Nobody really guarantees CPSC honestly." (Attacks competitors.)
- "If you want a guarantee, you should probably work with someone else." (Closes the door.)
- "Our margins don't support a guarantee." (Signals low margin.)
- "Trust me." (Never works.)

### Do Say
- "What would have to be true for that to work?"
- "Where does that risk actually go?"
- "What does the creative have to look like to make that math work?"
- "What's the first question [decision-maker] would ask about that?"

## Trust / "We're New" Reframe — 2 Weeks Is My Normal {#trust-reframe}

> **When to use:** Any pushback on SC's newness — "you guys have only been around a few months," "why should we trust you with [top market]," or implicit skepticism.

### The core insight

Davis has spent 18 years at the top of global brand advertising — Disney, Google, NFL, NBA, Samsung, Uber, The Macallan, Optum, Vacation. In those environments, the normal pre-launch runway is **two weeks max.** Super Bowl work, Google launches, NFL campaigns — all two weeks.

For PI lead gen, Davis has had **four months**. That's 8x the preparation runway he's ever had.

### Verbatim opening — accusation audit
> *"[Name] — you're probably already thinking, 'these guys have been doing this four months, why should I trust them with [market]?' That's the exact question I'd ask in your seat. And it's completely fair."*
*(pause)*
> *"Let me answer it the only way I know how, which is by telling you what my normal looks like."*
> *"I've spent 18 years building advertising at the highest level — Disney, Google, Samsung, the NFL, NBA, Uber, The Macallan. In that world, my normal is this: I get two weeks, max, to understand a market and build a campaign that has to compete against billion-dollar rivals. That's the runway I've had for Super Bowl work, for Google product launches, for NFL brand campaigns. Two weeks."*
> *"For PI lead gen, I've had four months. That's eight times the runway I've ever had in my career. I've gone deeper into Market demographics, SB-37 compliance, Meta learning-phase mechanics, and creative archetypes for [local-specific audiences] than any NFL campaign I've ever built — because I've had the time."*

**Psychology:**
- **Pratfall Effect (Aronson 1966):** admitting the "4 months" fact increases credibility on every other claim
- **Loss aversion flip:** reframes "new = risky" to "4 months of deep immersion = the preparation number"
- **Confidence modulation:** calm, not defensive. The calm is the credibility signal.

### The tenure-as-red-flag reversal
> *"And here's the question I'd honestly ask your current vendors: what have they figured out in their ten years that we haven't in four months?"*
*(silence)*
> *"Because if the answer is 'territory exclusivity in every Market, SB-37-compliant creative, Projected ROAS per lead, every ad visible to the client' — great, they're ahead of us. Tell me to go away, they're doing the job."*
*(pause)*
> *"But if the answer is 'we've been running the same formula for a decade' — then tenure isn't experience. It's inertia. Ten years running the wrong playbook isn't more experience than four months running the right one."*

### Socratic reveal (the actual "aha")
> *"Let me ask you something. What do you think the bar is for running a $100 million brand campaign for the NFL?"*
*(silence)*
> *"And how long do you think the creative team gets to understand the market before launch?"*
*(silence — they'll probably guess 'months' or 'quarters')*
> *"Two weeks. Maximum. Sometimes five days. Months is a luxury I have never had in my career before this. That's what PI lead gen has given me."*

### SEC escalator (deploy if compliance is live)
> *"And the compliance piece specifically — I've built compliance-grade creative for SEC-regulated fintech. That's a regulatory environment that makes state bar rules look relaxed. If I can run ads that clear the SEC, I can run ads that clear SB-37 in my sleep."*

### Vacation sunscreen proof point (deploy if zero-to-launch skepticism is live)
> *"I built a sunscreen brand from scratch — first employee at Vacation. 14 beauty awards in year one. 300+ PR mentions in year one. 27 million earned impressions for the launch. I know how to go from zero to launched at scale. That part isn't new."*

### Team-level layer
> *"And this isn't just me. Alex built TradeStation mobile — a million users, 99.96% crash-free, 100% FINRA and SEC compliant. Regulated software at the securities-exchange level. Sasha's run programmatic for Bose, Coach, Diageo, H&R Block, Tinder, PepsiCo. Company is four months old. Capability is 50 combined years. The thing that's new is the application to PI lead gen."*

### No-oriented close on trust
> *"Is it ridiculous to think that 18 years of brand advertising, 4 months of PI-specific immersion, and a compliance-first platform adds up to more preparation than a 10-year commodity vendor has ever done for a single client?"*

### Decision-maker-facing version (for them to repeat upstairs)
> *"The guy leading Second Chair spent 18 years running brand campaigns for Disney, Google, and the NFL. He told me — and this surprised me — that in that world, he normally gets two weeks to understand a market. For us, he's spent four months preparing. Eight times what he's used to. That's not a new company story. That's a new application of a proven team."*

### What NOT to say
- *"We're just as experienced as the others."* (Defensive — you lose the moment you say it.)
- *"Our team has 50+ years combined experience!"* (Recruiter-speak. Kills credibility.)
- *"We're better because we're newer / faster / hungrier."* (Underdog posturing.)
- Never compare unfavorably to specific named competitors.
- Never apologize for being new. Acknowledge, reframe, move on.
- Never list every client logo.
- Never say the word "startup."

### Full sequence quick reference

1. Accusation audit: *"4 months, why trust them with [market]? That's exactly what I'd ask."*
2. The 2-weeks verbatim: *"18 years at Disney/Google/NFL. My normal is two weeks max."*
3. Tenure reversal: *"What have your vendors figured out in ten years that we haven't in four months?"*
4. Socratic reveal: *"What do you think the NFL campaign runway is? [silence] Two weeks."*
5. SEC escalator (if compliance live): *"I've cleared SEC. SB-37 is lighter."*
6. Vacation proof (if zero-to-launch skepticism live): *"First employee at Vacation, 300+ PR mentions year one."*
7. Team layer: *"Alex — TradeStation, FINRA. Sasha — Bose/Coach/Tinder/PepsiCo. 50 combined years."*
8. No-oriented close: *"Is it ridiculous to think that adds up to more preparation than a 10-year commodity vendor?"*

## Universal Objection Handlers {#objection-handlers}

| Objection | Response pattern |
|---|---|
| "We're happy with our current vendors" | **Label:** *"Sounds like the relationship works at some level."* **Calibrated:** *"What's the piece that works best? And what's the piece you've had to work around?"* → arm for decision-maker |
| "This is more expensive than we're paying" | **Accusation audit:** *"Figured you'd say that. On per-lead, we are."* **Reframe:** Projected Attorney Fee → Projected ROAS math. *"A cheaper lead with half the projected value isn't cheaper — it's just a lower denominator."* |
| "I need to run this by [decision-maker]" | *"Of course. What would help you most when you bring it to them? Summary email? One-pager? A second call?"* |
| "Can you do better on price?" | *"How would you want me to handle it if [top Market] and [bottom Market] cost us the same to operate in?"* → fairness trigger |
| "What if the leads don't convert?" | **Label:** *"Sounds like you've been burned before."* **Reframe:** Projected Attorney Fee visible day one + 100 leads per Market past learning phase. *"If the numbers don't work, you don't renew."* |
| "Send us a proposal" | *"Absolutely. Which markets? And what level of detail — top-line or fully broken out?"* |
| "You guys are new" | → Deploy **Trust Reframe** above |
| "Can you guarantee signed cases?" | → Deploy **Guarantee Reframe** Sequences 1, 2, 4 minimum |
| "What about [vendor name]?" | *"I'm not here to talk about any specific vendor. Every vendor in this space has a hard time with the same problem — compliance, visibility, attribution."* Redirect. |
| "We need to think about it" | *"Completely fair. What specifically would be most helpful for me to send you and [decision-maker]?"* |

## Post-Call Ritual {#post-call-ritual}

Within 30 minutes of the call ending:

1. **Write down their verbatim answer to "what would [decision-maker] need to see?"** — single most important output
2. **Log any Black Swans that surfaced** — what they mentioned that you didn't expect
3. **Note the emotional arc** — when did their voice change? Where did they open up? Where did they resist?
4. **Identify the ONE thing they were most engaged by** — that's the anchor for follow-up
5. **Draft the follow-up email** — pricing sheet + compliance memo, addressed to what THEY actually asked for

Within 1 hour: Short thank-you email with commitment for next-day deliverables.

Within 24 hours: Pricing sheet (customized to the Markets they focused on) + one-pager memo. **Do NOT send a proposal or contract yet.**

Follow-up: **5 business days** — not sooner.

## Lines We Don't Cross

1. **No discounts for geographic spread** — scattered Markets = relationship, not a price break
2. **No flat state fees** — always per-Market via MOI
3. **No shared exclusivity** — one firm per Market, period
4. **No guaranteed CPSC** — Socratic refusal only
5. **No stacking discounts** — 15% CA lockdown is the ceiling
6. **No free leads as remedy** — we publish CPSC methodology, we don't backfill
7. **No premium tier pitch on Call 1** — that's Call 2+
8. **Never name a competing vendor** — critique the industry, not a specific vendor
