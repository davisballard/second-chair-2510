## The Core Problem with Their Proposal

### Problem 1: Learning Phase Makes Guarantees on First 100 Leads Unreasonable

**Why we can't do less than 100 leads:**

The first 100 leads span learning, stabilization, and early optimization. Performance varies significantly during this ramp:

| Leads Delivered | Timeline | Performance State |
|----------------|----------|-------------------|
| **1-30** | Weeks 1-2 | Learning phase — highest cost, least optimized targeting |
| **30-60** | Weeks 3-4 | Stabilization — performance improving, creative being refined |
| **60-100** | Weeks 5-7 | Early optimization — campaigns finding consistency |
| **100+** | Week 8+ | Optimized state — predictable, steady performance |

**At 100 leads:** You've seen the full performance arc — not just the expensive learning phase.

**At 50 leads:** You're evaluating entirely on pre-optimization performance. Not representative, not fair to either party.

**This is why guaranteeing a specific CPSC on the first 100 leads is like guaranteeing a restaurant's food quality based on soft-opening night.**

---

### Problem 2: Case Value Variance Makes CPSC Metric Unreliable

**What J&M actually makes per signed case:**

- **Soft tissue cases:** $5,000-$7,000 in attorney fees (whiplash, minor injuries)
- **Major injury cases:** $50,000-$300,000+ in attorney fees (spinal, TBI, trucking, wrongful death)

**The CPSC metric doesn't account for this:**

**Scenario A:** We deliver 12 signed cases, all soft tissue
- J&M fees: 12 × $6,000 = $72,000
- J&M costs: $31,000 (leads) + $24,000 (case expenses) = $55,000
- **J&M profit: $17,000** (31% margin)
- We "passed" the 10-case target

**Scenario B:** We deliver 7 signed cases, including one major injury ($80K in fees)
- J&M fees: 6 × $6,000 + 1 × $80,000 = $116,000
- J&M costs: $31,000 (leads) + $14,000 (case expenses) = $45,000
- **J&M profit: $71,000** (158% margin)
- We "failed" the 10-case target but they made 4x more profit

**The real question isn't "how many cases signed" — it's "did you make money?"**

That's return on ad spend (ROAS), not cost per signed case.

## Colorado Pricing: The Correct Formula

**What we quoted verbally (simple average):**
- Denver ($315) + Colorado Springs ($285) / 2 = $300/lead

**The correct population-weighted formula:**
- Denver DMA: 3.0M population (78.95% of Colorado)
- Colorado Springs DMA: 0.8M population (21.05% of Colorado)
- **Weighted rate: ($315 × 0.7895) + ($285 × 0.2105) = $310/lead**

**100 leads at $310 = $31,000**

**Why this matters:**
- Demonstrates precision (not arbitrary pricing)
- Shows we understand our own economics
- Signals engineering rigor to Tommie (former mechanical engineer)
- $310 vs. $300 round number = credibility

**How to present it:**

> "For Colorado, we have two DMAs: Denver (3.0M population, $315/lead) and Colorado Springs (0.8M population, $285/lead). The state blended rate is population-weighted: ($315 × 0.7895) + ($285 × 0.2105) = **$310/lead**.
>
> Denver metro drives 79% of Colorado's population, so that weight dominates the calculation. This ensures you're not overpaying for Colorado Springs leads while we're not undercharging for Denver leads."

### Option 3: The Capped Downside Guarantee (IF THEY INSIST ON FIRST 100)

**If they push hard on "we need the guarantee on the first 100":**

**The Structure:**

**Guarantee: $3,100 CPSC on first 100 leads, with protective terms:**

   - They claim 4-minute response time — put it in writing
   - If leads sit, guarantee voids

2. **Cap on Free Leads**
   - If CPSC > $3,100, SC delivers **up to 30 additional leads at no charge**

   - Caps our downside at $4,500 media cost

3. **90-Day Measurement Window**
   - CPSC calculated 90 days from first lead delivery

   - Prevents indefinite "they haven't signed yet" reporting games

4. **Verification Rights**
   - SC receives weekly signed case count (no PII, just count)
   - Right to audit signed case count via redacted retainer agreements if dispute arises
   - Clear definition: "Signed case = executed retainer agreement"

5. **Case Value Qualifier** (OUR KEY ADD)
   - If 30+ free leads delivered and CPSC still > $3,100, **but total attorney fees generated exceed $93,000** (3x ROAS), guarantee is deemed satisfied
   - Prevents situation where we deliver high-value cases but get penalized for lower case count

**Why this works:**
- We're saying "yes" to the guarantee (meets their ask)
- But with protections that make it fair
- And we're adding the case value reality (ROAS qualifier)
- Shows confidence while protecting downside

**Risk Level:** Medium-High — still exposed to learning phase risk, but capped

## Chris Voss: Call vs. Email Strategy

From `Chris_Voss_Tactical_Empathy.md` and `Agency_Client_Negotiation_Guide.md`:

### When to Use Email vs. Call

**Email is better when:**
1. You need them to process complex information (economics, formulas, options)
2. You want them to think without pressure (gives them control)
3. You need a paper trail for verification/terms
4. The counteroffer is detailed and needs review time

**Call is better when:**
1. You need to read their emotional state (labels, mirrors, tone)
2. Negotiation requires back-and-forth calibrated questions
3. You need to build rapport through voice/silence
4. They're stonewalling via email

**The Voss Hybrid Approach (RECOMMENDED):**

**Step 1: Email First (Give Them Control)**

Send a detailed email that:
- Acknowledges their position (accusation audit)
- Presents your counteroffer with clear rationale
- Shows you understand the economics (credibility signal)
- Ends with a "No"-oriented question about a call

**Why email first:**
- Demonstrates thoughtfulness (not reactive)
- Gives them time to process without feeling pressured
- Shows operational maturity (you thought this through)
- Creates paper trail for terms/verification

**Step 2: Calibrated Question + "No"-Oriented Close**

Don't demand a call. Ask if they're opposed to one:

> "I realize this is more complex than a simple yes/no. **Would you be opposed to a brief call to discuss the structure?** Happy to answer any questions about why the tiered approach protects you better than a flat guarantee on learning-phase campaigns.
>
> If you'd rather respond via email first, that works too. Whatever's most useful from your perspective."

**Why this works:**
- "Would you be opposed" = "No"-oriented (feels safe)
- Gives them control (email or call, their choice)
- Positions call as helping them, not pressuring them
- "Whatever's most useful from your perspective" = deference to their process

### If They Respond to Email Without Committing

**Use the "That's Right" Summary + Call Ask:**

Reply with:

> "Just to make sure I'm understanding your position correctly:
>
> [Summarize their objection/concern in their own words]
>
> Is that right?
>
> If I've got that right, I think a quick call would help — I can walk through [specific thing they're concerned about] and answer any questions in real time. **Are you against scheduling 15 minutes this week?**"

**Why this works:**
- Get them to say "That's right" first (breakthrough moment)
- Then the call ask feels like a natural next step
- "Are you against" = safe "No"-oriented question
- Specific, bounded time commitment (15 minutes, not open-ended)

### If They Ignore Email or Ghost

**Use the "Have You Given Up?" Email (Voss's Non-Responder Email):**

**Subject:** Have you given up on Colorado lead generation?

**Body:**

> Tommie,
>
> I haven't heard back on the Colorado package structure we discussed. Have you given up on moving forward?
>
> If timing's not right or the structure doesn't work for J&M, no problem — just let me know so I don't keep following up.
>
> Best,
> Davis

**Why this works:**
- Compels correction: "No, we haven't given up..."
- Triggers loss aversion
- Direct without being pushy
- 80%+ response rate on non-responders
