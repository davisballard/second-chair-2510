## How to Approach Tommie — Behavioral Framework

### Richard Shotton: The Psychological Levers

**1. Cognitive Ease (Primary)**
Tommie processes structured information faster and trusts it more. He builds KPI dashboards. He creates quarterly business reviews.

- Present pricing in clean tables, not narrative
- Use precise numbers ($315, not "around $300")
- Structure the process explanation as a visual flow: Ad → Funnel → Qualification → Routing → Delivery → Dashboard
- When he asks a question, answer it directly before adding context

**2. Anchoring (Critical for Pricing)**
He has a $300/lead anchor from Lead Faucet. SC's prices are higher ($315-$460).

- **Anchor on cost per signed case BEFORE revealing per-lead price.** "At a 10% sign rate, our leads produce signed cases at roughly $3,500. Industry average from other vendors is $8,000 to $14,000."
- Once the CPSC anchor is set, per-lead prices of $315-$460 feel justified.
- CA lockdown blended at ~$320 is close to his $300 anchor — that's the easiest sell.

**3. Authority Bias (Subtle)**
He respects credentials but evaluates them — he's an investor, not a fan.

- Don't recite resumes. Let credentials emerge naturally: "When we built the compliance infrastructure, Alex applied the same approach he used at TradeStation — FINRA and SEC compliance, crash-free mobile platform."
- Tommie ran ISD Corporation building software for California's court systems. Alex built TradeStation. Let them talk as peer engineers.

**4. Commitment & Consistency**
He mentors MBA students. He invested in 140+ startups. He's a man who commits and follows through.

- Start with a small commitment: a pilot in one market. Once he says yes to a test, the consistency principle makes expansion natural.
- Frame the pilot as a "prove it" opportunity: "Start with 2-3 markets at 100 leads each. By lead 100, you've seen the full performance arc — not just the learning phase. If the numbers work, we expand. If they don't, you walk away."

**5. Social Proof (Investor's Lens)**
As a 15-year angel investor, he thinks in terms of validation.

- If SC has any early clients, mention them.
- If not, the validation is the team's track records: "Between the three of us, we've built products for audiences of a million-plus, managed eight-figure ad budgets, and handled regulatory compliance at the securities-exchange level."

### Richard Roma: The Negotiation Approach

**1. Don't Pitch — Let Him Discover**
Tommie's mentoring style is Socratic. He asks questions to help people arrive at their own conclusions. Mirror this.

- Instead of "Here's why we're better," ask: "What would a better lead gen setup look like for your markets?"
- Let him describe the gap. Then fill it.

**2. Match His Tempo**
He's quiet. He processes before responding. Don't fill silence with words.

- When he pauses after you say something, wait. He's thinking, not disengaging.
- If you rush to fill silence, you signal nervousness. He'll notice.

**3. He's an Expert Negotiator — Expect Precision**
Per Brianna Porter, he's an "expert negotiator." He won't push hard — he'll ask precise questions that shift the frame:
- "What's your cost basis on a lead in the LA market?" (He's looking for margin)
- "How many firms are you running in California right now?" (He's testing scarcity)
- "What happens if the lead doesn't convert?" (He's testing risk-sharing)

**Be direct back.** He respects straight-shooters (per Adam Nathan). If you don't know an answer, say so. He'll trust you more than if you bluff.

**4. The $300 Anchor Counter**
When pricing comes up (and it will), he's benchmarked at $300/lead from Lead Faucet.

> *"$300 per lead across the board tells me they were either averaging across markets or selling shared territory. LA and Denver don't cost the same to serve. Our pricing reflects the actual cost of operating in each market — and the fact that no competing firm can buy in your DMA while you hold it. That exclusivity is built into the price."*
