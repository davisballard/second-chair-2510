## Why Voss Works for Tommie

Tommie is quiet, analytical, and an expert negotiator himself. He won't respond to pressure, hard closes, or aggressive pitching. He WILL respond to:

- **Being understood** — Voss's core: tactical empathy. Demonstrate you understand his situation before you present anything.
- **Being given control** — Calibrated questions and "No"-oriented questions give him the feeling of driving. He's Socratic. Mirror his style.
- **Silence** — He processes quietly. Voss says wait 4+ seconds. With Tommie, wait as long as he needs. Don't fill the void.
- **Precision over promises** — He ran P&Ls, he's an angel investor, he evaluates with data. Specific numbers feel calculated. Round numbers feel like guesses.

## How to Run This Call — Voss Method

### 1. Open with an Accusation Audit

Before you get into anything substantive, call out what Tommie is probably thinking. This disarms defensiveness and creates trust immediately.

> *"Tommie, before we get into anything — I know you probably get pitched by lead gen companies regularly. You're probably thinking, 'Here's another vendor telling me their leads are different.' And honestly, that's fair. You might be wondering if we can actually deliver what we say, or if this is just a slick pitch. You've probably been through this before."*

Then pause. Let him respond. He'll likely say something like "Well, yeah, but I'm here, so go ahead." Defenses lowered. Permission granted.

**Why this works with Tommie specifically:** His recommendations describe him as someone who values honesty and integrity. An accusation audit IS honesty — you're naming the awkward truth out loud. He'll respect it.

---

### 2. Discovery: Mirrors + Labels + Calibrated Questions

**Don't interrogate Tommie. Draw him out.** He's Socratic — he leads through questions. Mirror his method.

**Mirrors (repeat his last 1-3 words):**

Tommie says: "We've been working with a vendor but the quality hasn't been great."
You: "Hasn't been great?" *(silence)*
Tommie: "The leads come in but half of them don't answer the phone, and the ones that do aren't really cases."

Each mirror extracts deeper information without you asking a direct question. Tommie is a talker when he feels heard — the recommendations all say he opens up with the right person.

**Labels (name what he's feeling):**

> *"It sounds like you've invested in lead gen before and didn't see the return you expected."*
> *(silence)*

> *"It seems like lead quality — not volume — is what's actually keeping you up at night."*
> *(silence)*

> *"It looks like you're evaluating whether we're a real operation or just another pitch."*
> *(silence)*

**Layered label (go deeper):**

Surface: *"It sounds like you're concerned about the cost per lead."*
Deeper: *"It seems like what you're really asking is whether these leads will actually sign — because that's the number that matters to the attorneys."*

**Calibrated questions (What/How — give him control):**

- *"What's the biggest challenge with your current lead gen setup?"*
- *"How are you measuring whether your lead spend is actually working?"*
- *"What would need to be true for you to feel confident in a new vendor?"*
- *"How does your intake team handle leads right now?"*
- *"What does a great lead look like when it arrives at intake?"*

---

### 3. Get to "That's Right"

This is the breakthrough moment. Before you present pricing or next steps, summarize what Tommie told you and get him to say "That's right" or "Exactly."

> *"So let me make sure I understand. You've worked with lead gen vendors before, and the quality hasn't been where you need it. The leads that come in either don't answer or aren't real cases. Your intake team is spending time on contacts that go nowhere. You're not against spending on lead gen — you know it works when it works — but you need leads that actually convert to signed cases, at a cost that makes sense. And you need to be able to see exactly what you're paying for."*

> *"Is that right?"*

**When he says "That's right":** He feels understood. Now you can present. He's ready to listen because you've demonstrated you get his situation.

**If he says "You're right":** That's NOT the same thing. "You're right" means "shut up." Go back to discovery.

---

### 4. Pricing: Accusation Audit + Anchoring + Precise Numbers

**Before revealing any price, run the accusation audit:**

> *"When I walk you through pricing, you're probably going to think it's higher than what you've paid before. You might compare it to other vendors and think we're expensive. That's fair."*

> *"What I'd ask you to evaluate is cost per signed case, not cost per lead. Because a cheaper lead that doesn't sign isn't cheaper — it's a waste."*

**Then anchor on CPSC:**

> *"At a 10% sign rate, our leads produce signed cases at roughly $3,200 to $4,600 depending on the market. Industry average from other vendors is $8,000 to $14,000."*

**Then give precise per-lead prices (precision = credibility with an engineer):**

- Denver: $315
- LA (CA lockdown): $360
- SF (CA lockdown): $325
- CA blended: ~$320

**Use odd, precise numbers wherever possible.** $315 feels calculated. $300 feels negotiable. Tommie is an engineer — precision signals rigor.

---

### 5. "No"-Oriented Questions

Tommie will feel safer saying "No" than "Yes." Use this.

**Instead of:** "Are you interested in starting with California?"
**Try:** *"Would it be a terrible idea to start with your three strongest California markets?"*

**Instead of:** "Can I send you a proposal?"
**Try:** *"Would you be opposed to me sending over a market-by-market breakdown?"*

**Instead of:** "Should we set up a follow-up?"
**Try:** *"Is it a bad idea to schedule a follow-up to walk through the creative for your markets?"*

**Why this works with Tommie:** He's deliberate. He doesn't want to be pressured into "yes." A "No"-oriented question gives him control and paradoxically makes him more likely to agree.

---

### 6. Strategic Silence

After any mirror, label, calibrated question, price reveal, or close attempt — **shut up.**

**With most people:** 4+ seconds.
**With Tommie:** As long as he needs. He processes. He's described as "patient" and "even-keeled." He's not uncomfortable with silence. Don't be either.

The person who speaks first after a price reveal loses. State the number. Stop talking. Let him respond.

## Objection Obliterators (Voss + Data)

### "We're paying less with our current vendor."

**Label:** *"It sounds like the per-lead cost is lower than what I'm showing you."*
*(silence)*

**Calibrated:** *"What's your cost per signed case at that rate?"*
*(let him calculate or admit he doesn't track it)*

**Reframe:** *"Shared leads convert at 2.5 to 5% to signed case. Exclusive leads convert at 15 to 25%. A $200 shared lead at 3% conversion is $6,700 per signed case. A $350 exclusive lead at 15% is $2,300. The cheaper lead is the more expensive case."*

---

### "This is more expensive than expected."

**Accusation audit:** *"I figured you'd say that. And honestly, comparing just the per-lead number, we are more expensive than most vendors."*

**Reframe:** *"The question is whether you're buying a lead or buying a signed case. At our rates with a 10% sign rate, you're looking at $3,200 to $4,600 per signed case. Against a median MVA case generating $8,300 to $16,500 in fees, that's a 3.5 to 7x return."*

**Label:** *"It seems like what you're really evaluating is whether the quality justifies the price."*
*(silence — let him confirm)*

---

### "We need to think about it."

**Label:** *"It sounds like something specific is giving you pause."*
*(silence)*

If he names it, address it. If he's vague:

**Calibrated:** *"What would you need to see to feel confident this is the right move?"*

Then provide exactly that.

---

### "Can you do a flat rate / lower price?"

**Calibrated:** *"How would you want me to handle the difference between what it costs to operate in LA versus Denver?"*
*(makes him think through the pricing logic himself)*

**Fairness trigger:** *"I want to make sure this feels fair for both sides. LA has 5,000 PI firms competing for attention. Denver has a few hundred. Those are different costs for us. Pricing each market at what it costs to operate there is the fairest way we've found to do it."*

---

### "What if the leads don't convert?"

**Label:** *"It sounds like you've been burned before — leads that look good on paper but don't turn into cases."*
*(silence)*

**Reframe:** *"That's exactly why we track cost per signed case, not just CPL. And that's why the first package is 100 leads — so you see real performance data, not just learning-phase noise. By lead 100, you know exactly what these leads produce. If the numbers don't work, you don't renew."*

---

### "I need to run this by someone else."

**Label:** *"It seems like there are other stakeholders involved in this decision."*

**Calibrated:** *"How can I help you present this internally? What would they need to see?"*

Then provide whatever materials he needs — pricing summary, Attorney Pitch doc, process overview. Make him the champion by giving him the ammunition.

## Black Swans — What to Watch For

Every negotiation has hidden pieces of information that would change everything. With Tommie, watch for:

- **His actual authority level.** His LinkedIn says CSO/President/Board Trustee. He might have full authority. He might need a managing partner's sign-off. You don't know until you do.
- **Why he's really on this call.** Is he actively shopping for a new vendor? Was he told to evaluate options? Is he unhappy with a current vendor? The reason behind the call shapes everything.
- **His relationship with the current vendor.** If he has a long-standing vendor relationship, switching has emotional cost even if the data supports it.
- **Internal politics.** Is anyone at J&M pushing for or against this kind of change?

**How to find Black Swans:** Listen for anomalies. Ask unexpected questions. Note what he emphasizes and what he avoids.

> *"What surprises people about how J&M handles lead gen?"*
> *"If you could change one thing about your current setup, what would it be?"*

## The Golden Rules for This Call

1. **Slow down.** Calm = control. Late-night FM DJ voice.
2. **Listen more than you talk.** Aim for 70/30 — Tommie talks 70%.
3. **Mirror, label, calibrate.** In that order. Extract information without interrogating.
4. **Get to "That's right" before presenting.** If he doesn't feel understood, nothing else matters.
5. **Silence after every technique.** 4+ seconds minimum. With Tommie, longer.
6. **Use precise numbers.** $315, not "around $300." $97,500, not "about $100K."
7. **Don't sell. Discover.** The call is a process of discovery, not a pitch delivery.
8. **Name the negatives first.** Accusation audits disarm. Tommie values honesty.
9. **Give him control.** "No"-oriented questions, calibrated questions, "What would be most useful from your perspective?"
10. **Don't compete for alpha.** Two quiet, capable people build trust through substance, not through dominance.
