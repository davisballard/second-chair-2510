## The Core Argument

Second Chair builds advertising specifically for each DMA (Designated Market Area) it serves. This is not a minor operational detail — it's the reason Second Chair's leads convert at a fundamentally different rate than leads from vendors who run national or statewide campaigns with local phone numbers swapped in.

DMA-specific advertising means every ad is built for the audience in that market: their demographics, their commute corridors, their injury patterns, their language, their psychology. It means the person who sees the ad and fills out the form was **honestly persuaded** — not tricked by a generic funnel.

---

## 4 Reasons This Matters for Jacoby & Meyers

### 1. Ad Specificity Drives Conversion

A person in Colorado Springs who sees an ad referencing *"Accident cases in Colorado have settled for millions"* with visuals built for a working-class family on the I-25 corridor converts at a higher rate than someone who sees *"Injured in an accident? Call now."*

This isn't theory. It's how advertising works:

- **Geographic anchoring:** When people see their state, their city, their commute corridor named in an ad, it triggers recognition. The ad feels relevant. Relevance drives click-through. Click-through drives qualified form fills.
- **Audience psychology:** A 34-year-old healthcare aide in Metro Denver responds to different emotional cues than a 52-year-old retiree in San Diego. DMA-level creative lets us build for the actual audience, not a national average.
- **Platform algorithms reward relevance:** Meta and Google's ad delivery systems prioritize ads with higher engagement. Locally relevant creative gets higher engagement, which gets better delivery, which lowers CPL. This is a compounding advantage.

### 2. Jacoby & Meyers Already Believes This

J&M has **14 offices** across California, each with its own local phone number and local address. That's not an accident — it's a deliberate investment in local presence. When a potential client in Fresno calls the Fresno number and visits the Fresno office, they feel like they're hiring a local firm.

Second Chair extends that philosophy to the top of the funnel. The advertising that generates the lead should feel as local as the office that serves it. When a person in the Sacramento DMA sees an ad built for Sacramento — not a recycled LA ad — they enter the funnel with the right expectations and the right emotional frame.

The match between the ad experience and the intake experience matters. When the ad says "Sacramento" and the intake call comes from a Sacramento number, the lead trusts the process. When the ad is generic and the call feels disconnected, leads go cold.

### 3. Compliance Varies by State — Creative Must Follow

California, Colorado, and Washington each have different state bar advertising rules:

| Rule Area | California | Colorado | Washington |
|-----------|-----------|----------|------------|
| **Special legislation** | SB37 — significant restrictions on PI advertising | None | None |
| **Crash footage in ads** | Banned | Cautious — use carefully | Cautious — use carefully |
| **Injury imagery** | Restricted | Cautious | Cautious |
| **Specialist claims** | Cannot claim unless certified | Cannot claim unless certified | Cannot claim unless certified |
| **Enforcement level** | High | Medium | Medium |
| **Risk rating** | High | Low | Low |

DMA-level creative means each market gets ads that comply with **its** state's rules — not a lowest-common-denominator creative approach that waters everything down to California's restrictions. Colorado and Washington allow more aggressive creative than California. That latitude should be used, not wasted.

### 4. Quality Signal to Intake

When a lead arrives at J&M's intake team, the metadata tells a story:

- **DMA-specific lead:** *"This person responded to a Sacramento personal injury ad on Instagram Reels. They completed a 6-question qualification quiz. They reported a rear-end collision on I-80, medical treatment received, other driver at fault."*

- **Generic statewide lead:** *"This person filled out a form on a California injury website. Location: somewhere in California."*

The first lead is actionable. The intake team knows the person's location, injury type, and intent. They can route to the Sacramento office immediately with context.

The second lead requires the intake team to do the work the advertising should have done: figuring out who this person is, where they are, and whether they're a real prospect.

**Better advertising produces better intake calls. Better intake calls produce higher sign rates. Higher sign rates produce lower cost per signed case.**

## What DMA-Specific Creative Looks Like in Practice

### Example: The Same Concept, Two Markets

Second Chair has an active Colorado campaign called **"On The Nose"** — a 15-second story slide for Meta (Reels/Stories/Feed):

**Colorado Version (Denver DMA):**
- **Script:** *"Accident cases in Colorado have settled for millions. Most people never find out what they're owed. Free claim estimate. Sixty seconds. See what experienced attorneys can do."*
- **Visuals:** Cinematic style — a Colorado family, winter light, I-25/I-70 corridor imagery, booster seat detail (family-centered)
- **Target audience:** "Maria" — Metro Denver/Aurora Hispanic working-class family, 28-48, $42K-$68K household income, healthcare/warehouse/retail/delivery jobs
- **VO:** Gruff, working-class male voice — stability and authority, not corporate polish

**How this would adapt for San Francisco DMA:**
- **Script changes:** "Accident cases in California have settled for millions" (state-level legal framing for CA). References to Bay Area corridors, not Colorado highways.
- **Visual changes:** Different demographics. SF DMA is more ethnically diverse, higher income range, tech/service economy. Different visual language — urban density, BART, bridge crossings, not mountain highways.
- **Target audience shift:** Different archetype — SF audience skews younger, more diverse, higher digital fluency. Creative tone adjusts.
- **Same production quality.** Same cinematic standard. Same emotional arc. Different market-specific details.

### Example: The Same Concept, Simpler Market

**Colorado Springs DMA:**
- **Script:** Same core — "Accident cases in Colorado have settled for millions"
- **Visual changes:** Military presence (Fort Carson, Peterson SFB). Different demographic mix — more military families, more suburban, less urban density than Denver.
- **Audience shift:** Military spouse archetype, slightly different income/age profile, different commute patterns.

**The point:** SC doesn't just swap a phone number. The creative is actually different for each DMA because the people in each DMA are actually different.

## What This Means for J&M's California Markets

J&M serves 7 California DMAs. Each one is a distinct advertising challenge:

| DMA | Pop | Key Creative Considerations |
|-----|-----|----------------------------|
| **Los Angeles** | 13.2M | J&M's home market. 85K-100K annual PI filings. Extreme competition (5,000+ PI firms). SB37 restrictions apply. Heavy Hispanic population — bilingual creative required. |
| **SF–Oakland–San Jose** | 4.7M | Tech economy, younger, higher income. Different injury patterns (bicycle, rideshare, pedestrian). Strong environmental/safety consciousness. |
| **Riverside–San Bernardino** | 4.7M | Inland Empire — working-class, high commute times, trucking corridors (I-10, I-15). High uninsured rate. |
| **San Diego** | 3.3M | Military presence (Camp Pendleton, Naval bases). Border proximity. Tourism/transient population. |
| **Sacramento** | 2.7M | State capital, government workers, suburban sprawl, agricultural economy to the east. |
| **Fresno–Visalia** | 1.1M | Central Valley agriculture, heavily Hispanic, lower income, high uninsured rate (20.4% statewide). |
| **Bakersfield** | 0.9M | Oil industry, agriculture, trucking corridor (I-5/CA-99). Rural-to-suburban. |

A single "California" ad campaign treats all of these markets identically. DMA-specific creative treats each one as the distinct market it is.

## The Business Case

**The math is simple:**

| Metric | Generic Statewide Ads | DMA-Specific Ads |
|--------|----------------------|-------------------|
| CPL | $100–$150 | $75–$100 |
| Lead-to-intake quality | Lower (geographic/intent mismatch) | Higher (market-matched) |
| Intake-to-sign rate | 5-8% | 10-15% |
| Cost per signed case | $8,000–$14,000 | $2,600–$3,200 |

The difference is not marginal. DMA-specific creative doesn't cost more to run — it costs more to **build**. That's Second Chair's investment. The client gets the benefit without the overhead.
