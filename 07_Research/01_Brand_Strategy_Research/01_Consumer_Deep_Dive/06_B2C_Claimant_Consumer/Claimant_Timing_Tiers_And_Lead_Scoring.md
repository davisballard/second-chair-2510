# Claimant Timing Tiers and Lead Scoring Framework
## Operational Targeting, Messaging, and Lead Quality Model

**Classification:** Consumer Deep Dive — B2C Claimant Research | Operational Strategy  
**Date:** March 2026  
**Authors:** Davis Ballard + Research Synthesis  
**Purpose:** Define the three post-accident windows in which accident victims are reachable, what they look like in each window, what messaging breaks through their barriers, and how timing interacts with injury severity to determine lead quality.

**Cross-reference:** `Insurance_Claims_Process.md` | `Claimant_Decision_Architecture.md` | `Consumer_CEP_Map.md` | `Perfect_Lead_From_Attorney_POV.md`

## Foundational Premise

The personal injury advertising category operates on a false assumption: that getting to accident victims as fast as possible is always the highest priority. This document corrects that assumption.

**The real framework:**

- **Timing** determines conversion likelihood — how likely a lead is to sign with an attorney.
- **Injury severity** determines case value — how much that signed case is worth to the attorney.
- **Neither dimension alone tells the full story.** A 7-day-old soft tissue lead has high conversion likelihood but low case value. A 9-month-old spinal injury has lower conversion likelihood but very high case value. Both matter. They need to be scored and routed differently.

**The 32% who are reachable:**

68% of accident victims accept the first insurance offer without consulting an attorney. These people are gone. Our entire universe is the 32% who don't immediately sign — and within that 32%, there are three distinct windows, each with a different consumer profile, different messaging need, and different lead value profile.

## Special Ad Category Constraint (Read This First)

All personal injury legal advertising on Meta, Google, and major digital platforms falls under **Special Ad Categories**. This is not optional or avoidable — it is platform policy.

**What is locked:**
- Age, gender, income, education, relationship status targeting — all disabled
- Interest-based audience targeting — disabled
- Behavioral retargeting based on demographic attributes — disabled
- Lookalike audiences built from demographic data — disabled

**What remains available:**
- Geographic targeting (state, Market, city, zip code)
- Creative self-selection: the ad itself determines who stops scrolling
- Conversion-signal lookalikes (Meta can find more people who look like those who submitted a lead form — based on behavioral patterns, not demographics)
- Time-of-day and device targeting

**The strategic implication:**

We cannot surgically target accident victims within any specific time window. We reach the right people by producing creative that speaks so specifically to their situation that they self-select. A person who had an accident two weeks ago and sees an ad that says "Before you accept what the insurance company offered..." will stop. A person who hasn't had an accident will scroll. We don't target — we attract.

This is why the 72-hour targeting window is not operational. We can't find those 40–50 newly injured people in LA metro on any given day through Meta ads. What we can do is be the brand that's in their head when they're ready to act — which is what the three-tier framework addresses.

## The Three-Tier Framework

### Tier 1 — The Pre-Decision Window
**Days 0–30 post-accident**

---

#### Who They Are

The adjuster has called. The claim is open. No settlement agreement has been signed. The claimant is in active consideration mode — they know they need to do something, they haven't done it yet.

This is the window where the claim is most fluid and most winnable. The insurance company's frame has been established but the door has not closed.

**Sub-profile A: The Overwhelmed Acceptor-Pending**
Most likely to be in the first 0–14 days. Received the adjuster's call, said they'd think about it, got caught up in everything else. Not actively researching — just busy and in pain. Soft touch needed. Not yet in research mode.

**Sub-profile B: The Active Researcher**
More likely 7–30 days in. Has started Googling. Typed "what to do after a car accident" or "is my car accident settlement fair." Has visited at least one attorney website or lead gen site. In research mode, comparing options, looking for a reason to act or a reason not to.

#### Common Injuries in This Window

Predominantly **soft tissue and whiplash** — the injuries that appear immediately and feel significant but can be minimized by insurance adjusters as non-serious. Also: moderate fractures that are clearly documented but haven't reached MMI yet.

These are **volume injuries** — they represent the largest share of MVA claims. Case values are typically lower ($10K–$75K range for soft tissue; higher for documented fractures), but attorney fees are predictable and the cases are relatively straightforward to litigate.

#### Why They Haven't Acted Yet

1. **The adjuster framing.** The adjuster made it sound like the claim is almost done — "we just need you to confirm a few things and we can get this resolved." The claimant doesn't know they're being maneuvered.

2. **Cognitive overload.** First weeks post-accident involve simultaneous management of pain, vehicle repairs, insurance paperwork, work absence, family logistics. Calling an attorney is the thing that keeps getting pushed.

3. **Identity barrier.** "I'm not the kind of person who sues." This is the most powerful single barrier across all tiers. The adjuster reinforces it by positioning the claim as a simple administrative process, not a legal matter.

4. **Financial pressure to accept.** Bills are starting to appear. The adjuster's number, while inadequate, is real money available right now. The prospect of waiting 12–18 months for a larger number is abstract.

5. **No frame of reference.** They don't know what their injury is worth. They can't evaluate the offer because they have no benchmark. They don't know that the average attorney-represented settlement is 3.5x higher.

#### Emotional State

- Primary: Overwhelmed and anxious
- Secondary: Wanting resolution and normalcy ("I just want to get back to normal")
- Tertiary: Some distrust of the insurance company but not confident in their alternative
- Risk: Approaching the decision as "what's the easiest path out"

#### What Breaks Through

The minimum viable action. Not "hire a lawyer." Not "sue." One free call before signing. This is the only message small enough to penetrate cognitive overload + identity barrier + present bias simultaneously.

**Message architecture:**
- Lead with the consequence of signing, not the benefit of calling: "Once you sign, this claim is closed forever."
- The call reframes: information-gathering, not litigation
- No dollar promises, no victim framing, no legal jargon
- Tone: calm, specific, knowledgeable — not aggressive

**What doesn't break through:**
- "You may be owed millions" — triggers identity rejection
- "Fight the insurance company" — too aggressive for someone who wants normalcy
- "Free consultation" — sounds like every other attorney ad
- Generic victim imagery — they don't identify as a victim

#### Channel and Targeting Notes

- **Meta — Facebook and Instagram (primary, 95–100% of B2C spend):** Creative self-selection is the mechanism across FB and IG. The ad is specific and honest enough that a person who had an accident recognizes themselves in it and stops scrolling. Everyone else scrolls. This is the targeting. Google Search is not in the current plan — cost-prohibitive at this stage, harder to manage campaign-to-campaign. Long-tail keyword content is a long-term consideration.
- **Google Search (not current):** Highest-intent channel for this window in theory — queries like "should I get a lawyer after a car accident" capture active searchers. CPCs in PI are prohibitively expensive for an early-stage operation. May be revisited as the business scales and long-tail keyword strategy matures.
- **The 72-hour window specifically:** Not targetable via paid social — on any given day in an average metro there are 40–50 newly injured people and no platform can surgically reach them. This window is reached via Meta creative self-selection: the claimant who is in Tier 1 and sees a Second Chair ad that speaks directly to their situation will self-identify and stop scrolling.

#### Lead Value Profile

| Dimension | Score |
|---|---|
| Conversion likelihood | Very High |
| Typical case value | Low–Moderate |
| Attorney preference | High volume, reliable |
| Lead scoring priority | Conversion Score: HIGH / Case Value Score: LOW–MODERATE |

### Tier 2 — The Accumulation Window
**1–6 months post-accident**

---

#### Who They Are

Three distinct sub-groups, each requiring different messaging:

**Group A — The Active Treatment Patient (High Value)**
Has a more serious injury (fractures, disc herniation, surgical recommendation, TBI). Is in ongoing medical treatment. The claim has not resolved because their injury legitimately hasn't reached Maximum Medical Improvement (MMI). They may not even have an attorney yet — they've been so consumed by treatment that the legal dimension has been deferred. These are the highest case-value leads available in this window.

**Group B — The Stalled Soft Tissue Claimant**
Had a soft tissue injury. Received a low settlement offer (averaging 23% of actual documented costs) and didn't sign — but also didn't seek legal help. Has been waiting for something without knowing what. Bills are now arriving. The gap between what they were offered and what they owe is becoming visible. Financial anxiety is rising. This group is now more receptive to legal help than they were immediately post-accident because the abstract "future cost" has become a concrete "present bill."

**Group C — The Delayed Presentation Claimant**
This group thought they were fine. Adrenaline and stress hormones suppress pain in the acute aftermath — many victims genuinely feel okay immediately after an accident only to develop symptoms over days or weeks. Whiplash symptoms often peak 24–72 hours after impact. PTSD symptoms may not manifest for weeks. Some disc injuries don't produce significant pain until inflammation develops. This person didn't think they had a claim, then symptoms appeared, and now they're wondering if it's too late. (It's not. It's usually the same window with the same rights.)

#### Common Injuries in This Window

- **Group A:** Fractures with extended recovery, herniated discs (imaging confirmed), spinal injuries (non-surgical but significant), concussion/mild TBI, shoulder and knee injuries requiring orthopedic evaluation
- **Group B:** Soft tissue, whiplash — persistent but not yet resolved
- **Group C:** Late-presenting soft tissue, whiplash, concussion, PTSD

#### Why They Haven't Acted Yet

**Group A:** Treatment is consuming everything. The attorney priority keeps getting deferred. Some have a vague understanding that they shouldn't settle until treatment is done — but they don't have an attorney to guide the process. Some may not realize they can sign with an attorney now and settle later after MMI.

**Group B:** Procrastination reinforced by optimism bias ("I'll feel better soon and it won't matter"). Growing shame at not having acted sooner — which becomes a barrier itself ("it's probably too late now anyway"). Financial stress is motivating but so is inertia.

**Group C:** Genuine uncertainty about whether their claim is still valid. The dominant belief: "I should have done this sooner — now it's too late." (This is factually wrong in most states, but the belief is common and potent.) Validation that delayed symptoms are legally recognized breaks this barrier immediately.

#### Emotional State

- Group A: Fatigued from treatment, beginning to feel financial strain, motivated but stretched thin
- Group B: Frustrated, financially stressed, some shame/regret, "why didn't I do something sooner"
- Group C: Confused about their legal standing, anxious that they've missed their window, relieved when told they haven't

#### What Breaks Through

**For Group A:** Practical, treatment-framing message. "You don't have to wait until treatment is done to talk to a lawyer. Most cases settle after — but your attorney should be involved now." Low friction. Makes the action feel like part of the treatment process, not a separate undertaking.

**For Group B:** Financial gap awareness. Specific numbers. "The average car accident settlement with an attorney is 3.5x higher than without one. Your bills are real. The gap is real. It's not too late."

**For Group C:** Validation + legal education. "Did you know delayed injury symptoms are legally recognized? Most people don't. You may still have a claim." Specific statute of limitations information for their state removes the "too late" belief.

**Shared message thread across all Group B/C:** Loss framing, but not manufactured urgency. "It's not too late — but the clock is real." No scare tactics. Specific information that lets them make an informed decision.

#### Channel and Targeting Notes

- **Meta (retargeting):** By 1–6 months post-accident, Group B and C have almost certainly Googled their situation and visited websites. Meta pixel retargeting can reach them. This is the window where Meta retargeting becomes genuinely operational.
- **Google Search:** Still active — queries shift from "what to do after car accident" to "car accident settlement too low," "is it too late to get a lawyer," "can I sue after accepting a settlement offer."
- **Meta broad awareness:** Group A is still reachable via creative self-selection — the person in ongoing medical treatment who sees an ad that speaks to their situation.
- **Settlement offer moment:** When the insurance company sends a formal written offer (typically 2–6 weeks post-accident for minor injuries, longer for serious), this creates a high-intent window. Loss-framing creative timed to this moment (if possible via retargeting of visitors who reached the settlement-stage content) is the highest-converting available mechanism.

#### Lead Value Profile

| Dimension | Group A | Group B | Group C |
|---|---|---|---|
| Conversion likelihood | Moderate (stretched, harder to reach) | Moderate | Moderate–High (once barrier broken) |
| Typical case value | HIGH | Low–Moderate | Low–Moderate |
| Attorney preference | Very high — serious injury, ongoing documentation | Standard | Standard |
| Lead scoring priority | Conversion: MODERATE / Case Value: HIGH | Both: MODERATE | Conversion: MODERATE / Case Value: MODERATE |

### Tier 3 — The Long-Tail / High-Severity Window
**6 months to statute of limitations (2–3 years depending on state)**

---

#### Who They Are

Predominantly claimants with **severe injuries** — spinal cord damage, traumatic brain injury, major orthopedic surgery, catastrophic injury — who for various reasons have not yet secured legal representation. Also includes: people who genuinely didn't know they had 2–3 years to act, people referred much later by a family member or friend, and people whose symptoms progressively worsened to the point where they realized the original injury was more serious than they thought.

This is the smallest segment in volume but the highest in individual case value. A single Tier 3 lead who converts can be worth more to an attorney than 10–20 Tier 1 leads.

#### Common Injuries in This Window

- Spinal cord injuries with lasting impairment
- Traumatic brain injury (moderate to severe)
- Major orthopedic surgery (spinal fusion, joint replacement)
- Nerve damage with functional limitations
- Injuries that initially appeared moderate but progressed
- Wrongful death cases where family members are only now seeking counsel

#### Why They Haven't Acted Yet

1. **"It's been too long" belief.** The single most common reason. They believe the window has passed. It hasn't — but the belief functions as a real barrier until broken.

2. **Treatment consumed all bandwidth.** Serious injuries require intensive treatment — surgeries, rehab, specialist appointments, adaptive equipment. The legal dimension was genuinely unable to compete for attention while survival and recovery were the focus.

3. **Nobody told them.** The knowledge that the statute of limitations is 2–3 years is not common knowledge. Many people assume there's a 30-day or 90-day window, similar to insurance reporting deadlines. The gap between what people believe and the actual law is the specific opportunity here.

4. **Family member referral — timing is unpredictable.** Many Tier 3 conversions happen because someone in the victim's network learns about their situation and says "have you talked to a lawyer?" The social referral CEP can activate at any point.

5. **Insurance company delay tactics.** In some cases, the insurance company has deliberately prolonged negotiations, denied legitimate claims, or otherwise created a situation where the victim is still without resolution many months later.

#### Emotional State

- Often frustrated and resentful — particularly toward the insurance company
- In ongoing pain or living with permanent impairment
- Financially strained after months or years of medical bills
- Sometimes hopeful when they discover the window hasn't closed
- Potentially angry at having suffered without resolution for so long

#### What Breaks Through

**Statute of limitations education first.** This barrier must be removed before any other message can land. "Most people don't know you have [2 years / 3 years] from the accident date to take action." State-specific is better than generic.

**Validation of their experience.** They've been through something serious and long. The message should acknowledge that before moving to action. People who are suffering don't respond to transactional pitches.

**Real urgency based on actual deadline.** Unlike manufactured "act now" urgency, the statute of limitations creates genuine time pressure for Tier 3 leads. If their 2-year window is approaching, this is real and communicating it clearly is honest and helpful.

**The "your case may be worth more than you think" angle.** For serious injury cases, many Tier 3 claimants underestimate their case value because they've been anchored by insurance low-ball framing for months. Content that educates on surgical case values, TBI case values, etc. can shift this.

#### Channel and Targeting Notes

- **Meta and Google:** Both operational. Creative must lead with statute of limitations education to break the "too late" belief before anything else can land.
- **Content / organic:** Long-form content answering "is it too late to sue after a car accident" and "statute of limitations personal injury [state]" captures this audience in high-intent search moments.
- **Social referral activation:** Shareable content that creates vocabulary for friends and family to refer — "one call to find out if it's too late costs nothing" — enables the social referral CEP that is otherwise uncontrollable.
- **This audience is not high-volume.** The absolute number of Tier 3 leads is smaller. But because single-case values are very high, the economics still work even at lower volume.

#### Lead Value Profile

| Dimension | Score |
|---|---|
| Conversion likelihood | Lower (belief barriers, harder to reach, some may have given up) |
| Typical case value | VERY HIGH (spinal, TBI, surgical) |
| Attorney preference | Extremely high per individual case |
| Lead scoring priority | Conversion: LOW / Case Value: VERY HIGH — requires special routing |

## Composite Lead Scoring Framework

### The Problem with Single-Dimension Scoring

Most lead gen companies score on freshness alone. This creates a systematic error: it deprioritizes the most valuable cases (serious injuries that take months to emerge) in favor of the most easily converted cases (fresh, low-value soft tissue).

The correct model uses **two independent scores**, not one combined score.

---

### Score 1: Conversion Score
**Predicts likelihood that this lead will sign with an attorney**

| Factor | Weight | Scoring |
|---|---|---|
| Accident recency | 60% | 0–14 days = 10; 15–30 days = 8; 31–90 days = 6; 91 days–6 months = 4; 6+ months = 2 |
| Treatment status | 25% | Active specialist treatment = 10; ER + active chiro/imaging = 7; ER + follow-up = 5; ER only = 3; No treatment = 1 |
| Representation status | 15% | No attorney = 10; "Considering options" = 6; Already represented = 0 (disqualified) |

**Conversion Score interpretation:**
- 8–10: Very High — immediate priority routing
- 6–7: High — fast routing, standard intake
- 4–5: Moderate — prompt routing, nurture sequence if no response
- 2–3: Low — automated follow-up, low manual priority
- 0–1: Very Low — disqualify or archive

---

### Score 2: Case Value Score
**Predicts likely attorney fee revenue from this case**

| Factor | Weight | Scoring |
|---|---|---|
| Injury severity | 70% | Wrongful death / spinal cord / paralysis = 10; TBI / major surgery = 9; Fractures + surgery = 8; Herniated disc + ongoing specialist = 7; Fractures (non-surgical) = 6; ER + chiro + imaging = 5; ER + follow-up = 3; Soft tissue only, no imaging = 2 |
| Liability clarity | 20% | Clear (rear-end, head-on) = 10; Probable = 6; Disputed = 3 |
| Insurance status | 10% | Commercial vehicle = 10; Confirmed adequate = 8; Confirmed minimum = 5; Unknown = 3; No insurance = 1 |

**Case Value Score interpretation:**
- 8–10: Very High — significant case, high attorney fee potential
- 6–7: High — solid case, good fee potential
- 4–5: Moderate — standard soft tissue / moderate injury
- 2–3: Low — minor injury, limited recovery potential
- 0–1: Very Low — minimal case value

---

### Lead Priority Matrix

These two scores combine into a priority routing decision:

| Conversion Score | Case Value Score | Lead Priority | Routing Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| High (7+) | High (7+) | **Priority 1 — Premium** | Immediate call, senior intake, high-urgency flag to attorney |
| High (7+) | Low–Moderate (≤6) | **Priority 2 — Standard** | Fast routing, standard intake, soft tissue protocol |
| Low–Moderate (≤6) | High (7+) | **Priority 3 — VIP Flag** | Route with case-value flag clearly visible — lower conversion likelihood but high value if signed; attorney should invest extra follow-up attempts |
| Low–Moderate (≤6) | Low–Moderate (≤6) | **Priority 4 — Standard Queue** | Standard routing, automated follow-up sequence |
| Any | 0–1 | **Deprioritize** | Automated only; low manual resource investment |

**The critical rule:** A Priority 3 lead (old + serious injury) should never be treated as low-priority simply because the accident was months ago. It should be flagged and routed differently — with explicit communication to the attorney that this is a high-value case that warrants extra follow-up, even if conversion is harder.

## Lead Form Field: Accident Timing

### The Design Constraint

The timing question must balance two competing needs:
- **Enough granularity** to tier the lead accurately
- **Low enough friction** that people answer honestly without overthinking

The worst outcome is an overly precise question ("please provide the exact date of your accident") that causes hesitation, estimation anxiety, or abandonment. The best outcome is a natural-language question that people answer quickly and accurately because the buckets feel intuitive.

### Recommended Form Field

**Question:** *"When did the accident happen?"*

**Options:**
- Within the last 2 weeks
- 2–4 weeks ago
- 1–3 months ago
- 3–12 months ago
- Over a year ago

**Tier mapping:**
- "Within the last 2 weeks" + "2–4 weeks ago" → **Tier 1**
- "1–3 months ago" + "3–12 months ago" → **Tier 2**
- "Over a year ago" → **Tier 3**

**Why this works:**
- Natural language phrasing matches how people think about time ("a couple weeks ago" not "14–21 days")
- Five options hit the sweet spot — enough granularity without decision paralysis
- Each bucket maps to a meaningfully different consumer profile and messaging approach
- No exact date required → reduces friction and abandonment

**Implementation note:** This field feeds directly into the Conversion Score (accident recency factor) and determines which messaging sequence the lead enters after form submission.

## Messaging Summary by Tier

| Dimension | Tier 1 (0–30 days) | Tier 2 (1–6 months) | Tier 3 (6+ months) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Primary hook** | "Before you sign anything" | "It's not too late" | "Most people don't know you have [X] years" |
| **Core emotion** | Loss aversion — the door closes when you sign | Financial gap — the bills are real | Relief — the window hasn't closed |
| **Tone** | Calm, urgent, specific | Validating, financially specific | Educational, empathetic, specific |
| **Action ask** | One free call before you decide | One free call to understand your options | One free call to find out if your window is still open |
| **What to avoid** | Victim framing, dollar promises, "fight" language | Shame, "you should have done this sooner" | Manufactured urgency, scare tactics |
| **Injury to reference** | Soft tissue, whiplash, moderate fractures | Bills exceeding settlement, ongoing treatment | Spinal, TBI, surgical (specific to their situation) |
| **SOL mention** | Not necessary | Light touch ("you have time") | Central — this breaks the primary barrier |

## Integration Points

This framework should inform:

1. **Ad creative briefs:** Each tier needs distinct creative that speaks to its specific emotional state and barrier profile. One ad does not serve all three tiers.

2. **Lead form design:** The accident timing question above should be a required field in the quiz funnel, feeding directly into Conversion Score calculation.

3. **Attorney-facing lead delivery:** Conversion Score and Case Value Score should both be visible on every lead delivered. Attorneys need both numbers to allocate their follow-up effort correctly.

4. **Follow-up sequences:** Tier 1 leads get the "before you sign" nurture sequence. Tier 2 leads get the "it's not too late" sequence. Tier 3 leads get the SOL education sequence. These should be distinct automated flows, not one generic drip.

5. **Fair Case brand strategy:** The consumer brand's messaging architecture should reflect the tier the target audience is most likely in. The 60% brand-building budget reaches people before they have an accident; the 40% performance budget targets people in their active tier window.
