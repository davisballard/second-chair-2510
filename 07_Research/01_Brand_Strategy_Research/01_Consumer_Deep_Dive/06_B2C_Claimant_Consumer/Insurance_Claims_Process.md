# How the Insurance Claims Process Actually Works
## Foundational Context for Targeting and Messaging Strategy

**Classification:** Consumer Deep Dive — B2C Claimant Research  
**Date:** March 2026  
**Purpose:** Establish a shared understanding of the claims process lifecycle so that targeting windows, messaging strategy, and lead scoring are grounded in how this system actually functions — not how it appears from the outside.

**Cross-reference:** `Claimant_Timing_Tiers_And_Lead_Scoring.md` | `Claimant_Decision_Architecture.md` | `Consumer_CEP_Map.md`

## The Question This Document Answers

"If insurance companies reach out in the first 72 hours and offer a settlement, why would anyone wait weeks or months? Who passes up free money?"

The answer has three parts:
1. Most people **don't** wait — they take it. Those people are invisible to us.
2. The "free money" is structurally designed to be far less than what victims are owed.
3. The cases that take months aren't slow — **the medical process is slow**.

Understanding all three is essential to understanding who our leads are, why they're still available, and what they need to hear.

---

## Part 1: Who Takes the Money Immediately (And Why They're Not Our Leads)

**68% of accident victims accept the first settlement offer without consulting an attorney.**

This is not stupidity. It is the predictable outcome of six behavioral forces activated simultaneously at the victim's lowest point:

**1. Authority bias.** The adjuster represents an official institution. Their framing — "let's get this resolved for you quickly" — arrives before any competing frame. It is automatically credible.

**2. Reciprocity.** The adjuster calls first, is warm and helpful, and expresses concern. Basic social reciprocity makes the victim feel obligated to cooperate. Refusing feels ungrateful or aggressive.

**3. Present bias.** The first offer arrives when the victim is most financially stressed — medical bills accumulating, income reduced, vehicle repairs pending. A concrete amount of money right now is worth more psychologically than a potentially larger amount in 12–18 months.

**4. Cognitive overload.** In the first days and weeks post-accident, the victim is simultaneously managing physical pain, vehicle logistics, work absence, family disruption, and emotional processing. Calling a lawyer is one more overwhelming task. Most can't find the bandwidth.

**5. Identity barrier.** The dominant cultural narrative around lawsuits is negative. Most working-class and middle-class adults don't identify as litigants. "I'm not looking to sue anybody" is an identity statement, not a legal assessment. The adjuster reinforces this identity by framing the claim as almost complete.

**6. The recorded statement trap.** The adjuster's first call often includes a request for a recorded statement. The victim — in pain, in cognitive overload, socially trained to minimize — typically says some version of "I'm sore but okay." That statement follows the claim through its entire life and is used to minimize compensation.

**The result: the 68% who accept the first offer become satisfied insurance claimants. They never become leads. Our entire addressable market is the 32% who don't immediately sign.**

## Part 2: Why the "Free Money" Isn't Free Money

Initial settlement offers average **23% of total documented expenses** (Benji Personal Injury California study, n=1,200, 2024–25).

This happens by design. The insurance company's business model depends on settling claims for as little as possible. Their adjusters are trained professionals whose job is to minimize payouts. The tactics:

**Speed creates disadvantage.** The faster a claimant settles, the less they know about the full extent of their injuries. A cervical disc injury may not produce its full symptom picture until weeks after the accident. An ER visit on Day 1 that says "soreness and whiplash" doesn't reflect what an MRI three weeks later may reveal.

**The settlement permanently closes the claim.** Once a release is signed, the claim is closed forever. The claimant who signs a $6,800 offer and later discovers their cervical disc injury requires $40,000 in ongoing treatment cannot reopen the claim. The insurance company knows this. The claimant rarely does.

**The settlement math by comparison:**
| Outcome | Average |
|---|---|
| Settlement with attorney | $44,600 |
| Settlement without attorney | $13,900 |
| Multiplier | **3.5x** |

**91% of claimants who accepted quick settlements later found their compensation was insufficient** for long-term medical costs.

The "free money" is free in the sense that the claimant doesn't pay legal fees. But it is not free in the sense that it costs them $30,000+ in unrealized value on average.

## Part 3: Who the Settlement Offer Comes From

This matters for messaging. There are two distinct sources:

**The at-fault driver's liability insurer (most common).** The other driver's insurance company is legally obligated to cover bodily injury damages up to the policy limit. This is the adjuster who calls in the first 72 hours. Their financial incentive is to minimize your payout. They do not represent the victim — they represent the at-fault driver.

**The victim's own UM/UIM insurer (uninsured/underinsured motorist coverage).** If the at-fault driver is uninsured (15.4% of U.S. drivers, higher in some states) or carries minimum limits ($15K in California), the victim's own insurance becomes the primary recovery source. Critically: the victim's own insurance company has the same incentive to minimize payouts. They are not on the victim's side despite being the victim's own insurer.

**Why this matters for messaging:** The framing "don't let the insurance company lowball you" applies to BOTH scenarios. The victim's own insurer is just as adversarial as the other driver's insurer on UM/UIM claims.

## Part 4: Why Cases Take Months (It's Not Procrastination — It's Medicine)

The single most important thing to understand about the timing of leads is this: **cases that take months to resolve are not slow because people are slow. They are slow because the medical process is slow.**

**Maximum Medical Improvement (MMI)** is the point at which a treating physician determines that a patient has recovered as much as they are going to recover. It is legally and strategically critical: settling before MMI means settling before you know the full extent of the injury. No competent attorney settles before MMI.

**MMI timelines by injury type:**

| Injury Type | Typical Time to MMI |
|---|---|
| Acute soft tissue (mild whiplash) | Days to weeks |
| Moderate soft tissue (significant whiplash) | 3–6 months |
| Fractures, severe soft tissue | 6–24 months |
| Back and spinal injuries (non-surgical) | 6–18 months |
| Surgical orthopedic injuries | 12–24 months |
| Traumatic brain injury (mild) | 3–12 months |
| Traumatic brain injury (severe) | 2+ years |
| Spinal cord injury / paralysis | 2+ years |

This means the vast majority of cases that are worth significant money **cannot be closed for months**. An attorney who signs a client with a disc herniation will not submit a demand letter for 6–18 months. The case isn't stalled — it's in treatment.

**The delayed symptom phenomenon** compounds this further. Adrenaline and stress hormones suppress pain signals acutely — victims commonly feel "okay" in the hours after an accident only to experience worsening symptoms over days and weeks. PTSD symptoms may not manifest for weeks. Soft tissue injuries that seem minor at the ER often develop into chronic conditions that appear weeks later. Victims who felt fine at first and settled fast may have unknowingly closed claims that had significant value.

## Part 5: The Full Claims Lifecycle

From accident to settlement check:

**Phase 1: Immediate Aftermath (Days 0–14)**
- Accident occurs
- Police report filed (or not — this matters later)
- ER / urgent care visit
- Insurance adjuster contacts victim within 24–72 hours
- Adjuster requests recorded statement, assesses aggressive pursuit likelihood
- 68% of victims accept initial offer or begin negotiating directly → they disappear from our targeting universe
- 32% hesitate, research, or consult someone → they become leads

**Phase 2: Active Treatment (Weeks 2–18+ months)**
- Claimant is in ongoing medical treatment
- Bills are accumulating
- If represented: attorney is monitoring treatment, waiting for MMI
- If unrepresented: claimant may be negotiating with adjuster or waiting to see if they improve
- Insurance company may send settlement offer during this period to close the claim before the full injury picture emerges
- This is the primary Tier 2 targeting window

**Phase 3: Demand and Negotiation (Post-MMI)**
- Attorney sends demand letter with documented medical bills, lost wages, pain and suffering
- Insurance company has 30–60 days to respond
- Negotiation occurs — 95–96% of cases settle during this phase
- If negotiation fails, lawsuit is filed

**Phase 4: Litigation (if necessary)**
- Filing a lawsuit does not mean going to trial — most cases settle after filing
- Cases that reach trial: 3–5% of all PI cases
- Trial plaintiff win rate: ~61%

**Settlement timeline by case type:**

| Case Type | Pre-Litigation Settlement | With Litigation |
|---|---|---|
| Simple soft tissue | 6–13 months | 12–18 months |
| Moderate injuries | 12–18 months | 18–30 months |
| Back/spinal injuries | 18–24 months | 27–36 months |
| Traumatic brain injury | 24–36 months | 33–48+ months |
| Wrongful death | 12–24 months | 24–48 months |

## Part 6: Where Our Leads Come From in This Process

Our leads are drawn from the 32% of claimants who don't immediately sign. Within that 32%, three scenarios produce the leads we can reach:

**Scenario A: Pre-Signature Research (Tier 1 — Days 0–30)**
Adjuster has called. Claim is open. Claimant is Googling "should I get a lawyer for my car accident" or "what is my injury worth." They are in active consideration mode. This is the highest-intent moment and the window where Google Search advertising is most effective.

**Scenario B: The Stalled Claim (Tier 2 — 1–6 months)**
Soft tissue claimant received a low offer and didn't accept but also hasn't sought legal help. Bills are now arriving. OR: Claimant with a more serious injury is still in treatment and hasn't resolved the claim yet because it legitimately can't close yet. Both groups are now more receptive to legal help than they were at the immediate post-accident moment.

**Scenario C: The Late Discovery (Tier 3 — 6+ months)**
Claimant with serious injuries who didn't know they had 2–3 years to act. Often referred by a family member or friend. These leads are harder to convert but represent the highest case values — spinal injuries, TBI, surgical cases.

**The cases that are NOT our leads:**
- The 68% who accepted the first offer → permanently closed
- People who have already retained an attorney → ethically off-limits
- People past the statute of limitations → legally impossible (2 years in CA and NV; 3 years in CO and WA)

## Part 7: The Special Ad Category Constraint

Meta, Google, and all major platforms classify personal injury legal advertising under **Special Ad Categories** (housing, employment, credit, and issues of social concern — personal injury is classified under the latter in Meta's framework).

**What this means operationally:**
- **Zero demographic targeting available.** Age, gender, household income, relationship status, education — all locked. We cannot target "25–45 year old working adults." We get everyone.
- **No interest-based targeting.** We cannot target "people interested in car accidents" or "recent accident searches." We cannot target people who have visited competitors' sites (interest inference is disabled).
- **Geographic targeting only.** State, Market, city, and zip-code level targeting is permitted. This is our only available precision tool.
- **No lookalike audiences based on demographic attributes.** Lookalikes can be built from behavioral conversion signals (people who submitted a lead form), but Meta cannot use age, gender, or income to narrow delivery.

**What this means for the 72-hour targeting question:**
Even if we could reach someone within 72 hours of their accident, we have no mechanism to identify them as accident victims via Meta targeting. We reach the right people by producing creative that self-selects — the person who just had an accident and sees an ad that speaks to their exact situation will stop scrolling. The person who hasn't had an accident will scroll past. We don't target; we attract.

This is why the 72-hour window is unrealistic as a paid social targeting strategy and why the more achievable windows are the Tier 1–3 framework documented in `Claimant_Timing_Tiers_And_Lead_Scoring.md`.
