## CONTEXT — WHAT TRIGGERED THIS RESEARCH

Davis brought in the agency on 2026-04-23 with this brief: *"do research now into how great overlays in commercials that are fun but readable... at a Super Bowl commercial or at least big game level commercial level — not those shitty graphic overlays of a bad font with a white box absurdly over it... so these text overlay or graphic prompts are elite and are best at communicating to them the message through text."*

Davis later expanded the scope: *"this also includes graphics or charts or other ways to convey the message with something other than just text or coupled with text... animated bar charts or showing how number comparisons or averages or whatever in second chair (this is primarily for second chair). So it can be a lot of things i want to know about all the things other than the video i can do to get people to fill out the darn funnel."*

And: *"obviously look through social media ads! and ask the experts too because there brands that do a great job of this im sure out there that are modern and we are using them specifically in social media ads 90% on meta and facebook."*

And critically: *"these graphics text and stuff [should be] used as a tool when its needed and when it makes it better not just because... its all about everything combining in the right way to get people to fill click and fill out the funnel (the right people that have actually been in an accident, theyll know though if we communicate it well what to do)."*

**Strategic problem:** SC's AI footage is at Super Bowl-tier quality, but the overlay layer was previously white-box-bad-font garbage that defeated the rest of the work. Scrollers don't lock onto "this is an MVA ad" or "why should I care" inside the 1.7s median dwell, so spend is wasted. The framework needs to fix this without becoming the new failure mode (over-decoration, framework-applied-by-default).

**Conversion goal:** qualified case fills from people who have actually been in an accident — not raw clicks, not impressions, not "engagement."

## WHAT SC ALREADY HAS LOCKED (foundation)

Critical finding from the pre-research deep-dive (Explore agent, 2026-04-23): SC's visual system is already 80% locked. The framework doesn't replace anything — it fills the **kinetic / motion / overlay-execution gap** in an otherwise comprehensive system.

### Locked typography stack
- **Tiempos Headline** (Klim Type Foundry) — display / headlines
- **Adobe Caslon Pro** — body / classic
- **Söhne** (Klim) — UI / metadata
- **IBM Plex Mono** — data labels, legal citations
- **Non-negotiable rule:** true optical small caps only, never faux CSS small caps

### Locked color palette (with documented physical-object origins)
- **Cream** `#FFF7F0` — primary background (cotton-rag letterhead reference, Harvard Law Review paper)
- **Burgundy** `#490A0A` — 0.5pt rules, single chart accent, foil only (West Federal Reporter spine)
- **Warm Charcoal** `#1C1917` — primary text
- **Warm Grey** `#6E6862` — captions, labels, exhibit labels (marble courthouse floors, limestone)
- **Oxford Navy** `#0F1E3A` — foreground elements
- **Forest Green** `#163A21` — secondary variant (West state reporter spines)
- **Old Gold** `#E8C165` — physical foil/embossing only (1.6:1 contrast on cream — invisible on screen)
- **Critical rule:** cream is the field, never white. Single-color accent only. No gradients.

### Locked data visualization framework
- **EXHIBIT N: naming convention** — every data panel labeled in Caslon small caps, 9pt, Warm Grey. *"Zero PI lead gen vendors use this. Immediate categorical distinction."*
- **Tufte data-ink discipline** — maximize data-ink ratio, no decoration
- **Direct labeling** — no legends; labels at the data point
- **Conclusion-as-title** (McKinsey convention) — chart titles state the conclusion, not the variable
- **Reference sources documented:** Goldman Sachs Annual Reports (1982-1994, Arnold Saks Associates), The Economist (1988-1994), IBM Annual Reports under Paul Rand, Nigel Holmes / Time Magazine

### Locked behavioral science principles (14 documented)
Includes: scarcity & urgency, loss aversion, social proof, authority, cognitive ease, anchoring, commitment, emotion, identity, choice architecture, memory, time/delay, curiosity gap, pattern interrupt.

### Locked video formats
- **Format A — Motion Still** (5-6s, single image + one motion)
- **Format B — Story Slide** (15s, three 5s clips, hard cuts only)
- **Format C — Luca Maxim Cinematic** (15-20s, premium satirical, movie-trailer execution)

**The locked system gives SC something no PI competitor has.** The overlay framework's job is to make sure the locked system shows up in motion and overlay execution at the same level it's defined on the still page.

SC's existing competitive audit (2026-02) covers: Quintessa Intake, Smith.ai, Settlement Allies, Zero Risk Torts, Growth Legal, BrokerCalls, Martindale-Avvo, FindLaw, Upper Hand Injury, Injury Leads Hub.

**The unified category aesthetic:**

- Photography: suits at tables, lawyers reviewing documents, courthouse stock
- Process infographics: "Step 1 → Step 2 → Step 3" (universal convention)

**Result:** *"The category is a sea of navy blue tech company templates. Second Chair looks like a creative agency run by people who went to law school."*

**Strategic implication for the overlay framework:** there's no elite craft to copy in PI advertising. Morgan & Morgan ($218M ad spend in 2024, 80-person in-house agency) is the only firm operating at national scale, and even their work is ordinary at the design level. **The opening is wide.** SC has a clear lane to be the only elite-craft brand in the category.

## EXTERNAL BRAND SYLLABUS — RANKED BY SC RELEVANCE

The room identified seven brands SC should study, ranked by how directly they translate.

### 1. Lemonade insurance — the strategic analogue (highest priority)

**Why it matters:** Lemonade disrupted insurance — a category drenched in fear and formalwear, exactly like SC's PI category — by going pink+black with prestige type and an in-house creative agency led by Nuno Ferreira (ex-72andSunny on Facebook & Google). Reached 17% of traffic from social vs. ~1% for traditional insurers.

**What to learn:**
- In-house creative discipline (SC is operating the same way with the agency model)
- Tone that disarms: *"part pink soda, part TED Talk, part meme — humor is the differentiator in a category drenched in fear and formalwear"*
- Restraint as the brand mark — they don't fill every frame
- Category disruption strategy: refuse the category template, build a parallel design language

**What NOT to copy:** Lemonade's specific pink-and-cartoon palette is wrong for SC. SC's prestige register is the opposite of Lemonade's playful-disruption register. **Take their strategic discipline (refuse the category template, build a parallel language) without their specific aesthetic.**

### 2. Atticus (legal aid) — the only adjacent legal craft proof point

**Why it matters:** Atticus is a legal-aid platform for disability and workers' comp claims. Their identity work (initial branding by Lazaris) uses rich emerald green and a responsive logo system. They're the only adjacent legal brand doing modern designed work. Proof point that elite craft can exist in legal-adjacent advertising and convert.

**What to learn:**
- Designed-not-templated identity in a benefits/legal category
- Responsive typographic logo that works across scales
- Modern register that doesn't sacrifice institutional credibility

**What NOT to copy:** the emerald palette and the disability/workers'-comp specialty don't transfer. SC is in a more conventional PI lane and needs a different palette (already locked: cream + burgundy). **Use Atticus as proof that elite craft works in legal-adjacent; don't copy their specific look.**

### 3. Public / Robinhood / Cash App — the in-feed data-viz playbook

**Why they matter:** These three pioneered the contemporary in-feed financial data-viz aesthetic. One stat per spot, one color, animated reveal as the punchline. Built specifically for sound-off vertical scrolling.

**What to learn:**
- One number per spot — never three or five (the discipline that prevents stat-block clutter)
- Single accent color on a flat field (matches SC's burgundy-only rule)
- Animated reveal *as the punchline*, not as decoration
- Numbers comprehend faster than words — lean on $, %, ×, vs. signs

**For SC's "Hero Number" pattern:** this is the direct lineage. The $150K vs. $2.5K gap is exactly this pattern, currently rendered as generic stat-block but should be rendered as McKinsey-grade exhibit + Apple-keynote-grade reveal.

### 4. Liquid Death — counter-reference (irreverent type as identity)

**Why it matters:** Liquid Death built a $1.4B brand on TikTok with gothic typography and irreverent copy that parodies traditional beverage marketing. Type IS the identity, not labeling on top.

**What to learn:**
- Type carries the brand identity, not just the message
- Tone consistency across every asset (the gothic/metal aesthetic is everywhere)
- Anti-template stance in a templated category

**What NOT to copy:** the irreverent / metal / gothic register is wrong for SC's prestige register. **Use Liquid Death as a counter-reference: their commitment to type-as-identity is the principle; their specific tone is the wrong tone for SC.** SC's equivalent is institutional-prestige type-as-identity, not irreverent.

### 5. Spotify — best-in-class motion-type-as-ad in feed

**Why they matter:** Spotify's animated Story ads pulse type to a beat, with playlist names and artist images in rapid succession. The animation works entirely without sound, relying on visual rhythm and color transitions.

**What to learn:**
- Sound-off legibility (the type carries the meaning entirely without VO)
- Rhythm without dependence on audio sync
- Type that pulses *with the music's bpm* even though the user can't hear it (the visual rhythm signals the audio rhythm)

**For SC:** the kinetic cadence rule (5-6 phrases at 2-3s each, bottom-center) should be paced to the same kind of internal rhythm. Pull this discipline into the production room as a check: *"would this read as designed-rhythm if the user couldn't hear the VO?"*

### 6. Magic Spoon / AG1 / Ridge — the DTC craft register

**Why they matter:** These are the craft-register DTC brands. Magic Spoon for cereal, AG1 for greens powder, Ridge for wallets. Each has clean kinetic ingredient lists, percentage reveals, and aspirational restraint.

**What to learn:**
- Kinetic spec lists rendered with editorial discipline
- Numbered reveals as visual rhythm
- The aspirational register without overwrought production

**For SC:** when an SC ad needs to list multiple credentials / case-types / settlement averages, the Magic Spoon / AG1 spec-list discipline is the reference. Not for hero numbers (that's Public/Robinhood) but for supporting data presentation.

### 7. Borrowell — animated data-viz for finance, soft-narrative format

**Why it matters:** Borrowell's animated credit-building explainer demystifies a complex financial concept through reassuring narrative + clear 2D motion graphics. Data viz used as story, not as chart.

**What to learn:**
- Data viz rendered with narrative pacing
- Soft motion that serves comprehension, not decoration
- Complex financial concept made accessible without losing rigor

**For SC:** when an SC ad needs to walk a viewer through a process (case timeline, settlement journey, what insurers do), Borrowell's narrative-data-viz approach is the closest reference.

## THE 5 MODERN META-NATIVE CRAFT PATTERNS (Indigo's pull)

Reframed as design patterns that apply to BOTH static and motion overlays. The patterns are about *design principle*, not about whether the element moves.

### Pattern 1 — Type IS the visual (caption-first, not caption-after)

The 2026 benchmark from Motion (550K ads, $1.3B in spend across Sept '25 → Jan '26): *"text is no longer just for captions — it is the motion."*

Top-percentile ads design the type FIRST and the footage second. The text doesn't sit on top of the visual telling you what you're looking at — the text IS the visual.

**Implication for SC:** flip the production order. Don't generate gorgeous AI footage and bolt overlays on. Plan the overlay treatment AS the design moment, then generate the footage to support it. (This applies even when the spot is mostly footage and uses few overlays — the overlay moments are designed first, footage built around them.)

### Pattern 2 — Sound-off literacy

Median dwell is 1.7s. Sound is off until proven otherwise. Hook rate target above 30% (Motion's "winning creative" threshold).

**Implication for SC:** the ad must be 100% legible without audio. The overlay layer carries the meaning; VO is bonus. The first 1.5s has to land WITHOUT audio.

### Pattern 3 — One number, designed

Top DTC accounts (per the Performers' analysis of 40K ads across 11 nine-figure brands) lean on a single hero number per spot. Public / Robinhood / Cash App pioneered this in-feed data-viz playbook.

**Not** three bars and a legend. **One** bar, one number, the rest is white space. Borrowell does it for credit scores, AG1 for ingredients, SC's $150K vs $2.5K gap is exactly this pattern.

### Pattern 4 — Designed-in, not pasted-on

Spotify's animated Story ads pulse type to a beat, and the type lives IN the same color/light system as the album art — they share lighting, grain, motion blur. Liquid Death's gothic typography lives in the same world as the can.

**The amateur tell:** the overlay obviously sits on a separate plane from the footage.

**For SC AI workflow:** generate overlay graphics as stills in ChatGPT Image 2 with SC's locked palette / typography, then animate (when motion is needed) in Kling on a chroma background, then key into the spot. The graphic is generated with the same image system as the footage, so they share material logic.

### Pattern 5 — Restraint as the differentiator

Lemonade built $4B+ valuation in part by refusing the navy-blue insurance template. Patagonia's static type-only ads on Meta outperform animated production. The Apple/Acord school subtracts where everyone else adds.

**For SC:** restraint IS the brand mark. The 95% of PI advertising operating at noise-level is the opening. Going quiet, going designed, going restrained reads as the only adult in the category.

## THE HARD FEED PHYSICS

Non-negotiables for any spot that ships:

| Constraint | Threshold | Implication |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| Median dwell time | 1.7 seconds | Frame 1 must communicate (a) what this is about, (b) why this person should care |
| Hook rate (3s plays / impressions) | Target >30% | First 1.5s is the comprehension lock |
| Hold rate to midpoint | Target >50% | Body has to deliver on the hook's promise |
| Sound state | Off until proven otherwise | Overlays carry the meaning, not VO |
| Aspect ratio | 9:16 vertical | Bottom 20% reserved for UI; type lives in upper-middle 60% |
| Clicks (CTR) | >1.5% cold, >2.5% retargeting | Disciplined ads pass; over-decorated ads usually don't |

**Indigo's pressure-test verdict:** the framework's discipline rules — Indigo's >30% hook-rate gate, McCabe's brevity-as-filter, Massimo's "two design moves max per frame" — collectively make over-decoration impossible by construction. Disciplined ads convert qualified leads well; over-decorated ads convert everyone badly.

## THE "DESIGN-FIRST, MOTION-WHEN-IT-SERVES" PRINCIPLE

**Critical reframe from Davis mid-session:** the framework was originally tilted too motion-first. Davis corrected: *"it can be motion but it doesn't need to be always kinetic — it just has to be highly designed and evoke the right things, some can be motion but it doesn't need to be."*

**The corrected principle (which the framework now leads with):**

1. Most SC overlays should be **static** by default. A beautifully composed static overlay does the comprehension/credibility work as well as a kinetic one — often better, because there's nothing to wait for.
2. Motion is one tool — used when it adds **meaning** (narrative tension, reveal-as-punchline, structural transition between scenes), never as decoration.
3. The static-or-motion test: if motion doesn't add narrative meaning, the static version is the right answer.

**This principle is documented in:**
- The framework itself (lead section)
- The Designed Overlay Reference README (gating principle for the library)
- The Saul Bass entry (his static work documented at the same depth as his title sequences)
- The memory entry for the SC production room

The framework refuses motion-as-default. The Hero Number pattern defaults to a static weighted drop; the Burgundy Rule pattern defaults to a static placed rule; the Exhibit pattern defaults to a static composition. Motion variants exist for each, used when motion earns its place.

## WHAT'S NOT BEING STUDIED (and why)

- **95%+ of PI / mass-tort / legal advertising.** No elite craft to copy. Studying the category average would push SC toward the navy-template noise level. The opening is to refuse the category template, not to imitate it.
- **Generic motion graphics tutorials and "best motion graphics 2026" listicles.** These showed up in early web research but contain almost no actionable craft signal — they're aggregated portfolios of execution-level work without the discipline frameworks behind them. The Motion app benchmarks were an exception (they have actual conversion data).
- **Super Bowl-only references.** The Super Bowl reference pool is the *aspirational* register but not the *operational* register for SC. SC lives in feed at scroll pace, not in a 30-second broadcast slot. The Super Bowl reel is the bar; the Meta-native syllabus is the working playbook.
- **General TV/film title sequences not in the editorial-motion lineage.** Most title-sequence work is too long-form, too musical, too cinematic to translate to a 1-2 second SC overlay beat. Patrick Clair and Saul Bass are studied because their *discipline* transfers; most title-sequence work doesn't.

## THE THREE SC-SPECIFIC SIGNATURE PATTERNS

These emerged from cross-referencing SC's locked system (Tiempos / Caslon / cream / burgundy / Exhibit naming / Tufte data-viz) with the modern Meta-native craft register (one number / sound-off / restraint / designed-in).

Each pattern has a default **static** form and a **motion** variant for when motion adds meaning.

### Pattern 1 — The Exhibit
- **Static (default):** Exhibit label + chart composition rendered as a single still. Cream background, EXHIBIT N: in Caslon small caps top-left, Tiempos headline beneath, single-burgundy-bar chart, IBM Plex Mono labels, 0.5pt burgundy rule separating label from chart.
- **Motion variant:** "Exhibit Reveal" — 1.5s chroma-key path. Patrick Clair living-photograph discipline. EXHIBIT label fades up, burgundy rule draws across, chart bars fill in to final state in two beats.
- **Reference lineage:** McKinsey + Tufte + IBM under Paul Rand + Patrick Clair (motion).

### Pattern 2 — The Hero Number
- **Static (default):** Single hero number ($150K, 30%, $0, etc.) in Tiempos Headline with generous tracking, white or warm-charcoal on cream, no other elements. Bass distillation discipline.
- **Motion variant:** "Number Drop" — 0.8s blend-mode path. Number resolves from 8% blur to sharp over 0.6s, tracking tightens 5%, holds. When narrative tension calls for the resolve.
- **Reference lineage:** Apple keynote comparison reveals + Bass distillation + Public/Robinhood/Cash App in-feed data-viz.

### Pattern 3 — The Burgundy Rule
- **Static (default):** Thin 0.5pt burgundy rule placed deliberately as a structural divider on the frame. One placement per frame max.
- **Motion variant:** "Burgundy Rule Draw" — 0.5s blend-mode path. Rule draws left-to-right across the frame, locks, no re-animation. When used as a transition between scenes.
- **Reference lineage:** West Federal Reporter spine + Vignelli grid discipline + Bass title-sequence rule-draws (motion).

Full specs and Higgsfield-ready prompt templates for all three live in `../../../02_Visual/Designed_Overlay_System.md` §6 and §7.
